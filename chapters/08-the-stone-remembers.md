# The Stone Remembers
*Chapter Eight — The Last Fragment*

---

Korroc had been awake too long, and the part of him that mattered most was the part that ached.

It was not his own body he meant. His body ached the ordinary way — the bitten thigh, the claw-lines under his mail, the slow grinding fatigue of a man past fifty in full plate who has held a wall for a night with no end to it. He could set that aside. A smith learns young to keep working through the small complaints of his own hands. No, the ache that mattered was the other one, the one that did not belong to him at all.

It ran through him on a thread he had not known he could spin.

It had happened in the lower rooms, in the middle of the zombie-fight, when Nageru had taken a blow and Korroc had *felt* it — not heard it, not seen it, but felt it land, a flicker of cold fire low in his own chest where no blade had touched him. He had gone still inside for half a heartbeat, startled by it. Then he had understood, or thought he had, and he had reached toward it the way a man reaches toward a warmth he can feel but not yet see — stretched something out of himself, some line of his own grace, toward each of them, Nageru and Thane and Caleth both. An offer. *Let it come to me instead. Some of it. Whatever you can spare.*

And they had, without knowing it. The bond had simply answered, the way the fire-form had answered in the library, the way the stone-strike had answered before that — something in him finding a new shape it had always had but never been asked to wear. A measure of every wound drawn away before it could settle in them, finding him instead. He had carried it since, and it had become, in the long hours of the night, the truest map he had of where his people were and how badly they were holding.

Right now the thread was quiet. Tired, but quiet. They were all still standing.

He looked down the upper hall they had bought too dearly and rolled the new hammer once in his grip — the adamantine, the First Crusade weapon, the metal that drank light instead of throwing it. It had become the balance of his arm so fast it frightened him a little. *Their fathers' songs' metal.* He did not let himself follow the thought all the way down. Not yet. There was a door at the end of the hall.

"One more," he said. "Then we'll see what we've truly come for."

---

The door was locked — an honest lock, Thane's kind, and the dwarf knelt to it and made it open the way he made everything open, with patience and a little contempt for the men who thought a lock would stop him. Then Thane stood, and considered, and reached down and took up a dead tiefling by the collar, hauling the limp red thing upright in front of himself like a man holding a coat to the light.

Korroc watched him do it and said nothing. It was an old habit of his cousin's, that — a dead man for a shield, sour and practical, a joke that was also a precaution. *If something's waiting to bite the first face through, let it be a face already dead.* There was a grim grin somewhere under Thane's beard. Korroc did not begrudge him it. You took your small mercies in a place like this where you found them, and a Stonevein's mercies had always run dry and dark.

The room beyond did not bite. It only sat there and offended.

It had been a place of the faithful once. He could read that much in the bones of it the way he read the bones of any room — the swept lines, the focus to one wall, the worn place where the devout had knelt. But the dead in it had been Iomedaeans, and they did not rest as the dead should. They stood. Half a dozen of them, robed and rotted, their skin gone gray and split, their eyes the flat shine of things worked by another's will. And in the center of the room, set into the floor, an alabaster basin — pale, beautiful, the kind of stone a craftsman chose with love — and laid into the basin, with a patience that turned Korroc's stomach worse than any wound, were entrails. Arranged. Coiled into the white bowl like an offering, like a reading, like a thing a foul priest spreads to learn the future from a body that should have been put gently in the ground.

The stone in his veins recoiled from it. The basin had been good stone, holy stone, and they had made an altar of it for butcher's work, and the part of Korroc that was a son of Torag before he was anything else felt that as a personal grief.

"Zombies," Caleth said behind him — the professional note, flat and certain. Then, quieter: "Someone used them as part of a ritual. Look at the basin. Look at what they put into it."

Korroc had already looked. He did not need to look again.

"Saint's mercy," Caleth breathed, and this time it was not the professional note at all, only grief.

The corrupted dead came on.

They were slow — death had taken the quickness out of them and left only the hate. Korroc met the first one with the new hammer and it was not a fight so much as a labor, the grim work of putting down what should already be down. The adamantine head came round in a flat hard arc, a smith's stroke, all shoulder, and it took the lead zombie across the skull and the skull simply stopped being a skull. The thing folded. He set his boots and let the next one come into his shield.

Behind him the others moved into their places without a word, the way a good crew falls to a well-known job. Caleth's glaive ran its pale light past Korroc's shoulder and opened a robed corpse from collarbone to belt. Nageru went through two of them with bare hands, no anger in it, the violence clean and answering, the way it always was with him. Thane was a knife in the gaps, here and gone, putting his mother's blade into the soft seams the dead forgot to guard.

Korroc felt one of them get past his shield — a gray hand, raking, finding the claw-lines already opened in him. The hum in his chest did not move, because the wound was his own this time and so there was no thread to carry it. He grunted. He brought the hammer down. The thing came apart at the shoulder and dropped, and then there was the strange clean quiet of a fight finished, the kind that always made his ears ring with the absence of noise.

The basin sat there in the middle of it, fouled, waiting.

Caleth went to it. The half-elf knelt at the white stone and did not pray over the filth in it — he washed it. A word and a gesture, and a sudden clean weight of conjured water came down into the bowl, sluicing the offering out of it, the entrails and the dried blood and the whole sick reading carried away in a gray flood until the alabaster was only alabaster again. Then Caleth filled it. Clean water, brimming, still.

The water held a picture.

Korroc came to stand at Caleth's shoulder and looked down into it, and the stillness of the surface was not the stillness of water but of a held breath, and the picture in it was not a reflection of the fouled room above but somewhere else. A chamber bright with a sick light. Two pedestals, lit, and on each a helm — broken, both of them, the metal of them caved and split, and even cracked and dim in a vision in a bowl, Korroc's smith's eye snagged on the *make* of them and would not let go. *Good work. Old work. Familiar work.* He could not have said why the word familiar rose in him. It rose anyway, and sat there, unanswered, a flaw he could feel in the metal of his own thinking without being able to see it.

There was a woman in the picture, twisted by something, one of her hands grown into a claw that was not a hand. And standing over the bright room, wings folded against its back, the heavy horned head of a bull set on a body that was a man's and worse than a man's, a thing of muscle and malice keeping watch.

"That's above us," Korroc said. The stone in him agreed. He could feel it now that he looked for it — the weight of another floor over their heads, the held breath of the heart of the place. "That's where we're going."

"That's where the fragment is," said Caleth, very quiet. He looked at the broken helms in the water a moment longer than the rest, and something passed over his face that Korroc could not read, and then the half-elf rose and let the water in the basin go still and empty again.

---

There were rooms yet between them and the stair, and the garrison was not done offering up its horrors.

The next door let into a chamber where something waited that should not have been able to exist. A stag, or the ruin of one — a great demonic thing, antlered, its hide hanging wrong on a frame too long, eyes like coals banked low and hungry. A peryton, Caleth named it under his breath, and Korroc did not know the word and did not need to. He knew a defilement when he saw one.

It came for them fast, faster than a thing that size had any right to move, and the first thing it did was the thing Korroc would not forget — it lunged past all of them for the dead tiefling Thane had hauled in as his shield, and it took the corpse out of his cousin's hand and tore the chest of it open and *ate the heart.* Just like that, in the middle of a fight, a quick foul feeding, as if the dead thing's heart were a thing it wanted more than it wanted any of them.

Then it turned its banked-coal eyes on Caleth, and Korroc saw the half-elf's shadow *stir* — saw it peel a hand's width up off the floor, drawn toward the stag-thing like iron filings toward a lodestone, the creature reaching not for Caleth's body but for the dark Caleth's body cast. Trying to take his shadow. Trying to pull some piece of the man out of him sideways.

"*No,*" Korroc said, the way he said it to a weld that would not take, and he put himself between the half-elf and the thing.

The hammer came up and came down. The peryton was quick but it was not armored, and adamantine cares nothing for quickness once it lands; the first blow staggered it and the stolen shadow snapped back flat to the floor where it belonged. Nageru was already moving on its flank, two strikes, the heel of a palm and a fist, and Caleth's glaive came in low and bright and opened the long wrong belly of it. It screamed once, a sound like a stag and a man and neither, and then Korroc's hammer found its skull and the screaming stopped. The coals in its eyes went out.

The thread in Korroc's chest gave a flicker — Thane, a graze somewhere, nothing — and went quiet again.

"It ate the heart," Thane said, looking down at the corpse the thing had robbed and the corpse the thing had become. He sounded almost philosophical about it. "Out of a dead man. In the middle of a fight."

"Aye," Korroc said. "There's no understanding them. There never has been." It was an old refrain of his, that, worn smooth as a river-stone from a lifetime of turning it over. He had grown up baffled by the men who served such things and now that he was older he was still baffled. Demons stole. Demons defiled. Demons ate the hearts of the dead in the middle of trying to kill you. There was no door in him through which a man could walk to *choosing* that. He had given up looking for it years ago and only the grief of the looking remained.

---

The room after held a goat. That was the closest his eye could come to it — a thing on two legs with a goat's horned head and a goat's slot-pupiled eyes and a man's cruel cunning behind them, a cultist of the demon-prince whose name Korroc did not say aloud because his mouth did not want the shape of it. Baphomet's. It fought with a wicked curved blade and a low animal patience, and they put it down between the four of them, hard and fast, the hammer and the glaive and the fists and the knives, and Korroc took a cut across the forearm for it that the thread did not carry because it was his own.

The room after *that* held flies.

Demonic ones, fat and droning, big as a man's fist and worse, boiling up out of the corners of a chamber that had clearly once had some clerkly purpose — pigeonholes in the walls, a long writing-desk, the racked detritus of a place where messages came and went. Nageru stepped past Thane the moment the door opened, moving into the room without a word of preamble. He surveyed the droning things for half a breath. "Demons," he said, with the flat certainty of a man who has spent a lifetime learning what deserved that word and what did not. "They don't stay." His fists came down on the nearest one before it had finished turning to face him. The fight, such as it was, was on — a nuisance more than a threat, but a nuisance with teeth, and they had to be killed the same as anything, swatted and crushed and burned where Caleth's light could reach them, until the droning stopped and the air was only foul instead of alive.

Thane looked at the pigeonholes when it was done. "Messaging room," he said. "This was where their letters came. Their birds." He surveyed the empty racks, the fly-fouled desk. "Pigeons, most likely. Eaten, I'd wager."

Korroc huffed something that was almost a laugh, the first in a long while, and it surprised him to find he still had one in him. "Of course they were."

The stair stood at the far end. It climbed up into the dark that breathed — the heart of the place, the bright sick room the water had shown them, the helms and the woman and the winged bull.

It was time to go up.

---

Thane spoke a blessing over all of them before they climbed — a low Dwarven cadence, old words, the kind that settled into the bones rather than the ears. Caleth and Nageru each uncorked a small vial and drank, and the skin of their hands and faces took on the texture of bark, a hardness that was not armor but answered for it. Then Thane uncorked one of his own, and the air closed over the place he'd been, and he was simply gone.

Then Thane went up alone, and unseen.

Korroc watched his cousin go from a dwarf to an absence, the air closing over the place he'd been — and then there was only the soft report of careful boots on stone, climbing, and then not even that. They waited at the bottom in the breathing dark. Korroc set his shield. Felt the weight of the floor above them, the held breath of it, and under his own boots felt the deeper stone, the bones of Kenabres, the bones his Stonelord blood had begun, this past handful of days, to feel as *his.* He set his palm flat to the cold wall while he waited. The wall set its weight against his palm. They knew each other, the stone and the stone in him, and it steadied him the way a hand on a shoulder steadies a man.

Time stretched. Then Thane was back, not the sight of him, but the feel. He came to Korroc's side and did not speak. He only reached out and pushed against Korroc's shoulder. Once. Then again. Then a third time.

The old sign, from the work and from before the work, the one their fathers had used. Three pushes was the count of the enemies above. 

Korroc went still.

Korroc nodded once. He gave Thane time. Time to go up again, and Korroc followed, slow and silent for a dwarf in plate, which was its own kind of labor — every footfall set down soft, the ancestor-weight in his boots made light by will alone.

He cleared the top of the stair and the bull-thing saw him and put a horn to its lips and *blew.*

The note of it filled the upper hall like cold water filling a lung — a warning, long and brazen, and Korroc felt it more than heard it, the warning that said *they are here, they are upon us, wake.*

Nageru went over the railing.

He cleared it in a single motion, vaulting the banister while the horn-note still hung in the air, landing already in motion, and the nearest mirror-breaker had half a breath to register the barefoot aasimar coming at it before the kick connected — a flying strike, all the momentum of the vault behind it. The Abrikandilu's eyes went wide and it gibbered something in a language none of them knew. Then the fist followed. The demon sat down hard.

Caleth's voice came from the stair behind — low, focused, the cadence of a word with weight in it — and something appeared in the air beside the minotaur. A glaive, translucent, the pale cold light of conjured faith made solid, hovering at head-height with no hand to hold it. It swung at the bull-thing's neck and the minotaur twisted away just enough, the conjured edge passing through the air where its throat had been. But its attention moved. A fraction. Enough.

And then the bull-thing was on Korroc.

It was huge. Bull-headed and winged and armored in its own corrupt hide, and it carried a great axe, and the axe came at Korroc in a series of strokes so fast and heavy that the world narrowed to the small bright work of surviving them.

First stroke. He took it on the shield. The sunburst of Iomedae rang like a struck bell and the blow skated wide.

Second stroke. The shield again, his whole stubborn forge-built weight set behind it, the impact jarring down his arm and into his spine and the borrowed grace of the steel turning the edge.

Third stroke. The shield once more, and this one nearly drove him back a pace, his boots grinding on the stone, the stone holding him, the stone in him holding him.

Fourth stroke — and this one came round low and the shield was high and the axe found him, bit into the side over the hip where the plate gapped, a long hot tearing line. He grunted. He did not give the half-step back. *Three turned, one true. I'll take that trade all day.* He set his boots and brought the adamantine hammer round in answer, a forge-blow, all shoulder.

The conjured glaive swung again. This time it connected, pale light flaring where it bit into the corrupt hide, and the minotaur flinched from it — turned its horned head toward the thing that had no throat to threaten in return.

And Thane was there.

His cousin had come up behind the thing while it spent itself on Korroc's shield and Caleth's hovering blade — *I will be the wall, Caleth will be the distraction, and you will be the knife behind* — and Thane went to work on the bull-thing's flank with both blades, his mother's knife and his father's, fast and close and savage, the seams under the great corrupt arm where the hide gave way. The minotaur could not face three ways at once. Korroc's hammer from the front, Caleth's conjured glaive from the side, Thane's daggers from behind — it bellowed and staggered and went down, axe ringing on stone, the brazen horn rolling away across the floor.

The mirror-breakers came with it. The first was already staggered from Nageru's opening strike, and Nageru put it down with two more — methodical, the way he always put things down, not angry, just answered. The second had turned its attention to Caleth when the bull-thing fell, claws raking, and Nageru came across the hall in a leap, a flying kick that snapped the creature's head around. It didn't fall. He brought both fists down and it did. Then the upper hall was quiet, and held, and theirs.

Korroc pressed a hand to the axe-wound at his hip and let a little grace into it — a wash of golden warmth, the Smith's mercy, just enough to close it from a wound to an ache. He had learned long ago to spend grace on himself the way he spent coin, grudgingly, only what the job required. There was a more important door yet to open and he meant to have something left in him for what stood behind it.

---

The trophy room's door was barred from within.

Thane took out the chime — the plain silver thing from the old cache, the one that had dropped the bar at the garrison's front door a lifetime ago at the bottom of this same long night — and he struck it against the iron, and the long pure wrong note went into the wood and found the bar on the far side and asked it, politely, to stop being a door's enemy. Korroc heard the held weight let go. The bar slid free in its cradle. The door was a door again.

He went in first. There was always one more soul in him that he thought he might yet save, and tonight that flaw in him very nearly broke on what waited.

There were three things in the trophy room. A woman in red, tall and corrupted and beautiful in the way a blade is beautiful before it opens you, carrying a scythe whose curve caught the sick light. Two dretches at her feet, squat and gray-green and stupid and hard to kill. And on a pedestal at the room's heart, in a cage of barbed black iron, a chunk of stone.

"That stone in the cage," Caleth said behind him, quiet and certain, "is the wardstone."

It glowed. Softly, whitely, a clean light, the only clean thing in the room — and Korroc's blood knew it before his mind did. The stone in his veins leaned toward it the way a tired man leans toward a hearth. *Wardstone.* The last fragment of the Kenabres wardstone, the great holy bulwark that had held the border for an age, hacked down to this one barbed-caged sliver, and even caged, even broken, even one piece out of the whole, it sang to the stone in him a note so pure it hurt.

He stepped toward it, and the room hit him.

The woman's scythe came round and found him — the long curved blade catching the gap under his arm and laying it open deep. And the dretches were on him both at once, claws raking, and one of those found a true line too, deep, and a third blow landed that should by rights have put a lesser man on the floor — three killing strikes inside a single breath, the kind of arithmetic that ends most men.

It did not end Korroc.

The Stonelord in him answered. He felt one of the wounds simply *fail* to land the way it meant to — the stone-flesh under his skin turning the deepest of the three, his body refusing the blow the way an anvil refuses a hammer that's struck it wrong. He staggered. Blood ran hot down his side and his arm. He swayed and the stone under his boots held him and the stone in him held him and he did not fall.

"*Korroc!*" Thane, behind him, the thread in Korroc's chest flaring — no, that was his own pain, all his own, the bond carrying nothing because the wounds were his to bear.

"Standing," he ground out. "Get the cage."

Then the room came alive around him, his people closing in.

Nageru crossed the floor like a thrown spear and took the nearest dretch's head clean off with a single strike — Korroc saw it go, saw the gray thing simply *end*, decapitated by a bare hand, and even through the haze of his own blood it struck him as a wondrous and terrible thing.

And Caleth — Caleth did not come at the woman or the dretch. The half-elf set his eyes on the caged stone and the world *folded.*

That was the only word Korroc had for it. One instant Caleth stood at the door; the next a seam opened in the air, a fold, a crease in the substance of the room, and the half-elf stepped through it and was suddenly *there,* beside the pedestal, beside the barbed cage and the glowing fragment, the whole length of the trophy room crossed in the space between heartbeats.

Korroc held the woman and her scythe. That was his work now — be the wall, be the thing she has to deal with, buy the half-elf his moment. The scythe came at him and he caught it on the sunburst shield, and another dretch raked him, and he set his boots and bled and held.

He watched Caleth across the room, because he could not reach him and so all he could do was watch.

The half-elf drew the rod.

Korroc knew the thing for what it was — the rod of cancellation, the tool they had carried into this hell for exactly what Caleth intended, the one stroke that would unmake the wardstone fragment and deny it forever to the powers that wanted it corrupted. A tool. He understood tools. He watched Caleth thrust it through the barbed bars of the cage toward the glowing stone, watched the point of it reach for the fragment —

— and watched it *stop.*

Not stopped by the cage. Stopped by the stone itself. Korroc could not feel what Caleth felt, but he could read it off the half-elf's face the way Thane read a lock — the resistance, the *pushback,* the wardstone fighting the rod, near-living, refusing to be unmade. Caleth's arm trembled. The fragment glowed brighter, white and stubborn and old, and it did not want to die, and it pushed back against the tool that had come to kill it.

Korroc watched Caleth set his jaw.

He saw the half-elf's lips move — and he could not hear the words across the room and over the fight, but he knew the shape of prayer when he saw it, and he saw something more, something that made his own old heart turn: Caleth was praying *twice.* Two prayers braided into one, two names called at once. Shelyn and Iomedae, the half-elf's two private loves, the beauty and the sword, called together in a single breath while he leaned his whole weight and his whole faith onto the rod.

And he pushed it through.

---

The room went gold.

There was no warning and no edge to it — one instant the trophy room and its filth and its fight, the next a sun, a forge-heart, a furnace door flung wide, and the gold came out of the broken wardstone in a single soundless shout that picked Korroc up bodily and threw him.

He felt the floor leave his boots. He felt the wall meet his back. He felt the stone shards of the exploding cage tear past him, and he saw — in the long slow way the worst and the best things both arrive — the barbed black iron come apart, shredded, and the shards of it pass *through* the woman in red and the surviving dretch as if they were made of cloth, and the two of them simply *cease,* unmade in the golden shout, gone.

And then the gold did not stop.

It should have. An explosion ends. This did not end — it *gathered,* and Korroc felt the stone in him stand up and tremble, felt every fragment of wardstone along the whole vast Mendevian border, every brother-stone of this one, all the way to the edges of the world, feel the hole that had opened where the Kenabres fragment had been. And through that hole, all of them poured what they held.

An age of stored holy power. A hundred years of crusaders' faith and dying angels' grace and the patient consecrated weight of the longest war the world had known, all of it draining at once through the wound in the border, all of it rushing inward, all of it pouring down through the hole and *into them.* Into the four of them, the nearest living vessels to the breaking, the ones standing in the heart of it.

Korroc glowed.

He looked down at his own hands — the hands he had sat and stared at in the Blackwing Library when the fire had gone out of them, when they had been just hands again — and they were not just hands now. They were lanterns. Gold ran through him, through his blood, through the slow deep river of his grandfathers' blood in his veins, through the stone in him, *lighting* it, and every wound he wore closed at once. The axe-line at his hip. The scythe-gash under his arm. The dretch-claws, the long night's whole bitter ledger of hurt — all of it sealed in a single wash of grace so total it was less like healing and more like being *forged anew,* a blade drawn out of the quench true and whole and ringing. Every spell he'd spent came back into him. The thread in his chest, the bond to the others, flared and steadied and sang with it.

And he could not move.

None of them could. They hung in the gold, stunned, struck still as struck iron, and the power that had filled them was too vast for their small bodies to hold and do anything else but *hold* it. And while they hung there, the gold began to show them things.

---

It came as visions, and Korroc lived each one as if he stood inside it.

The first was a making.

He saw a high place in a city he knew young — Kenabres, but Kenabres long ago, the Kite jutting out over the chasm under a clean sky no demon had yet fouled. And on that high place, a *making,* the kind of making his whole faith was built to revere: the first wardstone, raised, set, consecrated by a ritual of terrible beauty. There was an angel at the heart of it. A golden winged thing with a halo not of light but of small swords, circling its head, turning slow. The Hand of the Inheritor. Korroc had heard the name in temple-songs and now he stood, in the gold, and watched the Hand raise the first of the great stones with a craftsman's care that made the smith in him want to weep — *good work, the best work, the work of saving a world.* The year sat in his mind like a date stamped in metal. Four thousand six hundred and thirty-nine. The first stone. The beginning of the bulwark.

The second was a burning.

The same high place, decades on, and the sky less clean. A man stood before the wardstone with fire in his hands and fire in his eyes — Hulrun, the name came to Korroc and sat sour in his mouth, the same Hulrun whose body he had knelt by at the garrison's doors and tried, fool that he was, to call back to grace. And around Hulrun, in the courtyard of the Kite, before the holy stone, they were *burning people.* Dozens of them. Bound and screaming and named witches, fed to the flames in the very shadow of the wardstone meant to protect them, and Korroc felt the old baffled grief rise in him again, the grief that had no door in it — *this, too, was done in the name of holding the line.* The Second Crusade, lit from this fire. Faith and cruelty so tangled that even the gold could not pull them apart.

The third was a battle.

A storm came to the Kite. A great winged thing of lightning and wrath, vast, kingly, terrible — Korroc knew it before the name arrived, knew the shape of the nightmare that the crusade's children were raised on. Khorramzadeh. The Storm King. The Balor Lord. He came against the wardstone with a sword in his hand and he *struck* it — struck the holy stone a blow that should have shattered a mountain.

The stone barely cracked.

The sword shattered instead.

Korroc, hanging in the gold, felt the stone in him *roar* with savage joy at that — the wardstone holding, the sky-tyrant's blade flying to pieces against the patient consecrated rock. And then a silver shape fell out of the high air onto the Storm King, claw and breath and fury, and Korroc's heart turned over because he knew her too, knew her better than any of them, had seen her fall — Terendelev, younger here, whole here, nearly ending the Balor Lord then and there before he fled the field. The Fourth Crusade. The year stamped itself in metal. Four thousand six hundred and ninety-two.

*The Fourth.*

Something in Korroc went very still inside the gold, because the Fourth Crusade was the one that had taken his father.

And the fourth vision came.

---

It came as a chase.

The Kite again, the courtyard before the wardstone, and through it ran two figures, both armored, both bleeding, the hunter and the hunted. The hunter Korroc knew at once — a half-orc in paladin's plate, jaw set, blood on her, running down her quarry with a grim relentless faith he would have known anywhere. *Irabeth.* Unmistakable, the same iron in her, chasing a man across the holy ground.

And the man she chased—

A dwarf. Burly, thick through the shoulders, a fighting dwarf in good plate, a hammer in his fist. He backed against the wardstone as Irabeth came on, and where his corrupt back touched the holy stone, *smoke rose,* and he screamed — the stone burning him, naming him for what he had become, a thing the wardstone rejected. He should have died there, with the stone burning him and the paladin's blade coming down.

He did not.

He dodged Irabeth's stroke — a quick brutal sidestep for a man his size — and brought his hammer round low and *smashed her knee.* Korroc heard it even in the vision, the wet ruin of it, and Irabeth went down, the knee gone, the paladin folding onto the holy stone with her quarry standing over her. The dwarf raised the hammer to finish her. He was a hand's breadth from it. Korroc, helpless, hanging in the gold, watched a man cripple Irabeth and lift a hammer to kill her—

—and then soldiers came. The thunder of them, rushing the courtyard, forcing the dwarf off her before he could land the blow. He cursed. He turned. He spread his arms and a thing answered him — a fiendish wasp, vast and droning and wrong, summoned out of the air — and he climbed onto its back and it bore him up and away, north, abandoning Kenabres, abandoning the field, fleeing with the wardstone's brand smoking on his back.

The name came with him. The vision laid it in Korroc's mind the way the others had laid their dates and their angels. *Staunton Vhane. Leader of the Hammers of Heaven.*

Korroc, in the gold, heard the name and turned it over and felt something he could not yet place — a snag, a flaw, a piece of metal that did not sit right in the die. He had heard that name. Where had he heard that name? A letter. Thane, reading a letter, days or hours or a lifetime ago, in a room with a sword in a case. *Lord Staunton.* A burly dwarf who served the Worldwound and crippled their ally and fled north to Drezen. Korroc filed it the way Thane would have, cold and exact, *Staunton Vhane, here, real, an enemy* — and the vision moved on before the snag could become anything more.

Two more came.

A demoness placed the fragment in the trophy room — beautiful and obscene, a thin tail, clawed hands, horns curling up from where her eyes should have been and nothing in the sockets but the horns themselves. Minagho. Korroc had named her once off a letter and here she was, setting the last broken sliver of the Kenabres stone into this very room. And beside her, another, working a *wish* into the barbed iron cage, building it around the fragment to hold it for the work to come.

And then the last vision, and it was the worst, because it was a thing that would never happen and showed them exactly why it mattered that it never would.

Areelu Vorlesh. He knew her too, now, off the same letters — one of the hands that had opened the Worldwound. She stood in the trophy room with a crystal in her arms, a Nahyndrian crystal, deep nauseating purple, the size of a man's head, and she lifted it to the caged fragment, and Korroc understood in a single sick flood what they had nearly let happen. The crystal touched the stone. The fragment did not break — it *corrupted,* and the corruption ran out of it the way the holy power had just run *into* him, out through the hole and along the whole border, stone to stone, all of them flashing at once with a foul mauve fire. And in that fire, every crusader drawn to the border — every soldier the crusade had gathered to hold the line — every one of them *transformed.* Twisted. Made half-fiend in an instant, the whole vast army of the faithful turned into slaves of the thing it had come to fight.

*This was the plan.* The knowledge landed in Korroc with the weight of a dropped anvil. The demons had not drawn the crusade to the border by accident. They had *gathered* them there, to be standing in the blast when the wardstone was turned, to maximize the harvest. Every faithful soul on the line, meant to become a slave at a stroke.

And they had stopped it. Caleth's rod, Caleth's braided prayer, Caleth's set jaw — the stone broken clean instead of corrupted, the power poured out as grace instead of as a curse. They had reached into the heart of the demons' great design and broken it with a tool and a faith and a single stubborn push.

The vision let him go.

---

And then the gold gave Korroc one more thing, and it was for him — for him and for Thane, the two of them, and no one else.

It came back to the chase. The burly dwarf. Staunton Vhane. But this time the gold did not show Korroc the name or the deeds or the wardstone smoking on the man's back. This time it showed him the *face.*

And the face was a face Korroc knew.

Not as a name he had now heard. Not as an enemy he had now learned. As a *face he had carried his whole life* — a face from before any of this, from the long ago, from the time when his father was still a living warmth in the world and not a grief worn smooth. He knew that burly dwarf. He had seen that face at his father's table. He had seen it in the company his father kept, the war-band, the dwarves who dreamed together of a sky-city lost — Khar-Zadûn, the song that had animated all of them, that had pulled them north again and again. Staunton Vhane had been *one of them.* Part of the team. A face in the company of Korroc's father and Thane's father both, the doubled grief, the doubled inheritance.

And it was the last face.

That was the thing that came down through Korroc like a piece of long-cold iron suddenly plunged red-hot into the quench — the recognition finding its mold, the snag of metal at last dropping into the die it had been cut for, every flaw he'd felt in his own thinking resolving into one terrible true line. *Staunton Vhane was there when our fathers left for the last time.* The last mission. The Fourth Crusade. The disaster that broke a generation of dwarven crusaders and took his father and Thane's father and never gave them back. And the man who had been with them — the man who had ridden out with them on that last road — was the same man the wardstone had branded and rejected, the same man who had crippled Irabeth, who had served the very enemy their fathers died fighting, who had fled north to Drezen on a fiend's wings.

The man who may have killed their fathers.

The stone in Korroc's veins did not roar this time. It went *still* — the deep stillness of bedrock, the stillness of a thing that has stood a long time and will stand a long time more and has just been given a new and permanent weight to bear. The forge in him knew what kind of work this was. Not the white-heat of rage. Something colder and slower and far more certain. The patient work. The work that does not care how long it takes. The shaping of iron toward an end you have decided on and will not unmake.

Then the gold went out of all of them at once, and the stun broke, and Korroc came back into his own body kneeling on the trophy-room floor with his hands flat on the stone and his face wet and the whole weight of it sitting in him like ore in the deep, and he did not say a word.

---

Irabeth found them.

She came through the broken door of the trophy room. She went from one of them to the next, shaking them, dragging them up out of the stun's last clinging grip. She'd been outside the garrison, watching for them to come out, and when they had not come out she had come in. Alone.

"What are you doing here?" Caleth asked her, gray-faced, the gold still fading from his skin.

"As long as you've known me," Korroc said, and his voice came out rougher than he meant it, scraped raw by visions, "you don't see it? She wasn't going to risk others." He looked at the half-orc paladin and the two griefs sat side by side in him, hers and his. "She came alone because she would not spend anyone else's life on it."

"That stubbornness," said Thane flatly, from where he knelt, "is not something to be proud of."

Korroc scoffed. It was the most ordinary thing either of them had said in an hour, and he was grateful for it past words.

He had no time to be grateful long.

The air at the room's heart curdled. A shape formed out of nothing — translucent, vast, a woman bat-winged and horn-browed and terrible, glowing red eyes set in a face that was beautiful the way the woman with the scythe had been beautiful, beautiful and *furious.* Areelu Vorlesh. Not in the flesh — a projection, a sending, the witch reaching across whatever distance held her real body to look upon the wreck of her great design. And her fury filled the room like the bull-thing's horn had filled the hall, cold and brazen.

She raised a hand, and Korroc felt the air try to leave him.

It was a spell to *suffocate* — he felt it close on his throat and his lungs, felt his companions all clutch at the same invisible thing, the witch trying to murder all four of them with a thought. And it *failed.* The dying wardstone energy still ran in their blood, the last guttering of the power that had filled them, and it would not let her in. The air came back into Korroc's lungs. The witch's fury redoubled.

"*No matter,*" she said, and her voice was the worst thing in a night full of worst things, smooth and final. "The death throes of the stone protect you. They cannot protect you from my slaves."

And she tore the world open.

A rift split the air of the trophy room, a wound in the substance of the place, and through it Korroc saw the deeper dark and the things in it — and his old baffled childhood hate woke and stood up in him at the sight. Babaus first, half a dozen of the tall thin clawed horrors tumbling through. And beyond them, through the rift, *worse* — the great vulture-shape of a vrock, the four-armed bulk of a glabrezu, and farther back, vast and many-armed and serpent-tailed, a thing Korroc's eye refused to hold for long. A marilith. The full weight of the abyss, leaning toward the open door.

Then the wardstone's last gift saved them.

The power that had filled them, draining now, pulsed one final time — out of the four of them, back into the breaking world, a last shout of the same gold that had thrown them across the room. And it did to the rift what it had done to the cage. The hole slammed shut. The greater demons beyond it were cut off mid-lunge, the door closed in their faces. And the feedback of it, the magical violence of a rift torn and sealed in the same breath, tore *back* up the connection into Vorlesh's projection — and Korroc watched the translucent witch *break.* Wings shredding. Bones snapping in the sending of her, the projection coming apart, severed, gone, the witch flung back to wherever her true flesh waited, broken for the reaching.

Six babaus remained, trapped on this side of the closed door, and they came.

---

The babau fight was the strangest thing Korroc had ever stood inside, because in it, his people stopped being able to die.

He understood it the moment the first claw opened him. A babau came in low and fast and raked across his side, a wound that should have been deep and lasting — and it *closed.* Not healed in the slow grace-fed way he was used to. *Closed,* almost before it had finished opening, the mythic energy still running through him sealing the hurt nearly as fast as the claws could deal it. He felt it happen on the thread, too — Nageru took a bite, and Korroc felt the flicker of it in his own chest, and then felt it *unmake itself,* the pain arriving and departing in the same instant, a wound that healed before it had finished hurting.

It was uncanny. It was almost obscene. He swung the hammer and took a claw and the claw's work undid itself and he swung again, and the four of them waded into the babaus with the strange light feeling of men who have, for one impossible stretch, been freed from the cost of the work.

The babaus were nasty. They were always nasty — claws and bites and that ropy horrible quickness — but they could not keep up with the healing, and they could not stand against the four of them with the cost taken out of the fighting. Caleth spread his hands at one and something that should have been fire came out wrong — not flame but cold, a rime of ice in the shape of burning hands, the half-elf turning his own spell inside out on the strength of knowing what demons were made of and what bit them. The nearest babau took it square in the body and the ice found purchase where fire would not have, and it staggered. Korroc's hammer finished the argument. Two went down hard under ice and hammer and glaive and fists, unmade, gone.

And then the survivors understood, in their animal way, that the door had closed behind them and the men in front of them did not bleed, and they tried to run.

One by one. A babau broke for the broken door and first Nageru's fists and then Korroc's hammer caught it mid-flight and ended it. Another tried to slip past Caleth and Radiance hummed, then the demon ran past Thane and met both daggers and folded. One got past them all, down the hall and away into the dark, and Korroc let it go because there was a thing in the room he wanted more than a fleeing demon. One simply *vanished* — teleported, the babau-trick, gone to wherever they go.

And the last one fell, and Korroc was already moving.

Because Irabeth was down.

Vorlesh's spell — the suffocation that had failed to kill the four of them, full of the wardstone's grace — had not failed against *her.* She'd had no shield of mythic power in her blood; she was only a half-orc paladin who had walked into a garrison alone, and the spell had taken her, and she had dropped in the chaos of the rift and the babaus and Korroc had not seen her fall.

He saw her now. He crossed the room and went down on his knees beside her, heedless of his own armor and the filth and the dead, and he put his hands on her, and he let the grace pour — the Smith's mercy, gold and steady, into the half-orc who had come alone so no one else would have to. His hands shook a little. He did not let them shake the grace.

"Come on," he said, low, in his beard, half a prayer and half just a man asking. "Come on, lass. You don't get to do this. Not after all that."

Her eyes opened.

Korroc let out a breath he felt like he had been holding since the gold first threw him across the room. It went out of him long and slow, and with it went a measure of the night's whole crushing weight, just a measure, just enough.

"You're a fool," he told her, gently. "Coming in alone."

"Takes one," Irabeth rasped, "to know one." And Korroc laughed — really laughed, rough and surprised — and helped her sit up.

---

The room, when there was time at last to look at it, had things in it worth carrying.

The shards of the shattered wardstone fragment lay scattered across the floor and embedded in the walls where the explosion had driven them, and Korroc found, going to one and laying his palm against it, that they still *pulsed.* Faintly. A last guttering thread of the holy power, the stone in him recognizing the stone in them, weak now but not gone. He gathered them with a care that the others, watching, did not interrupt. They were not done being precious, these pieces. He did not know yet what they were for. He knew enough to keep them.

And then he found the helms.

They were in another room, just past the door, where the room's true purpose had displayed them — two helms on the pedestals from the vision in the basin, the broken helms, the ones whose make had snagged his smith's eye in the water. He went to them and went down on one knee and picked the first one up, and turned it in the dim light, and the caved metal of it sat in his hands and *spoke* to him in a language he did not quite have the grammar for.

"This can be fixed," he said, half to himself. His thumb found the line of the break, the place where the good metal had failed, and the smith in him was already there, already at the bench, already reckoning the heat and the hammer-work it would take to draw the dent and true the line. Good work, broken. He could no more leave it than he could leave a soul unsaved. He frowned at it, and the frown was not for the damage. It was for the snag, the flaw in his own knowing, the familiarity that would not resolve. "I feel like I should know more about this helmet." He turned it again. The feeling did not answer. He let it lie, the way Thane would have, cold and exact and kept. He packed both helms into his kit, careful, beside the pulsing shards.

And then Thane was beside him.

His cousin had crossed the room while Korroc knelt over the helms, and he stood there a moment, and when Korroc looked up Thane's face was the still controlled face it always was, the door-shut face, the one that gave nothing away to anyone but the one man in the world who had spent a lifetime learning to read it.

"Cousin," Thane said. "Did you have the vision about Staunton?"

Korroc met his eyes.

There was a whole life in the space before he answered, and neither of them filled it with words, because words would only have been a clumsiness laid over a thing they both already held whole. The face at their fathers' table. The last road. The doubled grief. The man who had been part of the company and was now the brand-burned servant of everything that company had died to fight. They had carried the same loss their whole lives, side by side, rarely spoken and never spoken lightly, and now they carried the same answer to it, and there was nothing to explain.

Korroc nodded. Once.

That was all. No more was needed. Thane held his eyes a moment longer, and something passed between the two of them that was older than the garrison and older than the crusade and older than either of them — and then Thane nodded back, the same single economical nod, and the matter was sealed between them like a weld drawn true. Filed. Locked. Carried.

---

They left the garrison together.

Irabeth told them, on the way, what they had half-guessed — that she had broken her orders to come. That she'd been outside the garrison's wall when the whole border had flashed at once, every wardstone along the line lighting up together, and demons across the whole front had begun to scream — and that was how she had known something vast had happened, and that was the moment she had stopped waiting and gone in. She did not apologize for the breaking of orders. Korroc did not expect her to. He understood it bone-deep, the stubbornness that would not spend another's life on a thing it could spend its own on instead. It was a flaw they shared, and he would not have her without it any more than he would have himself.

Before they reached the garrison's doors, Caleth stopped and turned to Irabeth. "Lord Hulrun is dead," he said. "He was here. At the doors. Demons wearing his skin." He said it plainly, the way he said hard things, without softening and without cruelty.

Irabeth was quiet a moment. Whatever she felt about the man — and Korroc suspected it was complicated, a paladin's grief over a leader who had spent his life both defending and defiling the very faith he served — she kept it behind her eyes. Then she nodded once, the same economy Korroc recognized from the cousins. "He deserves a proper burial," she said. "Whatever else he was."

"Aye," Korroc said. "He does."

Outside, in the gray ruined daylight, there were soldiers.

They knelt at Lord Hulrun's body — the man Korroc had tried and failed to call back to grace at these very doors, the same man he had just watched, in a vision, burn the bound and screaming in the shadow of a holy stone. He looked at the body and felt the old baffled grief one more time and could not parse the man, in the end, any better than he could parse a demon. Irabeth spoke quietly to the soldiers, and they gathered Hulrun up for proper burial, whatever he had been. Others went into the garrison to bring out the armory's arms for the city that bled for every blade.

And then they went back to the inn, and there was dinner, and there was bed, and Korroc lay down in his dented plate-creased body and let the long night finally close over him.

---

He dreamed, and in the dream a woman came to him.

She was scarred and she was beautiful, both at once, the way a much-used blade is beautiful, and she wore plate that had seen every war there was, and she moved through his sleep with a warmth that was not the heat of his forge but kin to it. He knew her before she reached him. Everyone in the crusade would have known her. The lady of the dawn and the sword, the Inheritor, walking among sleeping men to thank them with her own face.

Iomedae.

She went to each of them in the dream — Korroc felt it more than saw it, the way you feel the others through the thread, knew she touched Caleth and Nageru and Thane each in turn, thanked each of them for their kindness and their service, told each of them, gently, that she would not be able to help them in the days to come, that this was the last of her reach. And to each she gave a touch of blessing, a kiss laid on the brow, a single grace pressed into them like a maker's mark stamped quiet into the base of a finished thing — Caleth's faith deepened, Nageru's stillness made surer, Thane's edge given an edge he would carry the rest of his life. One touch each. No more. The last gift of a goddess at the end of her arm.

Then she came to Korroc.

She looked at him, and she smiled, and the smile had a warmth in it that reached the cold place in him the visions had opened.

"I am honored," she said, "that you carried my shield in the defense of my garrison."

Korroc could not have answered if his life had hung on it. He had borrowed her sunburst with a borrower's care, meaning to give it back, meaning to ask leave before he wore another god's mark beside his own — and here was the goddess herself, in his dream, calling it an honor.

Her smile deepened. It went, almost, to a grin.

"But having a son of Torag carry the sunburst of Iomedae," she said, "will not do."

And then the dream let him go.

---

He woke before the others, the way he often did, in the gray of the morning after.

He went to his gear, and he found the shield where he'd set it — the borrowed shield, the Iomedae sunburst, the steel he'd carried through the whole long defilement of that place with respect and an intention to return it.

The sunburst was gone.

In its place, worked into the face of the steel in a craftsman's hand finer than any living smith Korroc knew, the metal reshaped clean and seamless as if it had always been so, was the hammer and the anvil of Torag.

His god's mark. His own. Given to him in the night by the lady of the dawn, because a son of the Father should not carry a daughter's sun — and because she had wanted him to keep the shield, and had found the one way to give it that would let him bear it without a borrower's guilt the rest of his days.

Korroc went down on both knees on the cold floor of the inn, and he set the shield before him, and he bowed his iron-banded head over the hammer and anvil that had not been there the night before, and he gave thanks. To Torag, for the work and the making and the keeping of his word. And to Iomedae, careful and full, the second prayer braided to the first the way he had watched Caleth braid his at the wardstone — thanks to a daughter-goddess at the end of her reach, who had taken a moment she did not have many of left to spend, and spent it being *kind* to a stubborn dwarf and his shield.

His eyes were wet again. He let them be. There was no shame in a man weeping over a gift in an empty room before the others woke.

---

Later, when the inn had stirred and broken its fast, the two dwarves sat with Irabeth, and they talked about Staunton Vhane.

It was Thane who carried the talk, because it was Thane's way, the careful exact questions, one laid after the next like a man setting pins. Had she known, before, that Staunton was evil? No, she said. Only after, only when the evidence came to light. He had been a crusader once, the leader of a famed company, and the rot in him had not shown until it was far too late. She had tried to take him *alive* — that was the chase the wardstone had shown them, the courtyard, the ruined knee — tried to take him alive to learn what he knew, what he served, what the shape of the betrayal truly was. And he had crippled her and fled instead, north, to Drezen, and the Worldwound had swallowed him and kept him.

Did she have more she could tell them, Thane asked. Was the Eagle Watch's hiring of him — Thane's own quiet contract, the thing Korroc only half knew — was that connected to any of this?

No, she said, to both. She knew no more than she had told. The hiring was its own separate thread.

Both dwarves nodded. Both thanked her. And then Thane made the only declaration he was going to make about the whole of it, and he made it the way he filed everything — without heat, without a raised voice, a thing set down and locked, more final for its quiet.

"When we find him," Thane said, "he is a dead man."

It was not rage. Korroc, who knew him, heard exactly what it was: a resolution filed and bolted shut, the way Thane shut every door he meant to keep shut. The hunter who does not jump but who, once the circuit is closed and the thread pulled and the name made true past all doubt, does not let go again. *No Stonevein has ever sheathed a wet blade.* There was a blade in Thane now that would not be sheathed dry, and Korroc carried its mate.

Korroc said nothing. He only nodded, the same single nod they had traded over the broken helms, and the stone in him settled around the new weight and made it part of the bedrock, permanent and patient and certain.

North. Drezen. A burly dwarf with a wardstone's brand smoking on his back, who had sat at their fathers' table and ridden out with them on the last road and come home when their fathers had not. The man who may have killed them. The man who served the thing that had killed a generation of his own people's sons.

A name that was, now, a target.

Korroc set his new shield on his arm — Torag's hammer and anvil, gift of the dawn-lady, carried with a clean conscience at last — and he hefted the adamantine hammer of the First Crusade, the metal of the lost sky-city, his fathers' songs' metal, and he felt the stone in his veins and the forge in his heart and the long cold patient certainty between them, and he knew exactly what kind of work came next, and exactly how long he was willing to take to do it.

The book of Kenabres was closing. The garrison was theirs. The wardstone was broken clean instead of foul, the great design undone, the city's worst day weathered and survived.

But the work was not finished. The work had only, this morning, found its true and final shape.

North. Drezen. And a man there who did not yet know that two sons of a doubled grief had finally seen his face.
