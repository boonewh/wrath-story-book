# When the Sky Fell on Kenabres
*Chapter One — The Hammer in the Deep*

The forge had been a furnace for three weeks straight.
Korroc wiped soot from his brow with the back of a thick-knuckled hand and surveyed the morning's work, a row of newly-tempered blades cooling on the rack, their edges catching the lantern light like rows of grim, hungry teeth. Beside him, Thane drove the hammer down one final time on a buckler's rim, the ring of steel on steel echoing through the smithy with the steady rhythm that had been their lullaby and their war-drum since the first commission had come in for Armasse.

"That's the last of them," Thane said, setting the hammer aside and rolling his shoulders. His chain shirt jingled softly beneath his leather apron. "If another farmer comes asking me to sharpen his grandfather's sword, I'll tell him to use it as a doorstop."

Korroc grunted a laugh. "You'd not. You'd take his coin and grumble about it after."

"Aye. But I'd grumble thoroughly."

The cousins shared a look, the kind of look only kin who had been through fire together could share—and then, by unspoken accord, untied their aprons and hung them on the iron pegs beside the door. Outside, Kenabres was already dressed for celebration. Banners snapped from every cornice and gable, the red-and-gold of Mendev flying alongside the white sunburst of Iomedae and the older, half-forgotten sigils of Aroden, dragged out of trunks by old men who still remembered what it had been to have a god you could name. Children ran past with wooden swords. A baker shouted at a thief. Somewhere, a fiddle was being tuned with the grim seriousness only a frontier town could muster.

Armasse had come.

It had been a holy day for scholars once, in the time before the Worldwound split open like a wound that would not close. A day for priests and historians to gather and study the lessons of wars long ended. But Aroden's death had changed everything, and Armasse with it. Now it was a day for commoners to be taught the grip of a spear, for boys to be named squires, for new priests to be ordained and, far too often, sent straight to the front. The jousts and the duels and the mock-battles had grown up around the older rites the way ivy grows around a gravestone, until you could scarcely see the stone beneath. But the people did not mind. On the front line of a war that never ended, you took your laughter where you could find it.

Korroc ran a hand down his beard, tugging at the iron-banded braids, and adjusted the haft of the warhammer at his belt. His shield, with the great anvil-and-hammer of Torag enameled across its face, hung from his back. Full plate clanked at every joint as he walked—he was a man who had long since stopped noticing the weight, the way a sailor stops noticing the roll of a deck. Thane fell in beside him, lighter on his feet, eyes already tracking every alley and rooftop the way his eyes always did, even on a feast day.

"Try to enjoy yourself, cousin," Korroc said.

"I enjoy myself by watching for the knife in the crowd."

"That's not enjoyment, that's paranoia."

"In Kenabres, the words are interchangeable."

They came out of the smith's quarter and into the broad sweep of Clydwell Plaza just as the sun climbed toward its zenith. The cathedral of Saint Clydwell rose at the plaza's eastern end, all soaring buttresses and weather-stained saints, and against its façade a wooden stage had been raised for the opening ceremonies. The crowd was already a press of bodies. Farmers and crusaders, half-elves and humans and the occasional dwarf, mothers holding small children up on their shoulders so they might see. Korroc and Thane shouldered their way to a good spot near the cathedral's steps, where the carved stone of Saint Clydwell's robe rose at their backs and the stage stood close enough to see the gray in the speakers' beards.

Noon came on the bells.

The crowd hushed as Lord Hulrun himself climbed the stage, his armor catching the sun in such a wash of light that some in the front rank had to shield their eyes. He looked old, Korroc thought—older than he had any right to look, an inquisitor who had outlived three wars and was halfway through a fourth. But his back was straight, and when he raised his hands the silence deepened, every voice in the plaza falling away as if some greater hand had laid itself across the city's mouth.

He cleared his throat to speak.

He never spoke.

The light came from the wrong direction.

That was what Korroc would remember after. Not the explosion, not the heat, not even the screaming. The light. The sun was overhead, where the sun was supposed to be, but suddenly there was a second sun rising in the west, brilliant and blood-red and wrong, and Hulrun's shadow stretched long and warped across the cathedral's face like the shadow of a man hanged from a gibbet.

Then the sound caught up.

It was not thunder. Thunder was a clean thing, a god's hammer falling cleanly on the anvil of the sky. This was the sound of the world being unmade, a roaring, shrieking, grinding bellow that hit the chest before it hit the ears, that rattled teeth in their sockets and stones in their mortar. The earth bucked. Korroc staggered, his shield slamming against the cathedral wall behind him, and Thane grabbed his arm to keep them both upright.

To the west, where the Kite had stood since the founding of the city. Where the wardstone had burned with its white, holy light against the demonic tide for a hundred years. There was nothing. Nothing. In the place where the fortress had been, a column of fire and lightning and black smoke climbed into the sky, blooming outward at its crown like some monstrous, infernal flower.

The wardstone was gone.

A roar split the air. Different, this one, a sound Korroc knew the way a child knows its mother's voice. A great silver shape erupted from the crowd itself, scales catching the doubled, ruined light, wings unfurling so vast they cast half the plaza into sudden shadow. Terendelev. Kenabres's guardian, the ancient silver dragon, who had, Korroc realized with a start that bordered on awe, been standing among them in human shape, watching the ceremonies as a citizen.

She climbed.

And the thing that met her in the sky was a thing that should not have been.

It stood three times the height of any man, were a man to stand beside it, and its skin was not skin but a writhing skin of fire and crawling lightning. A flaming sword burned in one fist; a whip of living flame coiled in the other. Its face was a horror Korroc would not be able to describe later, only feel, a presence so heavy with malice that the air itself seemed to thicken around it, like the air around a forge an instant before the bellows blew.

"Khorramzadeh," Thane breathed, the name barely a word, barely a sound. "Storm King. Torag preserve us."

A balor lord. A balor lord in the sky over Kenabres.

The dragon and the demon came together with a crash that shook the stones beneath Korroc's boots. Korroc had seen battles. He had seen war. He had seen dwarven thanes go sword-to-sword with orcs in the deep places. He had never seen anything like this. Terendelev was a storm of silver and lightning, her claws raking, her breath a freezing scythe. The balor met every blow and gave back two for each. His sword bit deep into her flank, and silver blood—silver blood—rained down across the plaza. His whip wrapped her wing and pulled, arresting her charge mid-flight, and then the sword came around again, and again, and Korroc heard himself shouting something, prayer or curse or both, his hammer somehow already in his hand.

Then they were falling.

The dragon and the balor, locked together, plummeted from the sky in a tangle of wing and flame and silver scale and the great shape of Terendelev came down across the cathedral's façade with a sound like the world's heart breaking. Stone shattered. Saints toppled from their niches. Dust and rubble fountained outward, and somewhere a bell, the great bell of Saint Clydwell, tolled once on its broken mounting and went silent.

The plaza erupted.

At the far end of the square, the earth ruptured upward. Something crawled out of the rupture—something the size of a watchtower, wreathed in smoke, its face a ruin of teeth and eyes. Buildings fell where it stood. Buildings fell where it did not stand, simply because it was there. And from the place of its emerging a long, jagged crack ran outward across the plaza like a scar opening on a man's face, and the crack ran toward Korroc and Thane.

There was no time to run.

The stones beneath Korroc's boots gave way. He had a single instant to hear Thane's shout—"Korroc!"—and to feel his cousin's hand close around his pauldron, and then the world simply was not there anymore. They were falling. The plaza, the cathedral, the doubled sun and the burning sky, all of it shrank upward away from them, growing small, and the dark grew large.

Above them, through the closing wound of the rift, Korroc saw the last thing he would see of the surface for he did not yet know how long.

He saw Terendelev, broken on the ruined cathedral, raise one bloodied talon. He saw her lips move, shaping arcane syllables he could not hear. He felt something catch him, gentle as a mother's hand, and he knew that even dying, even with the Storm King's shadow falling across her, the silver dragon had spent her last magic on the strangers tumbling into the dark.

Then he saw Khorramzadeh stride forward.

Then he saw the burning sword fall.

Then he saw the dragon's head tumble from her shoulders and the rift slammed shut above him like a coffin lid, and the light of the world was gone.

Darkness.

True darkness. The kind that is not the absence of light but the presence of something else, a velvet weight pressed against the eyeballs. Korroc lay on his back in cold rubble, breathing—breathing, that was something—and listening to the wet sounds of other people who were also, miraculously, breathing.

Someone was sobbing. Someone else was groaning. Someone was praying in a language Korroc did not know.

A light bloomed.

It was small and white and quivered like a candle, and it hung in the air a few feet above the head of a slim figure who must have just spoken the word for it. A light spell. A simple cantrip. In that instant, in that pit, it was the most beautiful thing Korroc had ever seen.

The light revealed a chamber. A great rough cavern, water-carved, its ceiling lost in the dark above. Rubble lay everywhere—stones from the plaza, fragments of shattered statuary, here a bell-clapper, there a scorched banner with its colors burned away. And among the rubble, the bodies. Korroc counted six, then made himself stop counting. None of them were moving. Some of them were not whole.

He pushed himself up. Plate creaked. Every bone in his body ached, but Torag's grace was on him; he was alive and unbroken, and that was a debt he would pay back with hammer and prayer both. He looked around, taking the measure of the survivors the way a captain takes the measure of a remnant. Off to the right, a pile of stones was moving. Someone trapped beneath, struggling. Korroc was already moving toward it before he had decided to move.

"Easy, easy now," he rumbled, setting his shoulder to the largest stone. "Easy. I have you."

Stone grated against stone. He levered the boulder aside, and another, and a third, and beneath them was a dwarf—a dwarf in a chain shirt, with a sooty face and a familiar pair of eyes Korroc would have known in any darkness.

Thane.

His cousin coughed dust and grinned the tight, strained grin of a man who has decided to be alive in spite of evidence.

"Took your time," Thane wheezed.

"Couldn't find you under all the complaining," Korroc said, and hauled him out by the front of his chain shirt.

Even as Thane found his feet, a woman's voice cracked across the cavern—hoarse, urgent, edged with a pain she was trying very hard to swallow.

"Thane."

Thane's head snapped around. Korroc had never seen his cousin move so fast on stone. Already two others were converging on her—a half-elf, lean and sharp-featured, his glaive somehow still strapped to his back; and a man Korroc had not seen before, tall and wide-shouldered, with skin that seemed faintly luminous and eyes the color of deep gold. Aasimar. A scion of the celestials. The half-elf and the aasimar were already shifting stones away from the woman's pinned legs, working with the careful efficiency of men who had done this before in other dark places. Thane dropped to his knees beside her and laid his hand against her cheek with a tenderness that surprised Korroc, who knew most of his cousin's tender places had been hammered out of him long ago.

"Anevia," Thane said. "Be still. Your leg is broken. Don't move."

Anevia bit down on a sound that wanted to be a scream and turned it into a curse instead. Thane huffed something that was almost a laugh.

The half-elf had already moved on. He was bent over another fallen figure, an older elf man whose robes marked him a scholar, perhaps a wizard, and the half-elf's voice had gone tight with a fear he was not bothering to hide. "Aravashnial, are you—"

The older man turned his face toward the voice. The half-elf flinched before he could stop himself, and Korroc saw why. The right side of Aravashnial's face was a mask of burns and gashes, and where his eyes had been there was only ruin. The man was blind. Not gone-blind, not failing-blind. Wrecked.

"Is there a healer?" the half-elf cried out, his voice ringing off the stone. "We need a healer!"

Korroc stepped forward, hammer still in his hand. He hung it at his belt as he came.

"Aye," he said, the word rolling out of his beard. "I'm Korroc. What do you need?"

He bent over Aravashnial and laid his palm against the ruined brow, calling up the soft, golden warmth of Torag's mercy. He felt the wound. He felt it, the way a smith feels a flaw in a blade by running his thumb along the edge—and his face fell. He closed his eyes and shook his head once.

"I can't heal this," he said quietly. "Not here. Not with what I've got. It needs more than I can give."

The half-elf's jaw clenched, but he nodded once and laid his hand on Aravashnial's shoulder.

"My thanks for the trying," he said. "I am Caleth."

"Korroc."

The aasimar had finished helping with the woman's legs and now stood and inclined his head in the slow, formal way of someone who had been raised on rituals older than this city. "I am Nageru," he said, and his voice was deep and quiet and carried in the cavern like distant temple bells. "I am a wanderer. I came to Kenabres only this morning."

"Welcome to Kenabres," Thane muttered. "Sorry about the city."

A laugh, surprised, escaped Caleth, and was strangled almost at once. The grim humor of survivors. Korroc felt his own mouth twitch beneath his beard.

One more figure rose from the rubble, dusting himself with the offended dignity of a man who could not believe the world had dared inconvenience him to this degree. He was richly dressed beneath the dust—silks the color of wine, a heavy gold chain at his throat, fingers ringed with the kind of stones that would feed a frontier family for a year. Korroc placed him at once. Horgus Gwerm. One of Kenabres's nobles, a man who owned half the warehouses south of the cathedral and was rumored to own the magistrates of the rest.

Horgus surveyed the cavern, his face working through a series of expressions—shock, fear, calculation—and settling on a sour disdain Korroc suspected was its natural resting shape.

"Wonderful," Horgus said. "Wonderful."

"Aye," Korroc agreed cheerfully. "Glad you're with us."

Horgus did not appear to notice the irony.

The light spell, it turned out, had been Caleth's. He was paladin and something more—a wizard, by the careful way his fingers shaped the cantrip, by the spellbook Korroc glimpsed slung at his hip beneath the half-elf's traveling cloak. An odd combination. A useful one, Korroc thought.

They took stock. Five able bodies: Korroc, Thane, Caleth, Nageru, and Horgus, who counted only by the most generous reckoning, and two wounded, Anevia with her broken leg and Aravashnial with his ruined eyes. The dead they left where they had fallen, after Korroc spoke a brief blessing over each, his fingers brushing closed eyes that had not been closed before. There would be time for proper rites later, if any of them lived to make later happen.

The cavern's only exit was a passage at its far end, narrow and dark, slanting downward.

"Down," Horgus said sourly, "is in the wrong direction."

"Down is the only direction," Caleth replied, and there was a steel beneath his quiet voice that Korroc filed away for later. "We move."

They moved.

They had not gone twenty paces when something appeared in the dark.

Nageru's hand came up at once, fingers spreading. "Watch yourselves," the aasimar said, his deep voice gone soft and dangerous. "We are not alone."

Caleth slid in front of the wounded, glaive coming off his shoulder in a single fluid motion, the blade gleaming in the wan light of his cantrip. Korroc hefted shield and hammer and bared his teeth in the dark.

"If it's alive," he growled, "I'll squish it."

He started forward.

The thing in the dark was a spider. A huge spider, large as a hunting hound, crouched in the middle of the passage with its many eyes glittering. Korroc's hammer was halfway up before he registered that the eyes were not glittering. They were dull. Dust-filmed. Dead.

The spider had been dead for days. Maybe weeks.

The smell hit them a heartbeat later. The green, sweet, awful smell of corpse-rot in close air, and Korroc gagged behind his beard and lowered his hammer warily, because dead spiders did not, in his experience, move.

This one moved.

Inside it. Beneath the bristled carapace something shifted, swelled, crunched. Korroc had time to think oh, no in the very particular tone of a man who has hunted in too many caves, and then the abdomen split open like an overripe fruit and a pale, glistening thing rolled out onto the stone.

A maggot. The size of a small dog. Its mouth was a circle of grinding hooks.

Thane was already moving. The dwarf's daggers came out together, but only one flew a flash of silver across the cavern that punched into the maggot's pale flesh and stuck there quivering. Thane himself charged, the second blade slashing in a low arc, and the maggot's pulpy body parted in a ribbon of foul ichor. It thrashed once and lay still.

"One," Thane said.

A second maggot tore its way out of the spider's husk.

This one was faster. It humped across the floor with horrible insectile speed and was on Thane before he could recover his stance, jaws clamping down on the chain shirt at his side. Korroc heard his cousin's grunt of pain and saw blood well between the broken links. The dwarf-paladin abandoned the spell forming on his lips, a hum of divine power he had been gathering, and moved, faster than a man in full plate had any right to move, a charge of iron and outrage. His hammer came down in a two-handed arc of pure dwarven fury, and the second maggot ceased to be a maggot and became a smear on the cavern floor.

"Korroc—!" Thane started, and then a javelin hummed past Korroc's helm so close he felt the wind of it, and the third maggot, the one Korroc had not yet seen behind the spider-husk, was pinned to the stone with a foot of polished wood through its middle.

Nageru lowered his throwing arm.

"Three," the aasimar said.

Korroc let out a breath he had not realized he was holding. He turned to Thane. "You're hurt."

"It's not deep. Save it."

Korroc grunted but did not argue. Spells were precious. They might be a great deal more precious before this day was done.

It was as they straightened from the maggots' carcasses that Nageru went very still.

He was looking past them, at something on the cavern floor that the spider's bulk had hidden. Slowly, almost reverently, the aasimar walked forward and bent, and Korroc saw what he had seen, a glint of silver, scattered across the stone like coins from a spilled purse. Scales. Curving plates of metallic silver, each the size of a man's hand, some still tipped with the dark red of dried blood.

Terendelev's scales.

Nageru picked one up. He did not speak. He simply stood there, the silver scale resting in his open palm, his eyes half-closing as if listening to something far away. After a long moment, Korroc walked over and laid his own hand on his shoulder.

"What is it, lad?"

"She is here." Nageru's voice was very quiet. "Some part of her. There is—" He shook his head, searching for the word. "Empathy. Determination. She is with us, in these. Take one. Each of you. Take one."

Korroc bent and picked up a scale, and the moment his fingers closed around it, he understood. There was a presence in the silver, faint as a memory but unmistakable. A vast, gentle, dying will, pressing on his own with a single, final purpose. Live. Fight. Get them out. It was the last gift of a creature that had spent her last magic on strangers.

He bowed his head over the scale.

"My oath upon it, lady," he whispered. "My oath upon Torag's anvil."

Beside him, Thane and Caleth had each picked one up.

The passage tightened. They had to go single-file through a place where the walls came so close together that Korroc, broadest of them, scraped both shoulders of his plate and had to turn his shield sideways to pass. Nageru went first, his light step and unarmored frame slipping through with ease, and on the far side of the squeeze the aasimar called back softly.

"There is a ledge. The floor is fifteen feet below. There is a campsite. Old. And a backpack, hanging from the rim."

They came through one by one. The ledge was a stone shelf jutting from a higher cavern's wall. Below them, in a wash of Caleth's spell-light, they could see what Nageru had seen: the long-cold ash of a fire-pit, a tumble of bedroll-rags gone gray with dust, a tin cup tipped on its side. Above, a fissure climbed up into perfect blackness. Some forgotten crusader, perhaps, who had come down here decades ago and not come back. Or had come back. Or had simply wandered until the dark took him. The wardstone wars were old, and the dead were many.

The backpack proved a small treasure. Caleth knelt and went through it with the methodical patience of a scholar, drawing out a coil of rope, a flint and steel, two stoppered vials whose contents glinted faintly in the magical light, a little bag of trail rations that had gone hard but not yet to dust, and "Potions," Caleth breathed. "Two of them."

He held one up to the light, lips moving as he tested the resonance of it. Then the other. His eyebrows climbed.

"One healing. One restoration." The half-elf's voice was tight. "Saint's mercy. Saint's mercy."

"Save them," Korroc said at once. "Both. We don't know what's coming."

Caleth nodded, slowly, and tucked them carefully away.

The drop down to the campsite floor was short, but Nageru went first by acclamation—if anything was waiting at the bottom, the monk's bare hands were a faster answer than a man burdened with armor could manage. He swung over the edge and dropped, landing in a soft crouch.

The rubble at the campsite's edge erupted.

Two giant cockroaches, glossy-black and long as a forearm, came scurrying out from under the loose stone with the speed of arrows loosed. Their feelers whipped. Their wings rasped. They were on Nageru almost before his feet had settled.

The aasimar did not hesitate. His left fist came down like a smith's hammer and the first cockroach popped under his knuckles in a spray of yellow ichor, its shell crunching like a stepped-on nut. The second leapt at his face.

Korroc did not stop to think.

He slapped his palm to the silver scale at his belt, and felt the gift answer him, a sudden lightening, a rising warmth, as if Terendelev herself had laid a feather under each of his boots. He stepped off the ledge.

He did not fall. He drifted, plate and all, hammer raised, beard streaming behind him in the windless dark. A featherfall, gifted by a dragon's last grace. He came down beside Nageru as gently as a leaf, and the cockroach that had leapt at the aasimar found a dwarven warhammer where its head had been an instant before.

Above them, Caleth swore softly and loosed an arrow he had nocked—but the shaft glanced off Korroc's pauldron with a spang and skittered into the dark.

"Sorry!" Caleth called down.

"You shoot better with the glaive!" Korroc bellowed back, not unkindly.

The cockroach he had struck was not yet dead. It reared up, mandibles clashing, and lunged at him. Korroc's shield came around in a low sweep and caught the lunge on the anvil-and-hammer of Torag's sigil, deflecting the bite into empty air. From above, an arrow hummed down, Thane's, this one, true and clean, and punched through the cockroach's thorax and pinned it briefly to the stone.

Briefly.

The thing was still moving.

Nageru ended the matter with a single short, downward stamp, the heel of his bare foot driving the arrow the rest of the way through the creature and into the floor beneath. There was a small wet crack, and the cockroach was still.

Korroc and Nageru stood breathing hard in the lantern of Caleth's light.

"Welcome to Kenabres," Korroc said.

"Yes," said Nageru gravely. "I was just thinking that."

Getting the rest of them down was a matter of rope and patience. Anevia could not put weight on her leg; Aravashnial could not see the floor. Korroc and Nageru took the weight of each in turn, the dwarf bracing at the bottom, the aasimar holding the rope above, and one by one the company came down into the old crusader's camp. Horgus complained the whole way and let go of the rope a foot too soon and landed on his backside in the dust, which improved Korroc's mood considerably.

They searched the camp. Most of it was useless—rotted leather, rusted iron, a journal whose pages crumbled at a touch. But in a corroded tin box at the bottom of the bedroll, Caleth's careful fingers turned up something that gleamed.

A brooch. Silver, set with a single dark stone, worked in a pattern Korroc did not recognize, a bat on a mushroom, its eyes tiny amethysts. He passed it to Horgus, on the theory that if there was anything to be said about its value, the merchant would say it.

Horgus turned it in his fingers with a connoisseur's frown.

"Two hundred gold," he pronounced, "by weight and stone. But this is craft-work. Specific craft-work. This belonged to someone. A house piece, or a love-token. There's a story in it."

He looked up at the company and read, correctly, the absence of patience for stories.

"Fine," he sighed, and handed it back. "Fine."

They went on.

Another ledge. This one needed climbing up. Above their heads, a stone shelf opened onto a higher passage, and from a rusted iron piton at its lip a frayed but still-strong rope dangled invitingly down. Some long-dead crusader had left them a road.

Nageru and Korroc traded the same look they had been trading since the first cockroach.

"You and I," the aasimar said. "We are the strongest. Let me hand them up."

Korroc grunted. "I'll catch them at the top."

He went first. Nageru cupped his hands and Korroc set his boot in them and the aasimar lifted, a clean, unhurried motion that sent four hundred pounds of dwarf and plate and warhammer rising into the air as smoothly as if Korroc had been a child. The rope went taut in Korroc's grip. He climbed hand over hand, the muscles in his shoulders singing the old, familiar song of every smith who had ever bent steel, and hauled himself over the lip.

Then they passed the others up. Horgus first. He protested but did not refuse, which Korroc had half expected him to. Then Caleth, who climbed lightly and on his own. Then Aravashnial, whom they had to hoist between them, the blind man trembling but silent. Then Anevia, whose broken leg hung slack and whose teeth were clenched white against the pain. Korroc set his shoulder beneath her and pushed her gently up to where Thane reached down and caught her wrists.

Nageru came up last, on the rope, hand over hand, like a thing of light and wind.

They moved on.

The next cave was a death-trap waiting to happen.

The floor was a treacherous slope of rubble and broken stone, the ceiling sagging low in places, the walls riddled with cracks that breathed cold air. Korroc tested every step before he took it. Nageru moved ahead of him, light and silent.

The aasimar was just lifting his foot to nudge a fist-sized stone out of his path when the stone uncoiled.

Not a stone.

A snake.

It was as long as a man and the color of old rust, and it struck for Nageru's leg before the aasimar had fully registered that what he had been about to kick was alive.

Korroc was already moving.

His hammer came down in an overhead crash that the snake barely twisted aside from. The hammer struck stone and a chip the size of his thumb went spinning into the dark. Behind him, Caleth and Thane were calling, "What is it?" "Snake!" but Korroc had no breath for answers. The snake had recoiled, hissing, and Nageru's fists were already up, and the aasimar struck downward in a blur of knuckles. And the snake bit him.

Fangs sank into the meat of Nageru's calf. Nageru did not cry out. His face went very still, the way the face of a man who has been bitten by a venomous thing goes still while he counts his heartbeats and waits to see if the world is going to tilt. Then his fist came down again, and the snake's skull cracked beneath the blow.

Korroc did not give it the chance to writhe away. His hammer came down a second time, square on the broken head, and the rust-colored body went limp in an instant.

Nageru breathed out.

"Poison?" Korroc asked sharply.

The aasimar's eyes half-closed. He breathed slowly, listening to himself. After a long moment he opened his eyes again.

"My blood is older than its venom," Nageru said quietly. "I will mend."

Korroc grunted. "Aye. Lucky bastard."

"Lucky," Nageru agreed, "and bastard. Both."

They cleared the rocky path together, hauling the worst of the rubble aside so Anevia could be carried through and Aravashnial could be guided. Beyond the rubble, the passage narrowed again and then opened, and as it opened Korroc stopped walking.

He stopped walking because he had gone, in an instant, somewhere holy.

The cavern beyond was high—thirty feet, easily—and broad, sixty feet from end to end, and at its far wall stood a building.

A building of dressed stone, here in the deep dark. A small, square shrine of close-fitted basalt blocks, its roof arched, its door of iron banded with runework Korroc could read with his eyes closed. And carved into the stone of its right-hand wall, ten feet tall and worked with the patient love of some long-dead dwarven mason, was a hammer.

Not just any hammer.

Torag's hammer.

Korroc heard himself say the name like a prayer. "Torag."

He stepped down into the cavern, his plate clanking, his beard trembling.

Caleth's voice came from behind him, gentle. "Wait, Korroc—let us check it first. Stay back, Anevia. Aravashnial, my hand. We'll go ahead a little. Just a little."

Korroc barely heard. He was already moving toward the shrine, drawn the way iron is drawn to a lodestone, but he had enough wit left in him to angle wide and cautious. Nageru ghosted to his left. They had not taken six steps when Korroc became aware of a sound.

A buzzing.

A deep, droning, oily buzzing, the sound of something far larger than any fly had any right to be.

Above the shrine, two black shapes detached themselves from the cavern roof.

Flies. Carrion flies, swollen to the size of dogs, their compound eyes burning with the dim greenish light some unnatural insects carry in deep places. The first banked off the shrine's roof and dove at Korroc with a furious angry whine. The second crawled to the shrine's edge and dropped, wings whirring, straight down toward Nageru.

Korroc had a heartbeat to react.

His shield came up.

The fly hit it.

The impact rocked Korroc back a step—the thing was heavy, it weighed as much as a man and struck like a thrown anvil—and then, horribly, its barbed feet sank into the shield. The fly was stuck to him. Or stuck to the shield, which was stuck to him, which amounted to the same thing. It hung off his shield arm and its head darted around the rim, its proboscis stabbing for any seam in his plate. Korroc snarled and brought his hammer around in a one-handed swing, the awkward arc of a man with only half his body free, and the hammer's flat face caught the fly across the wing-root with a crunch of chitin.

The fly did not let go.

To his left, Nageru had spun aside from the second fly's diving strike, the aasimar's body bending in a way that should not have been physically possible, and his fists had come up beneath the thing's bulk to drive into its underbelly. The fly buzzed in a register that hurt the teeth. It struck back. Nageru took the hit on his shoulder and gave none of it back to himself, his counter-blow caved in the fly's left compound eye in an explosion of glassy fragments.

Caleth was running.

The half-elf came down the slope with his glaive already out and leveled, took three long strides, and thrust. The leaf-blade of the glaive punched cleanly through the fly that was savaging Nageru, pinning it for an instant against the shrine wall, and the half-elf yanked the weapon back with a twist that sprayed the wall with foul wet matter.

Behind him, Thane was up to the line, daggers out, throwing first and following with the second blade. The first dagger thudded into Korroc's fly's side. The fly did not seem to notice. It was pulling at Korroc's shield, pulling, trying to drag the heavy steel away. And Korroc, Korroc, was being pulled with it, his boots slipping on the rubble.

"Get this thing off me!" Korroc roared.

Nageru came.

The aasimar crossed the distance in two long strides and his fists came down on the fly together, both at once, with the strength of a man whose blood remembered the weight of heavens. The carrion fly broke. Its carapace caved. Its wings stilled. Its grip on the shield went slack, and the corpse slid down Korroc's vambrace and landed in the dust at his feet with a sound like a wet sack falling off a cart.

Korroc breathed out and reached over, without a word, and laid his hand on Nageru's shoulder. Warmth flowed from him into the aasimar's cuts and bruises. The torn skin closed. Nageru bowed his head a fraction in thanks.

"More?" Korroc asked.

Thane was already moving past them, around the side of the shrine, scouting with the wary instinct of a man who had been paranoid too long to stop. He came back with his face set.

"Maggots," he said grimly, and pointed. "That end of the building. A nest of them."

Caleth went and looked and came back. "Three. Three I can see."

"There will be more," Korroc said. "There always are. These things spawn from the dead."

He hefted his hammer.

"So," he said, "let's clean this place out properly."

They came around the shrine in a careful line—Caleth in front with his glaive's reach, Thane on the wing with his daggers, Korroc and Nageru as the heavy second rank. The maggots were already coming.

The first one humped across the floor with sickening speed, jaws working, and threw itself at Caleth.

Caleth's glaive met it.

He was fast, the half-elf, in a way Korroc had not yet had the chance to appreciate. The glaive's leaf-blade flicked out in a low, snapping thrust that caught the maggot mid-lunge and stopped its forward motion as cleanly as a hand stopping a thrown ball. The creature's foul mouth-hooks champed at the empty air an inch short of Caleth's hand. It tried to surge forward, drove itself down the glaive's haft toward him, but Caleth was already pivoting his hips, twisting the blade inside the wound, and the maggot shuddered and fell apart on the stone.

Behind Caleth, the floor moved.

One of the maggots—the third one, the one nobody had been watching—had vanished. Sunk into a crack. Burrowing.

"Watch your feet!" Thane snapped.

The second maggot was on them before the warning was finished. It came at Thane low and hard. Thane stabbed and missed—the dwarf cursed in old Dwarven—and the maggot rounded on him. Korroc was already there, hammer crashing down in a glancing blow that took a chunk out of the thing's pale flank. It thrashed and bit at his greaves, found nothing but plate, recoiled.

And then the third maggot exploded out of the stone.

It came up behind Caleth, in a spray of grit, jaws gaping, right behind the half-elf's unprotected back. Caleth, still committed to his thrust at the wounded second maggot, could not turn fast enough. Korroc, hammer raised, was a heartbeat too far. Thane shouted. Anevia, watching from across the cavern, screamed Caleth's name.

Nageru moved.

The aasimar was a blur. He had been ten feet behind the line, watching for exactly this, and when the floor erupted he was already in motion, and his fists came down on the third maggot the instant its head cleared the stone. Both fists. Together. With the full weight of his body and a faint, golden corona of celestial fire flickering along his knuckles.

The maggot did not get the chance to bite. The maggot was reduced, in one descending blow, to paste.

Nageru landed in his crouch beside the smear and rose, breathing easy.

Caleth turned, saw the aasimar, saw the smear, understood, and inclined his head a fraction. "I owe you," the half-elf said.

"You owe me nothing," Nageru replied. "We are kin in this dark."

They turned together to the second maggot—the wounded one, the one Korroc had cracked—and in a clean, near-silent moment of teamwork that Korroc would think on later, Caleth's glaive snaked between Korroc's and Thane's shoulders to skewer the thing through its center, and Thane's dagger came down on the pinned head, and the maggot was finished.

Silence settled over the cavern.

Korroc stood breathing hard, his shield arm aching from the fly, his hammer arm aching from the maggot, every joint of his plate ringing from the work of an hour that had felt like a day. He looked at the shrine.

The shrine looked back.

He walked to it.

The iron-banded door was stuck. Of course it was. Korroc set his back against the stone of the wall, planted both boots on the threshold, and pulled. Plate creaked. His teeth clenched. The hinges, frozen with a century of disuse and damp, groaned, complained, yielded. The door swung open with a screech that ran around the cavern like the cry of a dying gull.

Inside.

Inside there was dust, thick as snow, lying on every surface. Inside there was a small antechamber, its walls carved with the runes of Torag's covenants—law, family, hearth, anvil, the keeping of oaths, and its floor flagged in basalt. Inside, set against the right-hand wall, was a stone basin.

Korroc walked to it as a man walks toward water in a desert.

The basin held water. Pristine. Clear as a held breath. Not stagnant, not foul, not green. Pristine. Here, after no one knew how many years in the dark.

Korroc's fingers trembled as he dipped one. He brought the drop to his lips.

The water was holy.

He bowed his head and let the tears come, just a little, just for a moment, before scrubbing them away into his beard. He pulled an empty waterskin from his pack and filled it with shaking hands.

"Holy water," he muttered. "Holy water. Oh, my Father. My Father, you have not forgotten us."

Behind him, Horgus's voice cut into the moment like a knife into bread.

"Oh, splendid. And how long shall we be admiring the puddle, dwarf? Some of us would prefer to be out of this hole before the next century turns. Some of us have holdings to attend to. Some of us are not slowed by the dead weight of a—"

He gestured at Anevia, who was leaning against the far wall with her broken leg propped up.

"—a cripple."

Anevia's head came up.

If Korroc had been Horgus, he would have stepped back at what he saw in her face. Horgus, of course, did not.

"Worthless." Anevia's voice was low and steady, which was somehow more frightening than a shout. "Worthless man. Look at you. Standing in the dark whining about your holdings. It doesn't surprise me a man like you would talk this way—always creeping along the edges of the city, always hiding his face from honest folk. What did you ever do for Kenabres but skim the cream? What are you doing for Kenabres now?"

"How dare—"

Thane's hand came down on Anevia's shoulder, gentle, steadying. "Easy. Save your strength."

"Strength's the only thing I've got, Thane, and I'll spend it as I please—"

Nageru took a slow step forward and turned his quiet, golden gaze on Horgus. "If you are in such haste, Master Gwerm," the aasimar said, with perfect courtesy, "you may go on alone. None of us will hold you. Take your share of the ration. Take the corridor. The dark has been kind so far."

Horgus's mouth worked. Several responses tried to climb out and were strangled at his teeth.

Caleth stepped between them with his hands raised, palms out, in the universal gesture of please, all of you, not now.

Horgus did not say anything. Horgus stood there, his jaw tight, his eyes glittering with a humiliation Korroc would later learn to recognize as the most dangerous thing in him. The merchant turned, deliberately, and walked to the far side of the antechamber, where he stood with his back to them.

Korroc filed that, too.

Korroc had moved to the inner door while the noble was being scolded—the real door, the one between the antechamber and the sanctuary itself. He laid his hand on it.

It swung open at a touch.

Dust billowed out in a slow, grey curtain. Beyond, a small chapel-room. A stone altar at the far wall. Benches along either side, gone gray with dust. And on the foremost bench, slumped forward, head bowed, something that had once been a dwarf.

Korroc drew breath to call out a warning.

He didn't get the chance.

The figure raised its head.

The flesh of its face was withered, drawn back from teeth that had been bared for a very long time. Its eyes glowed a pale, wet, sickly green, like the lights that crawl in deep marshes at midnight. Its mouth was open in a silent, frozen scream that had become the permanent shape of its face. Tatters of vestments—Torag's vestments, gold on grey—hung from its shoulders.

The undead thing rose.

It shrieked.

The sound went into Korroc's bones like a nail. He had heard banshees in old tales and never wanted to hear one again. He hefted his hammer.

"Brother," he said—to the dwarf this thing had been, to the priest who had once stood before this altar—"we'll put you to rest. I swear it on the anvil. We'll put you to rest."

The undead came at him.

It moved fast for something that had been still for so long. Its claws raked across his breastplate with a screech of metal on bone, and Korroc felt the filth of them—not just the physical claws but a sick, crawling taint, a disease pressing at him through the gaps in his plate. He felt it try to climb into him. He felt Torag's grace meet it and turn it back. The disease slid off him like rain off a roof.

He brought the hammer down.

It hit the undead's skull with a sound like an axe on rotten wood, and the thing's head dented but did not break. It hissed and clawed at him again. Caleth dashed in past Korroc's flank, glaive thrusting, but the half-elf's strike skidded off the creature's ragged armor without finding flesh. Thane circled behind, daggers flashing in—both of them sank home, one in the back, one in the kidney—and the undead did not seem to feel them. It was on Korroc. It only had eyes for Korroc.

Why?

Korroc, parrying a claw on his shield, understood. The hammer-of-Torag on his shield. That was what it was looking at. That was what enraged it. This thing had once been a priest of Torag and had fallen, had renounced the anvil, had become this in the dark—and the sight of the holy sigil drove it past whatever scrap of reason it had left.

He hammered it again. The blow was solid. The undead staggered. And then it straightened and there was less damage on its withered flesh than there ought to have been.

It was healing.

"It's healing!" Korroc bellowed. "Burn it! Holy fire! Smite!"

A claw came around his shield's rim and raked him at the armpit, where the plate was thinnest, and Korroc felt a sudden hot wetness inside the steel and his knees nearly went out from under him. He staggered back a step, panting.

Nageru was already burning.

The aasimar's fists, raised, were limned in soft gold—a divine smite, the celestial blood in him answering Torag's call. Nageru struck. The undead's chest flared under the impact, a brief flash of holy light, and the creature howled—a real howl, a hurt howl.

Caleth shifted.

Korroc, half-blind with the pain at his side, did not see the spell at first. He only saw that Caleth was suddenly gone—no longer in front of the undead—and then there, behind it, glaive in hand, the air still crackling where his body had reassembled. Dimensional step. The half-elf's free hand came up, glowing, and Caleth pressed his palm flat against the undead's spine, and everything happened at once: a smite, a shocking grasp, a paladin's wrath and a wizard's lightning braided together into a single unholy unmaking. The undead went rigid. Lightning crawled along its bones. It screamed.

It did not fall.

It turned, slow as something dredged from under deep water, and looked at Korroc, and brought both clawed hands down on him together.

Korroc's plate caught one. The other found the same wound at his armpit and tore it wider.

The dwarf's vision went red.

He had a half-instant, falling, to gather what was left of him and cast—a word, a sign, a flicker of golden warmth pressed into his own torn flesh. The wound knit, just enough. He breathed. He lived.

Then the second claw came down again.

Then his eyes rolled back.

Then the floor rose to meet him.

He was vaguely, dimly, somewhere-deep aware of what came next.

He heard Nageru shout—not a word, a sound, the sound a man makes when he has decided that something will not happen on his watch. He felt the floor shake under the aasimar's stride. He heard the wet, definitive crack of two divine fists coming down on something that was not going to get back up.

He heard the undead fall.

He heard the silence after.

He heard footsteps. He heard the snap of a stopper being pulled. He heard a half-elf's voice, quiet and tight, saying his name. He felt liquid, cool and clean and tasting of summer, run between his lips and down his throat, and then the warmth of a healing potion bloomed inside his chest like a forge being lit, and his eyes opened, and he coughed, and he sat up, and Caleth's face was inches from his own.

"Welcome back, dwarf," Caleth said softly.

Korroc looked past him.

The undead lay broken in the dust. Nageru stood over it, breathing slow, the gold gone from his fists. Thane had moved to the doorway, already keeping watch on the antechamber and the cavern beyond, because that was what Thane did, and because someone had to keep watching the dark.

Korroc closed his eyes for a moment. Opened them.

"He fell," Korroc said quietly. "The priest who was here. He renounced Torag, somewhere in the dark, and this is what came of him." He looked around the small, dust-choked chapel. "We can't leave it like this. We can't leave him like this."

Caleth nodded. "We rest here, then. It's defensible. The wounded need it."

Thane spoke from the doorway without turning. "Horgus will throw a fit about waiting longer."

Caleth's mouth quirked. "So? If he wants to leave alone, the corridor is right there."

Korroc levered himself to his feet, and Nageru was at his elbow at once, steadying him. The dwarf laid a hand briefly on the aasimar's wrist in thanks. Then he turned to face the others.

"Listen," he said. "All of you. We've no idea what's happened on the surface. Demons came through—a balor lord came through—and the wardstone is gone. The wardstone is gone. For all we know, Kenabres is burning. For all we know, the only door we have left is this one, and we'll be coming back here whether we like it or not. We need a base. We need a place to fall back to. This is a shrine of my god in the dark beneath a city under siege, and we have just spilt blood to take it. We are going to clean it. We are going to consecrate it. We are going to make it ours."

He looked at each of them in turn.

"Aye?"

Nageru inclined his head. "Aye."

Caleth, after a moment: "Aye."

Thane, from the door, gruffly: "Aye, cousin. Aye."

Even Horgus, in his corner, did not say no.

They did the work.

Caleth, going through the backpack one more time as they prepared to settle, found something he had nearly missed—the second potion. He held it up to the light, frowning, mouthing the resonance, and his eyes widened.

"Restoration." He looked across the chamber to the bench where Aravashnial sat, the elf's ruined eyes turned vaguely toward the sound of voices.

Caleth lowered his voice and brought the others into a small council near the door. "It's only one dose. Only one. I can't make it stretch. It would set his eyes—" He hesitated. "Well, it would set some of his sight back, if cast soon. He's a mage. A real mage, not a hedge-conjurer. If we get him out of here whole, he could be—he could mean a great deal, in what comes."

He looked at each of them in turn, the question plain.

Korroc looked at Anevia, whose broken leg had been bound and splinted and could heal, given time and care and a proper bone-set. He looked at Aravashnial, whose eyes could not.

He nodded once. "Give it to him."

Thane nodded too. "Aye."

Nageru said nothing, but his expression said yes.

Caleth took the potion to the elf. He explained. Aravashnial's mouth worked silently for a moment, and then the wizard reached up with a shaking hand and took the half-elf's wrist, and bowed his head once, and accepted the vial. He drank.

The air in the chapel hummed for an instant, the way it does when a spell takes hold. Aravashnial made a small sound, a hurt sound, and his hands went up to his eyes—and when they came away, the eyes were not quite ruined. Not whole. But eyes. Hazed, dim, half-blind, but eyes that turned toward Caleth and saw him.

"Boy," the wizard whispered. "Boy."

Caleth's face broke. He hid it quickly.

Korroc, meanwhile, had gone to Anevia. He knelt awkwardly in his plate and laid his hands on the shattered leg and called up Torag's grace, and what he had he gave—not enough to mend the bone, but enough to ease the pain, enough to set the splint truer, enough that she could rest without her teeth clenched. Thane watched, his hand on her shoulder, his face very still.

"My thanks, paladin," Anevia said. Her voice was rough. "Forgive me, in my rudeness—what was your name?"

"Korroc."

"Anevia Tirabade. And you've already met Thane."

"Aye. We've met."

"He's can be a very difficult man, Thane."

"Aye. He can."

Thane snorted.

The work of consecrating the shrine took Korroc the rest of the day and most of the night.

He cleared the dust. He swept the floor with a frayed rag he made from the hem of his own surcoat. He carried out the bones of the fallen priest one careful armful at a time and laid them in a side-niche with what dignity could still be afforded the man, and he prayed for him—prayed for him, not over him, prayed for the soul of a brother who had broken under the weight of the dark and renounced what had once held him up. He filled and re-filled the basin from his own waterskin. He spoke the long forge-prayers, the prayers his mother had taught him at her knee, the prayers he had spoken at his ordination and his vigil and on a hundred bloody nights since. He spoke them all.

By the eighth hour his voice was a dry husk and his knees ached and his eyelids weighed a hundred pounds each. He set his back against the bench where the undead had risen and let his eyes close, just for a moment. Just for a moment.

He did not wake until the others let him.

When he opened his eyes the chapel was lit in the soft blue-gold of Caleth's dimmed cantrip, and Thane was sitting against the opposite wall keeping watch, and Anevia was asleep against Thane's shoulder, and Aravashnial was sitting up with his half-eyes turned toward a spellbook he could very nearly read again, and Nageru was in some meditation Korroc did not interrupt. Even Horgus was asleep, snoring sourly into his beard. Korroc rolled to his knees, put his palms together, and began the morning prayers of his order.

He had not finished the first verse before he felt it.

A wave of warmth rolled through the chapel, soft as a hand laid on the back of a tired child's neck. Every sleeper stirred. Caleth's eyes opened. Nageru's head came up. Anevia gasped softly, and Korroc saw the lines of pain on her face ease, just slightly, and the swelling under her splint go down a little. Thane's grip on his daggers loosened. Aravashnial lifted his face toward a light no one else could see and smiled, and the smile was the smile of a man who has remembered, after a long time of forgetting, that he is loved.

Korroc bowed his head until his forehead touched the cold stone.

"My Father," he whispered. "My Father."

Torag had blessed the work. Torag had blessed them. The shrine, after a hundred years of dust and a fallen priest and an undead horror, was holy again. Its anvil rang in the deep places. Its hammer remembered its name.

Korroc rose and turned to face the company. He felt, perhaps for the first time since the rift had opened, something like steadiness. Something like a foundation under his feet again. He had a base. He had companions, an unlooked-for company forged in a single bloody night in the dark. A half-elf paladin-wizard who fought like a thunderstorm with a glaive, an aasimar monk who could break a fly with his fists and burn an undead with his bare hands, a cousin he would have died for under any sun and now under no sun, two civilians and a noble who would all of them slow him down and all of them be worth the slowing. He had a holy water flask that had been filled from a basin no demon had touched. He had a silver scale at his belt that whispered, faintly, live, fight, get them out.

He had a city above him that, if it still lived at all, was in agony.

He wiped his beard with the back of his hand and squared his shoulders inside his plate.

"Right, then," Korroc said. "Up. All of you. Eat. Drink. Pray, if you've a prayer in you. We've a way out of here to find, and a world to find waiting on the other side—and if I know anything about this city, that world isn't going to be the one we left."

He looked at each of them in turn—Caleth's grim half-smile, Nageru's calm bow of the head, Thane's nod, Anevia's jaw setting in spite of her splint, Aravashnial's half-restored eyes burning faintly with the will of a man who had something to do at last.

And Horgus, scowling, dusting off his sleeves, but standing.

Korroc lifted his hammer.

"Onward," he said.

And somewhere far above them, in a city whose sky had broken, a horn sounded the first call of a war that would not end for a very long time.
