# The Strain the Smith Takes

*Chapter Twelve — Half Your Wounds*

---

The fire they gathered around was small on purpose, and that was the whole trouble with the night.

Korroc had banked a thousand fires in his life and never once had he wanted one to give less light than it could. A forge-fire you fed. You brought it up until the coals went white and sang, until the metal in them glowed the color you needed, and you did not apologize to anyone for the heat. But this fire was a hand-span of embers in a scrape of ruined stone, and the four of them and Aron Kir hunched close over it and kept their voices under the crackle, because somewhere out in the dark of two hundred sleeping crusaders there was a man who would sell everything they said tonight for whatever coin the Abyss paid its errand-runners.

*You do not plan a battle in a whisper,* Korroc thought. *Not unless you are more afraid of your own camp than of the enemy.* And they were. That was the shape of it, laid out plain and cold. He did not like the shape.

Aron had drawn a map in the ash with the point of his knife — Drezen, or the bones of it, the citadel a hard scratch at the center and the rest a scatter of marks around it. His flat voice went low and even, a man reading a ledger he had read too many times to be moved by it anymore.

"We can't just go for the citadel," Aron said. "I want to be clear about that, because I know how four heroes think." His eyes moved across them, unhurried. "You want to slip in behind the army, take the keep, cut the head off. And you'll die doing it, because you'll leave the whole outer city at your backs. Every tower still manned. Every camp still fed. The army makes its noise in the streets, and some of that filth peels off the fight and collapses down on the citadel behind you, and then it doesn't matter how loud the army is. You're four people in a stone box with the door shut."

"So we clear the outside first," Thane said. Not a question. His cousin sat with his forearms on his knees, both blades already wiped though he'd killed nothing since noon, reading the ash-map the way he read everything, from the ground up. "How much of it."

"Enough of it." Aron's knife-point touched a scratch near the citadel. "The bridge, first and last. It has to be taken and it has to be *held*. Lose the bridge and you lose the whole approach — they can pour reinforcement across it faster than we can bleed them for it." The knife moved. "Here. Paradise Hill. The Queen thinks they're holding prisoners there. People they took in the raids."

"Then we go get them," Thane said, before anyone else could. Flat and certain, and Korroc felt the small warm pull of pride at it — his cousin, who trusted almost no one and liked fewer, who would still hear *prisoners* and answer without a breath between.

"Aye," Korroc said. "We go get them."

Aron's knife moved a last time, and stopped, and rested there a moment longer than it had rested anywhere. "And the cemetery." His voice did not change and somehow that was worse. "That one's a wild card. Nobody goes there. The ones who do don't come back. So we don't know what's in it, and I'll tell you plain — I don't want to find out with an army camped blind beside it." He lifted the knife. "That's for later. But it's coming."

The fire ticked. Somewhere off in the dark a picket coughed. Korroc looked down at the scratched city in the ash and felt the old snag turn over in him, the flaw in the metal he had been carrying since a tent a night ago and a letter read aloud in his cousin's rough voice.

*Borin Stonevein. Dagna Stonevein.* His father's name and his mother's, dried into a seventy-year-old page in their own hands, signed under a promise that stone remembers. His people had built this city. His father's generation had raised these walls and then died losing them, and the demons had had it for three lifetimes of men, and somewhere ahead in the dark was a citadel with his family's work in its bones and something foul sitting in it like rot in a good beam.

He was not walking toward a battle. He was walking toward his family's stone. He had known it since the letter and he would go on knowing it every step of the way in, the way a man knows a wound under his mail without looking at it.

"Don't mistake me," Aron was saying. "This is not for four heroes. Not the outer city. You want a good slice of the army with you when you clear it, or you'll clear it once and never clear anything again."

They talked it around, low, and settled on half. A hundred of the paladins. Enough to hold a line and pour arrows and mean something when the enemy counted heads. When it was decided, Nageru rose from the fire without a word — he had a way of standing that started before you saw it, the stillness simply becoming motion — and Caleth glanced up at him.

"I'll tell Irabeth," the aasimar said. His bronze face caught the low light, and the faint gold that always hung about him, subtle as heat-shimmer, went with him as he turned. "Better one of us carries it than a runner. Fewer mouths."

*Fewer mouths.* There it was again. Korroc watched him go out into the dark toward the commander's tent, and he did not like the arithmetic of the night — that they had to weigh even *who carried a message* against the chance that a small bright chronicler with clever feet might be listening at the wrong seam of a tent. He put his palm flat to the cold stone beside the fire, without deciding to, the way he had started doing lately. The stone said nothing back that words could hold. But it was *there*, solid, old, patient, and the stone in his own veins leaned toward it and steadied, the way a tired man leans toward an anvil he has stood at all his life.

They would go in the dark. They would clear his family's city one bought yard at a time. And a traitor would walk in among them the whole way, unnamed, unproven, small.

*Later,* he told himself, in a borrowed cadence — Thane's cadence, the hunter's, the one that filed a thing and did not lose it. *Everything gets read eventually.*

He hoped it was true. He was less sure of it than his cousin.

---

They moved in the last black hour before dawn, and the city took them into itself a piece at a time.

The army had come up the dry riverbed and out of it well back, and now a hundred paladins and four heroes went forward through the outskirts of Drezen building by building, slow as a tide coming in, keeping stone between themselves and the walls. Korroc walked near the front with his shield slung and his boots set down soft, and the ruin closed around them, and he could not stop his eyes from doing the thing his eyes always did.

He read the work.

Even wrecked. Even fouled. Even with three generations of demon-filth ground into it, the bones of Drezen were dwarven bones, and Korroc's smith's eye found the joints of it in the dark and knew them the way you know a hand you have not held in years. *Good stone, cut true.* The corner of a fallen barracks where the blocks still married so close he could not have slid a knife-blade into the seam — that was somebody's pride, somebody's whole long day at the mason's line, seventy-five years and a hundred desecrations ago. A lintel cracked but holding, the load still finding its way down through the arch the way its builder had meant it to. A stretch of wall where the demons had smashed and burned and painted their filth, and under the filth, patient, the courses ran level and plumb as the day they were laid.

*My father might have set that stone.* He did not know it. He would never know it. But his father had been here — Borin Stonevein, who dreamed of a lost sky-city and helped raise a fortress on the road to it — and his father's brother Thorek, and their wives, and the whole doomed hopeful company of them, and some of this stone had passed through their hands before it passed under Korroc's boots. The thought sat in his chest heavy and warm and grieving all at once, ore in the deep, and he carried it and kept walking and did not let it slow his feet.

He would not weep in a war. But he marked the stone as he passed it, each true joint of it, the way you nod to a craftsman's grave.

They had not gone far when Thane, on the point, went low and still, and the whole line folded to a halt behind him without a sound.

Aron came up beside Korroc, and pointed, and Korroc followed the line of it and saw the tower.

It stood ahead of them, a squat watchtower on the near edge of the ruin, and on the top of it, black against a sky just barely thinking about gray, a shape. A lookout. Leaning on the parapet, unhurried, a demon or a man doing the oldest and dullest work of any war.

"There'll be an alarm up there," Aron murmured. "A horn, a beacon, something. He sets it off and the whole city knows we've come, and the citadel with it. Before we've taken a single yard." His flat eyes found Korroc's. "He has to go quiet. And he has to go *now* — much closer and he sees us for certain."

Caleth had drifted up on Korroc's other side, silent, already working the problem the way he worked everything, his gaze measuring the height of the tower and the angles of it. He cleared his throat, barely a breath.

"I can put us on top of him," the half-elf said. "The world folds, we're there before he's turned around. But only three." He said it with a wince in it — a man handing over a gift he wished were bigger. "Three, and no more. It's a narrow spell."

Korroc had his answer before Caleth finished. It was the easy answer, the obvious one, and he gave it low and quick. "Then the three of you go. You, Nageru, Thane." He tapped his own slung shield. "I'll hold here. My link with you stretches — I can feel you from here, easy, this is nothing to the length I've held it. I'll draw off what wounds I can from below while you take him. You three end it fastest."

He believed it as he said it. It was the sort of trade he liked best — himself set back, out of the good of it, carrying the strain so the others could do the fine work clean. The smith at the bench does not need to swing every hammer. He needs the metal to come off the anvil true.

Thane shook his head before Korroc had the last word out.

"No." Just that, first, and then the reasons, laid down fast and flat. "You've got the plate and the shield. If something's waiting up there worse than one bored lookout, they need a wall on that roof, and you're the wall. I'm not." His cousin's mouth moved, dry, under the beard. "And I'm the quietest thing in this company by a long mile. I'll go up the outside on my own feet, unseen, while they blink in on top. I don't need a spell spent on me to move through a dark I was made for."

Korroc looked at him a moment. It was a good argument. It was, curse the man, a *better* argument than his own, and Korroc had built his whole life on knowing good work from bad and not letting pride make him call the bad work good.

"He actually makes sense," Korroc said, to no one and everyone, and Caleth's mouth twitched, and the thing was decided.

"Get a start," Caleth breathed to Thane. "When you're at the base, I'll put us on the top. We go the moment you're close."

Thane grinned — a rare quick thing, gone almost before it came — and spoke a single word under his breath, and drew it out of himself the way he drew everything, cheap and practiced, and the dark simply *forgot him*. One moment a dwarf crouched in the rubble. The next an absence, a place the eye slid off, a knife nobody was watching. Korroc heard the softest scuff of a boot moving off toward the tower's foot, and then not even that.

The stone in Korroc's chest, the thread, the bond — it did not weaken when Thane went unseen. That was the strange grace of it. His eyes lost the man. The thread did not. He could feel his cousin out there in the dark, a warm sure weight, moving low and fast toward the base of the tower, and he held onto that feeling the way you keep one hand on a rope in the black.

Caleth's fingers moved.

Down the line, on some quiet signal Aron had passed, a knot of the paladins made a noise — a scuff and a mutter and the small deliberate clatter of a man being clumsy on purpose, thrown out into the dark to pull a lookout's eyes down and away. Korroc saw the black shape on the parapet straighten, turn, lean toward the sound.

And the world folded, and Korroc was on top of the tower.

---

There was no lookout.

That was the first wrong thing, arriving in the same half-second his feet came down on the cold flat stone of the roof — the shape they'd seen from below had a horn slung at its hip and a bored slouch to it, but it was not one bored sentry. There were four. Goat-headed things, tall and lean and rank, with slotted yellow eyes and curling horns and long cruel tridents in their fists, and Korroc's mind put the shape of them together and reached for a word he did not have and did not need. Demons. The kind that fought with reach, three prongs of iron looking to find the seam of a man's guard.

Caleth did not wait to name them either. Radiance was already in his hands, already burning, and he drove the glaive into the nearest one before it had finished registering that the air had spat three enemies onto its roof. The white blade went in, and the thing shrieked, and Caleth wrenched it free and struck again, twice in a breath, the holy edge flaring where it bit.

Nageru cleared the distance to it in a stride. No wind-up, no anger — just the still water breaking into motion, both fists coming up under the goat-head's jaw in a strike that lifted the demon off its cloven feet, and then a second blow, flat-palmed, into the chest, that sent it staggering back over the low parapet. It went off the edge with its trident still in its hand and its shriek trailing down after it into the dark.

*Three left,* Korroc's mind said, cold and quick, and one of the three had already peeled around to come at Caleth's unguarded back while the half-elf was busy with the first.

Korroc moved.

He was slow for the first step — he was always slow for the first step, plate and stone both, a mountain deciding to walk — and then he was not slow, because he did not need to be fast, he needed to be *there*, and there was a place between the goat-thing's trident and Caleth's spine and Korroc put himself in it. The demon's thrust came and Caleth, quick as water himself, twisted aside from it, and Korroc's hammer came round in answer, a flat forge-blow, all shoulder — and the demon dropped its trident across the arc and turned the adamantine head aside with a screech of iron on iron.

*Fast,* Korroc thought, with a smith's grudging respect for a thing that could parry a hammer. *These are fast.*

The last of the three came at him head-on, a full charge, horns down, trident leading, and Korroc took it on the shield and let the whole stubborn weight of himself soak the blow. The point skated off the steel. He set his boots. The stone under the roof was three floors up from the earth but it was still stone, still the bones of the world, and he reached for it out of long new habit and felt it there, faint, patient, holding him up the way it held up the tower.

Then the first demon, the one he and Caleth were trading with, brought its trident round low and *under* — under the shield, into the gap, the way a good spearman finds the place plate forgets — and one prong of it punched into Korroc's thigh above the knee.

It bit. He grunted, once, low. Blood ran hot down inside the plate. He did not give the half-step back. *A leg still stands on a wound,* he told himself, an old smith's arithmetic, *until it doesn't, and this one will.* He turned the hammer and made the demon pay attention to him again instead of the wound it had given, and beside him Nageru stepped into the same demon and put a fist across its goat-skull, snapping the horned head sideways.

They had the roof. Three demons, two of them staggered, the fight tipping their way with the clean momentum of surprise well spent.

And then the thread in Korroc's chest tore open.

---

It was not his wound. That was the thing he understood in the first sick instant, the thing that made it worse. He had a trident-hole in his own thigh, hot and honest and *his*, and this was not that. This came from nowhere his body had been touched. A sudden bright line of pain across the top of his own shoulder, deep, ragged, *talons* — and he had taken no talons, nothing on this roof had reached his shoulder, and so the pain was not his to own.

It was Thane's.

The bond carried it up the thread the way a bar carries heat from the fire-end to the hand — you feel it in a place the flame never touched, and you know exactly where the flame is. Korroc *felt* his cousin get opened somewhere below him, felt the talons go into Thane's shoulder as clearly as if they'd gone into his own, and in the same instant a screech came up the outside of the tower from down the winding stair — a screech he knew, a screech that had filled a cave two nights gone, the wet brazen scream of a vulture-thing wearing a raptor's naked skull.

*Vrock.* There was a vrock on the stairs. And Thane was on the stairs, unseen, alone, and something that could see through unseen had just laid his shoulder open, and Korroc was three demons and a parapet and forty feet of stone away from him with no way down that was not through the goat-headed things in his face.

"*Thane is being attacked below!*" The bellow tore out of him before he'd chosen to make it, huge, filling the roof, and he heard the raw edge in his own voice and did not care. He looked for the stairhead — found it — and it had a demon standing square between him and it, trident up, and a second angling to close the gap, and Korroc understood with a cold drop of the stomach that they were *keeping* him from it, that the roof-fight was a wall between him and his cousin's blood.

He did the only thing the roof allowed. He put everything into ending the demons faster.

The hammer came down on the one he'd been trading with, a hard clean stroke to the side of the goat-skull, and this time it did not parry in time — the head rang, the thing reeled — and it swung back at him wild and Korroc took the counter on his shield without looking, all his eyes on the stairhead, on the dark below it, on the place his cousin was bleeding where he could not reach. Caleth thrust Radiance at the demon between Korroc and the stairs and it flinched from the bright blade and hauled its trident round to block, and would not be moved off the door it was holding. The last of the three caught Nageru with a glancing prong along the ribs, and Nageru answered it with a fist across the head, unhurried even now, the still water refusing to be stirred.

And all the while the thread in Korroc's chest ran red with a fight he could not see.

He felt Thane *moving* — felt the wounds come and the man not fall, felt a flare that was his cousin healing himself on the run, a thin patch thrown over a tear, felt the ragged rhythm of a knife-fighter doing the one thing a knife-fighter must never be caught doing, which was *running from a fight in a straight line up a narrow stair*. He felt fresh hurts open and close and open. He felt the vrock's rage come up through the stone as much as through the thread.

This was the whole of what the bond was, and Korroc had never felt it so plainly as he felt it now, planted helpless on a rooftop: he could carry his cousin's pain, but he could not carry his cousin. He could take the strain. He could not take the blows. The smith bears the heat that runs up the bar. He cannot reach into the fire and pull the metal out with his bare hand.

So he bore it, and he hammered, and he kept one eye on the stairhead and prayed to the Smith in short flat words with no ceremony in them at all — *let him reach us, Father, let the stairs bring him up, keep his feet under him* — and he made the goat-things bleed for every heartbeat they stood between him and the top of that stair.

---

The vrock came up over the edge of the roof.

Not up the stair — up the *outside*, over the parapet on filthy beating wings, hauling itself onto the roof-stone in a stink of the Abyss, and the moment its clawed feet came down it began to savage the empty air.

That was what the others saw. Korroc knew, because he could see it too and knew what he was looking at, the way you learn to read a thing once you've felt it from the inside — the demon slashing at nothing, its raptor skull snapping at a space where no one stood, and then, on the bare stone of the roof, fresh blood appearing out of nowhere. A red line opening on empty air. A spatter striking the stone from a wound in a body no eye could hold.

*Thane.* He'd made it up. He'd thrown himself over the edge of the stair and onto the roof, still unseen, still bleeding, and the vrock had come up the wall after him and cornered him against the sky.

Korroc stopped feeling the thread and started using his hands.

The demon he'd been fighting — the reeling one, the one whose skull he'd rung — was still in front of him, and Korroc ended it. He set his feet, drew the deep breath, and brought the adamantine hammer down in a full two-handed forge-stroke, all his weight and all his grief and all the trapped helpless minutes of the last fight behind it, and the goat-thing got its trident up too slow and the hammer went through the guard and through the skull and the demon simply stopped being a thing that stood. It folded. Blood and worse sprayed the stone.

Radiance came over his shoulder — Caleth, close behind him, driving the glaive past Korroc's ear into the last of the goat-demons, the one that had been holding the stairhead, catching it in the chest. It shrieked and stumbled back. Nageru pressed it and it dodged out of his reach, quick even now.

Korroc left it. Let Caleth and Nageru have the goat-thing. He turned his whole self toward the vrock and the drops of blood appearing on the stone, and he went at it.

He reached down as he went — down through the roof, through the tower, into the deep bones under it all, and pulled the weight of the world up into his arms the way the Stonelord in him had learned to do, the way his blood remembered even when his head did not have the words. The hammer came round on the vrock once, and it landed with that wrongness of mass, too heavy, the strike of a mountain choosing a single point to fall on. The demon staggered. Korroc set and struck again, the second blow into the same reeling place, and the vrock screamed and turned—

—and Nageru was there, both fists lit.

The aasimar had left the goat-demon and come across the roof and his hands had taken fire — not flame, the clean cold-gold light of a holy smite, celestial fury banked along his knuckles — and he brought them down on the vrock twice, and where they struck they flared, and the thing born of the Abyss recoiled from the touch of what glowed on the monk's fists.

But the vrock was old and vast and full of hate, and it did the worst thing it could have done, which was to decide Nageru was the greater threat. It turned on him. Beak and talon and claw, all three, the whole reeking front of it coming down on the aasimar at once — and it *connected*, all of it, the beak into his shoulder and the talons down his chest and a claw raking his arm, and blood ran bright and sudden down Nageru's bronze skin. He rocked with it. He did not fall. He never fell. But the thread in Korroc's chest lit with the aasimar's pain, a whole handful of it at once, and Korroc took his measure of that too, drew it into himself off the bond, felt it settle in his own chest as a phantom weight beside the honest ache of his bleeding thigh.

And Thane came out of the vrock's blind rear with both blades.

He must have circled while the demon turned — unseen, low, into the dead space no demon watches once it has chosen a face in front of it — and the daggers flashed, mother's knife and father's, into the seams under the beating wings. Two red lines opened where nothing had touched the demon, and it screamed, high and cracking.

Korroc healed them.

He did it in the middle of everything, the way he always did, because the moment asked for it and the cost of waiting was worse than the cost of spending. He pressed a glowing hand to his own bleeding thigh first — lay on hands, the Smith's mercy, closing the trident-hole from a wound to an ache because a man carrying three men's pain has to keep his own body under him — and then he swept the other hand wide and let a wave of gold roll out over the whole roof, over Nageru's torn chest and the invisible ruin of Thane's shoulder both, a wash of grace that found them all on the strength of the bond that already tied them. He felt his cousin's worst hurts cool and knit even unseen. He felt Nageru steady.

The last goat-demon caught Caleth.

It had gotten past the half-elf's guard when Caleth turned to help, and a trident-prong went into his chest — a hard hit, deep, and Caleth's breath went out of him in a grunt and blood struck the roof-stone. Korroc started for him and could not reach him in time and did not have to. Caleth answered it himself. The half-elf reached out with his off-hand, fingers spread, and spoke a word — and Korroc knew the shape of the spell that should have come, the crackle of it, the smell of a storm — and instead of lightning there was *cold*. A burst of killing frost bloomed off Caleth's palm into the goat-thing's chest, the spell turned inside out the way only Caleth could turn it, electricity made ice. The demon convulsed, rimed and shuddering. And Caleth, wounded, gave ground clever — a step, a slide, the world folding a short hop under him — and came down clear across the roof, out of the demon's reach, buying himself the length he needed.

Which left the vrock, and Nageru facing it, and a mistake waiting to be made.

Korroc saw it happen and could not stop it. The aasimar stood between the reeling vrock and the rimed goat-demon and had to choose, and for one heartbeat he *hesitated* — weighing them, reading them, deciding which death to deal first the way a careful man weighs two flaws in one blade — and the vrock did not give him the heartbeat. It swept his half-formed strike aside with one great arm and came in on him while he was choosing. Claws. Talons. The beak. All of it landed, all at once, and Nageru staggered under the worst blow he'd taken yet, and the thread in Korroc's chest flared white with it.

But hesitation in the monk was a knife's chance for the man behind.

Thane took it. He'd never left the vrock's rear — patient, exactly where his mother had taught him to be — and when the demon overcommitted to Nageru it turned its whole attention forward and forgot the dark at its back, and Thane's daggers came up into it, both of them, deep and final, into the place under the wing where the joint gapped. The vrock arched. Its scream climbed and broke.

It fell.

It came down on the roof-stone in a heap of stinking feathers and folded wrong-jointed limbs, and the raptor skull cracked against the stone, and it did not move again.

And Korroc was already turning, because there was still a demon on the roof, the rimed goat-thing that had opened Caleth's chest, and it had gathered itself off the cold and squared again on the half-elf. Korroc crossed the distance in three driving strides and brought the hammer down on it from behind while its slot-eyes were on Caleth, a plain flat killing stroke, and the frost-brittle thing came apart under adamantine and dropped and was still.

The roof went quiet.

Four demons dead. A vrock cooling in its own reek. And the four of them standing on the top of a tower in the last dark before dawn, bleeding, breathing hard, alive.

Korroc got the wand out of his belt with a hand that shook only a little, and went from one of them to the next, and healed what the wave hadn't reached. Nageru's chest, laid open in three places. Caleth's, the trident-hole. Thane's shoulder, honest and visible now that the man stood plain in front of him, ragged and deep and already half-closed by the grace Korroc had thrown wide.

He set his broad hand on the back of Thane's neck while he wanded the shoulder, once, brief, and did not say the thing that was in him — *I felt it, cousin, I felt every one of them, I stood on a roof and felt you get torn open and could not reach you and it was worse than any wound I have ever taken myself* — because Thane knew, and Thane would not want it said aloud, and there are things two men who share a thread do not need words for.

"You made me feel that from up here," Korroc said instead, gruff. "Whole climb of it."

"Then we're even," Thane said, dry, testing the shoulder. "I felt you fret."

Korroc snorted. It was a bad joke and it was exactly the right size, and the strain of the last few minutes went out of him a measure at the sound of it.

Below, out in the ruined dark, a new noise had risen while they fought — a clash and a shout, steel and cries, spreading. The hundred paladins had found the sleeping cultists Thane had slipped past on his way to the stair, and had fallen on the camp, and were finishing what surprise had started. Korroc could hear it turning their way even now, the fight going out of the enemy and the ground going to the Crusade.

"They took the camp," Nageru said quietly, looking down over the parapet, one hand pressed to his newly-closed chest. "The paladins. It's nearly done."

"Good." Korroc rolled the hammer once in his grip, and set his eyes north, toward the deeper city and the citadel he could not yet see. "Then let's go find the Queen's prisoners."

---

Paradise Hill was a supply-dump before it was anything else, and Korroc read it as one.

They came into it behind the last of the fighting, the four of them and a knot of paladins, and the first thing they found was the plain evidence of an army being fed. Cultist stores — sacks of hard bread and dried meat stacked under a lean-to, barrels of water ranked along a wall, the dull unglamorous stuff that let a horde sit outside a city and starve it. Korroc noted it the way a quartermaster's friend notes it: *ours now.* Horgus would be pleased. Every barrel here was a barrel Irabeth's people did not have to haul up the riverbed.

Then, deeper in, past the stores — voices.

They came muffled through stone, low, and Thane's hand went up and the party went quiet, and Korroc heard it clearer: many voices, a crowd of them, packed close somewhere ahead and going suddenly still. They followed the sound to a storeroom, and in the storeroom a heavy door, and set into the door a barred window, and behind the bars — faces.

A press of them. Gaunt, dirty, staring out into the torchlight with the flat wariness of people who had learned that nothing good came through a door. As Thane moved toward the lock the voices behind it broke out again — questions, sharp and frightened, some of them, and Korroc saw shapes shift back there into fighting crouches, men who had decided that if the door opened on more misery they would meet it with their fists and their bare hands and whatever was left of them.

Korroc stepped up to the bars and put his hands where they could be seen, open, empty, and pitched his voice down into the warm blunt register he used on frightened beasts and frightened children and, he'd learned, frightened men.

"Easy, now. We're with the Queen's army." He let it land. "We are here to help."

For a moment nothing. Then the words seemed to reach the back of them, and something broke — a rush of voices all at once, disbelieving, half of them refusing to believe, a man's cracked shout and a woman's sob and three people talking over each other. Hands came up to the bars. Korroc lifted his own hands higher, patient.

"Whoa. Whoa, now." Gentle, immovable, the same tone. "We'll have this door open. Stand back from it and let my friend work the lock."

They explained it as Thane opened the door — the army come north, the wardstone, the city being taken back — and Korroc watched the thing happen that he would carry out of this whole grim day as the one clean piece of it: he watched hope come back into two hundred faces. Not slowly. All at once, like a forge-door flung open, the tired gray leaving them and something bright and painful taking its place.

Because there were two hundred of them, at the least. More, maybe. The door opened and they came out and kept coming out, filing into the torchlight, blinking, and Korroc stood back and let them come and did the thing he could not help doing, which was to read them.

He read them like metal.

Good metal, most of it. Bent — every one of them bent, worked cold and hard by whatever the cultists had done to them, some of them near to breaking. But not broken. He could see it in the way they carried themselves even now, half-starved and filthy and stunned with sudden freedom: a stubbornness in the spine, a refusal in the jaw. These were people who had kept themselves alive in a demon-held city for who knew how long, who had woken every morning in that cellar and decided, again, not to die. You did not do that if you were soft ore. Korroc had spent his life learning the difference between metal that would take a temper and metal that would shatter under the hammer, and he looked at these two hundred and knew what he was looking at.

*These will fight,* he thought. *Give them half a reason and they'll fight like the damned.*

They gave them the reason without meaning to. As the freed prisoners crossed back over the ground the paladins had won, they began, one by one, to bend and pick things up — a dropped sword here, a spear there, a cultist's fallen axe — arming themselves off the leavings of the fight, no one telling them to, a hundred small decisions made in the same direction. One man, lean and hard-eyed, carrying a notched blade he'd taken off a dead tiefling, came to Caleth and looked him square in the face.

"We can help in the fight." No preamble. A man who'd spent a long time with nothing to bargain with, saying the only thing he had. "Most of us are healthy. All we need is a weapon and we'll go with you. Let us fight these bastards."

Caleth looked at him for a long moment — Korroc watched the half-elf weigh it, and knew he was weighing more than one thing, the man's strength and the man's readiness and the plain hard truth that untried men in a battle-line died in heaps — and then Caleth nodded.

"Those who find a usable weapon out there, carry it," the half-elf said. "Those who don't will be given one as we're able. Most of our army carry bows that can be put in your hands." A thin tired smile. "Archers are always valuable in a war."

The man grinned at him, all his hard-held stubbornness lighting up at once. "I thank you, sir."

Korroc watched him go back to the others with the notch-bladed sword held like a thing worth more than gold, and felt something ease in his chest that the healing had not reached. Two hundred souls who had kept themselves alive in the dark. Now they had weapons in their hands and a fight to spend themselves in and a reason to believe the door would stay open this time.

*Bent, not broken,* he thought. *And metal that's been bent and holds is stronger than metal that never bore a load at all.*

His father would have said that. His father had been here, in this city, seventy-five years ago, believing the same stubborn dwarven thing — that a broken thing could be reforged, that stone remembered, that you did not walk away from work worth doing just because it had been ruined once. Korroc set his shield and looked north again, and felt his father very near.

There was a bridge to take.

---

They came up on the bridge with a small army at their backs, and it was already half a battle.

Aron had not waited. When Korroc found him the tactics-man was standing at the near end of the span with his flat eyes already reading the far side, and he spoke without turning. "I sent runners to Irabeth the moment we cleared the hill. She's bringing the rest up the riverbed behind us. And the lookouts on the bridge are down — the paladins got them on the way in." He jerked his chin at the span, where bodies lay crumpled at the near end. "Bought us a little. But look."

Korroc looked.

Across the gorge, on the far side of the long stone bridge, there was an army waking up. Two of them, in truth — Korroc could see the split in it plain, the way you could see a weld between two metals that had never quite fused. Cultists on the one hand, a mass of hooded human filth. Tieflings on the other, red-skinned and horned and moving with a soldier's discipline the cultists lacked. Two forces, camped separate, mustering separate, and both of them now fully awake to the fact that the near end of their bridge had been taken.

Aron was already moving, barking low orders, fanning the freed prisoners out along the lip of the gorge — the archers, the men Caleth had armed, spreading into a loose line with bows in their hands. The paladin leader brought his hundred up to the front and set them at the near mouth of the bridge, a shield-wall across the only crossing, ready to hold the throat of it. The whole thing came together fast, the ragged instant machinery of an army that knows its work.

The paladin leader turned to the four of them. "What are our orders?"

Korroc answered before the others, because this part he knew in his bones — the patience of it, the letting the fire come up before you struck. "We wait." He pitched it to carry, a bellow with the weight of the whole line behind it. "Let them cross that bridge under our arrows."

A bridge was a chokepoint. A bridge was a forge-channel, a narrow race that made the enemy come single-file into the killing. You did not charge across it into a waiting army. You made *them* come to *you*, across the open stone, while your archers rained down the length of it. Every crusader on the line knew it. Korroc saw them settle at the word, bows coming up, the wall setting its feet.

"Leader." Caleth's voice, quiet, beside him. The half-elf was not looking at the bridge. He was looking across the gorge at the far army, his gaze gone narrow and fixed the way it did when he'd found the pattern under the noise, and his inclined head pointed like a compass. Korroc followed it and saw them — a knot of figures behind the front ranks, better-armored, unhurried, the ones the others moved around. Leadership.

Korroc opened his mouth to say something and Caleth was already casting.

A chant, quick and rising, and a *whoosh* as a ball of fire left the half-elf's hand and arced out across the gorge — except it was not fire. Korroc knew it the moment it left, knew it by the color and the smell and the way Caleth's spells came out wrong-on-purpose: the fireball reached the enemy leadership and burst not in flame but in *acid*, a spreading green ruin that fell across a whole segment of the far ranks and stayed, hissing, eating. A large swath of them went down and did not get back up. The screaming reached across the gorge a half-second behind the sight of it.

"Saint's mercy," Caleth murmured, watching his own work with the flat professional grief he always wore for it. "That'll wake them properly."

It did. The far ranks filled the hole where the acid had fallen and began to move, both armies of them, flowing toward the bridge — and Korroc saw, in the same moment, that not all of them marched. Behind the advancing lines, spellcasters had stopped and set their feet and raised their hands, and the air over the far end of the bridge tore and spat, and squat gray-green things tumbled out of nothing. Dretches. Summoned. Stupid and hard to kill and exactly the sort of thing you threw at a shield-wall to blunt it.

"Hold arrows for humans and tieflings," Korroc bellowed down the archer line. "The paladins can deal with dretches. Don't waste a shaft on a summoned thing that'll pop on its own — save it for the ones that stay dead."

The line adjusted. Bows tracked past the tumbling dretches to the cultists and tieflings crowding onto the bridge behind them.

Beside Korroc, Nageru had gone very still — the deep stillness, not the fight-stillness, the aasimar reading the field. And Korroc saw where he was reading: not the bridge, not the dretches, but the far side, the deep rear, the critical points behind the whole enemy mass where the casters and the leaders sheltered. Nageru's hand came up, one finger, marking places. A man planning a road.

Caleth saw it too. "You want over there," the half-elf said. Not a question. Reading his friend the way he read enemies.

Thane cast without being asked — a low Dwarven cadence, the doorkeeper's blessing rolling out over all four of them, that settling weight in the bones that Korroc had learned meant *he has laid the good favor on us, we go in marked*.

And Korroc understood what was about to happen, and knew his part in it, and turned to Nageru.

Because if Nageru was going over there — into the deep rear, into the middle of an army, to break the leaders the way only he could — then someone had to carry the strain of it. That was the whole of the bond, and Korroc had never once used it more deliberately than he was about to. He reached out along the thread that tied him to the aasimar and he *strengthened* it, poured a working into it, thickened the channel between them until it was less a thread and more a shared vein, so that what struck one of them would run to the other and be split between them.

"I'll be taking about half your wounds now, Monk," Korroc said, blunt and warm, setting his shield. "Don't overdo it."

Nageru's bronze face creased — a rare thing, the ghost of a real smile, there and gone. "I'll do my best," he said, and Korroc heard the chuckle under it, and loved him for the calm of it.

"You never do," Korroc said. "That's the trouble with you."

Caleth's hands came up, gathering the three of them with a gesture, and spoke the word that folded the world.

---

They came down behind the enemy army, and the world was full of backs.

Caleth had put them at the deep rear, with the enemy's own rearguard between the four of them and the sheltered leaders — a wall of unsuspecting soldiers facing entirely the wrong way, toward the bridge, toward the fight they thought was in front of them. Several of the nearest tieflings felt the displaced air and turned, and their red faces went slack with the particular horror of soldiers who have just discovered the war has appeared behind them.

It happened at the same instant the first dretches crashed into the paladin shield-wall across the gorge. Korroc heard it start behind him — the clash of the line taking the summoned things, and over it, the hiss and thunk of the freed archers' arrows arcing down onto the cultists and tieflings crowding the bridge. The trap closed from two sides at once. Bodies began to fall on the span.

And here, in the rear, the four of them went to work.

A cultist dropped under Nageru's fists before he even knew what had killed him — the aasimar simply arrived among them, a bronze blur, and men fell where he passed. Thane went one way and Korroc the other, and cultists began to fall between them, the hammer on the one side finding skulls and the daggers on the other finding seams. Then Caleth was suddenly ahead of them all, the world folding under him, appearing deeper in to cut at the soft middle.

Korroc had put down his third man when the ground betrayed him.

Slimy black tentacles erupted out of the earth beneath their feet — thick, glistening, whipping up from the stone as if the ground had grown arms — and two of them wrapped Korroc before he could set his shield, coiling his arms, his shield, hauling him down and holding. He felt them close on Caleth too, off to his left, the half-elf snared the same instant. And then a cloud came — a rolling green fog that filled the air around the tangle, and the smell of it hit Korroc's gut like a fist, a nausea so total and sudden that his stomach heaved and his knees went weak and the hammer sagged in a hand that no longer wanted to grip. He felt Thane hit it too, saw his cousin lurch, gray-faced, in the edge of the cloud. And Caleth, wrapped and retching, the worst-placed of all of them.

*A caster,* Korroc thought, through the churn of his own belly. *Behind the rearguard. Made the cloud.* And even as he thought it Nageru was through the rearguard and *on* the caster — the aasimar had punched clean through the wall of soldiers and reached the man who'd made the fog, and his fists came across in a blur and connected with both, and Korroc felt the answering flare on the thickened thread, a bright hard shock of the caster's death-struggle running into his own chest and being split between them the way he'd promised. He took his half gladly. It was better than the nausea.

Around the caster's fall, the other spellcasters and the leaders broke and retreated — back into the middle of their own crowd, hiding in the press of their soldiers, the cowards' move, the move of men who spend others' lives before their own.

Korroc set himself to the tentacles. His stomach was still turning but his arms were his own, and he *pulled*, and the black coils resisted and then gave, and he tore free and picked his way out of the writhing patch of them with his feet careful and his gorge rising, dodging the ones that lashed for him as he went. Thane, at the cloud's edge, was already nearly clear, working his way out with a knife-fighter's economy. Korroc came out the far side of the green and hauled a clean breath and felt the nausea start to loosen its grip.

Caleth did not come out.

The half-elf was still wrapped — tentacles around his arms and his chest, and the nausea in him too deep to shake, and Korroc understood the trap of it with a cold clarity: Caleth could not cast, because a man too sick to hold his gorge could not shape a spell, and a man who could not shape a spell could not fold the world and slide free. The tentacles held him and the cloud kept him too sick to escape the tentacles, and the two spells wound tight around each other like a lock built to hold exactly one wizard.

Korroc turned back into it.

He had just gotten *out* of the green and the grabbing dark, and he turned around and went back in, because Caleth was in there and could not get himself out, and that was the whole of the sum. He waded back into the nausea, into the reach of the tentacles, feeling his own stomach heave again the moment the fog closed over him, and he got close enough to the wrapped half-elf to reach him, and he pressed a hand to Caleth and spoke a working into him — the grace that scoured sickness, poured along the bond and into his friend, the green fog's grip on Caleth's gut simply *ending* under Korroc's palm.

Caleth's eyes cleared. His hand shot out and closed on Korroc's forearm, hard, and the half-elf gasped a word, and the world folded around the both of them—

—and they came down together on clean ground, outside the cloud, outside the tentacles, out where the air was only air.

"That," Caleth breathed, still gripping Korroc's arm, "is twice you've come back into a thing I was trapped in."

"Keep count," Korroc said. "I'll not."

Nageru was wading deeper. He'd left the dead caster and pushed on toward the leaders sheltering in the crowd, and the enemy army, waking at last to the disaster in its rear, had begun to turn — men peeling off the bridge-fight and the front to come back at the four demons who had appeared behind them. Korroc saw it coming and moved up behind Nageru, and then the two of them were in the thick of it, mobbed, soldiers closing on them from three sides.

Korroc set his feet and his shield and became a wall.

That was his work in a press like this — not to reach for the killing but to *stand*, to be the thing the blows broke on, to hold the ground beside the man doing the fine work so the fine work could be done. Caleth's hands swept over them and a quickening ran through Korroc's limbs, a haste, the world going a half-step slower around him — the half-elf spending a spell to speed his friends' hands. Thane's daggers found a tiefling and folded it. And Nageru, in the middle of it, reached a leader at last and set on him.

The leader fought back, and he was good, and he got his nicks in — a blade opening a line along Nageru's ribs, another catching his forearm — and Korroc felt every one of them arrive in his own body a heartbeat later, split down the thickened thread, half the sting of each wound blooming in his own chest and side where no blade had touched him. He took it. He took it the way a smith takes the heat that runs up the bar, steady, without flinching, because that was the whole reason he had thickened the thread before they crossed: so the monk could stand in the middle of an army and pound a leader to ruin and pay only half the price for it.

And Nageru paid it, half-price, and it was enough. His fists came down on the leader once more, twice, and the man went down and did not rise.

The enemy broke.

Korroc had seen armies break before and it was always the same and always a little terrible — the moment the will went out of a mass of men all at once, the thing they'd been holding together simply coming apart in their hands. With the leader down and demons in their rear and arrows in their front and paladins grinding the dretches to nothing on the bridge, the far army stopped being an army. Some threw down their weapons and dropped to their knees. Some ran for the gaps and fled into the ruins. Some — Korroc watched it, grim — simply threw themselves off the bridge into the gorge rather than face what was coming, choosing the fall.

The paladins came off the near end in a hard advancing line, and the freed archers poured their last arrows into the rout, and the human cultists and the tieflings lay heaped along the span, and it was, all at once, over. Korroc stood in the wreck of the enemy rear with his shield up and his breath sawing and the phantom ache of Nageru's wounds fading out of his own chest, and healed himself, and then swept a last wave of gold over the three of them, and let the fight run out of his arms.

The paladins began gathering the ones who'd surrendered, herding them into a knot, searching them for blades and worse. And the four of them, catching their breath, moved back into the ground the enemy had camped on — and found the last gift of the day.

Supplies, first. More of the same dull crucial stuff, food and water and the makings of a siege. And then, stacked in a lean-to under a cultist's tarp, a thing that stopped Korroc where he stood: crusader gear. Armor and weapons, a whole stash of it — plate and mail and good honest steel, taken off the Crusade's own dead over years of raids, hoarded here like a magpie's pile. Korroc knelt and put his hand on a breastplate and knew the make of it, the Mendevian pattern, the work of a living smith somewhere south who'd never know his craft had come home.

"Get word to the lieutenants," Korroc said, rising. "The freed men. Bring them up and let them arm themselves proper. This was theirs once." He looked at the pile, and at the north, where the citadel still hid behind the ruin. "Let them go into the rest of this fight wearing their own steel."

And they did. Within the hour the men from the cellar under Paradise Hill were pulling crusader plate over their filthy rags and buckling on swords that had been made for hands like theirs, and Korroc watched them do it and thought again about metal that had been bent and had held, and did not say any of it aloud.

---

Irabeth came up the riverbed with the rest of the army as the sun cleared the ruins, and the siege of Drezen began in earnest.

They made a camp on the south bank of the dry river, in the ground they'd bled to win, and threw up a makeshift prison of stakes and rope to hold the ones who'd surrendered, and the whole grinding apparatus of a besieging army began to settle itself around the bones of the city like a second, living wall. Korroc walked the edge of it once the healing was done and the wounded were seen to, and he could not help but read the ground under the new camp the way he'd read everything all day — dwarven work under the crusader tents, his people's stone holding up a Crusade come at last to take it back.

*We're inside it now,* he thought. *The first stone of it, anyway. The outer wall of it.* His father's city, cracked open at last, one bought yard of it turned back to the light.

The command tent went up, and when it did, Aron and Irabeth called them in, and the map was on the table again — Drezen, drawn true this time, not scratched in ash — and Aron's finger came down on it in a place Korroc had been half-dreading since the fire two nights gone.

The cemetery.

"I hate to belabor it," Aron said, in his flat spent voice, "but." The finger tapped the mark. "We're camped on this side of the riverbed now. We're *staying* here, for a while. And I will not have this army sitting blind next to a place where everyone who walks in stops walking out." He looked around the tent. "We don't know what's in it. That's the whole problem. And 'we don't know' is how a camp gets taken in the night."

The four of them looked at the mark, and at each other, and it was Thane who said it — flat and dry and certain, a door opening onto the next dark room without any drama at all.

"Well," his cousin said. "That's our next target then."

Korroc grunted his agreement. Of course it was. You did not walk away from the thing you did not understand and hope it stayed asleep. You went in with a light and you looked, and you named what you found, and you dealt with it. It was the demon-work of the whole crusade in small: go into the dark, learn its shape, break it. He was not looking forward to it. He had spent a long day already and the cemetery had a bad feel to it even secondhand, even as a mark on a map. But it would keep till they'd slept, and then they would go and read it the way they read everything.

Aron spent the rest of the evening working the freed men for what they knew of Drezen and the citadel, and got little — nearly all of them had been dragged in from outside, farmers and travelers taken in raids, who'd seen no more of the citadel than the inside of a cellar. But by full dark one of them, the lean hard-eyed man who'd taken the notched sword and asked Caleth to let them fight, stood before Irabeth and swore himself to the Crusade in a rough plain voice, and Irabeth made him leader of the new men — the two hundred out of the cellar, now armed in crusader steel and folded into the army they'd been captured trying to escape.

Korroc watched the man kneel and rise and did not say what he was thinking, which was that his father would have liked him. Bent, not broken. Metal that would take a temper.

Later, when the tent had emptied and the camp had banked its fires and the four of them found a stretch of ground to lay their bedrolls, Korroc sat a while before he slept with his back to a dwarven wall seventy-five years defiled, and put his palm flat against the old stone, and let the stone in his veins lean toward the stone at his back the way it wanted to. The wall did not speak. It only held, the way it had held for three lifetimes under filth and fire, waiting.

*We're here,* he told it, without words. *We're inside the wall. We took the bridge. We got the prisoners out.* And, because it was true and because he could say it to stone if he could say it to no one: *I felt him get torn open today and I couldn't reach him, and I'd feel it again a hundred times before I'd let him carry it alone.*

The bond was quiet now. Tired, but quiet. All three of them were still standing, healed and fed and breathing, laid out in the dark within an arm's reach of him. Korroc could feel them there on the thread, three warm sure weights, and he held onto the feeling the way you keep one hand on a rope in the black.

He thought of the smith's oldest lesson, the one his father had drilled into him before he could lift a full hammer: that the strain in a thing had to go *somewhere*. You could not make it vanish. You could only choose where it went. A blade that took all its own strain cracked. A good smith drew the strain out, spread it, carried it in the work of his own hands and his own back so the metal came off the anvil true and whole and ringing. That was the whole of the craft, in the end. Not the striking. The *bearing*. The taking of the strain so the thing you loved did not have to break under it.

He had a thread that let him do that for the three men sleeping beside him. He had a hammer and a shield and a curse and a gift and a god who answered when he spoke. He had his family's city cracked open around him and a citadel ahead with his father's work in its bones and a burly branded traitor somewhere in it who had sat at that father's table.

And he had, still, whole days of the work ahead — the cemetery, the walls, the citadel, the man. The outer city was theirs. The rest was not. Their jobs in Drezen, Korroc knew, laying his head down at last against his family's stone, were only just begun.

He slept, and the stone held him, and the thread stayed warm, and somewhere north in the dark a citadel waited to be read.
