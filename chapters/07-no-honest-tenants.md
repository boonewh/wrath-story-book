# The Threshold and the Bar
*Chapter Seven — No Honest Tenants*

---

Thane did not like the windows.

That was the first thing — before the door, before the strike team's breathing settled behind him, before any of it. He stood at the foot of the Gray Garrison and read it the way he read everything: from the ground up. *Look at the floor first, lad. Walls lie. Floors don't.* The floor here was old flagstone, fouled, and the marks on it told him bodies had been dragged across this threshold and not always the dead ones. He filed that.

Then his eyes went up the wall to the high windows, dark slits set too far to reach without a climb, and he didn't like them. A climb meant rope, meant noise, meant four men strung out vertical with their bellies open to whatever waited above. Bad entry. He turned his attention back to the door, which was a better door than the building deserved — heavy, iron-banded, set square in its frame.

No lock.

He crouched anyway. Listened first. Looked second. Touched third. There was nothing for his picks to argue with at the keyhole because there was no keyhole worth the name, just a latch-plate cold under his fingers and a seam he could read with his thumbnail. He set his shoulder to the wood and pushed, low and steady, the way a man tests ice.

The door did not move. Not stuck — *held.* A weight behind it. Something laid across the inside.

"Barred," he murmured. "From the far side."

Korroc shifted his grip on his hammer. "Then they know we're coming."

"They suspect." Thane was already reaching into the satchel of things from the old cache, the one Aravashnial had sent them to before the square. His fingers found the small cold shape he wanted — a chime, plain silver, heavier than it looked. He didn't know half of what had been waiting in that cache and didn't much care. It had given him fire and arrows and this. That was enough to be going on with.

"Stand off the frame," he said, and they did, the three of them peeling to either side, Caleth's glaive a low band of light against the dark stone, Nageru barefoot and still as standing water.

Thane struck the chime against the iron band.

It made a sound that was wrong for its size — a long pure note that went *into* the wood rather than off it, that found the bar on the far side and the bracket holding the bar and asked them, politely, to stop being a door's enemy. Thane felt the held weight let go through the soles of his boots. A bar, somewhere inside, sliding free of its cradle and settling.

The door was a door again. Unbarred. Willing.

He moved fast.

---

He hit it with his shoulder and his weight together and stayed *off* the opening as it swung, hugging the jamb, because the man who stands square in a doorway he's just opened is the man who gets a crossbow bolt through the teeth. The door went wide. The dark inside resolved into a long room, and in it — movement. A shape ducking sideways, trying to fold itself behind an overturned bench, too slow, caught half-out of cover by the wash of gray daylight he'd just let in.

Reddish skin. The bend of horn against a lean skull. The wrongness of a thing that wore a man's frame but had been signed by something else.

"Tiefling," Thane said, and the flask was already in his hand.

He threw it underhand and flat, the way his mother had taught him to throw anything you didn't want arcing where a guard could track it. The alchemist's fire took the tiefling square across the chest. Glass burst. The chemicals went over him in a sheet, and for half a heartbeat nothing — and then the air remembered itself and *caught*, and the tiefling came up out of his crouch wearing a coat of flame, screaming, beating at himself with hands that only spread it.

Other voices answered the scream. The dark farther in lit up with them — high, frightened, *more.* The room was full.

Good. Thane had a second flask. He took it, weighed it, threw —

— and it slipped.

He felt it go wrong the instant it left his fingers, a slick half-turn off his palm where his grip had rushed, and the flask flew sour and wide and high and shattered against the far wall on nothing, on bare stone, a bright useless bloom of fire lighting up an empty corner while the men who needed burning stood untouched. He swore in Dwarven, short and ugly.

*Two daggers get noticed. The third one wins.* Patience was the third dagger. He had thrown his patience at a wall.

Korroc came past him into the breach.

"Nice throw," the dwarf said, dry as ash.

Then he cleared the doorway, and Thane saw the smirk go off his cousin's face like water off hot iron, because the door was *flanked* — a tiefling pressed to the wall on either side of it, knives low, waiting for exactly the fool who'd come through fast and proud.

Korroc was not slow. Whatever else the years had taken from the two of them, they had not taken that. His hammer was already moving before the surprise finished crossing his face, a downward stroke that took the one on his left high on the body and folded him against the stone with a sound like a sack going down a stair.

"Right side!" Korroc barked, and Nageru was there.

The aasimar came through the door behind him without seeming to hurry and without making a sound, and the tiefling on the right turned into his arrival, and Nageru struck him twice with bare hands — two blows, no flourish, the heel of one palm under the jaw and the other fist into the chest over the heart — and the man simply stopped. Went down. Did not move again. There was no anger in it that Thane could see, which was the thing about Nageru that he kept turning over and never wore smooth: the violence was *clean.* It had no temper in it. It was just the answer.

Another came out of hiding — a third, lunging for Nageru's unguarded side while he was still settled over the second — and Nageru shifted his weight a hand's breadth and the knife went past him through empty air.

Then Caleth was through the door.

The glaive came up. It had been a sword once, Thane knew, before it chose the half-elf over him; it was a glaive now because Caleth's hands wanted reach, and it stabbed past Nageru's shoulder and took the lunging tiefling under the arm and out the other side, all that cold pale light running down the haft, and the tiefling died with his knife still hunting a man who was no longer where the blade thought he'd be. Caleth pulled the weapon free clean.

"Saint's mercy," the Spireborn breathed — not a curse, the way he said it, more an apology to the room.

Thane stepped in.

He didn't go for a kill. He went for *attention* — into the open middle of the floor, where every frightened tiefling left standing could see him and want him, the bright loud problem, the one to deal with first. That was the knife's other job, the one nobody put on a banner. Be the thing they look at. Let your cousin's hammer do its work on a man who's watching you instead of him.

The one Korroc faced lashed out — but at Korroc, not at Thane — and Korroc turned the knife on his shield without looking, the way another man might brush off rain.

A door at the far end of the room banged open and a fourth tiefling came running through it straight for Nageru, shortsword leading, and this one was lucky or quick or both, because Nageru was mid-turn and the blade found him, slid in along the ribs, and Nageru took it the way he took everything — without a sound. Just a small tightening at the corner of his mouth, there and gone.

The burning one had finally beaten the flames out of his clothes. He came up off the floor smoking and ruined and *furious*, and he came for Thane, and Thane let him.

Korroc's hammer found the man in front of him again. This time the tiefling went down the wall and stayed there, sliding, leaving a long smear.

Nageru answered the sword in his ribs without retreating from it — pivoted into the man who'd cut him and put a single fist through his guard, and that one folded too.

Which left two. Both of them turned, by some animal arithmetic, on the dwarf standing alone in the middle of the room with his daggers out. Both came at once.

Thane gave them nothing to hit.

He read the lines of both knives in the half-second they committed — one high, one low, the oldest pairing there was — and he wasn't between them when they arrived. He'd stepped the gap, dropped his weight, let the high blade comb the air over his shoulder and the low one bite the floor where his boot had been. *Patience.* He came up inside the burned one's reach with his mother's blade leading and put it into him twice, fast, finding the seam under the arm where the ribs gave way, and the tiefling who had survived being set on fire did not survive that. He folded against Thane and Thane let him fall.

Korroc's hammer took the last one in the shoulder, half-crushing it. The man staggered, turned, and met Thane's daggers coming the other way. Two more strokes. Down.

Quiet.

Thane stood breathing in the middle of a room full of the recently dead. Korroc was already scanning the corners. Nageru had a hand pressed flat to his ribs, and when Thane glanced at him the aasimar gave the smallest shake of his head — *later, not bad enough yet* — and Thane believed him because Nageru did not lie about wounds, he simply declined to mention them.

Thane crouched. Wiped his mother's blade on a dead man's sleeve, then his father's, both edges, careful, all the way to the point. *No Stonevein has ever sheathed a wet blade.* It took four seconds and it was not optional and it never had been.

Only then did he look up and *see* the room.

---

There was a mural on the long wall. It had been beautiful once. Thane could tell that the way he could tell a forced lock had been forced — by what was left after the violence. Crusaders in ranked armor breaking like a tide over a churning dark mass of demons, the whole thing carved deep into the stone and once, surely, painted; you could read the artist's love in the set of every shoulder, the lift of every banner.

The faces were gone.

Not weathered. Not faded. *Chiselled off* — every human face in the mural hacked away to a pale raw wound in the stone, deliberate, one at a time, patient work by something that had wanted the men in the carving to have no eyes to see what had been done in their house. Over the ruin of it, scrawled edge to edge, was graffiti in a dozen filthy hands. And under all of it, the blood, gone brown and black, sprayed up the wall and pooled at its foot and tracked across the floor in the shapes of the dragging he'd read at the threshold.

Thane stood there and felt the small clean flame in his chest tick up a degree.

He didn't think of it as anger, exactly. He thought of it the way his mother's lessons had set him to think of such things, without ever giving the thinking a name. These were not tenants. They had no business inside. They had come through a door that was not theirs to a house that was not theirs and they had spent their time scratching the eyes out of the men who'd built it. False occupants. Trespassers, top to bottom. You did not ask whether a man kicking down your neighbor's door was a wicked man. You asked whether he belonged on this side of it, and he did not, and that settled the only question worth asking.

"They had time," Korroc said quietly, looking at the faceless crusaders. "To do this. They weren't afraid anyone would come."

"We came," Thane said.

---

Two ways out of the entry room. He took the north one first, because you cleared the dead ends before you committed to the live paths.

It was a dead end. He'd half known it from the air — no draft, no carry of sound from beyond. The room held smashed statuary, the plinths empty, the figures that had stood on them reduced to rubble and the rubble kicked into corners. Portraits had hung along one wall; they hung there still, but every canvas had been slashed corner to corner, the faces — always the faces — given special attention with a knife.

And there was a table.

Someone or something had been sitting at it. Cards lay scattered across the wood, a game abandoned mid-hand, and around the cards, stacked and counted into neat little piles the way a man stacks his winnings, were the chips.

They were not chips.

Thane had seen a great deal. He had been a long time at the work of being a knife, and the work showed you things. He looked at the table for a moment and named what was stacked on it — fingers, mostly, and other small things off a human hand, and one piece that had clearly been worth a great many of the others to whoever was ahead — and then he looked away, because looking longer served nobody.

"Saint's *mercy*," said Caleth from the doorway, and this time it was a curse.

"They were *betting* with them," Thane said. His voice came out flat. The flat was a door he kept shut on purpose; behind it the flame was very bright. "Somebody was up. Somebody was losing." He turned his back on the table. "Nothing out of here. We go the other way."

Korroc looked at the table a moment longer than Thane had, and Thane watched his cousin's jaw work, and watched him decide — the way Korroc always decided — to carry the weight of it rather than set it down. That was the difference between them, in the end. Thane shut the door on the dead. Korroc tried to walk every one of them home.

"Aye," Korroc said at last, rough. "The other way."

---

The other way was a shrine.

Thane knew it for one before he knew whose. There was a shape these rooms took — the floor swept in a different rhythm, a focus to one wall that the rest of the room bent toward, the worn place where knees had been. His mother had taught him to read those rooms without ever telling him she was teaching him anything but how a building was used. *Some doorways people only cross one way, lad. Mind those most.*

A sunburst dominated the far wall. Iomedae's, then — the crusade's own goddess, the lady of the sword and the dawn, in her own house in the heart of the city the crusade had bled to hold. The walls around the sunburst were dense with prayers, written close and reverent in the high curling script Thane didn't read, Celestial running floor to lintel in a hand that had clearly loved its work.

The shrine had been fouled.

He would not catalogue this one the way he'd catalogued the table. It was enough to say that everything a body could do to a holy thing to insult it had been done here, with excrement and blood and garbage, smeared deliberate across the sunburst and ground into the prayers, and that the smell of it stood in the doorway like a guard.

Caleth stepped past him into the room.

"We should at least clean up the shrine a little," the half-elf said.

It wasn't Thane's god. He stood in the doorway with that thought sitting plain in him — this lady of dawn and swords was not the quiet keeper his mother had handed him, not the presence he felt at every threshold he crossed, the one he had never once in his life called by a name. He had no reading-experience here, no answering warmth. This was a stranger's house and a stranger's grief.

And yet.

He understood, watching Caleth shrug out of nothing and find a rag and kneel, that a threshold set wrong could be set right by anyone who cared to, and that the caring was its own kind of credential. You didn't have to share a man's faith to know that a door spat on ought to be cleaned. Thane could respect a threshold restored even when the god behind it wasn't his. So he held the doorway, and he watched, and he let them work.

Korroc nodded and knelt beside Caleth without a word. The two of them set to it — the paladin's long careful hands and the dwarf's broad scarred ones, working the filth off the sunburst, clearing the prayers stroke by stroke, and Thane noticed the exact moment the room's air changed, the smell giving ground, the focus of the wall sharpening as the insult came away from it.

He noticed something else, too, because noticing was the whole of what he was. As the Celestial script came clean under their rags, Caleth's hands slowed. The half-elf tilted his head at the lettering. Said something low to Korroc. Thane couldn't read the script but he could read *Caleth*, and Caleth was looking at the prayers the way a man looks at a coin that's the wrong weight in his palm — there was a wrongness in the writing, some quality Thane had no name for that the half-elf clearly did. Too ornate. Too curled. *Florid*, was the word Caleth murmured, half to himself.

When the wall was clean Caleth knelt before it a moment with his head bowed, and Thane looked away, because a man's prayers were his own business and the keeper Thane served had taught him that much about doors at least: you do not stand and stare through one a man has opened in private.

Whatever passed between the Spireborn and the dawn-lady, it settled something. When Caleth lifted his head his eyes had changed — a focus in them, a man who'd just had a lock turn under his hand.

"I can read it," he said.

"You could read it before," Korroc said.

"Not like this." Caleth rose. Looked across the shrine, and his gaze found Nageru, who had been standing apart by the door with his hand still flat to his ribs, watching all of it with that still-water patience of his. "Nageru. Come here. Kneel at the prayer bench."

Thane watched the aasimar cross the room and kneel where he was bid without a question, which was its own small remarkable thing — most men wanted a reason first. Nageru only wanted a direction. He bowed his head before the cleaned sunburst, and he looked at the lettering, and after a moment he read it aloud in a voice that filled the little room more than its size should have allowed.

"*Let us inherit thine arms, Iomedae.*"

The words went into the stone the way the chime's note had gone into the door. Thane felt the back of his neck prickle and didn't know why and didn't trust the not-knowing.

He had no idea what it meant. He suspected the words were a key and the lock was somewhere deeper in this house of no honest tenants, and that the why of *Nageru specifically* was a thread he couldn't see the far end of yet. That was fine. You didn't have to understand a clue to keep it.

"That sounds like a clue," he said. "Most likely for later."

Nobody argued. They all nodded, the way men nod over a thing they've all agreed to carry until it's needed.

---

Two doors led out of the shrine.

Thane went to the east one and crouched. *Listen first.* He set his ear near the seam of it, not touching, and held still, and let the room behind him go quiet.

Not locked — he could tell that much from the slack feel of the latch. But there was sound beyond it. Voices. More than one, low and conversational, in a language that crawled in his ear without ever resolving into words he could hold. Not Tiefling-pidgin. Not Dwarven, not Common. Something with edges in it that human throats weren't shaped to make.

He raised a hand without turning. The others stilled.

He signed it the way the crusade taught and the way he'd taught himself: two fingers up — *hold.* A point at the door. Then his hand at his own jaw, fingers and thumb opening and closing — *talking. Voices beyond.*

They understood. Korroc rolled his shoulder and set his shield. Caleth's glaive came down to a low ready, its light dimming as the half-elf willed it quiet. Nageru took his hand off his ribs at last, and whatever it cost him to do that he kept to himself, and reached over his shoulder to the cache javelins racked there.

Thane set his hand to the latch. Touched it third, last, the way he'd been taught his whole life, and felt nothing in the plate to say the door had ever been forced — these had come in some other way, then, these voices, through some breach that wasn't this one. He filed it.

Then he threw the door open.

---

The room beyond had been a museum.

He understood that in the first half-second the way he understood everything, all at once and from the floor up — the long broken shelves, the cases with their glass burst out across the stone, the careful labels still clinging to plinths that no longer held anything. A room built to keep made things and show them. A room built for the exact and only purpose that the things in it should *exist* and be *seen.*

Every made thing in it had been broken.

Not looted. Broken. There was a difference and the difference was the whole of it — a thief takes what's worth taking and leaves the rest; this had no leaving in it, this was destruction for its own sweet sake, every blade snapped, every case shattered, every crafted thing in the room reduced to its ugliest possible pieces with a thoroughness that had taken patience and joy.

Two bodies lay near the center of the floor. Knights — he read it off the armor, good crusade plate, the kind men were proud to die in. They had not been allowed the dignity of dying in it cleanly. Both had been decapitated, and both had been done *more* to than that, mutilated in the particular way that meant the doing had outlasted the killing by a long while.

And at the far end of the room, among the wreckage of all those beautiful broken things, were the two demons.

He knew them on sight and his stomach dropped the way it had in the bookshop.

Abrikandilu. The mirror-breakers. Ropy and wrong and far too fast, their hands ending in claws made for unmaking, their bodies put together out of spite and bad angles. He'd fought their kind a handful of days and a whole lifetime ago, among the burning books, and he knew two true things about them: his steel bit them well enough, and they would die hard and ugly and try to take your hands with them.

They were talking to each other in those high crawling voices, the language he hadn't been able to hold. And between them, they had one of the knights' heads.

They were playing with it.

Working the dead man's jaw with their claws, tilting it this way and that, making it nod and bow and *speak* in little squealing voices they put on for it, a grisly puppet-show staged for an audience of two in a room full of everything beautiful they had already destroyed. To a thing that could not bear a graceful made thing to exist, a museum and a murdered knight were not a battlefield. They were a *playground.*

The flame in Thane's chest stopped being small.

He had a grammar for trespass that his mother had built into him before he knew it was being built, and it had no word large enough for this. This was not men who'd crossed a threshold they had no business crossing. This was the unmaking itself, given hands and a high giggling voice, squatting in a house meant to hold made things and turning a man who'd died defending it into a doll. There were no credentials you could check here. There was only a door he'd just opened and the violation standing on the other side of it, and the only thing left to decide was who went through.

Korroc decided.

The dwarf pushed into the room first — he always went into the open, his cousin, he couldn't help himself, there was always one more soul he thought he might yet save — shield up, hammer back. And the nearer Abrikandilu dropped the dead knight's head and *came*, a blur of wrong limbs sprinting straight down the wrecked room at him, and Thane's heart went into his throat —

Korroc met it. Set his shield and his stance and his whole stubborn forge-built weight against the charge and *stopped* it, the impact ringing off the steel, the demon's rush breaking against him like a wave against a sea-wall. But a wave finds the gaps. One clawed hand came round the edge of the shield, snake-quick, and raked across Korroc inside his guard, and the dwarf grunted — Thane heard the grunt, heard what it cost.

Caleth was behind his cousin in a flash. The glaive came alive again all at once, light flooding back down the haft, and he stabbed it past Korroc's shoulder into the demon's ropy body, drove it in and the blade's pale fire flared where it bit.

Korroc followed, no hesitation, hammer coming round and down into the thing while it was still pinned on Caleth's reach, and the blow landed solid, a wet ringing crunch.

The demon screamed — that high mirror-cracking voice, all the worse for what it had just been using it for — and it did not go down.

Nageru moved into the room past Thane's shoulder, silent and barefoot over the broken glass as though it weren't there. He drew a cache javelin off his back in one motion and his eyes were already on the *second* Abrikandilu, the one still at the far end among the broken things, and he set his weight and threw —

— a long clean line, the javelin crossing the whole ruined length of the museum —

— and struck it.

The far demon shrieked and tore the javelin from its own body and *looked up* at the four of them, and the dead knight's head rolled off the wreckage and lolled to a stop with its slack ruined face turned toward the door, toward Thane, and the second mirror-breaker came up off its haunches into the light with its claws spread and its broken-glass voice climbing into something that was almost, horribly, a laugh —

And Thane stood in the doorway he had opened, his mother's blade leading and his father's reversed in his off-hand, the small flame in his chest gone to a clean white roar, and there was nothing patient left in him to spend.

The blur of motion that should have come for him went the other way.

The second Abrikandilu chose the blood it could already smell. It came off the wreckage in a low scrambling rush — not at Thane in the doorway but at Nageru, who had walked three steps into the room ahead of all of them and was therefore the nearest thing that bled. Its claws spread. Its broken-glass voice climbed.

And that was its mistake, because it took its back off the wall and gave Thane the room behind it.

He moved.

No threshold to weigh now, no patience to ration — the door was open and the business inside was simple. Thane went low and fast across the broken floor, glass biting through the knees of his leathers, and he did not go *at* the demons. He went *around*. Past the one snarling at Korroc's shield, past the reek of them, into the dead space behind — the angle no creature watches when it has chosen a target in front of it. He came up out of his roll on the far side with both blades and a clean line into the back of the thing savaging Nageru.

His mother's knife went in under the shoulder. His father's, reversed, followed it home a finger's width lower.

The demon screamed — not pain, demons did not feel pain the way meat did, but *insult*, the outrage of something that had not expected the world to bite back from behind. It bucked. Thane rode it, tore both blades free, and the night went red and loud.

Across the room Korroc was a wall doing wall-work. His shield came up, the boss caught the demon's lunge and turned it — but the thing was quick in a way nothing that size had a right to be, and it ducked *under* the rim, low and fast, and got its teeth into the dwarf's thigh above the greave.

Thane saw his cousin's face go strange.

Not pain. He knew Korroc's pain-face; this wasn't it. This was the look of a man who has put his hand into clean water and felt something in it move — a wrongness, a *foulness*, the demon's bite carrying more than the demon's teeth. For half a breath the foulness reached up into Korroc looking for a seam to widen.

It found Torag's anvil there instead, and broke on it.

"*No,*" Korroc said, almost conversational, the way a smith says it to a weld that won't take, and the wrongness sluiced off him and was gone. He set his boots. He did not give back the half-step the bite had cost him.

Behind his shoulder a long blade of light slid forward — Radiance, in Caleth's hands, the holy edge reshaped to that wicked glaive-point — and stabbed past Korroc into the demon's flank, and where it touched, the thing's hide *smoked* the way nothing else in the room smoked. Korroc's hammer came down a heartbeat after, a forge-blow, full of shoulder.

The demon on Nageru would not die.

Thane had it open in two places and it still stood, still raked, and Nageru hammered it with both fists — clean, brutal, the wet crack of knuckles on demon-bone — and *still* it stood, and worse, it answered. A claw came up and around and dug deep into the aasimar's side, deep, the kind of deep that empties a man, and Nageru made no sound at all. Just a tightening at the jaw. A man closing a door on something so it could not get out of his face.

That decided it for Thane.

He stopped reading the fight. He stopped angling. There was a place under the demon's ribs where the two wounds he'd already made nearly met, a seam he'd opened himself, and he put his mother's blade into it to the guard and *twisted*.

The Abrikandilu's eyes — too many of them, all the wrong shape — went wide. Wide like a thing that has finally understood the floor is gone. It folded. It came apart out of the air and the wreckage and the broken-glass voice all at once and was simply down, a heap of violation collapsing back toward being nothing.

"One," Thane said. Old habit. The flame in his chest banked itself a notch.

The other still had Korroc.

His cousin re-set the shield — shoved the boss up hard against the demon's snapping jaw, levering the mouth away from his face, a man holding a door shut against something pushing — but the claws came in low again, under, *in*, and raked across the dwarf's belly hard enough to ring the mail and stagger him a full pace.

Radiance and the warhammer came down on it together. Light and forged iron, the same instant.

It still stood.

Thane was already moving to flank it when he didn't have to.

Because Nageru — bleeding from his side, one arm pressed tight to the wound, silent — stepped off his back foot and *spun*. The whole turn of him, hip and shoulder and the long lever of his leg, every ounce of an aasimar who had taken the worst of every door tonight and asked for the next one — and his heel took the second Abrikandilu across the skull.

The sound was enormous.

The demon left the floor. It crossed half the museum's ruined length the way Nageru's javelin had crossed it earlier, only this was the whole creature, flung, and it hit the far wall under a smashed crusader-banner with a wet final crunch and slid down it and did not rise. Did not come apart screaming like the first. Just *stopped*, the way a struck thing stops, broken at last by something simpler and older than any of their faith.

Quiet came back into the room a piece at a time.

Thane stood over the first demon's unmaking with both blades dark and ran a finger of his attention down each. Then, because he was a Stonevein and his hands knew the rule before his head did, he wiped them — mother's knife, then father's — down a strip of relatively clean banner-cloth at his feet. Long, even strokes. No Stonevein had ever sheathed a wet blade, and he was not about to be the first in a defiled museum a hundred feet under the worst place in the world.

He did not sheathe them. Not yet. He only made them clean.

"Everyone keeping their blood inside them," he said, "or is that optional now."

"Mostly inside," Korroc grunted. He had a hand pressed to his belly and another to his thigh and the foulness-look had not entirely left the back of his eyes. "It tried to put something in me. The bite."

"I saw your face do something I didn't like."

"It didn't take." Korroc said it flat and certain. *It found the anvil.* He didn't say that part aloud. He didn't have to; Thane had watched it break.

Nageru had sat down. Not collapsed — *sat*, deliberate, lowering himself to one knee among the glass with the care of a man who knows exactly how much he's bleeding and intends to spend the next moment on it. He laid his palm flat against his own torn side. The light came up under it, soft and gold and steady, the way it always did with him — no flourish, no plea you could hear, just the still water of him pouring some of itself into the wound until the wound closed.

"Brother," he said to Thane, when it was done and his breathing had evened. "You came in behind them."

"That's the idea."

"It is a good idea."

"I have them sometimes."

---

Caleth was already walking the long room, his head turning slow from wreck to wreck like a man reading the room's history off its ruins.

"First Crusade relics," he said. Quiet. The formality in his voice had gone somewhere careful. "All of this. This was a *museum*, before. They kept the First Crusade here."

Thane followed his eyes and stopped following them when they reached the two bodies.

He'd marked them on the way in — corpses in a room full of demons are furniture, you note them and move past — but he looked now, properly, the way you look when the demons are dead and the room is yours and the dead are owed it.

Two men. Or they'd been two men. Armored, or they'd been armored; the demons had been at them a long time, and the worst of it was not that the armor was broken but that it was broken *artfully*, with attention, with the patient malice of things that had nothing but time and a temple to defile. Both had been decapitated. The heads had been moved. Arranged.

He knew the surcoats under the filth even so. He'd seen them on living shoulders in the upper city, on the Watch, on the dead in the streets of Kenabres.

"Paladins of Iomedae," Thane said.

Caleth nodded once, and something in the set of his jaw went very still and very old.

It was the corner that caught Thane's eye. The reading part of him, the part that walked into a room and asked *what doesn't belong*, snagged on it — a thing on the floor in the corner that the demons had set down rather than smashed, which meant it had been *useful* to them, which in a room like this meant it had been used for the worst work.

He crossed to it. Crouched. Did not touch it yet — looked first, the way he always looked first.

A warhammer. A good one. Even fouled, even with the head crusted in things he didn't want to name and could name anyway, the *line* of it was beautiful — the balance of a master's work, a head that had not so much as chipped against bone and armor and the long defilement it had clearly been put to.

That was the obscenity of it, really. They'd used a crusader's own weapon, or a weapon worthy of one, to finish dishonoring crusaders' bodies.

"Korroc," he said. "Look."

His cousin came, limping a little on the bitten thigh.

"Looks like this is what they used to finish defiling the bodies." Thane kept his voice level. Let the thing speak for itself. "But — *look* at it."

Korroc looked.

For a moment he didn't say anything at all. Then he went down on one knee beside it, heedless of the glass and the filth, and Thane watched his cousin's whole face change — watched the soldier set down and the *smith* pick up. Korroc was a paladin of Torag the Father of Creation, but before he'd been any of that he'd been a dwarf raised on the proposition that good work is a kind of prayer, and there was good work on the floor in front of him being put to evil ends, and Korroc could no more leave it there than he could leave a soul unsaved.

He bowed his head. Said a few words low in his beard — Torag's, the maker's grace, the thanks a craftsman gives before he handles another craftsman's masterpiece. Then he reached out and hefted it, and Thane saw the weight of it surprise him — not heavy. *Light*. Lighter than steel that size had any right to be.

Korroc went very still.

"*Adamantine,*" he said.

He couldn't keep the awe out of it. Didn't try. He turned it in the dim light, and the head drank the light rather than threw it, that deep blue-black sheen there was no mistaking once you'd seen it, the metal that did not break, that the old songs said the sky-cities had been raised on before they fell. Khar-Zadûn metal. *Their fathers' songs' metal.* Neither of them said that either.

"First Crusade," Korroc murmured. "Made for this fight. The first one." He bent his head again, and this time the prayer was longer, and Thane noted that it went two ways — thanks to Torag for the making of it, and thanks, careful and respectful, to Iomedae, whose knights had carried it and died around it and whose house this had been. A dwarf of the Father giving a daughter-goddess her due over her own dead. Then Korroc took a corner of clean cloth and began, with great gentleness, to wipe the defilement off the head. Long even strokes, exactly the way Thane cleaned his blades, and the kinship of the gesture was not lost on either of them.

Thane leaned in. Looked closer, the way he looked at locks. There was a shimmer in the metal that wasn't only the metal.

"And," he said, "it looks to be enchanted."

Korroc finished a stroke. Felt the truth of it run up his arm the way a smith feels a weld take.

"*Aye,*" he said.

And that was that. Korroc did not announce he was keeping it. He simply did not put it down again, and the way his hand had already found the haft, the way the balance of it had already become the balance of *him*, told Thane the hammer had a new owner and the owner had a new prayer to say over it later. Thane filed it where he filed good news, which was the same place he filed bad: noted, kept, moved on. But he let himself feel it for one breath. A First Crusade weapon, adamantine and warded, taken back out of the dark and put into the hand of the best man he knew. There were worse things to carry out of a defiled museum.

There were a great many worse things in this place, in fact. He suspected they'd meet most of them before the night was through.

---

Caleth had gone to the bodies.

He knelt between the two slain knights and began, without a word to anyone, to set them right. Straightening what could be straightened. Drawing a torn surcoat closed over a torn chest. His lips moved — Shelyn's words, Thane guessed, or Iomedae's; the half-elf knew both and tonight was owed to both — low and steady, the cadence of a man who believed the words mattered to someone on the other side of them.

Nageru rose from where he'd healed himself and crossed to help. Knelt opposite Caleth. His hands were gentle in a way that always surprised Thane a little, in a man whose hands had just put a demon through a wall.

"Should we take them to the shrine?" Nageru asked.

Caleth looked up, and nodded.

They carried the knights between them. 

The paladins laid the two knights out side by side. Arms crossed over the breast, the old way, the soldier's way. Their heads — Caleth handled this part himself, and his face while he did it was a thing Thane looked away from, because some griefs aren't yours to watch — their heads were set back where heads belong, settled true on the cold altar-stone so that, if you did not know, you might think they only slept badly.

Then both paladins knelt, and prayed, and the gold came up faint and warm in the cleaned shrine.

Thane did not kneel.

He stood at the shrine's threshold with his back to the rite and his face to the dark museum, both blades clean and one of them loose in his hand, and he watched the room. That was his part. He knew it without anyone assigning it. There is always, at any rite, a man who stands at the door so the others can close their eyes — and Thane's god, the one he had never named and never would, the keeper of the space between *out there* and *in here*, asked exactly that of him and nothing more. He did not share these two dead men's god. He bowed to no altar of hers. But he could stand at the threshold of her house with a knife and make sure nothing trespassed on the moment her sons were owed, and there was no contradiction in him about it at all. A doorkeeper honors the rite by guarding the door. He listened to the soft gold cadence behind him and watched the broken dark ahead of him and let his friends have their grief, and kept it safe, which was the only kind of prayer he knew how to make.

The paladins finished. Rose.

"It's not enough," Caleth said quietly, of the laying-out. "But it's what we have."

"It's a door closed proper," Thane said. "That's not nothing."

Caleth looked at him a moment, the way he sometimes did, like he'd heard more in it than Thane meant to put there. Then he nodded, and let it be.

---

There was another door off the shrine, and beyond it a room that was, after everything, almost shocking in its plainness.

Empty. Or near enough — a holding space, an antechamber, scuffed and looted but not *worked* the way the museum had been worked. Two more doors led out of it.

Thane took the nearer. Listened first — palm flat to the wood, the old patience, ear to the grain. Nothing on the far side but the particular silence of a large space. He looked second: the frame, the floor, the threshold, all the places a clever enemy hides a goodbye. Clean. He touched it third, and opened it.

The smell hit before the sight did.

Blood and rot and the cold-iron reek of desecration, thicker here than anywhere yet. A temple. A *real* one, not the museum's small shrine — Iomedae's temple, the heart of her house in this place, and the demons had made of it exactly what demons make of holy ground when given time and hate enough.

Statues of crusaders smashed off their plinths, faces hammered to rubble. Filth on the walls in patterns that almost meant something, which was worse than if they'd meant nothing. At the far end an altar of pale alabaster had been heaved over onto its face, its consecrated top pressed down against the fouled floor like a thing being held under water.

And on top of the altar, where the eye couldn't help going—

Thane had seen a great deal tonight and a great deal in his life and he made himself look at it the way he made himself look at the dead knights, because not-looking is a kind of cowardice and his god kept the threshold between knowing and not-knowing and asked him to stay on the knowing side.

Body parts. Many people's. Arranged. Built, with care, with the same artful patient malice that had broken the knights' armor — built up off the altar into a shape, and the shape was *insectoid*, a great mockery of a many-legged thing assembled out of what had been men and women, a blasphemy that was also, unmistakably, a *message*. A signature. Something demonic saying *this is what your saints are for, now.*

"A temple of Iomedae," Caleth said behind him. His voice had gone down to almost nothing, and the deep frown on him was the frown of a man cataloguing a debt. "We need to clear that altar."

They went in.

Caleth and Korroc led — the two who had altars of their own to answer to, the two whose business this most was. Thane and Nageru came behind and a little wide, wary, both of them with the same instinct that a room arranged this carefully by a clever enemy was a room arranged *for* something, and the something was rarely the part you could see.

It was Korroc who caught it.

"Caleth." He'd stopped. His new hammer came up a few inches, not threat, just *attention*. "Look at the glow."

The left wall was glowing.

Not torchlight, not the gold of paladin grace. A steady seam of light along the stone, low and even and *there*, the way a line of light shows under a closed door in a dark house.

"Obviously magic," Caleth said, and crossed to it. He'd put up Radiance somewhere along the way; both his hands were free as he came to the wall and ran them just shy of the glowing stone, reading it.

Thane watched Nageru go still in a different way — the stillness of a man remembering something. There'd been words, earlier, somewhere in this place. A prayer-bench with a message scratched or set into it that Nageru had read aloud and they'd all filed away. Thane saw the aasimar's lips move, fitting the memory to the moment.

Caleth had clearly fitted it already.

The half-elf set his hand near the glowing seam, drew a breath, and spoke — not loud, but with the particular weight a man gives words he knows are a key.

"*Let us inherit thine arms, Iomedae.*"

The wall answered.

A grinding, deep in the stone — the grate of a mechanism old and heavy and not used in a long time waking up. The seam of light widened. A whole section of wall began, slowly, to draw back.

Caleth stepped toward it.

Thane's hand closed on his cloak.

"Whoa. Hold on."

He hauled the paladin back half a step — gently, but firmly enough to land — and put himself in front of the opening before Caleth could fill it with his face.

"After everything we've seen in this place," Thane said, low, "you're just going to *walk in*."

Caleth had the grace to stop.

Thane went to work. This was his door now, his threshold, his kind of prayer. He looked at the grinding gap before it had finished opening, read the floor of it, the lintel, the dark beyond the moving stone. A holy passphrase didn't mean a holy room; he'd seen enough tonight to know the demons had been *into* everything, and a thing that opens to a word can be made to bite the second word-knower through the door. He went forward slow, one careful pace at a time, watching where his weight went, watching the floor for the seam that didn't belong, the tile that sat proud, the wire-thin shadow.

Nothing. The mechanism finished. The dust of years and not the reek of recent malice. Whatever was in here, the demons hadn't gotten into it.

He let his breath out.

"Clear," he said. "Come on."

It was an armory.

Iomedae's arms — the temple's true store, the weapons and armor of her house, racked and stood and laid out in order along the walls of a room the desecration outside had never reached. The passphrase had held it shut and the demons had either never cracked it or never bothered, too busy with the slow art of defiling bodies to care about a sealed room of *steel*.

For a moment the four of them just stood in it.

"This," Caleth said softly, "the city needs."

He was right and they all knew it at once. This wasn't loot. Out there above them the forces of Kenabres were bleeding for every blade — and here was a sealed temple-armory, untouched, the war-stores of the Inheritor herself. Thane felt the soldier's arithmetic do itself in his head: every weapon in this room was a crusader who didn't fight bare-handed, a wall that held a day longer.

"We mark it and we get it to them," Thane said. "Whatever we don't carry, we leave findable for the ones who come after us."

They went through it with the brisk reverence of men who understood exactly what they were handling. They took what they knew and could use now — Nageru gathered a sheaf of good javelins and slung them; Thane found bundled arrows in oilcloth, dry and true, and took what he could carry against the bows they'd want above. Useful things. Recognized things. They left the rest in better order than they'd found it, a gift to the city, marked in Thane's memory by the surest map he owned.

Korroc lingered at a wall-rack and lifted down a shield.

Thane saw the symbol on it before Korroc said anything. The sunburst. Iomedae's, worked into the face of the steel in a craftsman's hand, and even across the room he could see the thing was old and good and *humming* in the way the hammer had hummed.

Korroc turned it in his hands, and there was the smith's look again, and under it something more careful.

"Aye," he said, before Thane could even raise the question. "But look at the work." He ran a thumb along the rim. "It radiates magic. Same as the hammer." He was quiet a moment, weighing more than the shield. "It's hers, not the Father's. I know it. I'll take it and bring Iomedae with us — carry her through this place." He settled the shield experimentally on his arm, and the rightness of it sat strangely beside the foreignness of the symbol. "Until I can stand still long enough to ask the gods' leave to put on Torag's hammer and anvil. Borrowed, till then. Carried with respect."

"She won't mind the loan, today," Caleth said. "Not for the use you'll put it to."

"No," Korroc agreed. "I don't expect she will."

---

There was one more door at the temple's far end, and it was locked.

A real lock, not a passphrase — a worked mechanism in a good door, the first honest lock Thane had met all night, and despite everything something in him eased at the sight of it. A lock was *his* language. A lock didn't lie to you the way a glowing wall might. You listened to it and it told you the truth.

He knelt. Drew his picks. *Listen first.* He set the tension and felt the plug's small resistances, the pins waking one at a time under the steel, and the world narrowed to the good clean problem of it the way it always did.

*Look second. Touch third.*

The last pin set. The plug turned. A single clean *click*, the sweetest sound he knew.

The instant the latch cleared, the world broke.

Glass shattered somewhere just inside, a sharp bursting *crash*, and the room beyond vomited smoke — a flask broken on purpose, the whole space going gray and blind in the space of a breath. An ambush, sprung the moment the lock gave. *Of course.* The honest lock, and a trap behind it after all, just not the kind he could read from outside.

Thane didn't hesitate. The one thing you do not do when smoke floods a doorway is stand in it; you go *through*, while there's still a through to go through. He shoved in low and fast and made it past the worst of the billow before it closed, came up on the far side of it with both blades, and saw —

A tiefling. Red armor, heavy and worked, the dull crimson of dried blood. A dagger in one hand with a wicked recurved belly to the blade. And in the other hand a flask, already cocked back.

Thane lunged for the flask hand. Knock it loose, end it before it started.

The tiefling read him and gave ground — a quick step back that took the flask out of Thane's reach — and then, instead of guarding it, *threw* it. Not away. At *him*. Point blank.

It burst across Thane's chest and the world turned to fire.

He'd been burned before. This was worse — alchemical, clinging, it went where the liquid went and the liquid had gone all down his front and up his arm, and the pain was a white scream and the *smell* was his own gear and his own self starting to cook. He did not drop his knives. Some cold dwarven core of him held onto that even as the rest of him was a torch. But he staggered, blind in his own flames, and his next strike went nowhere worth going.

Nageru came through the smoke.

The aasimar materialized out of the gray at a dead run and swung — a strike that should have folded the tiefling in half — and the fist met the heavy crimson armor and *stopped*. Turned. The armor was as good as it looked. Nageru rocked back, recalibrating.

Korroc shouldered in beside him, the adamantine hammer already swinging, and the blow was true, the line perfect, the hammer coming down right where the tiefling's skull was—

—and the tiefling *blurred*.

Thane saw it even through his own fire: the red shape smeared sideways, came apart into two half-real images of itself and slid, and the hammer passed clean through where the head wasn't. The thing wore a spell that made it lie about where it stood, and now they were four men trying to hit a liar in the smoke while one of them burned.

"*I'm on fire,*" Thane observed, with the flat clarity of a man too busy being on fire to scream about it, "in case anyone's keeping a list—"

Water hit him.

It came from nowhere, a sudden drenching weight out of empty air — Caleth's hand outstretched, a word on his lips — and a whole cold wash of it sluiced down Thane head to foot and the fire went out all at once with an angry hiss and a cloud of steam. The relief was so total and so violent that it threw him; he was soaked and gasping and the dagger-strike he'd already committed to went wild, his mother's blade carving smoke a foot from the tiefling's blurred ribs.

The tiefling laughed and threw a flask at Caleth.

It burst against the half-elf's guard and sprayed fire wide — across all of them, a fine clinging mist of it. Thane, soaked through, didn't catch. Nageru twisted clear. Caleth's create-water-damp turned most of it. But Korroc took it across the shoulder and the borrowed sunburst shield and the dwarf was suddenly *burning*, flame running up his arm.

The tiefling stood in the spreading fire of his own making entirely unbothered, the way a thing is unbothered by what it has made its whole self proof against.

Nageru struck the blur and found nothing.

"Stop *moving,*" Korroc growled — not to the tiefling, to himself, a smith correcting his own eye — and he swung again, and this time he didn't aim for where the tiefling *was*. He aimed for where the lie wasn't. He'd watched the smear twice now and his craftsman's eye had the rhythm of it, and the adamantine hammer came down on the true body behind the false one and *connected*, a sound like a sledge on a side of beef, and the tiefling staggered for the first time. Still burning himself. Burning and unbothered and hurt.

Water hit Korroc — Caleth again, dousing Korroc the way he'd doused Thane, the fire on the dwarf's arm gone in a hiss.

Thane found his feet and his focus both. Stabbed twice, fast. The first found the true tiefling, his mother's knife biting in under the arm where the red armor gapped. The second found the blur and passed through nothing and he wasted no temper on it — that was the cost of fighting a liar, half your truths bought you nothing, you simply kept buying.

The tiefling, hurt now and knowing it, smashed a flask down at all their feet.

Fire bloomed up off the floor in a ring. Thane felt the heat wash his still-wet leathers and find no purchase. Korroc, doused over now, stood in the flames and let them gutter against his soaked mail and did not so much as flinch.

Nageru, who had spent the fight grasping at a lie, changed what he was buying. He stopped throwing his whole power into strikes that hit smoke. He shortened up. Traded force for *truth* — slower, lower, a precise short blow that did not commit past the moment he could confirm the target — and it landed, finally, solid on the real tiefling. Korroc's hammer followed it down, another true blow, the adamantine head ringing off the crimson plate.

Caleth crossed to Thane in the chaos and laid a hand on him. Gold poured in. The burned skin of Thane's chest and arm knit and cooled under a wash of warmth that was the exact opposite of the fire, and the white scream in his nerves dialed down to a manageable ache.

"Owe you," Thane grunted.

"Stab him," Caleth suggested.

Thane stabbed him. One true, under the same arm, deepening the seam he'd opened. One into the blur, gone. He didn't curse the wasted one. He'd stopped expecting better of the spell and that was its own kind of advantage.

The tiefling understood, now, that he was losing.

He did the sensible thing. He smashed his last flask — smoke, not fire — and the whole room went blind again, the gray flooding up to swallow all of them, and Thane felt rather than saw the red shape *break* and try to tumble past them for the door, low and fast, a professional cutting his losses.

He almost made it.

Nageru stuck out a fist into the gray on pure instinct — and missed — but the tiefling ran into the *arm* anyway, full tilt, and rebounded off it and stopped dead, his escape arrested by a limb he hadn't known was there.

And then Nageru did the thing that Thane would remember out of this whole ugly little fight.

The aasimar couldn't see him. The smoke was total. But Nageru went *still* — still water, the stillness he carried into every fight — and *listened*, the way Thane listened at locks, and somewhere in the gray the tiefling scrabbled and breathed and gave himself away by the smallest sound, and Nageru turned his head a half-inch toward it and brought one fist down out of the smoke toward the noise.

It connected.

A heavy, final *thud*, fist on skull, and then the softer sound of a body going down and not getting up.

They waited. None of them moved while the smoke thinned and settled and finally cleared.

The tiefling lay on the floor, crumpled in his fine red armor, bleeding from a dozen seams, his blur gone with his consciousness. Down. Maybe done.

"Remind me," Thane said, looking at the wreck of the man who'd set him on fire, "to never play cards with whatever made *that* one."

---

The room was a workshop.

A long table of alchemical equipment — burners and glass and racked flasks, the source of all that clinging fire — and against the wall, a weapon case. Good wood, brass fittings, the kind of case made to hold one thing well rather than many things poorly.

Thane went to it the way he went to everything, looking before touching. He found no trick in the lid, only a simple catch, and opened it.

A sword. A fine one, longsword by the length, well-kept under a film of neglect. And laid across the blade, a folded paper.

He lifted the note out. Unfolded it. Read it once to himself, fast, the way he read everything first — and then went still in a way the others noticed, and read it again, slower, aloud, because by the second line he understood that the whole party needed to hear it.

> *Lord Staunton,*
>
> *I believe you will find the enclosed weapon to be a singular and familiar delight, for it once belonged to your old "friend" Irabeth. She sold it to Kandro Nyserian, of all people—I've no idea why. Kandro's been sitting on it for years—still would be, except his home got smashed by an ulkreth. Shame. He managed to save the sword and came here, begging for an escort north to Drezen, doubtless so he could give you the sword and weasel some cash to help him set up a new home. I hope you don't begrudge a creativity I took—our friend Nyserian serves now as a warm suit for a vermlek as punishment for only revealing the sword now instead of when he first acquired it. I suspect the blade will take to your brother's touch nicely!*
>
> *your loyal servant,*
> *Othirubo*

The room was quiet when he finished.

It was the *warm suit for a vermlek* that landed in Thane first, and it landed like a dropped stone, straight down through everything else.

*Nyserian.*

He knew that name now. He hadn't, until just now — it had been a sound in a letter, a stranger's name — and then it dropped into the slot where a memory was already waiting and the two fit together with a click he felt in his teeth. The garrison doors. Hours ago, days ago, time had gone strange down here — the bloated wrong thing that had worn a man's skin like a soaked coat and split open wrong when they killed it, the demon that had been *inside* somebody. It confirmed a theory, the owner of Nyserian Manor, listed in the first letter as a safehouse, had met his end from the very side he had served. Here, in a gloating little aside in a servant's letter, was the cold confirmation of exactly what that skin-suit horror at the doors had been: a man named Kandro Nyserian, punished for the crime of being late with a sword, hollowed out and worn as a warning by a thing called a vermlek.

He filed it. There was nothing else to do with it. But he filed it heavily.

The second thing was a name he *did* know, and it sat wrong in a different way.

*Irabeth.*

Their ally. The Watch leader up in the broken light of the real city, the half-orc who'd sent them down here, the woman who — Thane knew, though it wasn't his to say — was married to Anevia. And this sword in this case had been *hers*, once. Sold off, years ago, to a man who got skinned for it. Why a paladin like Irabeth would sell a sword good enough for a demon-lord's brother to covet, Thane couldn't guess. A debt. A grief. A past she'd closed a door on. It wasn't his to open. But he marked it — a small sad mystery about a friend — and set it beside the others.

The third thing was the one that woke the hunter in him, and he kept his face very still while it did.

*Lord Staunton.*

Against his own skin, under his shirt, Thane carried a folded letter of his own — old, soft at the creases from handling, a lead he'd turned over in his mind for days. It was signed with two letters. *S. V.* And here, in his hands, was a second letter, and a lord, and a name that started with *S*, and a *brother* the blade was promised to.

The instinct pricked hard. *S. Staunton. A brother.*

He did not let it close the circuit. That was the discipline of the thing — the hunter who jumps to the conclusion he wants is the hunter who chases the wrong man into the dark. A name starting with S was not a name. A lord with a brother was not *his* lord, not yet, not on this much. He had taught himself a long time ago not to make two threads into one rope just because the dark made it tempting. So he did what he always did with a thread he couldn't yet pull: he noted it, cold, exact — *Staunton, a lord, a brother, here, now, in this fortress* — and he set it down unsolved, next to the soft old letter against his skin, and he let it lie there cold.

*Drezen.* The last name in it tugged at something too — north, a fortress, a place the songs put in demon hands. The place, if he remembered the temple-lore right, where a paladin had fallen long ago carrying a blade that one of his own companions now carried glowing in his hand. He glanced at Radiance without meaning to. Light recognition, nothing he could stand on. He let that lie too.

"Who's Othirubo," Korroc said. It wasn't really a question.

"Someone who enjoys his work," Thane said. He folded the note crisp along its old creases and tucked it away — not against his skin with the other, but close. "And who serves a Lord Staunton. Who has a brother. Who they think holds this place, or did." He looked at the fine sword in its case and did not touch it. "We take the sword. Irabeth might want to know it's above ground again."

No one argued. Caleth gently closed the case and took it under his arm.

Then the half-elf turned and put a hand on Korroc's shoulder, and one on the air between all of them.

"Let me help."

Two waves of gold rolled out of him, one after the other, washing over the four of them — closing the small hurts, the burns Caleth hadn't reached, the bite in Korroc's thigh, the lingering ache under Thane's healed skin. It was a generous thing and it cost the half-elf something to give; Thane saw the small fatigue settle on him as the light faded, the toll all of them were running up in this place.

"No telling what this place still holds," Korroc said, settling the borrowed shield, hefting the new hammer. A warning and a readiness both.

"No," Thane agreed. "There isn't."

---

Back in the plain antechamber, there was one door they hadn't opened.

It was barred. From the other side — Thane could tell by the way it sat, the slight give and stop of it, a heavy timber dropped into brackets by someone who wanted very much to keep the door between themselves and whatever came down the hall. Which meant people. Which meant they knew the party was coming.

"I can use the chime again," Thane started — there was a way to open a barred door from the wrong side, with patience and the right tool, and he had both—

Nageru stepped past him.

The aasimar set himself in front of the barred door, and breathed, and dropped his weight into a stance Thane had learned to read as *something is about to break*. Stillness, gathered. Then the whole line of him uncoiled and his bare heel drove into the door beside the bar.

The door did not so much open as *cease*. It blew inward off its brackets in a spray of splinters and the broken bar clattered away across the floor inside, and the way was simply, abruptly, *open*.

"Or that," Thane allowed.

Inside: a tiefling, mid-flinch, *stunned* — clearly the one who'd dropped the bar, who'd been standing too close to the door when the door stopped existing, and who now stood frozen with the dumb blank look of a man whose entire plan had just been kicked off its hinges. And behind him, at the back of the room, squat and gray-green and entirely unbothered by the violence of the entry, three dretches.

Thane knew dretches. He'd fought them in the cistern, and he knew the ugly truth of them — lesser demons, weak, stupid, *slow*, and yet maddeningly hard to put down with honest steel, their wet gray flesh turning a true edge aside like the blade had hit something half-real, which it had. They were not dangerous one at a time. They were dangerous the way a held door is dangerous — by being in the way while worse things happen.

Caleth's hand came up, a word, a gesture at Thane—

—and Thane *faded.*

He'd had this done to him before and it never stopped being strange: the sudden absence of his own hands at the edge of his sight, the world going on not-seeing him. Invisible. The half-elf had made him a knife nobody was watching, which was, Thane reflected, more or less his ideal state of being.

The fight was short and ugly and close.

A dretch lumbered at Nageru and bit him — and Nageru's free hand dropped to his belt, to the silver scale he carried there, the dragon's gift, and Thane saw the aasimar's knuckles take on a faint clean light, holy and cold, the charge of the thing waking up. Two strikes, flashing, and the dretch that had bitten him folded and went down twitching in the doorway.

The stunned tiefling came unstuck. He took one look at the room — the broken door, the glowing fists, the down dretch, the dwarf with the adamantine hammer filling the gap — and bolted for a staircase at the room's far side, taking the stairs two at a time, gone up into the dark above.

Thane marked it. *Up.* They were always going up in this place, toward the heart of it. He let the runner go; there was no catching him through the choke of dretches, and there'd be a reckoning with whatever was up there soon enough.

A second dretch got its teeth into Nageru. The aasimar's face went to stone — that closing-the-door look, taking the wound without a sound.

Thane, unseen, slid in beside the thing and put both blades into it. And here was a small strange thing he noticed and filed and did not stop to chew on: in the cistern his honest steel had skated off this same wet gray hide, turned aside as if by something half-there. Now it bit. Both blades found purchase and stayed and the dretch screamed its thin ugly scream. Whether his own edge had been honed sharper by something he'd taken up tonight, or whether it was only that he'd learned in the long hours down here exactly *where* on a half-real thing to put a real point — he didn't know, and he didn't stop to wonder. The dretch was dying. That was the relevant fact. Also relevant was that he was visible after his strike.

A third dretch clawed at Nageru's flank.

Korroc held the doorway like a stopper in a bottle, hammer up, shield up, *waiting* — the right call, let them come into the choke rather than spilling out into the open — and Caleth pushed Radiance forward over Thane's shoulder, the glowing point sliding past where Thane stood unseen, and stabbed, and the holy light *flashed* white, and a dretch came apart around it.

Nageru brought both glowing fists down on the last and it was simply gone, a gray smear collapsing into the floor.

The room was theirs. Three lesser demons unmade, a tiefling fled up the stairs, and a staircase yawning open at the far end into the dark of the garrison's upper floor.

"He went up," Nageru said, looking after the fled tiefling. The light was already fading from his fists, the scale's charge spent.

"Aye," said Korroc, settling himself, and there was iron under it. "Then up's where the rest of them are."

---

The stair was stone, broad enough for two, and it climbed into a darkness that breathed.

Thane went up it alone.

It wasn't discussed; it didn't need to be. This was the work he did. The others held at the bottom in the broken doorway — Korroc's shield first, Caleth behind, Nageru loose and ready — while Thane went up sideways and silent, back to the wall, one slow foot finding the next stair without sound. *Listen. Look.* He climbed into the breathing dark and let his ears go ahead of his eyes.

There were men up there. He could hear them — the small sounds of waiting men, leather and breath and the creak of a held crossbow — and the sounds said *more than a few* and *spread out* and *ready*.

He eased his head up over the lip of the top stair to look.

**THUNK.**

The bolt took him in the shoulder before he'd finished the thought *crossbow*. A hard punching slam, the head of it driving through leather and into the meat of him, and the shock of it rocked him back a half-step on the stair. Someone had been watching the exact spot a head would appear, and put a bolt there on instinct the moment the dark moved.

He grimaced. Got a hand to the shaft and left it; you don't pull a bolt in a fight, you fight with it in you and weep about it later.

Then Nageru went past him like a thrown spear.

The aasimar came up the stairs in a rush and over the top in a single flowing movement, past Thane's wounded shoulder and into the room, and the first thing in his way was a tiefling, and the tiefling went down under his fists before it had finished turning.

And then the room *stood up.*

Five more of them, springing from cover all at once — Thane's read had been right, *spread out*, *ready* — and the air filled with the snap of crossbows. A bolt took Nageru somewhere in the body; he absorbed it and kept moving. Caleth came up the stair into the noise and engaged and missed, his strike going wide in the chaos of the first contact.

Korroc came up behind Thane and put it together in a glance, the way Thane put a lock together — the whole shape of the trap, read in a heartbeat.

"Crossfire," the dwarf said. "Two yon end. Three the other. *Bows.* They've boxed the stair."

And they had. It was a good ambush, a soldier's ambush — two clusters of tieflings at opposite ends of the long upper hall with crossbows, so that anyone coming up the stair into the middle stood in the killing ground of both. Bolts came from two directions at once. There was no facing both.

So Korroc didn't try.

He turned his back to Nageru's back — the two of them spine to spine in the open, the dwarf's shield and the dwarf's faith making a wall one way while the aasimar's fists worked the other — and the borrowed sunburst shield came up, and bolts that should have found soft targets *banged* off the steel and skated away. One got past, found Thane, another bee-sting punch into a body already paying. Korroc was a fortress and a fortress draws fire, and Thane, behind him, felt the grim mathematics of it: every bolt that rang off the dwarf's shield was a bolt that didn't find a throat.

Nageru dropped another tiefling. Caleth got a true stab into one and took a bolt through the meat of his arm for the trouble. Korroc reached back without looking and laid a hand on Thane, and gold ran into the bolt-wound in his shoulder, the meat closing around the shaft, the worst of the shock easing even with the bolt still in.

Thane lost the fine grain of it, then. He always did, in close work like this — the world narrowed to the next target and the next angle and the wet specifics in front of him, and the wide shape of the fight went on somewhere above his attention. He stabbed and moved and stabbed. The hunter in him kept a running ledger even when the soldier couldn't spare a glance: *two down, the boxed crossfire breaking up as we close, get inside the bow-range and the bow stops being a bow—*

And then the hall got worse.

Doors. All along the upper hall, doors he hadn't cleared because he'd had a bolt in his shoulder the second he'd looked — and they opened now, and *men* came out of them. Not tieflings. Humans. Cultists, glaives in their hands, the long reach of the weapon ugly in a hall, and there were more of them than Thane wanted to count and no time to count them.

One drove a glaive at Caleth — and Caleth simply *wasn't there.* The half-elf side-stepped in that way of his, here and then a foot to the left, and the glaive carved the space he'd vacated.

Another door. Another human, blinking out into the slaughter with the dumb honest confusion of a man whose night had been quiet until this second: "*What is going on out here?*"

Nobody answered him.

A cultist came out swinging and put his glaive *into* Nageru — a solid hit, the long blade biting deep, and the aasimar grunted at last, the first sound he'd made, blood coming bright. His hand slapped over the wound and the gold welled up under his own palm, self-mending even as he fought, refusing to go down.

Thane finished his tiefling — a stab that found the seam and the thing collapsed off his blade — and turned into the new chaos of the doors.

Caleth, boxed suddenly by cultists spilling from two doorways at once, *vanished*. Gone from the closing trap, reappeared a stride away at Nageru's side, and his hand came out and a word came off his lips and a faint shimmer settled over the aasimar — *harder to hit*, some grace laid over Nageru like a slick of oil that shafts and blades would slide off of. The right man to put it on; Nageru was the one who went first into everything, the one the room aimed at.

Korroc stepped to Nageru's other side, a cure-word in his beard, and the glaive-wound in the aasimar's chest sealed.

A glaive scored Thane. A bolt slammed him from the dwindling crossfire. The ledger in his head was getting expensive. He pulled the potion he'd been saving for exactly this — the bad-arithmetic moment — and drank it fast, and the world un-saw him again, the comfortable wrongness of his own absent hands. Better. A knife nobody's watching, in a hall full of men with their backs to each other.

Caleth slid again — Thane caught it out of the corner of his fading-into-nothing sight — *behind* a tiefling, and the half-elf called on his goddess, and Radiance *blazed*. The whole glaive lit white, holy fire running its length, and Caleth jabbed it forward and the light erupted out the front of the tiefling's chest in a lance of judgment, then ripped back out, and the tiefling was a falling thing with a hole of light cooling in it.

And then.

Thane was invisible, moving along the wall, lining up the cultist nearest him, when it happened at the far end of the hall, and he saw all of it, every part, in the terrible slow way that the worst things arrive.

A cultist behind Nageru. A glaive, drawn back. And the cultist stepped in and drove it — not a graze, not a scoring hit, but a clean full thrust with all his weight behind it — and the long blade went *through* Nageru. Through the chest. The point of it came out the aasimar's back wet and bright, the slick of Caleth's grace not enough, the dragon's scale silent at his belt, all of it not enough at once—

—and Nageru, who had walked first through every door tonight, who had taken the bite and the bolt and the deep claw and the glaive without a sound, who was the still water at the center of all of them—

—folded.

The glaive came free and he folded down over it, down onto his knees and then his face, onto the bloodied stone of the upper hall, and did not move.

Thane was at the wrong end of the world.

The whole hall was between them. A hall full of cultists and bolts and the wreck of the fight, and Nageru was *down*, down the way the dead knights in the museum were down, and there was nothing Thane could do about it from here but watch — and watching, helpless, with the banked flame in his chest gone white and useless because there was nothing in reach to spend it on, was the worst thing the night had done to him yet.

A cultist stepped over Nageru's fallen body and thrust his glaive at Caleth, and missed.

The hunter in Thane took over because the man couldn't. *You can't reach him. Korroc can. Get Korroc the room to reach him. End the things between.* He went down the hall unseen and fast and put his mother's blade into the back of the cultist nearest, the seam under the arm, and the man dropped without ever knowing what corner of the dark had killed him.

Korroc had seen it too. Thane caught his cousin's face as the dwarf turned toward the fallen aasimar — and there was no panic in it, only the iron, only the terrible patience of a man who has decided to live through this and carry his people with him. Korroc's tiefling fired and the bolt banged off the sunburst shield. Another came at him with a shortsword and the point skated off his mail. The dwarf was a fortress and the fortress was *moving*, grinding step by step down the hall toward Nageru, killing what got in the way.

Caleth got there first.

The half-elf reached the cultist who'd put the glaive through Nageru, and his hand came up open, and he laid it flat against the man's chest — and the air *cracked.* That smell came off it, the one Thane had learned to dread on Caleth's behalf, the smell of baking flesh, sharp and wrong, as the lightning Caleth carried in his palm poured out through the touch and into the man. The cultist's eyes flew wide. His whole body locked, smoking, and then he came apart at the knees and went down dead with the burned-meat reek rising off him.

Korroc reached Nageru.

Thane couldn't watch it and fight, but he heard the cure-words, fast and certain, and from the edge of his eye he caught the gold flooding down out of the dwarf's hands into the ruin of the aasimar's chest — and then, the thing Thane needed more than air: a movement. Nageru's hand, on the stone. Closing. The still water not gone after all, only driven deep, and rising back.

The hall became a grind, after that. Positioning more than hitting — men shuffling for angle in a space too tight, the crossfire collapsed into a brawl. Thane stabbed a tiefling and it fell and he stepped up and stabbed the cultist past it; Korroc's hammer came down on the same cultist a beat behind him, adamantine ending the argument. A cultist got a glaive into Caleth, a real hit; another tried Korroc and the dwarf turned the long blade off his shield without seeming to notice. Thane pushed in and stabbed and that cultist folded.

There was one left at the far end, edging back, looking for the door he'd come out of, a man doing the arithmetic and not liking the sum.

Thane and Korroc went down the hall together — Thane with both daggers at ready and Korroc a wall of adamantine and borrowed sunburst beside him — and the cultist saw the dwarf coming and tried to slide wide of him to escape the pincer, out of Korroc's reach.

Right into the knife that was quicker than he thought it would be.

Thane stabbed hard, low and true, and found the man exactly where the man had stepped to be safe, and the cultist looked down at the wound opening in him with the same blank astonishment the stunned tiefling had worn, and then he collapsed, and the hall was quiet.

Korroc went to Nageru.

The aasimar was sitting up, gray-faced, one hand pressed flat to a chest that was whole again but remembered the wound, his breath coming careful. Alive. Korroc knelt by him and brought out a wand from somewhere in his kit — a thing of healing, charges to spend that didn't cost the dwarf his own marrow — and passed it over Nageru, and then over Caleth where the glaive had scored him, the gold rolling out of the wood. Then Korroc straightened, and lifted his hand, and sent the waves out wide one more time, washing over all four of them at once, closing the long ledger of small hurts that a fight this hard had run up.

It had been a hard fight. The hardest yet, maybe, of a night that had been nothing but hard.

Thane stood there in the bloodied upper hall and made himself do the thing the Stonevein rule demanded even now, even here — drew a strip from a fallen cultist's tabard and wiped his mother's blade, then his father's, long even strokes, clean. No Stonevein had ever sheathed a wet blade. He still didn't sheathe them. The night wasn't over.

He looked at the hall.

They'd taken it. Both floors, the museum and the temple and the armory and the workshop and the stairs and now this slaughterhouse of a corridor — taken it all, at a price that had nearly bought Nageru's life and might yet buy worse. Dead tieflings and dead cultists and the reek of baked flesh and demon-rot and his own scorched leathers. The garrison's ground was theirs.

But it was only the ground.

They weren't done — past whatever door at the end of this hall they hadn't yet opened — the heart of the place still beat. The wardstone fragment they'd come down into hell to find: unfound, the rod that would break it still cold and unused in Caleth's keeping. Areelu Vorlesh's purpose, whatever it truly was, still turning somewhere above their heads, unbroken. And two new names hung in the dark with the smell of the place, filed cold against the soft old letter on his skin — *Lord Staunton* and the *brother* a gloating servant had promised a stolen sword would suit nicely.

The group stood in the hallway.

Four of them, bloodied and breathing, on ground that had cost too much and bought too little, with the door to the rest of it waiting at the end of the hall like one no one had opened yet — and on the far side of it, Thane knew with the certainty of a man who had spent his whole life reading thresholds, was the part of the night they would not all walk away from clean.

He flexed his fingers on two clean knives, and watched the door, and waited for someone to say it was time to go.
