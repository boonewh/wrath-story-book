# The Patient Thunder
*Chapter Five — The Open Fist*

The lotus held him the way the river held a stone — without effort, without question, without end.

Nageru breathed.

The breath went down through the bronze of him into the bones, and through the bones into the floor of the manor, and through the floor into the broken earth beneath, and the broken earth answered with a low, wrong hum that had not stopped since they had climbed out of the dark. He had learned, in four days, how to listen past it. The way a man learned to sleep beside a forge.

Across the room, Anevia rolled the dice for Klarah and the child made a small triumphant sound.

"I won."

A real voice. A real word. The first he had heard from her that was not the careful, plain economy of a child who had stopped expecting anything. Nageru did not open his eyes. He let the smile move only inward, where it would not startle her.

*She has begun to be a person again,* he thought. *Order returns in small pieces. A face. A name. A win at dice.*

The breath went down.

The breath came up.

And between one breath and the next, something arrived at the edge of his awareness that was not sound.

He had felt it before, the wrong-shape of demon-things. He had felt it the moment they stepped out of the underdark into Kenabres and the city had pressed against his blood like a hand against a window. He had felt it standing prayer-still beside Korroc as the dwarf spoke the last rites over Sarenrae's knights. He had felt it every hour since, a constant low pressure his Archon blood would not let him put down.

This was different.

This was *near.*

Not a single wrong-shape but many, a pressure that prickled like a hand passed too close to a flame. Many small wrongnesses moving together. Moving *toward.*

He opened his eyes.

He stood.

He was already moving when he registered that he had decided to move. Down the hall to the room with the eastern window. Bare feet silent on the boards. Behind him a soft, swift step — Anevia, bow in her hand before she could have named why.

He had not heard her hear him. She had heard him simply by knowing him.

*Good,* he thought. *Good.*

He reached the window and put one hand to the frame and angled his head, and there in the gray Kenabres morning, between two collapsed roofs three streets out, he saw what his blood had been telling him.

The street was moving.

Not with people. With a black, glittering, low river of bodies — wing and chitin and too many legs, a tide of demon-insects pouring around a corner and on toward the manor with the unmotivated hunger of a swarm that had forgotten what it was hunting and only remembered that it was hungry.

Behind him Anevia's breath caught.

"How did they find us?"

Her voice was small and tight. He did not look at her. He watched the swarm and watched the way it moved — not arrowing, not searching. *Fleeing.* Spilling. Something behind them had set them running and they were tearing through the street with the panic of small evil things shoved out of their burrow by something bigger.

"They have not found us," he said. "They are running from something. Something worse."

Anevia's eyes — he saw them in the reflection — went very wide.

He nodded to her.

She nodded back.

It was the whole conversation. She was already turning.

"Klarah," Anevia called, even as she ran, her voice gone bright and warm and lying easily through her teeth. "Get the dice, honey. We have to go."

Nageru stepped from the window and crossed back into the front room in three long strides. Klarah was on her feet, the small wooden cup still in her hand, the painted dice clutched against her chest, her face that careful unreadable thing it had been when they first carried her up out of the cellar. He did not waste a word. He bent and lifted her and she came up against him as light as a sheaf of reeds, her arms looping around his neck because there was nowhere else to put them.

"Hold the dice," he said into her hair. "Hold them tight."

She held them tight.

Down the hall he could hear Anevia in the study — *Horgus, there is a wave of bugs coming, we HAVE to go, NOW* — and the muttered noble's curse, and the surprising scrape of furniture as the man moved faster than any of them had ever seen him move.

Nageru was already at the door.

He paused there only long enough to be sure they were behind him. Anevia first, bow strung, eyes everywhere. Horgus second, a thin satchel banging at his hip, his face the color of old paper.

Nageru carried the child out of the manor at a run.

---

The streets were a wound.

He had walked them once already in the half-light of their arrival, when Korroc had led them through with that quiet, devastated tread. He had not let his mind name what he saw then. Now the morning was on it, and the morning was honest, and the streets were a wound.

A house leaned across an alley like a drunkard against a wall. A wagon stood in the middle of a square with its team gone and a child's shoe on the seat. The dust of broken stone hung in the air the way mist hangs over a river before sunrise, and through the mist came the small evil shapes of quasits darting between rooftops, and the smell that had not left the air since the rift opened — sulphur and old blood and a sweetness underneath that was worse than either.

He ran with the child in his arms and felt each footfall settle into the breath, breath into footfall, the way running became prayer when there was no time to pray.

Klarah did not cry. He felt the shape of her against his ribs — the small fast heartbeat, the cheek pressed to the hollow of his shoulder, the bundle of her hands knotted at the nape of his neck around the dice cup. She weighed almost nothing. She weighed the world.

*This,* he thought. *This is what the thresholds were built to protect. This single weight. Repeat across a thousand thousand and you have what order is for.*

A bug-thing the size of a fist crossed in front of him, low and skittering. He stepped past it without breaking stride.

Behind him Anevia's boots were even and quick. Horgus was breathing like a bellows but he was keeping up. Nageru did not turn to check. He could hear them by the gap between footfalls.

At the corner of what had been a tannery he slowed and let them close the distance. Anevia came up at his shoulder, bow loose at her hip, sweat at her temples.

"We should go to the Blackwing," he said. "The library Aravashnial spoke of. The others are there."

She nodded, already taking the lead, already pointing.

"This way. Stay close."

He stayed close.

Klarah's grip on his neck did not loosen the whole way.

---

The Blackwing tower rose out of the broken cityscape like a tooth in a torn mouth — still standing, but only just. The upper floors were gone. Black scorch ran up one side like a long thrown shadow. The door was closed and a dwarf was standing in front of it.

The dwarf was Thane, and Thane was sliding back inside, half-turned, his hand already coming up in the unmistakable gesture of someone about to call a warning.

Then Thane saw what he was looking at.

Then Thane lowered his hand.

Then Aravashnial stepped out behind him, the wizard's blind-clouded eyes finding nothing in particular and his face finding everything anyway.

"You came," Aravashnial said.

"We came," Nageru said.

Anevia was already at the door. Horgus came up wheezing and collapsed against the frame and did not bother to be a noble about it. Thane took one look at Nageru's burden and his face did the small private thing it did when he was glad of something and would not say so.

"In," Thane said. "Quickly."

Nageru went in.

The library smelled of old paper and new smoke. Korroc was inside, a presence like a banked forge, his shield still on his arm. Caleth was leaning against a shelf with his eyes closed, mouth moving in something that was not quite prayer and not quite cursing. Around them, four men in robes — the librarians — stood with the tight contained postures of people who had been bound an hour ago and were not yet sure they had been let go.

A fifth man lay dead by the wall, blood pooled black around his shoulders. Korroc had put a cloth over the face.

Nageru set Klarah down on a low bench and crouched in front of her and put a hand on her head. "Stay," he said. She nodded. The dice cup did not leave her hands.

He straightened.

Aravashnial had come into the library proper now — the first time, Nageru understood; the wizard had been outside until this moment — and the librarians saw him and the room broke open.

They went to him all at once. Four men talking, four sets of hands at his sleeves, four faces white and gray and one of them weeping without any sound. Aravashnial did not move. He let them touch him. He let them speak. After a while he lifted one veined hand and they fell silent the way servants fall silent at the right gesture.

"Where," he said, "is everyone else."

The head librarian — older, with a long gray beard and the kind of stoop that meant a life of bending over books — lowered his head.

"They are all dead. Either killed fighting, or crushed when the tower fell."

A silence.

Nageru watched Aravashnial's face. He saw the wizard absorb the words the way a body absorbed a blow that had been expected for years. There was no surprise in him. There was only a closing.

"What took the tower?" Aravashnial asked, very quietly.

"The worm."

Caleth had been almost still against the shelves. Now he straightened.

"What worm?" he said.

The head librarian turned to him with the slowness of a man whose tongue had grown heavy in his mouth.

"The worm that walks," he said. "Xanthir Vang. He came and the upper tower came down. He took what he wanted from us and he left."

"What did he take?"

"We do not know."

Aravashnial was breathing carefully, the way a wounded man breathed against a cracked rib.

"And the rest of the order," he said. "The rest of us in this city."

"You." The old librarian's voice softened, the way a healer's softened over a wound that would not close. "I believe — I am afraid — you may be the last."

The librarians' eyes were on Aravashnial and so were Caleth's, sharp and concerned. Nageru watched the wizard and the room and the way the silence settled. He understood that there were things in this exchange that he did not have the shape of. He understood that he would not ask.

*A man's grief is his own house,* he thought. *I will not walk through it uninvited.*

Aravashnial turned, eventually, and looked at the dead man by the wall.

"That one came to the door wearing the armor of Iomedae," the head librarian said, and there was rage in his exhaustion now, threadbare but real. "We let him in. We thought he was a refugee. He bound us and told us he meant to burn us alive as an offering, to call the attention of a demon patron. He was — he was halfway to lighting the fire when —"

"When we arrived," Caleth said.

The librarian nodded.

Nageru looked at the body and felt nothing in particular. A man had decided to commit an evil. The evil had been answered. The line of order had been restored, here, in this one room, on this one morning. It was a small thing. It was the whole of his vocation.

Aravashnial was speaking again, low, to the librarians. Nageru did not eavesdrop. He went and stood beside Klarah, where she sat on the bench with her dice cup held in both hands as if it were a holy relic, and he put one bronze palm flat on the top of her head and left it there. She leaned the smallest fraction into the weight.

A long while later Aravashnial turned to the rest of them.

"The librarians will stay here," he said. "I will stay with them. We must determine what was taken. It will take time."

He paused. A look crossed his face — quick, sharp, as if a thought had reached him from a long way off. He turned back to the librarians.

"Where is Quednys?"

They looked at each other. They shrugged, all four of them, the small helpless shrug of men whose roll-call no longer made sense.

Aravashnial's mouth tightened. He turned to the party.

"Please, when you get to the inn," he said. "Most of what is left of the order — of any order — will be there. Ask after a man named Quednys. If you find him, send him back to us. If you find anyone willing to help guard this place, send them as well. By then I may have an accounting."

Korroc nodded. Thane nodded. Nageru inclined his head.

They went out into the broken street and behind them they heard the door close, and they heard the slow heavy scrape of shelves being dragged across it from inside.

---

Defender's Heart looked like a fist that had refused to open.

Squat, thick-walled, gray stone — a building that had been built in a less hopeful age and had remembered how. Barricades had been thrown up around it, wagon-beds and roof beams angled into spear-walls. On the roofline he counted three archers. Probably there were more he could not see.

They came in slow with their hands clearly empty. Thane walked muttering something under his breath about how he had not survived four days underground to die of an honest arrow on the threshold of an inn. Korroc was at the front, shield slung, walking the way he always walked — like he weighed more than he looked. Anevia had her bow lowered to her side. Horgus was holding Klarah's hand. The girl was looking up at the inn the way she had looked up at the rift when they first carried her into the open.

"Stop there!"

The voice came from the roof. Hard, clipped, not unkind.

"We are friends," Korroc called back, his dwarven cadence carrying.

"Prove it."

Nageru drew one breath.

"Silver Strong," he said.

Not loud. He did not need to be loud. The two words went out across the broken street in his quiet flat voice and they were the right two words and the man on the roof heard them the way a lock heard a key.

A pause. A turning. A voice further along the wall: "Get the captain."

They waited.

Nageru let the breath out slow and stood with his weight even on both feet, the way he had been taught to stand when a moment was about to open. Beside him Anevia was very still. Too still. He glanced once at her face. She was looking at the door with the expression of a person who had decided not to hope and had been betrayed by her own body, because her hope was running ahead of her down the street without permission.

The door opened.

A woman came out.

Nageru had never seen her before. He saw the broad shoulders and the steady hands and the worn breastplate over a green-dyed gambeson and the half-orc tusk catching the gray light at the corner of her mouth. He saw a captain. He saw a leader. He saw the small careful stillness of someone who had learned to absorb impossible news without showing it.

He saw her see Anevia.

He saw her stop.

It was a stopping like a struck bell stopping. Her face did not change. Her eyes changed — opened, opened, opened, as if some long held breath were being let out through them.

"Anevia," she said. Just that. "You survived."

Beside him, Anevia made a sound. It was not a word. It was the sound a person made when something they had locked away to keep themselves alive came back out all at once and broke them open on its way past.

She ran.

She ran the way no professional ran. She ran the way a child ran, the way a soldier did not, the way a survivor never let themselves run because to run was to admit you had been waiting. She crossed the broken street in five long strides and crashed into the woman in the doorway with her bow caught awkwardly at her hip, and Irabeth — it was Irabeth, the name landed in Nageru's mind with the soft certainty of a stone settling in still water — Irabeth caught her, both arms, and held.

They held a long time.

Nageru stood in the street with his bare feet on the gray cobbles and watched.

He had never seen this before. He had seen many things. He had seen men die well and men die badly. He had seen a child stop crying. He had seen Caleth come back to himself with smoke still rising from his hands. He had seen Korroc set a hand on a stone wall and listen to it the way another man would listen to a friend. He had not seen this. He had not seen two people who had been told the world had ended discover, in a doorway, that one corner of it had not.

The Archon weight that lived behind his ribs was not quiet about it. The weight liked this. The weight approved.

*Love survives the apocalypse,* he thought, and the thought was not his own voice. *It survives. It is one of the things we are for.*

He bowed his head a fraction, because some moments deserved a small bow even from a stranger, and he waited until Anevia and Irabeth stepped apart enough to remember the rest of them.

"Come inside," Irabeth said, her voice gone rough. "All of you. Come inside."

They came inside.

---

Defender's Heart was full.

Refugees in every corner. Soldiers in mismatched colors. A man with a bandaged head trying to feed soup to a woman whose hands were shaking too badly to hold the bowl. A child sleeping on a bench, his fingers in his mouth. The smell of bodies and broth and woodsmoke and a long-burnt lamp, the smell of a room that had decided to live a while longer.

Irabeth led them to a back room with a long table. She sat. Anevia sat beside her, their knees touching under the table, neither of them looking down at it.

"It is so good," Irabeth said, "to see more survivors. The Eagle Watch is — I am in command, now, until the Mendevian army arrives. Such command as remains. Our ranks were —" She stopped. Did not finish. The word she had not said sat on the table between them.

She drew a breath.

"Where have you been. How did you get here."

Korroc looked at Nageru.

Caleth looked at Nageru.

Thane looked at Nageru, and there was the faintest flick of dry approval at the corner of his mouth, the look of a man recognizing that another man had drawn the watch he had hoped to avoid.

Nageru understood that he was being asked to speak.

He did not speak often. He had not spoken this much in any single day of his life, perhaps, as he was about to speak now. He felt the strangeness of it as a craftsman felt the strangeness of a tool he had not held in years. He set his hands flat on the table and considered the shape of the story and began.

He told it in order.

He told her about the festival. About the dragon falling and the city splitting open. About the dark and the stones that ran with veins of something wrong, and the krenshar and the fungus and the cultists in the cracked chapel of Sarenrae. He told her about finding Aravashnial blinded by wounds. He told her about Horgus. About Klarah — and here he gestured back through the door, where Horgus was already moving with the child toward the smell of soup, and Irabeth's eyes followed the gesture and softened. He told her about Korroc's burning, the Life Oracle fire that had come up out of the dwarf. About Caleth turning the dark cold with a single word. About Thane reading signs at every crossroads.

He told her about the wardstone. He felt her stiffen when he said the word. He kept telling. He told her about the Blackwing. About the cultist with the borrowed armor of Sarenrae and the librarians bound and ready to burn. About the worm that walks who had taken the upper floors of the tower with him when he left.

He told her, finally, about the S.V. letter. He laid the words out in order, the way the letter had laid them out, and watched her face close.

When he was done his throat felt strange. He had spoken more than the room expected and more than he had expected and he had not stumbled once. He sat with his hands still flat on the table and waited.

Irabeth was quiet a long moment. Then she nodded slowly, once.

"The wardstone." She breathed it out. "The whole network is weakened. The whole northern arc. It could —" She stopped. Did not say *be worse.* Said it anyway, with her face. "It could be worse."

She looked at the table.

"They have had spies among us for decades. We know this now. I caught one — a leader. The leader of a group that called themselves the Hammers of Heaven. Staunton Vhane." She gave the name like a stone she was setting down. "A model crusader. A man we held up to younger soldiers as what they should grow to be. He was theirs the whole time. He slipped my hand."

A silence.

"And now you bring me this." She tapped the letter, which Thane had slid across to her. "Safe houses. In my city. That my men do not know about." Her mouth thinned. "My men are stretched past breaking. I cannot send a squad to clear them. I do not have a squad to send."

She raised her eyes and looked at Nageru, because Nageru had been the one telling, and the look was not a request.

"Tonight you eat and you sleep," she said. "Tomorrow — could you. Could the four of you continue to look into these places. As you have been."

Nageru did not have to look at the others. He felt Korroc shift his weight to one side, which was assent. He felt Thane's small dry exhalation, which was assent. He felt Caleth's hand uncurl on the table, which was assent.

"We will," he said.

Irabeth let out a long careful breath. Anevia, beside her, did not say anything, but under the table her hand moved and Irabeth's hand moved and they did not need to be seen to be known.

"There are merchants in the common room," Irabeth said, gathering herself, standing. "Refugees who held on to their stock. They are trading. I have no authority over their goods and I will not ask them to give. But you can resupply."

She paused.

"Eat first. Please."

They ate first.

---

Klarah ate sitting on Horgus's lap, and Horgus held the bowl for her, and Horgus did not complain once about anything, and Nageru watched this from across the room and let it become one more piece of order in the world.

Then they bartered.

Then Nageru slept four hours on a stone-cold floor against a wall and woke before the rest of the inn and went and stood in the back yard barefoot in the gray pre-dawn and breathed.

The breath went down.

The breath came up.

Tomorrow's work was already arranged inside him, as orderly as a room swept clean and laid out for tools. He did not pray for safety. He prayed — if it could be called praying, the thing he did — for clarity. For the next strike to land where it needed to land. For the line of order to hold one more morning under one more onslaught.

When the first light came he opened his eyes and the city was still broken and the sky was still wrong and the work was still his.

He went back inside to find his brothers.

---

Topaz Solutions had a sign over the door of orange flowers and a beaker of orange smoke, and the door hung off its hinge like a tooth half-pulled from a jaw.

They came up to it quiet.

Thane took the lead, the way Thane took every doorway. Nageru moved up behind him at his right shoulder, close enough to step through the door in the same breath. Korroc came third, mail singing low. Caleth held the rear with his glaive in both hands and his eyes on every shadow.

Thane reached the threshold.

Thane stopped.

Nageru did not see what Thane saw. He felt Thane see it — a flick of attention, a half-breath of warning — and then Thane was moving, ducking low and to one side as something black-cloaked came out of the corner of the room with a sap raised high.

The sap whistled past Thane's ear.

Thane's daggers came up and went in.

Right blade first. Then left. The man made a soft surprised sound and folded down at the knees, and Nageru stepped past Thane and brought one fist down at the base of the skull as the man fell, and that was the end of that one.

"There is another in the room," Thane said, low.

The other was already moving.

A bowstring twanged from deeper in the shop and an arrow took Nageru in the side, just below the ribs. The shaft of it was a small bright pain, the way a thorn pulled through a thumb was a small bright pain. He did not look down at it. He turned toward the archer and began to move.

Korroc came in through the door behind him at a run, and Korroc had already seen something Nageru had not — a third man rising from behind an overturned shelf — and Korroc was on him before Nageru had taken three steps, the warhammer coming down with the patient inevitability of a stone falling out of a cliff. The third man came apart along his collarbone and did not get up.

Another arrow whispered out of the dimness. It found Thane high on the shoulder. Thane swore in dwarvish, quiet and detailed.

A longsword came at Korroc's head. Korroc's shield met it without seeming to move. The blade rang away.

A third archer rose up from behind a counter. An arrow snapped past Nageru's cheek and clattered off Korroc's plate.

Five men now. They had been four; now they were five; in a heartbeat they would be six. The room was full of them. *Looters had not put themselves in a shop in this number,* Nageru thought, somewhere underneath the body that was already moving. *Looters did not lay ambushes.*

He breathed.

The world narrowed.

He had been carrying the Archon weight low and quiet for days. He let it come up now, into his hands.

The first archer was still trying to nock a second shaft when Nageru reached him. He did not bother with the bow. He went in low and hit the man in the floating ribs and the man's breath left him and the bow dropped, and Nageru's second strike, an open-palm thing under the chin, lifted the man and set him down again very still.

Thane had moved on the same archer from the other side. Right blade. Left blade. The man went down in pieces.

The room turned into a long bright sequence of small decisions.

Korroc's hammer rose. Korroc's hammer fell. Korroc's hammer rose. An arrow took Nageru in the thigh and he absorbed it the way he had absorbed the first, drawing the pain into the breath and letting the breath carry it past — *not now, later, the body will remember, not now —* and pivoted into the next strike. Caleth's glaive came over Korroc's shoulder in a clever line and the cultist Korroc was on got a second wound he did not need.

A man broke around Thane's flank and stabbed Thane in the back. Thane went down on one knee. Caleth was already turning, the glaive snapping out at the length of his reach, and the flanker took the spear-blade through the ribs and choked. Thane, on one knee, came up under him and put both daggers into him from below. Nageru reached them in two long strides and finished it with a closed-fist downward strike that broke something important behind the man's eyes.

Three down. Maybe four. Nageru had stopped counting.

The one on Thane was the harder problem. He had a longsword and he knew which end of it cut, and he had turned now and was aware of how much damage he had been dealing.

Korroc made a low sound in his throat — not a word, the sound a dwarf made when he had decided to spend something he had been saving — and the sound spread out across the room as warmth. It moved through Nageru's chest like a swallow of hot broth and the arrow-wound in his side went from a screaming small fire to a steady ache he could carry. The thigh-wound dulled. Thane's breath came back. Caleth straightened.

Nageru gave Korroc the smallest inclination of his head. *Thank you, brother.*

Korroc was already swinging the hammer again.

The longsword-man on Thane stabbed and struck. Caleth answered, glaive in, glaive out. Thane answered, daggers in, daggers in again. Nageru tumbled past the longsword-man's open side — a low forward roll through the space the man's reach would not protect — and came up on the wrong side of him with both fists already moving, one to the kidney and one across the jaw, and the man dropped without ever knowing where the second strike had come from.

The last archer was at the back of the room.

Thane was running before Nageru saw him decide to run, and Nageru ran with him, and Korroc was behind them coming up at his patient devastating walk. The archer loosed once at Thane and missed wide. Thane stabbed him in the belly. Nageru's fists came down twice. Korroc, arriving late, did not need to swing — he stood with his shield up between the body and the room as if to say *no further* — but he swung anyway, once, for thoroughness.

The shop was silent.

Nageru stood in the silence and let his breath come down.

He looked at himself, briefly, as a craftsman looked at a tool after hard use. Three arrow-wounds. One in the side. One in the thigh. One — he had not noticed when this one had arrived — across the meat of his upper arm. None of them mortal. All of them honest.

The breath went down.

The breath came up.

He turned his attention to the room.

"No armor," he said.

Korroc looked around. The hammer was still in his hand. Blood ran down the haft and dripped quietly to the floor.

"Aye," Korroc said. "Not a piece of mail among them."

"Looters?" Nageru said, knowing the answer.

Korroc spat. "Looters do not lay ambushes for armed men with shields. Looters run."

"And these attacked us," Caleth said quietly.

Thane was already searching. He came up from a corner with a sack of coin and a leather pouch heavy with what sounded like rings. He set them on a table and looked at them and said nothing.

Nageru moved through the shop slow.

He found the secret door by accident, almost — by the shelf moving the wrong way under his palm when he leaned against it to catch his breath. The shelf wobbled. The wall behind it gave the smallest sigh, the air-shift of a door not quite seated.

"Brother," he said softly. "Look at this."

Thane came over. Thane did the small intent thing he did with his hands at any seam — running fingertips along it, watching for the click of a wire, the rise of a plate. He found nothing. He pushed.

The wall opened on a stair.

---

The basement air was damp and the basement air was wrong.

Packed earth walls. Packed earth floor. Gourds hanging from the ceiling on twisted cords like dried hearts. A thick low sludge of rotting plant matter underfoot, the smell of it sweet and rancid and chemical underneath, the smell of a still-room run by someone who did not love either plants or people.

Against one wall, a table. On the table, a small obscenity — a clockwork figure of a six-armed snake-bodied woman with a sword in each of her hands. Before the figure, a stoppered bottle of clear liquid. Beneath the table, a chest carved over with runes Nageru could not read but did not need to read. Above the table, painted on the packed earth wall, a star surrounding the face of a goat.

Nageru looked at the goat-face and felt the Archon weight come up against the back of his ribs like a held breath.

"Baphomet," he said, quietly.

"Aye," Korroc said.

Thane was moving forward, eyes on the floor, looking for the pressure plate that was not there. He paused at the line of the sludge and crouched and looked at it. The sludge did not move.

He stood. He took a step forward, careful. Another.

He stopped.

His head came up. He was looking at the bottle. He was looking at the clockwork woman with her raised swords.

"I do not," Thane said quietly, "like the look of that."

He examined the floor in front of him with the patience of a man who had not survived this long by hurrying. He saw nothing. He went forward.

The goat-face spoke.

The voice came out of the painted wall flat and amused, the voice of a thing that had been waiting a long time to make this joke.

*"Ah,"* it said. *"I do hope you are among Iomedae's slaves. I would hate to think a mere peasant was wasting Lord Baphomet's personal greeting."*

The clockwork woman's six arms moved.

The raised swords came down.

Thane went forward — not back, *forward* — in a single bright lunge, his hand closing around the bottle and his shoulder taking him out of the swords' arc by an inch, two inches at most. The blades crashed down through the empty air where the bottle had stood and rang off the wood of the table.

He came up on the other side with the bottle held against his chest.

Caleth crossed the sludge in two long careful strides and reached him and looked.

"Good grab," Caleth said softly. His face was very still. "Vapor. It would have filled the room."

Thane only nodded.

Nageru breathed out the breath he had been holding and let his hands unclench.

*Thane,* he thought, *is the reason this party will not all die in the same week.*

Thane was already moving to the chest under the table, ignoring all of them, his attention on the runes.

Then Nageru felt the second wrongness.

He had been still long enough to feel it. His Archon blood had not stopped listening. There was a thing in the sludge. A small thing. A wet thing. The pressure was very faint, the wrong-shape of something not-quite-natural that should not have been growing in a basement under a poisoner's shop, and as he turned his head it began to come up.

A child-shaped thing.

A wide mouth.

Bark for skin. Twigs for fingers. Tubers and leaves and damp black roots where there should have been ribs.

He opened his mouth to warn them, and Caleth had already seen it, and Caleth was already moving.

Caleth thrust with the glaive. The blade went past the bark-shoulder by a thumb's width.

The bark-child opened its mouth wider and *screamed.*

It was not a sound. It was a sound and a thing inside the sound. It tore through Nageru's head like a knife dragged across slate and his stomach turned over and his vision smeared at the edges. Caleth doubled forward and retched. Thane closed his eyes and dropped to one knee and rode it. Nageru clapped his palms to the sides of his head — not to block it, the sound was inside his skull, but to hold his skull together while it passed — and breathed through his teeth and waited.

The sound stopped.

He moved.

*The thunder is patient,* he thought, the old phrase rising up unbidden from the place where his bones kept such things. *But the thunder does not stay patient forever.*

He gathered his ki into one long bright thread and let it run down through his shoulders into his hands. The world narrowed to the small bark-creature and the line his strikes would travel. He flowed forward and his fists came down — one, two, three — and the second and third struck home, splintering bark. The thing rocked but did not fall.

Korroc was already there. He had pivoted around Nageru's flank the way two men who had learned each other's footwork pivoted, finding the second angle without needing to ask. His hammer rose. His hammer fell. A great gouging crack opened across the bark-thing's chest and pale wet stuff oozed out.

The thing did not die.

It came for Nageru.

It came at him low and fast, a child-sized weight of bark and root, and it struck his thigh and *latched.* He felt its mouth close on him through the cloth of his wraps. He felt its small twig-fingers find holds in his belt and grip.

Then he felt the worse thing.

Roots. Coming out of its mouth. Coming through his skin. Threading down into the meat of his thigh as if his leg were soil, drinking, drinking, the small wet sucking sensation of something feeding.

He pummeled it through the grip.

He pummeled it with both fists in short controlled downward strikes, the way a man broke kindling across his knee, and pieces of it cracked and fell. Korroc's hammer rose again and came down on a critical seam at the joint of its shoulder and the bark-arm came off entirely, and still the roots in his leg held, and still he felt his strength leaving him through them.

He could feel himself getting lighter. Greyer at the edges.

He hit it again. And again. And the body of it began to come apart in his hands, the leaves separating, the twig-ribs splintering, the wet hollow center of it caving inward, and then it was no longer a body, only pieces. Pieces and the roots.

The roots were still in him.

He looked down at his thigh.

He took a breath.

He set his hand around the place where the bark-mouth had been and he gripped the mass of root-and-tendril and he *pulled.*

It came out with the wet ripping sound of vine pulled from soft wood. The pain was bright. The blood was bright. He swayed once and steadied and stood.

A hand on his shoulder.

"Sit, brother." Korroc's voice was very close to him. The dwarf's hand was warm — not the warmth of a hand, the warmth of something else moving down his arm. The leg eased. The bleeding slowed. Not all the way. Enough.

"My thanks," he said.

Caleth was already at his elbow with a small glass vial flipped open and held out. "Drink this. You will feel better."

He drank.

The lesser restoration was a strange clean thing, like a cold draft of water on a hot day, but it ran inward instead of down. The grayness at the edges of his vision faded. The hollow place under his ribs where the bark-thing had pulled his strength out filled, slowly, with himself again.

He nodded to Caleth.

He nodded to Korroc.

He stood up straight.

Thane was already at the chest. The runes were not stopping him long. The lid came open under his careful hands.

Inside: five holy symbols of Iomedae, laid out neat. Thane looked at them and did not touch them. He bent close and sniffed and his nostrils flared.

"Poison," he said. "All five. Coated."

Beside the symbols, a single folded note. He lifted it with a blade and unfolded it on the floor.

*We don't need these anymore,* it read in a neat unhurried hand, *but figured if you survived our surprise, it would be just plain rude to leave behind an empty chest for your troubles.*

Caleth read it over Thane's shoulder. His mouth set.

"The Tower of Estrod," Caleth said.

Korroc nodded once.

Nageru tore the painted goat-face off the wall as they left, in two clean fistfuls. The packed earth gave the symbol up the way earth gave up a weed. He let the pieces fall into the sludge.

It was not rage.

It was tidying.

---

The Tower of Estrod was missing most of itself.

They saw it from two blocks back — a sixty-foot stone stump, the upper bulk of it sheared off as if a giant had taken a bite out of the city's skyline. Rubble heaped north of the building in a great pale mound. The remaining stump stood jagged-edged against the gray sky, a broken tooth in a city of broken teeth.

They moved in slow. Thane took the lead again. He went up to the door alone, set his ear to the wood, and stood a long time listening with his eyes half-closed. Then he came back to them, soft as smoke.

"Voices," he said. "Three or four. Easy talk. They are not on guard."

Then, more grim:

"Give me the bottle."

Korroc held a hand up.

"Thane. Are we sure these are bad ones?"

"We have been attacked twice at the last place, cousin."

"Aye. But we have to know."

Thane made a noise that was almost a growl, almost a sigh. Korroc looked at him steady. Nageru did not speak. The question was a Korroc question and the answer was a Korroc answer and there was a thing between the two dwarves in that moment that Nageru would not step into.

"Let me check," Korroc said.

He walked to the door.

He did not try to be quiet. He walked like a dwarf in plate walked, slow and heavy and unmistakable, and ten paces from the door he raised his voice and called out the password from the cultist letter.

A pause. Inside, the sound of bodies moving. Locks turning, one, two. The door opened a hand's-width and a face looked out — a man's face, pale, lean, marked at the temple with a small dark sigil that looked very much like a stylized minotaur head.

The face looked at Korroc. The face nodded. The face turned away as if Korroc had merely confirmed an expected delivery, and the door swung open and the man went back inside.

Korroc put one hand on the inside of the door.

He looked back at Thane.

He nodded.

Thane threw the bottle.

It went past Korroc in a high lazy arc and hit the floor in the middle of the room and shattered. A pale plume came up out of it — fast, almost colorless, the way steam came up from a kettle.

Korroc slammed the door shut.

He set his shoulder against it and set his feet and held.

There was a scrabble of hands on the inside. There was a man's voice rising into a shout that turned almost at once into a wet coughing. There were thumps — bodies — and then thumps lower down. Korroc held the door through all of it without moving. His face was set the way a forge-master's face was set, attentive to a thing he was making and not pleased to be making it.

Thane motioned Nageru and Caleth in close. They readied themselves a half-step from the door.

A breath. Another breath. Another.

Korroc looked at Nageru.

Nageru nodded.

Korroc threw the door open.

The ground floor of the tower was open to the sky — wooden bracing above marked where the upper floors had been, splintered and shorn. Two rubble-filled pools flanked a central walkway. Past them, an open area: shelves, a desk, a chair, and a stair leading down.

The cultists were not in good shape. Several were on the floor. Three were on their feet still, swaying, daggers in their hands, faces wet with the sheen of poison they had not died from quickly enough.

Nageru went in first.

He hit the nearest with a left-hand strike to the chest that should have broken something and might have. The man jabbed back with a dagger and missed entirely. A second cultist came in from Nageru's blind side and stabbed Thane, who had moved up at Nageru's elbow.

Korroc came in heavy and swung at a cultist near the wall and his boot slid on something — sludge or blood or both — and the hammer went past the man's shoulder. Korroc growled.

At the back of the room a cultist raised both hands and a wave of cold black wrongness rolled out across them, the negative-energy bite of a dark prayer. Nageru felt it pass through him like a cold wind through cloth. It hurt. It did not slow him.

Caleth was at glaive-reach, jabbing in over Thane's shoulder, careful as a fencer.

The trembling cultist in front of Nageru jabbed again, this time more accurately, and his dagger took Nageru low on the side, where the arrow-wound from Topaz Solutions still ached. Nageru breathed through it. He stepped inside the man's next stab, caught the man's wrist with his left hand, and brought his right fist down with all his weight onto the side of the man's neck. The man dropped. Nageru pivoted on the same heel and brought his second strike onto the trembling cultist who had stabbed him, and the man's eyes went very wide and then went out.

At the back, the last cultist who had thrown the cold-wave turned and ran. He vanished down the stair.

The three left were not going to last.

One stabbed at Korroc and went past his shield. Another tried to bolt past Korroc and Korroc's hammer caught him at the top of the skull as he ran, and at the same moment Caleth's glaive went into the man's stomach to finish what the hammer began. He folded in two and did not get up.

Korroc raised his free hand and the warm wave moved through them. Every wound that was screaming dropped to an ache. Every ache to a memory.

Then the stair gave back the cultist who had run.

He came up at a sprint with four more behind him, and he was not a man — he was red-skinned and his eyes were the wrong shape and his teeth, when his mouth pulled back, were the wrong teeth.

*Tiefling,* Nageru thought. *Or worse.*

He centered his weight.

The red-skinned leader gestured and a cultist behind him stabbed Caleth in the shoulder. Caleth's blood came up in a quick line. The half-elf stood. He stood the way he had stood in the underdark when the cold had gone out from him and stopped a demon's mouth.

Another cultist stabbed Thane and Thane swore and stayed up.

Korroc's hand came up and a small heat ran into Caleth and the wound's edges drew together.

A cultist gestured at Korroc, and the air rippled at the dwarf's eyes, and Korroc shook his head sharp like a man clearing water from his ears and the spell broke off him.

Nageru moved.

He moved into the press, finding the line between two men, and his fists came down on the cultist who had stabbed Caleth. The man dropped without a sound.

Thane gestured — a small clean gesture, the rogue-inquistor's quick blessing — and a soft brightness brushed all of them, and Nageru felt the world settle a fraction more under his feet.

Caleth stepped forward. He raised a hand. Cold gathered in his palm with a soft hissing sound and became, suddenly, a fist-sized white sphere of ice. He threw it. It hit the red-skinned leader in the face with a wet hard sound. The leader rocked back. The leader did not fall.

Korroc was moving forward into the middle of them now, deliberately, planting his feet between the cultists and the rest of the party. He set his shield. He set his stance. Nageru had seen this stance before. It was the stance of a wall.

Two cultists tried him. The first took his sword off Korroc's shield and rebounded badly. The second tried to slip a stab past the shield's edge and Korroc moved his shield half an inch and caught it.

Two more cultists vaulted past Korroc — a clumsy desperate leap over rubble and ruined floor — and now the back row was on the rest of them.

A cultist gestured at Korroc again. Korroc paused. His brow furrowed. Then his face cleared and he shook the doubt off the way a horse shook a fly.

Another gestured at Caleth and a cone of red flame erupted from his palm and washed over Caleth completely. The flame went through him and around him and past him and Caleth stood inside it untouched, his fire-resistance turning the spell aside like a curtain.

Nageru saw the line.

He sprinted across the room. He went between two cultists who were not ready for him to move that fast and reached the one who had cast the flame and brought his fist down on the man's collarbone. Something broke under the strike. The man went down to one knee. Nageru's second strike was already moving.

Korroc, behind him, stepped backward — not retreating, *resetting* — and pivoted to flank a cultist who was working on Thane. The hammer came down. The cultist collapsed without a noise.

The flame-caster who Nageru had knocked down jabbed up with a dagger from his knees and caught Nageru across the forearm, a deep cut, and Nageru absorbed it and finished him with a downward strike that turned the man's head.

The cultist standing over Korroc tried to stab and the strike skipped off Korroc's plate.

Then the red-skinned leader changed.

He had been waiting for the moment. He raised his arms and a sudden fire ran up him from heel to crown, not consuming, *belonging,* the way fire belonged to the things that lived in the deep places. His wounds began to close as the fire moved over them. His eyes glowed red.

He looked at Caleth.

He gestured.

Caleth burst into flame.

The fire wrapped Caleth from boot to brow and Caleth's face was almost bored. He did not even raise a hand to brush it off. The flames flickered. The flames thinned. The flames went out, leaving Caleth standing in his crusader's tabard not even singed.

Nageru hit the cultist in front of him one more time. The cultist fell backward into a hole in the floor and did not come up.

Thane stabbed and missed and swore. Caleth's glaive went into the cultist who had stabbed him and the man folded.

Korroc had two on him now, one on either side. He stood the way he had stood. His hammer came down on one. The other tried to stab him and could not find a way past his shield.

The leader gestured at Caleth a second time. Nothing happened. Caleth shook the spell off the way Korroc had shaken off the doubt.

Nageru struck again. Another cultist fell.

Thane's right blade went into one of the men around Korroc and the man collapsed.

Caleth was moving now — moving up — and Nageru saw what Caleth was preparing. The half-elf's left hand was glowing faintly with white-gold light. His right was crawling with small blue lightnings. He raised the glaive in both hands as he walked, and the holy and the storm met along the blade and the blade lit up like a struck bell.

Caleth reached the red-skinned leader.

The glaive came down.

The leader screamed once, a high choked sound, and the lightning ran into him through the wound and the holy fire bloomed inside his chest and he went over backward smoking, and the fire around him guttered, and he did not get up.

One cultist remained.

He had been backing toward the stair, eyes wide, the calculation on his face of a man who had been promised an easy day and was now revising his plans.

Nageru reached for his last javelin.

The wood of the haft slid into his palm with the familiar weight of a tool that had crossed a thousand miles with him from the Worldwound's edge to this room.

He set his feet.

He drew his arm.

"Evil," he said, "must die."

He threw.

The javelin took the cultist through the chest and pinned him a step short of the stair. The man's hands fluttered at the shaft. The man's hands stopped.

Nageru lowered his arm.

The Archon weight settled in his ribs like a stone that had found its place at the bottom of a riverbed.

*Order,* the weight said. *Order has been restored.*

---

The keys were on the leader's belt.

Thane took them. He did not speak. He looked at the dead man's face for a moment and then he turned away and led them to the stair.

The room below was a half-circle. Empty but for a few shelves and a desk and a chair. Past it, a great pair of double doors banded in iron and dark wood.

The keys fit the doors.

They opened into the chapel.

Green glass lanterns burned in iron brackets along the walls, and the light they shed was the wrong color for any light to be — a pale sick green that turned a man's skin the color of old bone. The room was a half-circle to match the one above. At the back wall, an altar. Above the altar a great brazen minotaur head, its horns turned outward, its mouth half-open. Across the altar a row of wavy-bladed knives and a pair of curved bull's horns laid like offerings.

The walls bore painted star-symbols, red, irregular, hand-drawn in something that was probably not paint.

Against the west wall, a wooden chest.

Caleth made a small sound at the back of his throat that was not a word.

Korroc said nothing.

Thane crossed to the chest, knelt, and began to work on it.

Nageru walked to the altar.

He stood in front of it and looked up at the minotaur head. It was a poor casting. The brass was unevenly thick. The horns were too long for the skull, the eyes too small. The work of a craftsman who had not loved the thing he made. It hung from two iron hooks driven into the wall above the altar, the hooks themselves crude and bent.

He took the horns in his hands, one in each fist.

He pulled.

The hooks came out of the wall with a grinding tear of plaster. The minotaur head came down, heavy, into his arms. He set it on the floor. He stepped back and lifted one foot and brought his heel down on it. The brass split along a poorly-sealed seam. He brought his heel down again. The skull caved.

He took up one of the wavy knives from the altar and broke it across his knee.

He took up the other and broke it as well.

He swept the horns onto the floor with one bronze forearm.

He stood looking at the green glass lanterns until the green light no longer offended him so much, and then he walked to the nearest one and put his fist through it. The lantern went out. He moved to the next. He put his fist through it as well. The third. The fourth.

The room went dark, lantern by lantern, until only the dim daylight from the stair behind them lit it.

He stood among the broken green glass on his bare bleeding feet and listened to his breath come back to something he recognized.

*Restoration,* the weight said. *Not rage. Restoration.*

He nodded once to no one and turned back to the chest.

Thane had it open.

Coin in the chest. Gold and silver and some loose gems. A small leather purse of cut stones. A bundle of rolled paper tied with a black cord.

Caleth knelt beside Thane and took up the bundle. He untied the cord with careful fingers and unrolled the pages on the floor of the chapel and bent over them. The others gathered close. Even Nageru came and stood at Caleth's shoulder, where he could read down the pages over the half-elf's arm.

The papers were a record of years.

Names. Names in Kenabres and in cities Nageru had heard of and cities he had not. A list of agents in the Templars. A list of supply caches. A diagram of the wardstone network — *had been* the wardstone network, before — with notations in a tight crabbed hand. Plans. Routes. Schedules.

Beneath them all, at the bottom of the bundle, a single folded letter.

Caleth lifted it.

He read it once silently. His face went very still.

He read it aloud.

*"Faxon,*

*"You did well to report your superior's mishandling of funds, and I trust that you will continue to serve as loyally in the weeks to come. You need not command the Kenabres Templars for long, for the city is about to die — I only wish I could be there to take part in its murder, for I have fond memories of my Red Morning Massacre. No matter, I suppose, for your mortal kin will all fall soon enough — as I said, Kenabres's days are short.*

*"Vorlesh has already left to meet with our Lord's daughter in the Abyss to secure a Nahyndrian crystal of the proper purity, and once she has what she needs, she'll arrive in Kenabres to finish the job and turn the wardstones fully to our use. You will know when she succeeds, I suspect!*

*"Praise Lord Baphomet!*

*"Minagho"*

Silence.

The two dwarves looked at one another.

A long look. The look of two people who had read different histories and remembered the same name.

Korroc spoke first, low, the way he spoke when a word was a stone he did not want to set down.

"Minagho."

Thane nodded.

"The demon," Thane said, "that holds the broken city of Raliscradin. In the Worldwound. A leader. Not a foot-soldier."

"And Vorlesh," Korroc said, slower still, "is the name they whisper as one of the hands that opened the Worldwound in the first place."

Caleth lowered the page.

Nageru stood very still in the green-broken dim of the desecrated chapel and let the names settle in his blood. He had not known Minagho. He had not known Vorlesh. He knew them now. They were no longer abstractions. They were two specific evils with two specific names, and one of them had written a letter, and the other was crossing into the Abyss to collect a tool with which to finish the murder of a city.

The Archon weight behind his ribs spoke.

It did not say words. It never said words.

It said: *These also.*

He breathed.

The breath went down.

The breath came up.

Outside, somewhere above them in the gray morning of broken Kenabres, the thunder turned over in its sleep and did not yet wake.
