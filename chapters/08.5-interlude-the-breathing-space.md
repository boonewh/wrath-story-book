# The Breathing Space

*Interlude — The Weeks After the Shattering*

The city learned to breathe again the way a man learns it after a broken rib: carefully, and not all at once.

For three days there was no rest in it. The shattering of the wardstone fragment had loosed a tide of holy fire that no one living had ever seen, and where that light had passed the demons had simply ceased — annihilated in their thousands, scoured from the streets they had held for a year. The warbands at the walls broke and drew back into the smoke of the Worldwound. But the dead do not bury themselves, and the living had questions. Every officer with a sash and a frown wanted to know what had been found beneath the city, and what it meant, and how four strangers had walked out of the Gray Garrison when so many had walked in and never come out. So there were debriefings. There were strategy meetings in rooms that smelled of cold candle-tallow and wet stone. There were interviews that ran past midnight and began again at dawn.

And then, between one morning and the next, the questions thinned. The officers ran out of them, or ran out of time. The work that remained was not the kind that needed heroes. The wounded had to be tended. The barricades had to be raised again from the rubble of their first raising. Supplies had to be counted, refugees named and numbered, the dead carried out by cart.

What was left, when the urgency drained away, was a thing none of them had held in a long while. Not victory — victory was too clean a word for what Kenabres was. Something quieter and stranger and almost frightening in its plainness.

Time.

Over the weeks that followed, each of them spent it as they were made to spend it, while the wounded city set itself, slow and patient, to the long work of healing.

Korroc Stonevein knew the moment the forge was alive again, because the stone in his own bones knew it first.

He had felt it as the bellows caught and the coals went from sullen red to that deep, breathing orange — felt it the way he felt weather coming, a pressure behind the sternum, a recognition. The Stonelord's gift was no metaphor in him. There was stone laid down in his marrow, and it answered to fire and anvil the way iron filings lean toward a lodestone. The little temporary smithy they'd raised in the yard behind the Defender's Heart was nothing to look at — a lean-to, a salvaged anvil with a chipped horn, a forge cobbled from brick reclaimed out of a collapsed bakery. But Torag did not require beauty. Torag required honest work, and Korroc gave Him that from the first ring of the hammer.

He and Thane had found what was left of the home. That had been its own kind of grave-work — the two of them standing in the gap where the front wall had been, in the gray light, neither one speaking. The roof was gone. The hearth had cracked clean through. But the cellar had held, and the cellar was where the Stoneveins had always kept what mattered, and so they came away with more than Korroc had let himself hope for. Tools. A box of his mother's pins. Thane's whetstones, the good ones, the river-grit and the hard black Mendevian slate. Half the forge's small gear, the tongs and fullers and swages, blackened but sound. They'd carried it back between them in two trips and not spoken of it, because there was nothing to say that the carrying did not already say better.

Then the work began, and the work was good.

Soldiers came with dented breastplates and shields split along the boss. Refugees came shy, holding out a cracked pot or a snapped hinge as though ashamed to ask. Korroc and Thane took every one of them. He'd straighten a buckled pauldron and pray over the heat while he did it — aloud, the way he always prayed, a low rumble under the hammer-fall that the soldiers had stopped finding strange after the first week. *Steady the temper, Smith. Steady the man inside it.* It was good work. It was the kind of work that fit into the hands and asked nothing of the heart but attention, and after the Gray Garrison, attention was a mercy.

Irabeth came one morning with a leather pouch and put it in his hand without ceremony. He'd felt the weight of it — coin, a great deal of it, two thousand if it was a piece. He'd tried to give it back. She had looked at him with that half-orc stubbornness that was a match for any dwarf's and said the city paid for the labor of its smiths same as it paid for the swords of its watch, and that he could argue it with the quartermaster if he wanted to waste an afternoon. He'd kept the coin.

The two helms came out near the end of the second week.

He'd taken them from the Garrison himself, in the smoke and the after-quiet, and he could not have said then why. They were broken — crushed at the crown, the cheek-plates sprung, the rivets sheared. Old work. Old, *good* work, and that was the thing that had snagged in him then and snagged in him now. He knew the make of them. Not the maker, not the place, not the year — none of that came when he reached for it. Only the shape of the knowledge, the way you know a tune you cannot name, the way the tongue finds the gap where a tooth used to be. He'd said it aloud in the Garrison, half to Thane and half to no one: *I feel like I should know more about this helmet.* And the knowing had not come, and it did not come now.

So he did the only thing a craftsman could do with a feeling that would not resolve into a fact. He put it into his hands.

It took the better part of a week, in the hours stolen at the end of the day's repairs. He drew the dents with a wooden mallet so as not to thin the metal, working from the edges in toward the worst of the crush, heating and quieting and heating again. He cut new rivets and set them. He found the cheek-plates wanted a particular curve and his hands found it for him before his head had worked out the angle, and that, too, snagged at him — *you have done this before, the hands say, even if the head says you have not* — and he let it lie. Some flaws in metal you draw out. Some you learn to forge around. He suspected this was the second kind.

When they were done they were not new. He had not tried to make them new. He had made them *whole* — the dents gone, the seams true, the old steel given back its dignity. He carried them across the rubble to the museum, where a handful of elderly scholars were sorting the wreck of a thousand years into salvage piles, and he set the helms on a trestle table among them.

The eldest of the curators picked one up with hands that shook, and turned it, and could not speak for a moment. Then he wept, plainly, the way the very old weep, without shame and without much sound. He said something about the museum's loss, and the city's, and how a thing thought gone forever could come back from the smoke; and the others gathered, and they thanked Korroc so many times and so warmly that he grew gruff with the embarrassment of it and made his excuses and went back to his forge.

The familiarity went with him. It did not lessen. He set it on a shelf in the back of his mind, the way he set aside a piece of stock too good to use and too curious to throw out, and he let the days go on.

The days became weeks. Each morning began the same: the cold walk to the lean-to, the coaxing of the coals, the first true ring of the hammer that told the stone in him the world was still being made. Thane was there for most of it, at the next bench over, doing the fine work — the edges, the points, the small precise repairs that wanted a knife-maker's patience rather than a smith's strength. They did not talk much. Two dwarves who'd been raised cheek by jowl in the same household did not need to. It was enough that the cousin's whetstone hissed under his, that there was a second shadow on the wall when the forge flared.

Then, somewhere in the third week, the second shadow stopped coming.

Korroc did not notice it the first morning. Thane kept his own hours, slept where the work took him. The second morning he noticed and thought little of it. By the third he had noticed in a different way — not with his eyes, but lower, behind the breastbone, in the place where the new thing lived.

He had no name for it, and he was wary of trying to give it one, because he did not understand it and was honest enough to admit so even to himself. It had come to him in the Garrison, on the night they'd burned through into something larger than themselves. A *thread*. That was the closest his hands could come to it. A line run from his own chest to each of the other three, fine and warm and tensile, so that when one of them took a hurt he felt the tug of it — a ghost-ache in his own body, a pull toward the wound. He'd felt Caleth crack a rib once across half the city and gone to him before any runner could have reached him. The thread did not lie about that kind of thing.

But Thane's thread had gone thin.

Not cut. He told himself that, hard, in the dark behind the sternum. Not cut — he was sure he would know *cut*, would feel it part like a snapped wire, though Torag keep him from ever learning whether that was true. It had only gone thin, gone faint, the way a far-off hammer-ring goes faint with distance until you cannot say whether the smith has stopped or simply gone too far to hear. *Far away*, the thread said, in the only language it had, which was no language at all. And the trouble with *far away* and *gone quiet* was that from where Korroc stood they felt exactly the same.

So he began to ask.

The last he could place for certain was the day Irabeth had come for a shipment of mended gear. He remembered Thane handing it across, the two of them speaking low, Irabeth's nod. After that the memory had no edges. A week, and no cousin. He asked the watch-sergeants. He asked the quartermaster who'd taken the shipment. He asked Irabeth, and that was where it turned strange, because Irabeth was the plainest-spoken creature in Kenabres and Irabeth would not meet his eye.

He asked her once and she said she'd see what she could find out and was needed elsewhere. He asked her twice and she remembered an urgent matter at the south barricade. The third time he placed himself square in her path in the common room of the Defender's Heart, all four feet and some of him, and folded his arms, and said her name once, and waited.

She was a paladin and a half-orc and she had walked into the Gray Garrison alone with the whole weight of the watch on her back, and she could not look at him.

When she finally spoke, she spoke like someone setting down a load she'd carried too far and hated the whole way. Thane had volunteered. South of the city, a special mission, the details not hers to give — only that contact had been lost. Days ago. No word since. No one knew what had become of the team. She said the last part flat, because there was no soft way to lay it down, and a flat thing is at least honest: *I fear the worst.*

Korroc heard her. He heard the words go in and settle, the way cold water settles into stone, finding every crack.

*There is nothing to do now but wait,* she said.

He went back to the forge because his legs took him there and he did not stop them. He coaxed the coals. He lifted the hammer. And he stood a long while with it in his hand and did not bring it down, listening past the wind and the city and his own thick pulse for a sound that would not come — for the faint far ring of a cousin's whetstone, for the thread to firm and warm, for any sign at all that the quiet behind his sternum meant *distance* and not the other thing.

It did not come. He waited. There was nothing else a dwarf could do, and so he did it, with all the patience the stone in him had taught him, and it was the hardest work of his life.

Caleth had learned long ago that the most dangerous thing about a half-blind man was the part of him that refused to admit it.

He kept three paces back and one to the left as they moved through the ruin of the great library, which put him where he could see what Aravashniel could not and reach him before anything else did. The elf did not know he was being herded. That was the art of it. You did not steer a proud man; you arranged the ground around him so that his pride walked him where you needed him to go and thought the whole way it was leading.

It was lovely, the library, even now. That was the first thing Caleth's eye gave him — before the threat-count, before the exits, the eye his foster-mother had trained when she put a brush in his hand at five years old and told him to paint what was *true*, not what was pretty. The roof had fallen in three places and the morning came down through the gaps in long gray columns, full of slow dust, and where the light struck the spilled drifts of pages it made of them something like snow, or like the inside of a shell. Beauty did not require permission to exist. It went on existing in broken things, stubborn as moss. He had stopped being surprised by that a long time ago and had never once stopped being moved by it, and he was careful to let neither show on his face.

The second thing his eye gave him was the wrongness in the rubble to the left — a stillness inside the stillness, a shadow that held its shape too well.

"Aravashniel," he said, level, already moving. "Down and back."

The wizard's reflexes were faster than his years. A ward of force snapped up around him, hard light bending the air, even as the first of them came out of the dark — a thing of joints and hunger, a dretch-shape but quicker, two more behind it scrabbling over the broken shelving. Aravashniel's defenses held. They held *beautifully*; his wards were the work of a lifetime and they turned the demons' first rush the way a sea-wall turns surf. But the wards were a wall, and a wall does not win a fight. To win he needed to *strike*, and a strike has to find its mark, and the eyes that should have aimed it were two clouded windows that let in light and little else.

Caleth saw the bolt of fire leave the wizard's hand and go wide — a hand-span wide, but a hand-span is a miss, and a miss against a thing like this was an invitation.

Where another man would have charged, Caleth positioned. He had Radiance in his hands before the second demon reached the broken arch, and he set his feet at the place the geometry told him they would have to pass, the choke between two fallen cases, and he made them come to him one at a time. The glaive was a patient weapon for a patient man. He took the first across the throat as it committed to the gap. The second tried to climb the case to come over the top and he was already there, the silver length of the blade catching the light from the broken roof so that for one instant the whole grim business looked, absurdly, like something out of an altarpiece. He did not let the thought slow him. He killed it on the descent. The third he and the wizard took together, Aravashniel's force-bolt finally finding flesh now that the thing had nowhere left to dodge.

Then it was quiet again, and the dust came down through the gray light, and the elf stood in the wreck of his life's archive with his clouded eyes fixed on nothing, breathing hard.

He was not afraid. Caleth watched that come clear and filed it. The elf was *furious* — and not at the demons, and not at Caleth. At himself. Caleth knew the shape of that anger from the inside; it was a familiar coat in an unfamiliar size. Pride built on the mind, on the independence of the mind, the certainty that one's own intellect was a tool that would never fail you — and then the body failing under it, the eyes going, the world having to be handed to you secondhand by someone three paces back and one to the left.

"I had it," Aravashniel said, which they both knew was not true.

"You had the wards," Caleth answered. He gave the elf that much, because it was accurate, and because cruelty was a waste of breath. "I had the angles. Between us we had a fight." He wiped Radiance clean on a rag of fallen tapestry. "It is an inefficient man who refuses half a victory because he wanted the whole of it."

A long silence. Then a dry sound that might, charitably, have been the ghost of a laugh — Aravashniel's voice was like that, like pages turning, like something thin and dry and not without its own music. "Spoken," the wizard said, "like a man who has already lost the same argument with himself."

Caleth did not answer that, because there was no answer to it that he was willing to give aloud.

But he relented, after that. It was the closest he came to thanks, and Caleth had learned to read it for what it was. Over the weeks that followed they went back into the ruin again and again, and Aravashniel let himself be herded, and between the wizard's knowing and Caleth's seeing they pulled a great deal of salvage out of the dark.

The herbalism came to Caleth as a thing the city plainly needed and almost no one else could do. The apothecaries were burned out, their stores ash, and the wounded were everywhere — crusaders with rot setting into wounds that should have been simple, refugees coughing the gray dust out of their lungs, children with fevers no one had time to sit beside. So Caleth went out past the broken walls into the countryside, into the cold tilted light of the Worldwound's near edge, and gathered. It was wizard's work as much as healer's — knowing which leaf held what virtue, what the heat would draw out and what it would destroy, the patient arithmetic of dose. He spent his evenings over a low fire with a mortar in his lap, making poultices and salves and bitter dark tinctures, and he found in it the same quiet he found in painting: the hands busy, the mind free, the small good thing taking shape under his attention. The priests and quartermasters saw the worth of it and saw him paid for it. He kept the coin without comment. He noticed that the people he tended remembered his face and were glad to see it, and that this gladness was a thing that happened to other men more easily than it happened to him, and he did not examine the small cold ache of that too closely.

It was the conversations that undid him, in the end. Not the demons. The talk.

Aravashniel's mind was a country, and the more time Caleth spent walking in it the more he understood how much had been lost when this library burned — the breadth of the man, the depth, the casual mastery of a dozen disciplines. And in among all that knowing, the wizard kept brushing against things that had no business being in a stranger's mouth. A turn of phrase. A name dropped sideways into an unrelated story and then hurried past. The particular cold of a particular winter in a particular place, described too precisely, as though he had been *there* — as though he had stood in a room Caleth had spent his whole life trying to find the door to.

*Your parents' order.* Caleth never said it. He knew it — had known since the Garrison's deeper rooms, since the truth of who Aravashniel was had come clear to him and to no one else in the party. The Riftwardens. The same secret company into which his mother and father had vanished a month after he was born, north into the Worldwound, and never come out. The Blackwing Library had been theirs. *This* had been theirs. He was standing in the wreck of his parents' world and walking beside one of its elders, and the elder did not know that Caleth knew, and Caleth had no intention of telling him — not yet, not until he understood what telling would cost.

So when Aravashniel let something slip, Caleth pressed. Carefully. The way he did everything. And every time, the door shut.

Sometimes the elf simply changed the subject, smooth as a card-sharp. Sometimes he went silent, that thin music stopping all at once. Once — only once — Caleth pressed a half-step too hard and the wizard's temper cracked open like ice over deep water, and the cold that came up out of it was old and personal and frightening, and Caleth did not make that mistake twice.

The worst of it came on an ordinary evening, near the end. They were sorting recovered books by lamplight, and Aravashniel had taken to listing aloud what was still missing, as if naming the absences could keep them from being permanent. Several magical weapons. A chest of rare artifacts that had never surfaced. Historical records of the crusades. First-hand accounts, irreplaceable, the kind no copy existed of. He listed them in his dry turning-pages voice, item after item, and then he stopped.

He went still. Caleth, watching him sidelong the way he watched everything, saw the change come over his face — saw something move behind the clouded eyes that he had not seen there before. Not anger. Not the smooth deflection.

For one moment Aravashniel looked genuinely *shaken*.

"There was one other book," he said. Quietly. Almost to himself.

Caleth did not move. Did not breathe loudly. Held the moment open the way you hold open a window before a wary bird, knowing the wrong gesture will close it.

The elf's mouth worked. He shook his head, once, as though arguing with someone Caleth could not see.

"Never mind."

And that was all. The conversation died there. Aravashniel went back to the books and his hands were not quite steady, and for the rest of that evening and many evenings after he was distant in a way that had nothing to do with his eyes.

Caleth said nothing. He was very good at saying nothing; it was among his oldest skills. He sorted the books and let the silence stand and turned the moment over and over in the cold strategic dark of his own mind, examining it from every angle the way he examined everything, hunting the one fact that would unlock the rest.

He had spent his whole life living ahead of himself — planning, positioning, running the futures one against another like figures on a board. He could see a dozen tomorrows clearly. But he could not undo a single yesterday, not one, not the day a month after his birth when two people he had never been allowed to remember went north and did not return. And here beside him sat one who had known them. Who knew something now. Who held it behind his teeth like a stone he could neither swallow nor spit.

*He knows something he is not ready to tell me,* Caleth thought, and the thought sat in his chest with the cold weight of perfect certainty, and there was nothing in all his planning he could do to hurry the day the elf would be ready.

So he waited too, in his own way, three paces back and one to the left, and watched the door.

Nageru did not argue with Horgus Gwerm. He had found, early, that argument was what the man wanted — the friction of it, the proof that he still mattered enough to be opposed. So Nageru gave him no friction. He gave him a still surface, and watched the man's complaints break against it and run off, and waited to see what was left when the noise was gone.

What was left, on the afternoon the nobleman came to find him, was frustration of a different grain. Not the performed irritation Nageru had grown used to. Something closer to the bone.

"They will not spare me so much as a single soldier," Horgus said. He was a heavy man and the weeks had not been kind to the bulk of him; the fine coat hung looser than it had. "Because I am Horgus Gwerm, and Horgus Gwerm wants only to dig his silver out of the rubble. That is the whole of their thinking. I can see it in their faces." His jaw set. "It is not silver."

Nageru breathed once, slow, and let the breath stand between them in place of a reply.

"You do not believe me either," the noble said.

"I have not said so," Nageru answered. His diction was always formal, always level, the words placed down one at a time like stones across a stream. "You wish to reach your estate. No one will go. You have come to me." A pause. "I will go."

Against his own better judgment, perhaps. The Archon blood that ran somewhere far back in his line had a way of paying attention to people, and what it noticed in Horgus was not the snobbery, which was loud, but something under it, which was not. So he went.

The Gwerm mansion still stood — which set it apart from half the street. Nageru had slept under its roof not so long ago; it had been their refuge in the first days, grand and shuttered and whole, and to see it now was to see a strong man after a hard fever. The weeks had marked it. Doors hung torn from their hinges, cabinets stood stove in, a dark scorch climbed one wall where something had burned and gone out; the floors lay gritted with the gray dust that got into everything in Kenabres now, and the fine things spoiled across them did not look noble, only wasteful. But it was the wreck of a house broken into, not a house brought down — the bones of it were sound. Horgus moved through it with a sureness that surprised him, no hesitation, stepping past the looted and the ruined as if the place still stood unspoiled around him in his mind's eye. He led Nageru down through a servants' passage, to a wall that was not a wall, to a hidden seam that opened on a stair going down into the dark.

The cellar was cold and dry and very large, and it was full of food.

Crates of it. Casks and barrels and waxed wheels and salted weight, rank on rank of it receding into the lamp-dark — preserved meats, hard cheese, dried fish, grain in sealed tuns, root vegetables packed in sand. Nageru stood at the foot of the stair and let his eyes adjust and did the only arithmetic that mattered, which was not the worth of it but the number of mouths.

Hundreds. It would feed hundreds. For weeks.

"Carry what you can," Horgus said, already turning toward the deeper dark, taking a lamp. "Up to the inn. I will follow."

Nageru began to load his arms. He was strong, monk-strong, and he made the work efficient. He did not ask what the noble had gone deeper to find. He had learned that Horgus told you things or he did not, and that pushing only closed him.

But Horgus paused at the edge of the lamplight, his back half-turned, and spoke without looking around — which was, Nageru understood, the only way the man could say it at all.

"I don't care who likes me." The voice was flat and tired and, for once, entirely without performance. "I never have. Let them think what they want of me." He was quiet a moment. "But I understand better than most what it means to lose everything to demons."

Then he walked away into the dark, and the lamp went with him, and Nageru stood among the crates in the dimness and said nothing, because there was nothing to say that would not have been smaller than the silence.

When the noble rejoined him a long while later, he carried a small bag. He held it close and he did not speak of it, and the set of his face when Nageru's gaze passed over it was a closed door. So Nageru let it be a closed door. A man was entitled to one thing he did not have to explain.

Convincing Quednys of the rest was simple. The old wizard heard *food enough for hundreds* and stopped listening to anything else; within the day there were work-crews and carts and a chain of hands passing crates up out of the dark into the gray afternoon. And the thing that Nageru had not predicted — the thing that no arithmetic of mouths had told him — was what it did to the *city*.

He watched it happen over the days that followed. Hunger is a kind of cold that gets into a people and makes them small and sharp and turned-inward, each one hoarding the last of their own warmth. The food did not only fill bellies. It thawed something. Refugees who had not spoken above a whisper in weeks were heard, the second evening, *laughing* — a child's high laugh first and then an adult's, rusty with disuse. Klarah, the small one the party kept close among the noncombatants, got an actual apple, withered and sweet, and ate it in tiny ceremonial bites to make it last and showed everyone the core afterward as though it were a trophy. Strangers stopped Nageru in the street to press his hands and bless him in the names of gods he did not follow and one or two he did. He received the thanks the way he received most things, with a small bow and few words, and turned the credit aside toward Horgus whenever he could, and watched to see whether the man would let himself take it.

The man did not know how. That much was plain.

Several days after the stores came up, Horgus found him again. The noble was visibly uncomfortable, shifting his bulk, looking at the middle distance.

"You helped me," Horgus said, "when no one else would lower themselves to it." A beat. "I am aware of how that sounds. I find I do not have a better way to say it."

Nageru waited.

Horgus put a pouch on the table between them — coin, by the weight and the sound, a great deal of it. And beside the pouch he set down something else, and his hand stayed on it a moment before he drew the hand back, which told Nageru more than anything the man had said.

It was a bracelet. A string of prayer-beads, old, the cord worn soft and dark with handling, the beads themselves carved with a craft and a care that did not belong to this century — small and beautiful and clearly long-loved by someone.

"I do not have a use for it," Horgus said, too quickly. Which was a lie, Nageru thought, and a kind one, and the kindest thing about it was that the man wanted to be caught in it.

"It is yours," Nageru said. "It has been someone's, for a long time. I will not take a thing like that."

"You will." Horgus's jaw set in the old stubborn way, but his eyes were not stubborn at all. "Take it. Before I decide to be insulted by your refusal."

Nageru looked at the beads a moment longer. Then he took them up, and he wound the worn cord twice about his wrist, and he inclined his head — a real bow this time, lower than courtesy required.

"Then I will keep it well," he said. "Thank you, friend."

Horgus made a sound that was meant to be dismissive and did not quite manage it, and turned, and went, and his step was a little lighter than it had been, though he would have died before admitting it.

Nageru sat a while with the beads against his pulse. Order, he thought, is owed to the innocent — but it is not the only thing owed. Sometimes what is owed is patience, and the willingness to stand still long enough for a hard man to find, in his own time, the part of himself that wanted to be good.

He was tending a fresh wave of refugees, days later, kneeling to check the splint on a child's arm, when the front doors of the Defender's Heart slammed open hard enough to silence the room.

Thane Stonevein had been the one to say it, on the morning he agreed to ride out: *Look at the floor first, lad. Walls lie. Floors don't.* He'd said it to a young watchman, an old habit, his mother's lesson worn smooth by a lifetime of repeating it. It was good advice. He'd find, in the days after, that it kept him alive.

The forge weeks had been good. He could admit that to himself now, looking back from the far side of everything — the steady fine work at the bench beside Korroc, the whetstone in his hands, the small clean satisfactions of an edge brought back true. It had almost felt normal. He had let it. That had been his mistake, perhaps, though he did not believe in calling peace a mistake. A man took the breathing space when it was offered. The Stoneveins were patient. They did not begrudge a quiet morning.

Then Irabeth had come for the gear, and asked him to meet her when he had time, and the meeting had a wizard in it.

Quednys did not waste words, which Thane respected. The army out of Mendev had been expected weeks past. No word had come. Communication beyond the walls was a guess at best and a corpse at worst, and the silence from the south had gone on too long to be weather or bad roads. The old man wanted eyes. A small team, fast and quiet, south to find the Mendevian host, learn what had befallen it, and come back with the truth of it. He asked it plainly and he did not pretend it was safe.

Thane said yes. He did not make a thing of it. Within the hour he and four others rode out of a sally-gate in the gray of early morning, and Korroc was at the forge and did not see them go, and Thane did not seek him out to say it, because there was a superstition in him, old and Alsetan and unspoken, about how you crossed a threshold. You did not make a ceremony of a leaving. You stepped through clean and you carried the door behind you closed in your mind, and you did not turn around, because turning around at a threshold was how you left part of yourself jammed in it.

The land south of Kenabres was wrong, and the wrongness was the worst thing about the ride — worse than the scattered cultists they skirted, worse than the demon-sign they read off the ground and went around. It was the *emptiness*. This had been settled country once, farms and waystations and the small mended fences of people who meant to stay. Now it was hushed. No smoke. No stock. No dogs. The kind of quiet that is not peace but absence, the quiet of a held breath, and Thane rode through it reading the floor of the world — the road's churned mud, the way the grass lay, the wear and the want of wear — and the floor told him over and over that things had passed this way that he did not want to meet.

Three days out they found the campsite.

He read it before they reached it and read it wrong, which was a thing he would chew on for a long time after. Banked fire, picketed horses, the disorder of men at rest — and his mind, primed by three days of the empty land, filled the gaps with cultists, and he set the ambush. He had them in position, daggers loose, the shortbow strung, the angles all worked out cold. Mother's blade in the right hand. Father's wedding-knife reversed in the left, the parry-knife, the way he always carried it, the dead held close.

And then he looked at the floor again, because the floor does not lie, and the floor was wrong for cultists. The boot-prints were drilled and even. The picket line was dressed. The latrine was *downhill and downwind*, dug to a standard, and demons do not dig latrines and cultists do not dig them well.

He stayed his own hand a breath before it fell, and called the soft word that meant *hold*, and the moment teetered on the edge of a killing and then came down on the right side of it.

They were scouts. Mendevian scouts, outriders of the very host he'd come to find, and the look on their faces when four armed dwarves and a sharp-eyed man rose up out of the dark where they'd been about to be murdered was something Thane carried with a private grim humor for days. Cooler heads, he'd say later, if he said anything at all. Cooler heads. He did not mention how close it had run. No Stonevein boasts of the kill he didn't have to make.

The scouts brought news that was worth the whole ride and the fright of it twice over. Less than half a day's march behind them came the army.

In the morning they took him up to a high ridge, and they handed him a spyglass, and Thane Stonevein saw a thing he would describe to no one in full because no telling would hold it.

The host filled the plain. It ran to the horizon and did not stop at the horizon, only grew too far and too hazed to count — thousands upon thousands upon thousands of them, the dust of their passage standing in the still air like a second range of hills. And above them, snapping in a wind he could not feel from the ridge, the standards. The sword-and-sunburst of Iomedae. The rose and the songbird of Shelyn. The hammer and anvil of Torag, of *Torag*, and Thane's throat closed on that one in a way he had not expected, because it was Korroc's banner and his father's and his father's father's, and seeing it come up out of the south at the head of a host meant the Smith had not forgotten this corner of the world after all. A dozen others he half-knew and a hundred he did not. All of it moving north. All of it coming.

Hope. He had not had a word for the thing in his chest in a long while and that was the word, plain and unfashionable and true.

One of the team stayed behind to guide the host the last of the way. The other three turned north with Thane, to carry the news ahead, and that was when the sky came apart.

It came from above, which was the only direction Thane had not been reading, because you cannot read the floor of the air. The first he knew of it was the sound — a leather crack of wings, wrong-angled, too large — and then Bryd was down, the Mendevian dwarf who'd ridden at his right shoulder for four days, down with a thing the size of a horse riding him into the dirt and his scream cut off short.

Then there were more.

*More* was the whole of it. A hunting-pack. They came down out of the high air in a stoop, four, six, he never had time to count them true, a pack the way wolves are a pack — coordinated, patient, herding. The fight was a fight for the length of one held breath and then it was a rout, and a rout is not a battle, it is a slaughter that has not finished sorting out who.

He killed one. He would hold to that. It came at him low and he went lower, under the wing-beat, mother's blade up into the soft place where the leg met the body, the whole weight of the thing trying to crush down on him and his arm locked and screaming and the knife finding the artery his mother's lessons had taught his hand to find without his eyes. It died on top of him and he heaved it off and was already moving, because to stop moving was to die.

Sorn fell next. He saw it happen and could not reach it and the not-reaching went into him like a splinter that would never come out — Sorn pulled up off the ground, ten feet, twenty, and let go, and Thane looked away before the landing because some things you do not need to have seen to know.

The fourth of them, the youngest, broke and ran south, back the way they'd come, screaming for the army, and a demon peeled off the pack after him and Thane never learned how that ended and chose, in the cold clear floor-reading part of his mind, not to follow. South was where the pack expected the prey to run. South was the open plain. North was broken ground, rock and gully and the long memory of a land that had once been settled, walls to put his back to, holes to fold himself into.

So Thane went north. Alone.

For the better part of a full day he ran and hid and ran. He went to ground in a dry culvert while wings beat overhead and he made himself stone, made himself nothing, breathed slow and shallow the way his father had taught him to breathe in the dark — *small, lad, be small, the dark is a door and you don't have to open it.* He moved when the sky was empty and froze when it was not. He took a wound he did not feel until much later, a raking thing along his side that soaked his armor warm and then cold. He did not bind it well because there was no time to bind it well. He wiped his blades — both of them, mother's and father's, every time, because no Stonevein has ever sheathed a wet blade and he was not going to be the first to die having broken that — and he kept north.

He thought about Korroc, in the culverts, in the cold. He thought *the cousin will be at the forge*. He thought *I should not have left without a word*, and then he thought *no, the threshold, you cross it clean*, and he held to that the way he held to everything, stubborn, banked, the small clean flame of his father's memory burning low and steady at the bottom of him where the wind couldn't reach it.

He did not think about dying. There was no use in it. The dead do not need your attention; the living moment does. *Look at the floor.* He looked at the floor of the broken land mile after mile and the floor brought him home.

The walls of Kenabres came up out of the gray late in the second day, and he wept a little, which he would deny, and then he was through the south gate and the watch was shouting and he had no breath to answer them, only the strength to point himself at the one door that mattered.

The Defender's Heart. He came up the steps half-falling. He set his shoulder to the doors.

---

Korroc felt it before the doors opened.

He was in the common room — he had taken to keeping near it, in the third week, the way a man keeps near a thing he is afraid to be far from — and the thread in his chest, the thin quiet thread, *flared*.

It came back all at once, warm and bright and *near*, snapping taut the way a slack line snaps when the weight comes onto it, and it carried with it a pull he knew in his marrow, the pull that meant *hurt, here, come*. He was on his feet before he'd decided to stand. He had his eyes on the front doors before his mind had told him why.

The doors slammed open.

Thane stood in them. Filthy, gray to the eyes with dust and dried blood, gaunt in a way no three weeks should have made him, swaying on his feet — but standing. Standing, and breathing, and *alive*, and the relief of it went through Korroc so hard and so fast that the room swam and he had to set a hand on the table.

His cousin's eyes swept the silenced room and found Irabeth and did not stop on anyone else. Not on Korroc. Korroc understood that, even through the flood of it — the work first, always the work first, that was a Stonevein too. Thane pushed through the parting crowd and went into the council room and the doors closed behind him, and Korroc let him go, because the thread said *near, alive, hurt-but-not-dying*, and that was enough, that was everything, he could give the cousin five minutes more if the city needed them.

Minutes later Irabeth came out of that room moving fast, her face lit with something Korroc had not seen on it since before the siege, and she began barking orders that sent runners scattering toward the walls. Korroc did not wait to parse them. He went for the back room.

Inside, Quednys and Thane were speaking low. The old wizard had a hand on Thane's shoulder, and as Korroc came in he was finishing something — Korroc caught only the tail of it, *…tell your cousin to stop worrying* — and then Quednys straightened and made for the door. He paused as he passed Korroc. He gave him a single nod, dry and weary and not unkind, the nod of a man who has just been handed good news and remembers, distantly, that it is also someone's family.

Then it was the two of them.

Thane slumped into a chair like a marionette with the strings let go. Up close he was worse than the doorway had shown — the gauntness real, the lips cracked, a tremor in the hands that the will alone had been holding still until it didn't have to anymore. And there, at his side, the dark soak Korroc had felt before he'd seen: blood, old and new, the armor stiff with it.

"Hands off it, you stubborn whetstone," Korroc said, and his voice came out rougher than he meant, thick. He knelt. He pulled Thane's hand away from his ribs, gentle for all the roughness, and got the buckles, and found the wound — a long raking tear, ugly, gone the wrong color at the edges. He put his palm flat over it.

"Right, then," he murmured, and the murmur slid into prayer the way water slides downhill, the way it always did with him, no seam between the speaking and the asking. "Torag. Great Smith. Here's a flaw in good metal, and I'd have it drawn out. Steady the temper. Steady the man." The warmth came up out of his palm and into his cousin, and the stone in him sang low with it, and under his hand the torn edges began to knit, the wrong color easing back toward the right. "Steady, cousin. I've got you. I've got you."

Thane let out a breath that had a shake in it, and let his head fall back against the chair, and for a moment neither of them said anything at all.

The door opened. Nageru came in, barefoot and quiet as ever, the worn prayer-beads dark about his wrist, his still face giving away nothing but his amber eyes saying plenty. Behind him, a half-step later, Caleth — measured, taking in the room in one pass the way he took in everything, his gaze stopping on the wound under Korroc's hand and reading it and easing by a fraction when he saw the color coming back.

The four of them. Together again, in one small room, for the first time since the world had broken open under the Garrison. Korroc felt the thread of them all, all three, warm and near and his, and he kept his hand on his cousin's side and did not trust himself to speak.

It was Thane who spoke. Of course it was. He pushed himself up a little in the chair, wincing, and looked at the three of them, and there was something working at the corner of his cracked mouth.

"I found the army," he said. His voice was a ruin but the words were clear. "The Mendevian host. It's coming north." He drew a breath, and it caught on the half-healed wound, and he pressed on through it. "It's almost here."

A beat. The room held it.

A tired grin spread across Thane's filthy face — the first true one Korroc had seen on him in a month, the old dry crooked Stonevein grin, the one their fathers had worn.

"And Queen Galfrey," he said, "is leading it."

No one spoke. There was nothing to say that would not have been smaller than it. Caleth's careful face had gone still in a way that was its own kind of stunned. Nageru closed his eyes a moment, the way a man does when he sets a stone down on an altar. And Korroc knelt there with his hand on his living cousin and the warmth still running out of him and the thread singing in his chest, and he bowed his head, and what came up in him was not words but the older thing under words, the gratitude that has no language, offered up to the Smith who keeps the forge of the world and does not, after all, forget.

---

The following morning, trumpets sounded from the southern walls of Kenabres — high and clear and answered, peal upon peal, until the whole wounded city had climbed to the ramparts to see what the dawn was bringing up the road; and against the gray edge of the world the standards came on, sword and rose and hammer, and the breathing space was over, and something else, at long last, had begun.
