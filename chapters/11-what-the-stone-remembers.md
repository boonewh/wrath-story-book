# The Latch That Held

*Chapter Eleven — What the Stone Remembers*

---

Thane woke before the horn, the way he always did, and lay still in the gray with his hand already on his father's knife.

He did not sleep well and had not for a long time, and he'd made his peace with that the way he made his peace with most things — by turning it into a use. A man who wakes early has time to read the camp before the camp knows it's being read. So he lay in the cold under his blanket and listened first, the old order, the only prayer he knew: the creak of a picket line settling, the cough of a soldier three tents down, the small dry rustle of two hundred people breathing in a place they did not trust. He listened for the wrong sound. The one that didn't belong.

He didn't find it. That was the whole trouble.

Somewhere in this camp a man had cut a wagon axle three-quarters through and left the weight to finish it. Somewhere in this camp a man had opened Thane's own pack and folded three vials of poison down under the spare cord, patient as a mason setting stone, and closed it again so neat that Thane's own eye hadn't caught the disturbance until his hand did. He had walked the whole length of the column with the sight that named corruption, four servants of the light doing the same, and the camp had looked back at them clean as a temple novice's confession. Every face a tired crusader. Not one door out of true.

*Someone forced the lock,* he thought, *and left no marks on the plate.* His mother's third rule, the one that told you a thing had been broken into. There should have been marks. There were none. That was not innocence. That was a better hand than his.

The banked flame in his chest ticked up a degree, small and clean, and he let it, and did not feed it more than that. It would keep. He'd learned to carry it a long way without being burned by it, and today was going to be a long day, and a man spends his fire where it does the most good.

The horn went. The camp began to become an army.

Thane sat up, wiped both blades though he'd killed nothing in the night — habit, ritual, the one thing his hands did before his head was awake — and went to see what the day would try.

---

The day tried the Ahari Gorge.

They'd been three hours on the march, the column grinding west through the dead land in that long jointed way an army moves, when Aron Kir came back down the line at a hard walk with his flat face flatter than usual, and that was the first sign Thane read: Aron did not hurry. Aron had stopped expecting anything worth hurrying toward years ago. A man like that breaking his own pace meant the thing ahead was bad enough to override a habit built out of grief.

"You'll want to stop the army," Aron said to Irabeth. "The leadership needs to see this before we walk into it."

So Irabeth stopped the army — a thing easier said than done, two hundred souls and a dozen wagons folding to a halt behind her word — and stayed with it, because a commander's place is where the most of her people are. Six went forward on foot: Aron, Aravashnial, and the four of them, low and quiet across the last stretch of open ground to the lip of a dry riverbed.

Thane heard it before he saw it. They all did.

A droning. Low and vast and *layered*, a sound with too many voices in it, coming up off the gorge and echoing wall to wall until you couldn't say which way it started. Beside him Korroc had gone still, head cocked, and when Caleth glanced at him the dwarf gave the smallest nod.

"Buzzing," Korroc said. "I hear it too. I don't like it."

"I can't place it." Caleth was scanning the walls, and Thane knew that look — the man reading angles, sight-lines, the arithmetic of where a thing could hide. "It's coming off everywhere. The gorge is throwing it back at us."

They crept the last few yards on their bellies and looked down.

The riverbed was full of green.

Not water — the water had died out of this place with everything else — but a writhing green vapor, thick and low, coiling along the dry stone bed like something poured out of a broken jar and still moving because it did not know it was supposed to be still. And through the vapor, when Thane made his eye slow down and *read* it instead of just seeing it, there was motion. Currents in the green that were not the green. Densities. The vapor bunched and thinned and crawled, and the crawling was the droning, and the droning was—

"Vescavor swarms," Aron said, flat, and half the party winced.

Thane knew Vescavor. He'd killed them in the underdark under Kenabres, in the barricade fights, the little insect-demons that swarmed and stung and had once, in a dark room, been the shape of a young half-arachnid thing he'd had to put down. But those had come in patrols of a handful. What was down in that riverbed was not a patrol. It was a *tide*, and it went on farther than the eye wanted to follow, and the sound of it stood the hair up on the back of his neck.

"I've never heard of swarms that size," Caleth murmured. "From the sound alone it's — that has to be enormous."

Aron nodded once. "Take an army through that, you don't have an army on the other side."

Thane swore in Dwarven, short and to the point, because a soldier learns the exact weight of a thing that cannot be gone around. "And no way past it. The gorge is the road."

"There is one thing." Aravashnial had come up beside them, and Thane noted, not for the first time, how the elf had changed since he'd got his sight back — a brightness in him, a lean forward into every problem, a man who could see and meant to make it count. He was looking down at the green with an expression Thane could only call *professional*. "The only time I have ever seen swarms grown to this size, there was a queen. A single breeding matriarch, deep in a nest. The swarm is an extension of her." He said it the way another man might name the weather. "Kill the queen, and the swarm has nothing to organize it. It disperses."

"You've seen this before," Thane said.

"Once." Aravashnial did not elaborate, and Thane filed the not-elaborating and moved on. There was a great deal about the elf that came with the door already half-shut.

It was Nageru who found the way in.

The aasimar had said nothing this whole time — he rarely did, until the words were worth the breath — but now he lifted one bronze hand and pointed, down into the riverbed, to the far bank where the stone was pocked with dark holes. Tunnel-mouths, a dozen of them, worn into the undercut bank where a river had once carved and something else had burrowed since.

"There." His voice was quiet and certain. "The biggest hole. Nothing goes in or out of it."

Thane followed the line of the finger and saw it, and saw that Nageru was right. The other holes had traffic — the green crawling in and out of them, the swarms cycling through their nest — but the largest, a black mouth low on the bank, sat dead and still while the tide moved around it. A door nobody used. In Thane's experience, a door nobody used was a door that led somewhere the users were afraid of. Or a door the queen kept for herself.

"That's our way down," he said.

---

They went down into the green.

Thane had braced himself for the swarms to turn — for the droning to change pitch, for the tide to notice six warm bodies wading into it — and it did not happen. The vapor closed around them, thick and cool and stinking faintly of crushed insect, and the Vescavor simply did not care. They crawled past his boots. They coiled around his knees. They cycled in and out of their holes an arm's length away and did not so much as pause, as though the six of them were a rock the river had always run around.

"They're ignoring us," Thane said, low, and he didn't like it. Being ignored by demons was worse than being attacked by them. It meant something bigger had first claim on their attention, and that something was down the hole.

They reached the dead mouth. Nageru crouched at its lip and looked down.

"It drops about twenty feet to a ledge," he said, "then again below that. Forty feet, all told."

Aron already had a piton out. He hammered it into the stone at the hole's edge — three flat clean strokes, a man who'd set a hundred of these — tied off a rope, and threw the coil down into the dark. Then he straightened, looked down the hole, and Thane watched the man's whole face fill with a distaste so deep it was almost holy.

"I'll stay up here," Aron said. "Keep the watch. If it's all the same."

It was not all the same. Thane frowned.

"You're in our scout services," he said. Not sharp, but not soft. "We could use every hand down there. You most of all — you read a room fast." He gestured at the hole. "There's a whole nest under us and six of us going into it."

Aron's mouth opened — some flat reasonable refusal already forming, some line about being more use up top, a line Thane could have finished for him—

—and Korroc simply touched the silver scale at his belt and stepped off the edge.

The dwarf did not fall. A soft silver light bloomed out from the scale, ran up the plates of his armor and haloed him, and Korroc Stonevein sank into the hole slow and stately as a leaf on still water, hammer across his back, shield on his arm, disappearing down into the dark at a pace that said the argument was over and he'd rather be fighting than watching two men have it.

Thane felt the small pull of grief he always felt when a scale spoke. That light was Terendelev's light — the dragon's last will, *get them out, get them out* — and every time one of them woke it, a little more of the dragon's dying gift was spent. Korroc had three charges in a day and had just burned one to skip an argument. It was, Thane thought, a very Korroc way to win.

Nageru went next, down the rope hand over hand, silent, barefoot on the coarse hemp.

Thane looked at Aron one more time. The man looked back, and there was nothing to read in his face but the old spent grief and the fresh disgust, and Thane understood, the way his mother had taught him to understand people even when he didn't like the shape of them: some doorways a man will not cross, and it is not always cowardice, and it is not always yours to judge. Aron had lost too much to too many holes in the ground. Let him keep his threshold.

"Watch the top, then," Thane said. "Watch it *well*." And he took the rope and went down.

---

The bottom was slick.

That was the first thing his boots told him — a film over the stone, wet and faintly yielding, the whole floor coated in it. Walking careful would be fine. Running would be a way to break a hip. He filed it, adjusted his weight, and read the room the way he read every room: floor first. *Walls lie. Floors don't.*

The floor here told him a story he didn't enjoy. Drag-marks in the ick. Scored patches where something heavy had been hauled through. The wet coating thicker in some places, matted, and in those places the smell got worse — and it was already bad, a green rot that sat at the back of the throat.

"The floor and walls are covered in it," Caleth said, coming down last, wrinkling his nose. "Bug ick."

"It smells like more than ick," Thane said.

He pointed south, where the nearest passage bent away into the dark, and moved that way at a careful pace, both blades loose but not drawn, feeling with his eyes ahead of his feet. The others fell in behind and let him have the point without discussion. This was his work. Everyone knew it, and everyone knew that a man who reads floors goes first or the reading does nobody any good.

He'd gone maybe thirty feet when he held up a hand. The party froze. He crouched, watched, and let his ear go ahead of his eye into the dark of the room beyond.

"A few ahead," he murmured, coming back. "But they're busy."

"Busy how?" Caleth asked.

"They're eating."

"Eating what?"

Thane looked at him. There was no soft way to say it, so he didn't bother. "A hand."

Caleth's face did something. "A hand."

"Just the hand." Thane had seen it clearly in the dim — three of the small Vescavor, the little ones, hunched over a severed human hand on the wet floor, working at it with a horrible patient industry, taking no notice of anything but their meal. He did not let himself dwell on where the rest of the person was. That was a door he shut on purpose. Behind it the flame was very bright.

They agreed, without needing many words, to simply walk past. And they did — six of them stepping careful and quiet through a room where three demons ate a dead man's hand, and not one of the little things so much as lifted its head. The tide had bigger business. It ran around them still.

They pressed on through two more rooms, and in the third Thane's hand went up again.

"Full swarm," he said, coming back from the mouth of it. Ahead, the whole passage boiled — Vescavor by the hundred, a living wall, the droning close and enormous. No going through that. Not six of them. Not sixty.

"Try the other ways," Caleth said, and they backtracked and tried, and found another swarm blocking another route, a churning gray-green mass filling a room hip to ceiling.

"Are we even sure the queen's down here?" Thane asked, quiet, at the second dead end.

"No," Caleth admitted. Then, after a beat: "But it's where she'd be. A thing that breeds a tide like this doesn't do it in the open. It does it deep, in the dark, with her children between her and the door."

Which was, Thane thought, exactly how you'd build it if you were smart and evil and wanted to be hard to kill. Swarms for a moat. He'd have done the same. He filed the queen a grudging professional respect and kept moving.

They worked north, and found a passage that ran empty, and Thane went a little way down a branch of it and came back.

"Room to the south is crawling," he reported. "Floor to ceiling. All of them small, though. Moving slow."

Caleth and Aravashnial came to look, and the elf studied the seething room for a moment and then, oddly, relaxed.

"Newly hatched," Aravashnial said. "See how they drag themselves, how they cluster to the warm stone? They're drying. They come here after the shells split, before they can fly or bite properly." He said it with something almost like scholarly pleasure, a man glad to have his sight back to see the awful thing clearly. "Where the hatchlings dry, the eggs are not far. And where the eggs are—"

"—the queen," Thane finished.

He went back to the empty northern passage and worked east, and the droning got louder as he went, louder than it had been anywhere, until it wasn't a sound he heard so much as a pressure he felt in his teeth and the bones of his face. He slowed. He went silent, back to the wall, one careful foot finding the next. *Listen. Look.* He came up to the mouth of a great cavern and eased his head around the stone, and stopped.

He had found it.

A queen. Vast and bloated and wrong, a Vescavor swollen past all reason, her body a pale sac heaving in the dark, ringed by attendant swarms that moved around her in constant orbit — never fewer than two, some peeling off to tend the clustered egg-sacs that glistened along the cavern walls, others cycling back. She was the eye of the whole storm, the thing all that green organized itself around. Thane read her the way he read a locked door: the guards, the approaches, the ways in and the ways killed.

And past her, at the cavern's southern edge, there was a light.

Purple. Pulsing. Coming up out of a low tunnel-mouth in a slow sick rhythm, like something breathing. It threw mauve shadows across the queen's heaving sac, and it made the small flame in Thane's chest go cold and careful, because he did not know what it was, and a thing he didn't know that pulsed like a heartbeat in a nest at the bottom of the world was not a thing he liked.

He backed out silent and went to tell the others.

---

"A queen," he said, low, when they'd gathered in the empty passage. "Big as the swarm promised. Egg-sacs on the walls. Two swarms on her at least, all the time, more coming and going as they tend her." He paused. "And a light. Purple. Pulsing, out of a tunnel behind her. I don't know what it is."

Aravashnial's head came up at *purple*, and Thane marked that too — the elf knew something, or feared something — but the elf said only, "If the hatchlings we saw are fresh, they're no true threat. It's the grown ones and the queen. Deal with her and the rest is noise."

They crept up together to the cavern's mouth, all five, and looked in at the thing they'd come to kill.

"So what do we do?" Caleth breathed.

"Fireball," Thane said. Flat. It was the obvious answer and he saw no reason to dress it. Drop something big in the middle of the swarms, break them all at once, go in behind it.

"You'll follow it up?"

Thane nodded. So did Korroc. So did Nageru — a single dip of the chin.

Thane glanced at Aravashnial, who had his book out again and was sketching something with quick absorbed strokes, oblivious. "You've a fireball in you too?"

The elf looked up, blinking. "What? Sorry — what?"

"Fireball," Thane said, and couldn't help the small dry chuckle. Even here. Even now.

"Ah. No. Not a spell I carry." Aravashnial went back to his book.

Thane shook his head and turned to the work.

This part he knew. Before the killing came the readying, and the readying was as much a discipline as the wiping of a blade. He gathered them with a look and called down the blessing — the Welcomer's gift, though he'd never once named it that, the doorkeeper's sight that let him mark who had business inside and who did not, laid now over his friends like a hand on the shoulder. Korroc followed it with a wave of protection from evil, the words rolling out in his beard, a ward settling over all of them against the touch of the Abyss. Nageru breathed once, deep, and his skin took on a faint hardness, a toughening, the still water setting like stone.

And Thane drew the invisibility potion, and drank it, and the world went on not-seeing him.

It never stopped being strange — his own hands gone from the edge of his sight, his body a fact the room had forgotten. He'd been made a knife nobody was watching, which was, as far as he was concerned, the natural and correct state of a knife.

"Now," he breathed, to no one, and to all of them.

Caleth's hand came up.

---

Thane was already moving when the spell left the half-elf's fingers.

He couldn't have said how he knew — the intake of Caleth's breath, the particular set of his shoulder that Thane had learned to read across a dozen fights — but he was off the wall and into the cavern, low and fast and unseen, before the thing even landed. He wanted to be inside the swarms' guard when the world broke, not outside it.

Caleth cast a fireball, and turned it to ice.

That was the half-elf's gift, the thing the wizard-school had never understood about him — he took a spell and turned it inside out, and where three feet of roaring flame should have bloomed in the center of the cavern there was instead a detonation of killing cold, a white starburst of frost that cracked out across the wet floor and the heaving sacs and the orbiting swarms all at once. The green died where it touched. The two swarms on the queen came apart in a rattle of frozen wings and dropped to the stone like flung gravel, and the queen — the vast bloated queen — threw back what passed for her head and *screamed*, a sound that came up through the buzzing and drowned it.

Nageru crossed the cavern.

Thane, unseen, saw it from the side and would remember it: the aasimar simply *arrived*, a bronze blur over the treacherous floor that should have spilled a running man, and his bare heel took the queen in the swollen flank with a wet enormous crack. She screamed again and answered — her whole front convulsing, a jet of acid spat point-blank into Nageru's chest. It caught him square. Thane saw the wraps smoke, saw the flesh beneath redden and blister, and Nageru took it the way he took everything: a tightening at the jaw, no sound, a door closed on the pain so it couldn't get out of his face.

Korroc came in a beat behind him, sprinting as fast as a dwarf in full plate could drive himself, and set his feet beside the queen and drew.

Thane felt it happen — felt the stone under his own boots go taut, as though the floor had drawn a breath. Korroc had reached *down*, through the wet rock, into the deep bones of the world, and pulled the weight of it up into his arms, and the adamantine hammer came down on the queen once, twice, each blow landing with a wrongness of mass, too heavy, the strike of a mountain deciding to fall on a single point. The sac split under it. Foul stuff sprayed.

Then the queen's droning changed.

It dropped, deepened, became a *humming* that Thane felt reach for the inside of his skull — a fog rolling into his thoughts, a cloudy confusion, a suggestion that he was not sure where he was or why he had come. And through the cavern's mouths, answering her call, more swarms began to pour in.

Thane shook the fog off. He felt them all shake it off — a ripple of clenched wills around the cavern, five minds refusing the queen's hand at once, the protection Korroc had laid and the blessing Thane had called doing their quiet work. The confusion slid off him and was gone.

The queen lunged at Nageru, who was nearest and bleeding, and got him — mandibles closing, a claw raking down across the burn the acid had already made — and the aasimar rocked with it and did not fall.

And Thane came in behind her.

This was the whole of what he was, the reason a knife nobody watches exists at all. He'd worked around to her blind rear while she screamed at the men in front of her, and now he came up out of the dark at the base of her heaving bulk with both blades — his mother's knife leading, his father's reversed in his off-hand, *both ways*, the way he'd been taught by hands that never told him they were teaching him anything but knifework — and he opened her up.

The mother's blade went in low and long and dragged. The father's followed a hand's width over, and where the two paths crossed the sac gave way entirely, and the queen came apart from the inside in a collapse of gore and thin screaming, folding down and down onto the wet stone until there was nothing left of the eye of the storm but a slumped ruin.

Thane stepped back out of the fall of her, and the world remembered him, and he stood there suddenly visible with both daggers dripping.

He looked up. Korroc and Nageru were looking at themselves, then at him, and both of them wore matching frowns of disgust, because both of them — and now Thane, too — were painted in the stuff of the queen, glistening head to boot in a foulness that dripped.

"Ugh," Korroc said, with feeling.

And around the cavern, the swarms the queen had summoned reached the edge of the light, and hesitated, and — with nothing left to organize them, no will in the dark to hum them into a tide — simply drifted apart. The droning frayed. Went ragged. Died. The green currents lost their shape and became only vapor, and the vapor thinned, and the great pressure that had stood in Thane's teeth since the gorge was, all at once, gone.

The nest was just a cave. And the purple light still pulsed at the southern wall.

Thane wiped his blades — mother's, then father's, long even strokes down a strip of his own less-fouled sleeve, because no Stonevein had ever sheathed a wet blade and he was not going to be the first, not standing in the wreck of a queen — and he'd barely finished when he saw Aravashnial moving.

---

The elf had stayed back through the fight, off to the side, a caster's discipline. Now he was crossing the cavern toward the pulsing light with a fixed, hungry purpose, and Thane read the walk and did not like it.

He got in front of the elf and put a hand flat on his chest.

"We don't know what's in there," Thane said.

Aravashnial looked past him, at the light, and there was something in his face Thane couldn't name — not fear exactly. Recognition. "I do."

Thane cocked his head, a question he didn't bother to voice.

"There could be demons in there," he said.

Aravashnial nodded, calm as pages turning. "Probably."

And that, Thane thought, was the whole trouble with the elf in one word. He filed it, and let his hand drop, and they all moved toward the light together, because there was no version of this where five people who'd come this deep turned around at a door because it was dangerous. Danger was the reason they'd come.

The next cave stopped him at its threshold.

A crevice split the southern wall — a long rippling tear of nauseating mauve, and *tear* was the honest word, because it was not a crack in the stone. It was a crack in something behind the stone. It pulsed and writhed, its edges widening and contracting with a slow organic rhythm, and through it churned a roiling mist that was not smoke and not fog but something the eye slid off of and the stomach turned from. The air here was hotter than anywhere in the caves, close and thick, and it carried a smell Thane's mind flinched from naming and named anyway: burning flesh.

He set the back of his hand, unthinking, against the doorframe of stone as he came through it — a small touch, a thing he did not know he did, an old gesture at a threshold his mother had built into him before he could name it — and stepped inside.

"Abyssal rift," Aravashnial said. Calm. Certain. A man naming a thing he'd seen before.

"I see movement in it," Thane said, and heard the wrong note of excitement in his own voice and didn't care. His eyes were on the tear, on the shapes moving in the churn.

"We need to close this," Caleth said, and started forward, reading the rift the way he read everything—

The screech came out of the crevice like a blade.

It went through Thane's skull and took his body with it — a sudden total lock, his muscles seizing, his knives useless in hands that had stopped obeying him. Beside him he saw Nageru go rigid the same way, the still water frozen mid-motion, and Thane's whole world narrowed to the awful fact of not being able to *move*, of standing planted and helpless while the tear in reality tore itself wider — and a demon stepped out of it.

A thing crossed of man and vulture. Tall, hunched, its head a naked raptor's skull, its body wrong-jointed and reeking, great filthy wings folding as it cleared the rift.

"*Vrock!*" Korroc bellowed.

Aravashnial cursed — in Elven, sharp and startled, the first time Thane had ever heard the elf lose the dryness — and cursed again as a second vrock came through behind the first.

"Keep them away from me," the elf called, backing off to the side, and his hands were already moving, already shaping something — but he'd put himself alone, off the flank, exposed.

"Well then *move back*, then!" Korroc roared, and did the thing Korroc did — put his own body where the danger was going. He crossed to plant himself between the wizard and the demons, shield up, boots wide, and Thane felt the floor draw taut again as the dwarf reached down into the stone and *rooted*, becoming a wall, an immovable fact, a door that would not open.

The first vrock reached him and loosed a cloud — spores, a gray-green burst that fountained around Korroc and *hurt*, Thane could see it hurt, the dwarf flinching inside his own ward as the cloud sank into him. Then the demon came in with beak and talons. The claws found the hammer-and-anvil, the claws found the shield and skated, but one great hand hooked the rim and *hauled*, dragging the shield down with a demon's strength, and the beak came in over the top and bit down on Korroc's shoulder.

Nageru broke his stun a half-breath before Thane broke his and charged the vrock on Korroc, one fist landing solid.

And the second vrock turned toward Thane, who still could not move.

He saw it come. That was the horror of it — the stun had let go of his mind but not yet his body, and he stood there fully aware and fully locked while a thing of beak and wing crossed the ground toward him with its talons spreading. He could read every line of its approach. He could see exactly where the strikes would land. And he could do *nothing*, and the old cold thing turned over under his ribs, the fear he'd carried since the cistern in Kenabres, the fear of being the man who stands useless while better men bleed—

Across the cave, Korroc saw it too.

Thane felt what happened next more than he understood it. A surge — a warmth flung across the whole width of the cave, mythic and sudden, the dwarf pouring protection out of himself and into Thane from thirty feet away, a shield of grace laid over a cousin he could not reach with his hands. One talon that should have opened Thane's throat turned aside on nothing, deflected by a ward that hadn't been there a heartbeat before.

The others got through.

The vrock's remaining claws raked him — one across the chest, one down the arm, the third hooking into his side — and the pain broke his body free of the stun at last, too late to matter, and he staggered under it with blood running warm down his front. He got his blades up. But he was hurt, and slow with hurt, and the demon drew back for another pass that he could see coming and could barely answer.

Caleth reached him.

The half-elf was suddenly at Thane's shoulder, a dimension slide, and cast — and Thane shimmered and was gone, invisible, the world once more forgetting he existed. It was the right call and the best gift, and Thane took it and hated the shape of the moment even as it saved him: bleeding, half-broken, made useful only by being hidden, while across the cave Korroc bled to buy him a ward and Caleth spent a spell to hide him and Nageru put his own body in the vrock's mouth. Everyone spending themselves. And Thane a wound that needed protecting.

*You're quick, lad. You see the cracks. The men who slip in after the walls fall — those we never have enough of.* The old sergeant's lesson, the one he'd hated as a boy. He'd made his peace with being the knife. But there was a hard difference between being the knife and being the thing carried out of the fire, and standing there unseen and bleeding, watching his friends spend their strength to keep him alive, he felt the difference in his teeth.

So he did the only thing the moment allowed. He stopped being a wound and started being a knife again. Invisible, low, fast, he circled — around behind the churn and the wings, into the dead space no demon watches when it's chosen a target in front of it — and set himself at the vrock's blind rear, both blades ready, and waited for the beat.

---

The fight became a grind, the way the worst ones did, and Thane lost the fine grain of it the way he always did in close work — the world narrowing to the next angle, the next seam, the wet specifics in front of him while the wide shape of it went on above his attention. But he kept the ledger. The hunter in him always kept the ledger, even when the man couldn't spare a glance.

Aravashnial cast, and the rift *shrank*. Thane saw it out of the corner of his eye — the tear shifting, wobbling, drawing in on itself a little, its mauve light guttering. The elf immediately began a second casting, calm in the chaos, working his own strange war against the door itself while the rest of them fought the things that had come through it. And a *third* vrock stepped out of the crevice as it closed, unfolding into the cave.

Korroc pressed a glowing hand to his own body — lay on hands, the paladin's gift, closing the spore-burn and the bite. And Thane understood, in a flicker, why the dwarf healed himself first and hardest: the wounds Korroc carried weren't only his. There was a thread that ran between them all, Korroc's life-bond, and it had been drinking down Thane's hurts and Nageru's, taking a measure of their pain into his own chest to keep them standing. He healed himself because he was carrying three men's wounds in one body. Then, that done, he swept a wave of gold out wide, positive energy rolling over the whole party at once, and Thane felt his own torn side knit and cool under it even unseen.

The vrock that had savaged Korroc turned on Nageru and hit him — talon, claw, beak, all three — and blood ran bright down the aasimar's chest. And Nageru answered.

He drew himself up, breathed, and *called* — and his fists took fire. Not flame; the clean cold-gold light of a holy smite, celestial fury lit along both hands, and he brought them down on the vrock four times in a blur, each blow flaring where it struck, driving the demon back shrieking. It reeled. It could not answer that. Nothing born of the Abyss loved the touch of what glowed on Nageru's knuckles.

The vrock that had been on Thane — the one he now stood behind, unseen — turned instead toward Caleth, and connected hard: a bite, a claw, a talon, the half-elf rocking under the triple hit. And the third vrock, the new one, *vanished* — blinked out of the churn and reappeared behind Caleth, flanking him, claws already rising for the paladin's unguarded back—

—and suddenly teleported again sideways against the cave wall.

Thane saw it happen and did not understand it. One instant the third vrock was cocked to open Caleth's spine; the next it was as if the teleport went wrong and he was against the stone, as though a giant hand had swatted it. And the only thing in that direction was Aravashnial, who had made the smallest flick of his fingers, barely a gesture, his attention already returning to his spellwork.

Thane filed it. Hard. A wizard who threw demons with a flick of the wrist. *Noted.* But there was a knife's work in front of him and no time to chew on it.

Caleth answered his own vrock with Radiance. The glaive came alive, white fire running its length, and the half-elf drove it home — three flashing thrusts, one after another, the holy edge plunging into the demon and blazing where it bit. The vrock screamed.

Aravashnial's second casting landed, and a flash of pale energy went out from him, and the rift shrank *again*, drawing in harder now, its edges pulling toward the center. All three vrocks screamed as it did, as though the closing door cost them something, as though they were tethered to it.

And Thane took his beat.

The vrock between him and Caleth had its back full to him, all its attention on the glaive opening its front. Thane came up out of the dark unseen and put both blades into it — mother's knife under the wing where the joint gapped, father's reversed a hand lower — and the demon jerked, and where his daggers went in, holes opened that no one had seen coming. He worked them, fast, deepening the seams, and the vrock's scream climbed and cracked and cut out as it folded and came apart onto the stone.

Nageru's vrock fell a heartbeat later, two more smiting blows finishing what four had started, the demon collapsing under fists that glowed.

Which left the third. The one Aravashnial had somehow thrown into a teleport. It gathered itself off the wall, ignored everyone else, and squared again on Caleth — and this time both Caleth *and* Korroc were watching the elf, Thane saw it, saw both their heads turn to catch him in the act—

Aravashnial gestured.

The vrock *shifted*, and it teleported to stand in front Korroc and Nageru, but just as fast, it was gone again, teleported *into* the rift in the same instant that the rift — worried down to a slit by the elf's long work — finally snapped shut. It closed on the demon like a door on a hand. There was a sound Thane would not try to describe. And then the crevice was gone, the mauve light gone, the heat and the stench of it gone, and the only thing left of the third vrock on this side of reality was its head, sheared clean off at the closing, rolling to a slow stop on the cave floor.

Silence.

The four of them stood in it and looked at Aravashnial, who had already taken out his book and begun to write.

---

Caleth walked to the elf.

"How did you do that?"

Aravashnial glanced up, mild, genuinely puzzled. "Do what?"

"That." Caleth gestured — at the fallen vrocks, at the shut wall, at the severed head. "You threw them. You changed where the last one teleported. You pulled it out of a flank and put it into the rift."

"Ah." Aravashnial went back to his sketch. "What do you think a Riftwarden does?"

Thane went still.

He kept his face where it was — a knife-fighter's face gives away nothing it doesn't choose to — but inside he felt the word land and settle into a slot he hadn't known was empty. *Riftwarden.* He had heard the name before, distantly, the way you hear the names of old orders in a war that has too many of them; keepers of some watch against the demon-tides, a thing older than the crusades. He had not known the elf was one. He had traveled with Aravashnial for weeks, fought beside him, shared fires, and taken him for what he seemed: a scholarly elf wizard with a burned face and a dry manner and a book he was always writing in.

And here, in a cave at the bottom of a Vescavor nest, the elf had thrown demons across a room with a flick of his fingers and named himself, casual as weather, as something with power over the movements of the Abyss.

Caleth threw his hands up, and there was a frustration in it Thane understood — the frustration of a man who has been circling a locked door for a long time. "Well, no one's ever really told me the full story."

"A Riftwarden," Aravashnial said, patient, still writing, "has power over the movements of demons. Their comings and goings. Their crossings." He turned a page. "It is a great deal easier, I'll grant you, when they are busy attacking someone else."

Korroc snorted and gave the elf a nod — a dwarf's rough respect, one who valued a thing done well acknowledging a thing done very well indeed.

Thane filed all of it. The name. The power. The casual way the elf wore both, and the way Caleth pushed at it like a man who wanted more than he was getting. It was another door, he thought, with a great deal more behind it than the frame suggested — a wizard was rarely only what he looked like, and this one had just shown a hand nobody had known he held. It was not Thane's door to open. But he marked the frame of it, and the hinge, and set it beside the other things he carried unsolved, cold and exact.

*Later,* he told himself. *Everything gets read eventually.* And he went to help gather the party for the climb out.

---

The way out had closed behind them.

They came back through the tunnels to the room under the original hole, and found the mouth capped — a slab of rock rolled over the opening, sealing it from above. The other holes in the ceiling were too small to squeeze a dwarf through, or a man.

"Someone's shut the door on us," Thane said, and did not like the way it sat.

Caleth solved it the wizard's way. He gathered Nageru and Korroc to him with a gesture, spoke a word, and the world *folded* — the three of them simply gone from the room and standing, Thane knew, on the surface above, on the far side of the sealed stone. A moment later he heard the grind of it: Korroc and Nageru putting their backs to the slab and heaving it aside. Light fell down the hole. The rope came down with it.

Thane climbed out into the gorge, and the first thing he read, before his eyes were even fully up, was that the piton stood alone.

Aron was gone.

Thane came off the rope and turned in a slow circle, reading, and the ground gave it up to him fast because whoever had done it hadn't cared about being read: a drag-trail, leading away from the hole, dark drops strung along it. Blood. He crouched over the trail and let it tell him the story. Furrows where heels had dragged. Smaller footprints to either side of the furrows — a small person, pulling a larger one. And here and there a stretch where the furrows stopped and two sets of ordinary steps went on, close together.

"He was dragged," Thane said, following it with his eyes. "Mostly. He walked, some — here, and here. Whoever took him is small. Smaller than us." He rose. "This way."

They followed it out of the gorge and along a stony fold in the land, and came over an edge, and there was Aron. Face down at the bottom of a short drop, unmoving.

Nageru was over the edge before anyone spoke — dropped the height easy, landed soft, and was at Aron's side. His hands went to the man's throat, his back, the still chest. "Alive," he called up. "Barely. Breathing." He laid a palm flat between Aron's shoulders and the gold rose under it.

Korroc came down after him, knelt, ran his broad hands over the man, and found it. "Blood on his back. Here — a wound. Went in from behind." He pressed his palm to it and spoke a cure into it, and the worst of the wound closed under his hand. Aron shuddered, and stirred, and after a moment got an elbow under himself and, with Nageru's arm, stood.

"I was on the watch," Aron said, hoarse. His flat face had gone flatter with pain and with something else. "Waiting. Something hit me from behind. I never saw them. Never heard them. One moment the watch, the next the ground." He touched his own back, gingerly. "I don't know who. I don't know what."

Thane was already climbing back up to the attack site.

He went over the ground at the piton where Aron had stood watch, and he read it the way he read locks, slow and total, and he found what the small careful part of him had been afraid he'd find. Footprints. Not Aron's. Not any of theirs. Smaller — smaller than any foot in their whole company that Thane could name.

He crouched over the clearest of them and studied it, and the cold thing in his chest turned over.

"The size of a gnome or halfling," he said, quietly, to himself. Then, louder: "Or near it. Small. Light on the feet." He looked up. "I think we at least have a suspect."

The axle. The shadowblood. And now a knife in Aron's back from someone small enough to leave a print like a child's. The saboteur who read clean under the sight of four servants of the light had made a mistake at last — had left a mark on the plate. Thane felt the flame in his chest come up a degree and *stay* up, steady, banked but bright.

Caleth had come to look, and now took out his book and, with quick sure strokes, sketched the footprint — its length, its shape, the odd narrow heel of it.

Thane held out his hand. "I want that."

Caleth glanced at him, and something in Thane's face must have said the size of it, because the helf-elf didn't argue. He tore the page cleanly from the book and laid it in Thane's palm.

Thane looked at the sketch. A footprint on a page. It was not proof — a print was not a foot, and a foot was not a name — but it was *evidence*, the first hard evidence in a hunt that had given him nothing but ghosts, and he held it the way his mother had taught him to hold the things that mattered. Careful. Both hands. A thing you do not fold twice.

"We take it to Irabeth," he said. "The moment we're back."

---

Irabeth did not have the moment.

He found her as the strike team rejoined the halted column, and asked her — quietly, off to the side — for a private word. And Irabeth, who was a good commander and an honest one, looked at the sky and the ground they'd lost and shook her head.

"We've bled half a day already, Master Thane," she said, not unkindly. "There'll be time to talk when there's time. Right now I need miles behind us before dark. Whatever it is, it keeps." She was already turning back to the column, already lifting her voice to move the army. "It keeps."

Thane grumbled. He nodded. He complied, because she was right about the miles and he knew it, and a soldier does not stand in the road arguing when the road is the whole point.

But it did not keep. Not really. It sat in him through the whole afternoon's march, the page against his chest beside the old soft letter, the *it keeps* turning over and souring. He'd tried to open a door and she'd shut it in his face for good reasons, and good reasons did not stop a shut door from being shut. The flame banked and glowed and did not go out.

The army made good miles that day. By early evening they were close — within striking distance, the scouts said, of Drezen itself. And Irabeth called the leadership to her tent.

---

The map on the table was Drezen.

Thane read it the way he read everything, from the ground up, and it read to him like a lock built by someone who did not want it picked. A citadel at the heart, drawn large: *Citadel Drezen, the main target.* Watchtowers marked around the walls, some inked with a small sword-and-flame that Irabeth's finger tapped as she named them — *still armed, still manned.* A road up from the south, and across it a scrawl of rubble where the towers had been deliberately toppled to block the way. A district marked off to one side, cramped and grim: *a prison, now. They may be holding people they took in the recent raids.* And around all of it, the walls, and beneath the walls the thin blue line of a dry riverbed, the only way in that wasn't a wall or a fallen tower.

"How long," Caleth asked, "has the city been theirs?"

"Seventy-five years," Irabeth said.

Caleth nodded slowly, and Thane watched the number land in the half-elf the way it landed in him: seventy-five years of demons in a place built by dwarves. Seventy-five years of the work they'd found in the Chapel of Shelyn and the Gray Garrison, but a whole city of it.

"The riverbed's the approach," Irabeth went on. "It's not a good one — it leaves us open from above, the whole way in, walls looking down on us. But it's the only one. Once we're in the city, the army engages the demons in the streets, holds their attention — and the four of you slip behind and take the citadel." Her finger came down on the great inked keep. "That's the war, in the end. The citadel. The rest is noise to buy you the walk to its door."

"How do we get up," Caleth asked, "from the riverbed to the city? If they're looking down the whole way—"

"That's the question I brought you here to answer," Irabeth said. "Without being slaughtered from the walls before we've begun."

"At night," Caleth said, working it. "Quiet as we can. And the army builds ladders — goes up under the dark, before they know we're at the wall."

Aron, healed and grim, stepped up to the map. "There are two armies out there, not one. A camp of cultists and a camp of tieflings — both south of the river, one to the east and the other to the west."

"How do we make them fight each other?" Nageru said, quietly, and Caleth nodded.

"I wondered the same," the half-elf murmured.

And Thane, who had been moving the whole time — drifting through the gathered leadership with Aravashnial's page half-hidden in his hand, trying to get a look at feet, at the small feet, at any foot that might match the sketch and the print in the gorge — grunted in frustration. People shifted as he moved. Boots slid under the table's edge, behind chair-legs, out of his line. Nobody stood still to be measured, and he could hardly kneel in the middle of a war council and lift each commander's foot in turn.

So he did the thing his patience finally gave out on doing quietly.

He slammed the page down in the center of the map.

"We have a traitor among us," he said, and his voice was not diplomatic and he did not try to make it so. "And we need to find them. Now. Tonight."

The tent went still. Irabeth's face registered a flash of shock, mastered fast.

"What are you talking about?"

"I *tried*," Thane said, and there it was, the whole afternoon's sourness coming up out of him, "to tell you this the moment it happened. And you decided the army's miles mattered more."

Irabeth's frown went deep. To her great credit she did not defend herself — she absorbed it, and asked the next right question. "All right. What happened?"

Thane turned to Aron, and Aron told it — the watch, the blow from behind, the drag, the drop, waking under Nageru's hands. Irabeth's frown deepened with every line of it.

"And what else do you have?" she asked when Aron finished. "Beyond the attack."

Thane's fist came down on the table beside the page. "We have a *footprint*." He pointed at the sketch. "Small. Smaller than any full-grown fighter's foot in this company. Left by whoever put a knife in Aron's back and dragged him off to die."

Irabeth looked at it a long moment. Then she sighed, and the sigh had weight in it — the weight of a commander who wanted to believe a thing and could not, on this, afford to. "It's a footprint, Master Thane. It isn't a name."

"So we start where we can," Korroc said, stepping in beside his cousin, blunt and warm and immovable. "Right here, in the leadership. Anyone whose foot could make that print." And the dwarf turned, deliberately, to the smallest person in the tent.

Nurah Dendiwhar. The halfling chronicler. Sent by the Queen to write the history of the road to Drezen — small, quick, easy to overlook. She looked back at Korroc with a bright startled expression.

"What?"

"Your foot," Thane said, holding out the page, "sure looks like it'd fit this one."

"Put it up on the table," Korroc said. "Simple thing."

Nurah's brightness went defensive so fast Thane saw the seam of it. "Why should I do that? I'm no fighter. I was sent by the Queen to *chronicle* — that's all I am, a chronicler. A keeper of records." Her hands had come up, small and open, the picture of the harmless. "I write down what you do. I don't—"

"The one who took Aron came from behind," Thane said. "Never showed themselves. Never traded a blow. That's not a fighter's work. That's exactly the work of someone no one thinks to watch." He kept his voice level and let the flat of it carry the fire. "A chronicler moves through a camp and nobody marks her. A chronicler has a reason to be anywhere. A chronicler is *small*."

"And if you're innocent," Korroc added, reasonable as stone, "then it costs you nothing to show us. Put your foot on the table."

She did not put her foot on the table.

Thane watched her not do it, watched her keep her hands up and her face bright and offended and her small feet tucked back under the hem of her chronicler's robe, and he *knew* — knew the way his god let him know a trespasser at a door, without proof, without evidence he could lay on a table, only the doorkeeper's certainty that this one did not have business inside. He knew. And he could not prove it.

Irabeth stepped forward and put a hand on his shoulder.

"Master Thane." Her voice was low, and it was not unkind, and that was almost the worst of it. "I know you're angry. I know you've a right to be — you were attacked, and one of ours nearly died, and no one heard you when you first tried to speak. That was mine, and I own it." Her hand tightened, once. "But you have a footprint and a feeling, and this army marches on Drezen at first light. If I let a knight start accusing the Queen's own chronicler in the war tent on the strength of a sketch, I don't catch a traitor. I breed *strife*. I put suspicion in every heart the night before the worst fight of their lives. I can't afford it. Not tonight." She held his eyes. "Get me more than a footprint, and I will tear this camp apart to the last tent-peg. Tonight, we ready the army."

Thane steamed. It was the Kenabres contract all over again — the whole shape of it, the exact same shape, a truth he could see and touch and taste and *not lay down where another person had to believe it*. He'd spent three years in the gutters of a city learning to know a false credential at a gate on sight, and it had never once been enough to simply *know*. You had to prove it. And the ones who were good — the ones who were really good — never gave you the proof.

He looked at Nurah. She looked back, small and bright and unmeasured, her feet under her hem.

He took a step back.

"That's enough for tonight," Irabeth said, into the silence, and there was gratitude in it and steel under the gratitude. "Ready your people. We'll speak again — perhaps tomorrow, when there's a tomorrow to speak in." She began, gently, to empty her tent.

Thane went. He did not sheathe the certainty. He never sheathed a thing while it was still wet. But he stepped back and let the door stay shut, because a good commander had asked him to and the army had a wall to climb in the morning, and there were, he was learning, doors you could only open with a key you did not yet hold.

---

He was near the tent-flap, the last to leave, when Sosiel stopped him.

The young cleric of Shelyn had waited through the whole council in the quiet way he had, and now he stepped forward as the others filed out and drew a satchel from under his arm. It was sealed — Thane's eye went to the seal at once, unbroken, an official wax stamp pressed clean.

"The Queen bade me carry this," Sosiel said, "and not open it, and not speak of it, until we reached Drezen. She is not a trusting woman — she suspected there might be an infiltrator among us, and she was, it seems, not wrong." His open young face was grave. "I've carried it the whole way and I do not know what it holds. As you can see, it's still sealed. I hope it helps you." He held it out — not to Thane, but past him, to Caleth, who had turned back at the door.

Caleth took it. Sosiel bowed, slightly, to all of them, and left.

Caleth broke the seal.

He drew out a folded parchment first — unfolded it, and Thane saw the shape of it past his elbow: a map. Another map of the Citadel. But *dense*, this one, covered edge to edge in a small tight hand, annotations crowding every wall and gate and chamber, a map that had been *lived* rather than surveyed.

And as Caleth unfolded it, a second paper slipped loose and fell.

Thane bent and caught it off the tent floor before it landed. He straightened, opened it, read the first line — and stopped.

He read it again. Slower. Aloud, because by the second line he knew the others had to hear it.

> *To Her Majesty, Queen Galfrey of Mendev,*
>
> *Five years have passed since the disgraceful loss of Drezen and the deaths of so many of our kin. Though the citadel remains in the grasp of the demonic horde, we have not forgotten our duty.*
>
> *By gathering the testimony of every surviving dwarf we could find — soldiers, engineers, stonemasons, and laborers alike — we have painstakingly reconstructed these plans. Before you lies the most complete replica of Drezen's blueprints that can now be made.*
>
> *Its walls and battlements. Its gates and defenses. Its barracks, forges, and storerooms. Even those hidden passages and secret chambers known only to trusted officers and master builders.*
>
> *No copy can perfectly replace the original records lost during the fall, yet we believe this work to be accurate enough to serve those who would one day reclaim the fortress.*
>
> *May these plans aid in the liberation of Drezen and the restoration of dwarven honor. Let the demons learn that stone remembers, and so do dwarves.*
>
> *Your humble servants,*
>
> *Thorek Stonevein*
> *Borin Stonevein*
> *Dagna Stonevein*
> *Helja Stonevein*
> *Durgrim Ironfist*
> *Kazra Deepdelver*
> *Harbek Granitehand*
> *Orsik Hammerfall*
> *Vondal Emberforge*
> *Rurik Blackmantle*
> *Gimra Coppervein*
> *Tordek Runecarver*

Thane's voice stopped on the first name.

*Thorek Stonevein.*

He looked at it. At the ink. At the shape of the letters, the particular slant of the down-strokes, the way the *k* at the end hooked back the way it always — the way it—

He knew that hand.

He had a scrap of it at home, in Kenabres, in a box he did not open often: a few lines his father had left, the only writing of Thorek's the family still had, worn soft from being held. And here it was again, on a seventy-year-old letter to a queen, in ink that had dried before Thane was born. *Thorek Stonevein.* His father. Signed by his own hand, each name on the list set down by the individual who owned it, twelve dwarves each in their own script — and the first of them was his father's, and it was his father's hand, and Thane's breath went out of him and did not come back for a moment.

And then his eye went down the column, and the letter was not done with him. *Borin Stonevein.* Korroc's father. *Dagna Stonevein.* Korroc's mother. *Helja Stonevein* — his own mother's careful upright hand. The first four names on the page were Stoneveins. Both his parents. Both of Korroc's. The whole near line of their family, set down together seventy years ago under a promise that stone remembers.

"Seventy years ago," he whispered.

Korroc had come to his shoulder and was reading down the list, and Thane heard the dwarf's breathing change when it reached the second name, and the third. Then the big hand settled on him — his cousin's hand, broad and scarred and warm, closing on his shoulder the way it had a hundred times. Not only giving, this time. Holding on to something too.

"Almost twenty years before we were born," Korroc said, low. And then, quieter still: "This is why the Queen looked at us the way she did, when we first met her. She knew our parents." A pause. "All four of them."

Thane could not answer for a moment.

He knew what his father had been — a Skyguard of Khar-Zadûn, a dreamer of the sky-city, a man who fell in the final push of the Fourth Crusade and whose body was never brought home, only his sky-steel badge. He knew the shape of the grief. He had carried it his whole life, banked and careful, a fire he fed one demon at a time. He thought he had known all the pieces of it.

He had not known this piece.

After the city fell — after Drezen was lost and his father's generation was broken on its walls — Thorek Stonevein had not gone quietly home to grieve. He and eleven others — and the nearest of those eleven were his own kin: his wife, his brother, his brother's wife — had gone out into the wreck of a crusade and gathered the testimony of every surviving dwarf they could find, soldier and mason and laborer, and pieced together from all those broken memories the bones of the fortress that had killed their friends — its walls, its gates, its hidden ways known only to trusted men — and set it down whole, and sent it to a queen with a note that said *stone remembers, and so do dwarves*, so that someone, someday, might use it to take the city back.

And the Queen had kept it. Seventy years. Through the loss of the wardstones and the long slow bleeding of Mendev, she had kept a dead dwarf's blueprints sealed and waiting for the hand they were meant for.

And she had put them in the hands of Thorek Stonevein's son, on the eve of the assault on Drezen.

Thane stood in the tent with the letter in both hands and felt his father reach across seventy years and put a key in his palm. That was what it was. His whole life he had carried the death — the badge, the empty grave, the dream deferred — as a weight, as a debt, as fuel for the flame. And here, unlooked-for, was the other side of it: not a weight handed down but a *tool* handed forward, his father's mind and his father's love and his father's stubborn dwarven refusal to let a thing be truly lost, reaching out of the dark of seventy years to arm a son not yet born for the fight of his life. *Let the demons learn that stone remembers.* His father had believed it enough to spend his grief on making this. And now the son would spend it too.

The banked flame in Thane's chest did not roar. It *steadied* — the way it had steadied in the ritual chamber under Kenabres, the day he first felt his father's death had not been only a holding action. His father had died fighting Baphomet's chain. And his father had also, it turned out, spent his last strength building the door his son would walk through to break another link of it. The grief and the gift were the same thing. They always had been. He had only ever seen the one face of it.

He looked down at the map — the dense, living map, every wall annotated in a dozen dwarven hands. Twelve dwarves' memory of a fortress, offered up to whoever would carry it home.

"They gave us the way in," he said, and his voice was rough, and he let it be.

Aravashnial had come to stand near them, and he spoke gently, for the elf.

"Sosiel carried it the whole way, under orders, not knowing what it was. Even he could have been the leak, and the Queen made sure he could betray nothing he didn't know he held." The elf looked at each of them in turn. "She has put her full trust in you. All of you. This map is the assault." His voice sharpened. "Which means the traitor cannot know it exists. If Nurah — if *whoever* it is — learns of this, learns what it is, and it is taken or copied or destroyed, then the whole plan dies with it and a great many of our people die on that wall for nothing. Guard it as you guard your own throats." And with that he inclined his head, and left the tent to see to his own preparations.

Caleth folded the dense map along its creases, careful, and slid it deep into the pages of his spellbook, and closed the book on it.

"People don't lightly meddle with a wizard's spellbook," he said, with the ghost of a smile. "Least of all one that's killed as many things as this one has."

And that, small and dry and exactly the right size, broke the weight of the moment enough to let them breathe. Korroc huffed a laugh. Even Thane felt the corner of his mouth move. He folded his father's letter, once, along its old crease — a thing you do not fold twice — and tucked it away against his chest, beside the soft old S. V. letter that had been riding there for weeks. Two letters now, against his heart. One from an enemy. One from his father. And both of them, in the end, pointing north to the same city.

---

The camp had settled while they were in the tent.

They came out into a field of low fires and the particular hum of an army the night before a battle — not fear, not exactly, and not quite courage either, but the taut waking hum of two hundred people who have decided a thing and are now only waiting for the dawn to do it. Thane read the camp as he walked and found it, for once, about what it seemed. Men sharpening. Men praying. Men eating because it might be the last hot meal for a while. And moving among them, small and bright, Nurah Dendiwhar — telling a story to a knot of soldiers by a fire, her hands sketching the air, the men laughing at something she'd said.

Thane watched her work the fire and did not let his face change. *Chronicler.* A keeper of records, moving through the camp, marked by no one, welcome anywhere. He filed it. He'd been filing her all evening.

Anevia found them before they'd gone ten steps.

She came out of the dark with a scout's silence, fell in beside Thane, and looked him over the way she always did — a quick professional inventory, checking her partner was whole. "Plans for tomorrow?"

"Killing," Thane said.

Her eyebrows went up.

"Demons," he added, and let the smallest dry thing into it, and Anevia's mouth twitched.

"Just checking." She matched his pace. "You've been in a mood lately."

"Someone's trying to kill us and I can't find them. It sours a week." He turned, then, half a thought becoming a whole one, and drew Aravashnial's page from where he'd tucked it. "Here. Do me a favor. Put your foot up."

Anevia rolled her eyes at him — the full weary length of the roll, a partner who had done a hundred stranger things at his asking — and lifted her boot obligingly. Thane crouched and set the sketch beside it and compared, and it was half a joke and only half. Her foot was too large. Well larger than the print. He'd known it would be — Anevia was no gnome-small saboteur, and he'd trust her at his back in the dark before he trusted his own shadow — but he was a man who did not leave a door unchecked simply because he was sure of what was behind it, and checking her, ruling her out clean and certain, was its own small mercy in a camp where he could rule out no one else.

He grinned up at her and nodded, and put the page away. "Larger. You're clear."

"I'm *honored*," Anevia said, dust dry, and put her foot down.

Caleth stretched, and looked at Thane with a paladin's plain firmness. "We rest tonight. All of us." His eye stayed on Thane a beat longer than the others. "Not walking the column. Not questioning knights. Not doing *things*. Tomorrow's long, and we go into it whole or we don't come out." He raised a hand, and a cool current moved through the four of them — the wizard's gift, a grace laid over them that Thane felt settle into his bones like the first ease of lying down. "There. You'll rest and wake refreshed, all of you. But you'll rest *watching*." A thin smile. "Sleep is a luxury for camps without traitors. We'll take the refreshment and keep the eyes."

It was a strange gift, and a good one. Rest without sleep. His body would mend and his mind would clear as though he'd slept a full night, and yet he would lie awake and alert through the whole of the dark, watching, listening, a knife that never quite closed its eyes. On the night before Drezen, with a saboteur in the camp he could not name and could not prove, Thane could have asked for nothing he wanted more.

They went to their tents. And Thane lay down in the dark near the picket line, whole and healing and wide awake, his father's letter over his heart, and he watched.

---

It was Thane who heard it.

Deep in the night, in the small hours when even a waking camp goes quiet — a movement, close, a whisper of cloth and a shift of weight where there should have been nothing. His hand was on his father's knife and his body was turning toward it before his mind had finished waking, the old readiness, the reflex a hundred locked rooms had built into him—

—and then a voice, very low, at his ear.

"Easy." Anevia. He knew her the way you know a partner in the dark, by the shape of the silence she made. He let the knife-hand ease.

"You may be right." The barest breath of sound, meant for no ear but his. "Something might be strange with Nurah." A pause; he felt rather than saw her glance across the dark camp toward a certain low fire. "I've no proof. Nothing I could put on a table any more than you could. But maybe tomorrow." A last whisper, closer, and then already withdrawing: "Watch your back."

And she was gone — the sense of her presence simply absent, the way a good scout leaves, no cloth-rustle, no shift of weight, nothing for even Thane to read but the shape of where she'd been.

He lay still in the dark and looked up at the dead unmoving sky.

Tomorrow they would go up a riverbed under the walls of a city held by demons for seventy-five years, with his father's map folded in a wizard's spellbook and a traitor moving somewhere among them, small and bright and unproven. He did not have the key yet. He could feel the shape of the lock — could feel it the way he felt every lock, patient, waiting, telling him the truth if only he could get his fingers to the right pin — but the key was still in the dark.

*Patience is the third dagger,* his mother's voice said, from somewhere far back and long ago. *The other two get noticed. The third one wins.*

He would be patient, then. He would be a knife for a long time yet, if that was what it took — through the wall, through the city, through the citadel his father had drawn a door into with the last of his strength. Somewhere ahead, past all of it, there was a name he already carried against his heart and a name he could not yet prove, and he meant to be there when both of them were finally read.

Thane closed his hand on his father's letter, and watched the dark, and waited for the dawn to open.
