# Where the Daggers Sing
*Chapter Three — The Knife in the Dark*

The tunnels narrowed.

Thane saw it before the others did — the way the stone shouldered in on itself, the way the marks on the walls changed from the long parallel scrapes of bulk-cargo passage to the short angled scuffs of single-file traffic. Somebody used this corridor. Somebody used it carefully. A wide tunnel meant trade or ceremony or supply lines; a narrow one meant we don't want you bringing many friends.

His father would have read the same signs. His father had taught him to read them, in the brief years before the Worldwound had taken him.

Look at the floor first, lad. Walls lie. Floors don't.

Thane looked at the floor.

The floor said patrols.

Korroc's voice, behind him, broke his concentration. "You going to sneak out in front and scout?"

Thane snorted. "They can hear your armor clanking from a mile away, cousin. What's the point?"

"Aye." Korroc shrugged inside his plate, the joints creaking. "I'll lead, then. Any objection?"

There was none. Of course there was none. Korroc was a wall of dwarven iron with a hammer and a healing spell on his lips, and Thane was — Thane was the knife you used after the wall had absorbed the first blow. That was how it worked. That was how it had always worked.

It hadn't always sat comfortably in his chest, that arrangement. Thorek Stonevein's son had been raised on stories of the Skyguard of Khar-Zadûn — dwarves who walked the cloud-towers, dwarves who stood at the front, dwarves whose father had stood at the front of the front and not come back from it. Thane had spent half his boyhood resenting the lesson that some men were meant to stand in shield-walls and others were meant to slip in through the cracks. He had wanted to be a wall.

He had been told, gently, by an old Mendevian sergeant whose name he could no longer remember, that he was not built for it. You're quick, lad. You see the cracks. The Worldwound needs men who see the cracks. The walls we have. The walls we lose. The men who slip in after the walls fall — those we never have enough of.

Thane had hated that, at the time.

He understood it now.

He fell in behind Korroc as the dwarf-paladin began to walk, his shield catching what little light Caleth's cantrip threw. Thane's hand drifted, without thought, to the daggers at his belt — the right-hand blade, the one his mother had pressed into his palm the day she had finally stopped trying to make him a different sort of dwarf. If you're going to be a knife, son, be a true one. The weight of it was a comfort he had grown so used to he no longer noticed it, the way a man stops noticing his own breath until he is robbed of it.

Behind him, Caleth and Nageru and Anevia followed. Behind them, Aravashniel and Horgus brought up the rear, with Aravashniel leaning on his improvised staff and Horgus muttering complaints into his beard.

Anevia was walking on her own, now. The mongrel village's healers had done what they could — set the bone proper, packed it with poultices Thane did not know the names of, fitted her a real crutch carved from cave-pine — and Anevia, being Anevia, had insisted on testing the leg every chance she got. She caught him glancing back at her. Her mouth quirked.

"Eyes forward, dwarf. I'll keep up."

"Wasn't worried."

"Liar."

He grinned, just a little, and turned back to the tunnel.

He was worried, in fact, though not the way she thought. Not about her keeping up. About something else — a thing he had been chewing on since Nathholm, since Caleth had laid out the bull-headed symbol on Sull's table and named the Templars by name. Templars of the Ivory Labyrinth. Thane had heard the name in a back room three months ago, in a quiet meeting with two grim-faced women who had pressed coin into his hand and asked him to listen. To listen for it in taverns, in marketplaces, in the camps. To listen for it in the way certain crusader companies had begun to behave. To listen for it in the way certain priests had begun to phrase certain blessings.

He had been hired, in plain terms, to find evidence of the very rot that was now lying dead in caverns under the city.

The women had not given him their names. They had given him a sigil to recognize their courier by, and a place in the city to leave reports if he found anything. They had only said, when pressed, that they answered to the Watch, in the way a man answers to a hand he respects without quite belonging to it.

Thane had taken the work for two reasons. The first was money, and the money had been good. The second was older.

The second was that demon-cultists had killed his father.

Not directly. Not by name. But the logic of the Worldwound was a single chain — every cultist on the surface of Golarion was a knot in the rope that the demons used to pull more men into the dark, and every man pulled into the dark was another shovel in the grave they were digging for everyone Thane had ever loved. Thorek Stonevein had died in the Fourth Crusade pushing back against that grave. He had died with his face to it, and his body had never come home, only the sky-steel insignia of a Skyguard who had once dreamed of walking again the cloud-piercing towers of Khar-Zadûn.

Thane had been a child.

Thane was not a child anymore.

If there were Templars of the Ivory Labyrinth in his city — in his city, the city his father had bled for the protection of — then Thane had a debt, and Thane meant to pay it in the only currency such men understood. He meant to make sure the report he had been hired to write was a thorough one. And after the report was written and delivered, he meant to keep paying.

He had not told the others any of this.

He had not seen the need.

The bend in the tunnel came faster than he expected.

It was Nageru who caught it first. The aasimar's hand came up and settled on Korroc's pauldron with the same soft authority Thane had seen him use on terrified survivors and on furious Horgus alike — a hand that said be still, friend, without any need for words.

"Wait," Nageru murmured. "I hear something ahead. Sounds like animals."

Korroc paused. He cocked his head. He listened. Thane saw the dwarf's shoulders lift in the familiar shrug that meant I hear nothing, but I trust you.

"Let's see what it is."

Korroc rounded the corner.

Thane was a half-step behind him before he had decided to be — old training, older than the Watch contract, older even than his mother's daggers. Where the wall goes, the knife follows. He came around the corner with his shortbow already in his hand, an arrow already nocked, and his eyes already cataloguing the room beyond.

A barricade. Two mongrelfolk crouched behind it, one of them already drawing breath to shout — a sentry's shout, Thane's mind supplied, the sound an alarm makes a half-second before it becomes everyone's problem. Behind the barricade, a hanging hide-screen concealed whatever lay deeper in. Thane's eye picked out the structure of the position in a single sweep: cover, fallback, alarm, screen for visual concealment. They'd done this before. They'd held this point before.

These weren't Lann's people. These weren't Sull's tribe. These were the other.

These were the Vescavor.

Korroc roared and charged, hammer up and over and down, and the barricade exploded into splinters under the weight of dwarven plate and dwarven fury. Thane raised his shortbow and loosed —

— and missed. The arrow skittered off stone behind a Vescavor's shoulder. Bow's wrong tool, Thane thought, and was already dropping the bow and reaching for his daggers as the two mongrelfolk surged from behind the broken barricade.

Their blades came up. Their blades missed.

Both of them missed, on their first lunges — Thane saw it as he might have seen a fight in slow water, his trained eye reading the wrongness in their footwork, the way they over-committed, the way they drew their weapons back too far before the strike. Untrained. Or trained for ambush, not for stand-up fighting. They expected us to die at the sentry call. They didn't have a real plan for what came after.

Two more came out from behind the screen. One had a shortbow. The bow sang and the arrow took Nageru in the shoulder, the shaft punching through cloth and finding the meat beneath. Nageru did not flinch. The aasimar's face went quiet, in the way Thane had come to recognize meant he had decided to make whoever did that very sorry, and his fists came down on the mongrel in front of Thane in a single descending blur.

The mongrel crumpled.

Thane drew his daggers properly — the right blade, his mother's blade, leading; the left blade, his father's wedding-knife, reversed in his off-hand for parries — and moved. Three years of Mendev camp drills, two years of working the back alleys of Kenabres, all of it folding down into a single tight pivot on the ball of the foot. He stabbed. The blade went in clean under a Vescavor's collarbone. The blade came out clean. That's one.

Korroc swung at the second mongrel and the dwarf-mongrel — quick on his feet, that one — danced backward out of reach. An arrow from the bowman in the back hit Korroc's pauldron and bounced.

Nageru took two long strides and his fist came down on a third mongrel and that one crumpled too.

Thane saw his moment. He vaulted the broken barricade — the splinters Korroc had left crunching under his boots — and closed on the bowman at the back. The bowman tried to draw a blade. The bowman was too slow. Thane's right dagger caught him under the arm and Thane's left dagger caught his wrist and the bow fell. Blood flowered. Thane stepped back and let him drop.

The last mongrel — the one dancing back from Korroc — took an arrow through the side of the throat from somewhere behind Thane and dropped, clutching at the shaft.

Thane turned.

Anevia was still on her feet, leaning hard on her crutch, the shortbow she had unslung from somewhere held at the ready, her face white with the strain of standing on a leg that had no business holding her weight. The bow was empty. Her hand was still poised at the string.

Thane gave her a single slow nod.

She returned it.

They had been working together for the Watch's contract for some weeks now, Anevia handling the surface-level inquiries among the soldiery and Thane handling the gutters, and they had developed the easy professional rhythm of two people who did not need to talk much to know what the other was about to do.

He walked back to her, careful not to make a fuss of it, and offered her his free hand to lean on.

"Nice shot."

"Lucky shot."

"Best kind."

She took the offered hand, leaned, breathed out hard. "Don't tell Korroc I'm tired."

"Wouldn't dare."

He moved to the screen.

It was hide — some kind of tanned cave-lizard skin, by the smell of it, which was not a pleasant smell — and it hung loose enough that he could slip his fingers around the edge and ease it aside without making it sway. He looked through the gap.

A larger chamber. Sleeping spots, ten of them, clustered around a cold campfire. Furs. Three doorways, each curtained or shut. One of the doors stood open the smallest crack — perhaps a finger's width — with darkness behind it.

Empty of people.

For now.

Thane slipped through the screen on the balls of his feet, daggers still in his hands. He drifted to the center of the room, breathing through his mouth — the place stank of unwashed bodies and old grease — and listened.

Nothing.

He motioned the others through.

They came in one by one. Korroc last, his armor a small thunder. Caleth's light spread across the room, picking out the texture of furs and ash. Thane crossed to the door that stood cracked open — slow, slow, slow, the way his mother had taught him to cross a room that might be watching back — and reached for the latch.

The door exploded outward.

He had a single half-instant to register the shape — vast, lashing, jawed, a great lizard the size of a small horse with bone plates running down its spine and a tongue forked and black — and then it was on him, jaws wide, and his left dagger came up across his face on pure reflex and his right dagger came down and the lizard's teeth closed around his thigh.

The pain was immediate and enormous.

Thane heard himself shout. His thigh felt as if it had been crushed — the lizard's bite-pressure was beyond anything a dwarf's leg was built to absorb — and he could feel teeth going through cloth and skin and meat and grinding against bone. His knees almost went. He stayed up by sheer dwarven stubbornness and the wall behind him, and his right dagger came down in a desperate hammering motion on the lizard's snout. Stab. Stab. Stab. His blade was good steel and the lizard's hide was poor armor, and the lizard's pale blood ran out around his blade, but the jaws did not let go.

Nageru was there in a single bound. The aasimar's fist came down on the lizard's flank with a sound like a sack of wet meat dropped on stone. The lizard spasmed but did not release.

Korroc was beside him in plate — when had Korroc gotten there, how was Korroc there so fast, the dwarf had been across the room — and Korroc's hammer was up and Korroc's hammer was down and the impact rang through Thane's leg, through Thane's teeth, through the very stone he was pressed against.

The lizard bit harder.

Thane felt something in his thigh give.

He felt the tearing.

He felt his vision tilt sideways.

He felt, distantly, the strange floating sensation of a man whose body has decided to opt out of the next several seconds, and his knees buckled at last, and he was on his back on the cold stone of the chamber floor, and the lizard was still on his leg, and he could not feel his leg at all anymore, and the ceiling was very far away, and somewhere very far above him Caleth was bringing the leaf-blade of a glaive down on a lizard's skull in a thrust that he could not see but could only hear —

Crack.

The pressure on his leg stopped.

A wash of golden warmth rolled through him — Korroc, that's Korroc — and his vision steadied, and his leg came back to him in a flood of sensation that was nine-tenths pain and one-tenth profound relief that there was still a leg there to hurt. He blinked up at the cavern roof. He blinked up at Korroc, who had gone to one knee beside him, the dwarf's palm pressed flat against his chest, the second wave of healing pouring through him.

"You with us, cousin?"

Thane considered the question with care. "Hm."

"That a yes?"

"Probably."

Korroc snorted and helped him sit up. The leg hurt. The leg hurt. Thane prodded it gingerly through the torn fabric of his trousers, found bite-marks deep enough to fit a finger in, found Korroc's healing already knitting the deeper damage closed. He'd be limping for a day. He'd be alive for many more.

He stood. He rolled his shoulders. He retrieved the daggers he'd dropped — both still bloody — and wiped them clean on a fur from one of the sleeping spots, because no Stonevein had ever sheathed a wet blade and no son of Thorek's was about to start.

He walked, stiff-legged, to the door the lizard had come from.

It was a pantry. A cold, low-roofed pantry, stacked with strings of meat that Thane's nose informed him were not fit to feed a dog. Cave-rat, possibly. Bat, possibly. Things that had once been smaller things, dried and salted by hands that did not understand salt.

He shut the door.

"Charming people," he said.

Caleth, behind him, smiled the small wry Spireborn smile that Thane had begun to recognize as the half-elf's polite version of I agree but I am too well-bred to say it out loud.

The second door was locked.

Thane crouched in front of it, working through the small mental checklist his mother had drilled into him as a teenager. Listen first. Hinges and bolts speak. Look second. Marks at the keyhole tell you who picks it most often. Touch third. The plate around the keyhole tells you if the lock's been forced before. The door was old and the lock was poor. He could feel it before he even brought out his picks.

He brought out his picks anyway. Caution was a habit, not a calculation.

The pick slid in. The pick turned. The pick met a small dry click and the door swung in.

He looked through.

The room beyond was warmer than the others. A small lamp burned with orange flame on a crude wooden table, and beside the lamp sat a chipped clay cup half full of some dark liquid, and beside the cup sat a small heap of carved bone dice. Someone's been sitting here, Thane thought. Someone got up not long ago.

He raised his eyes to the corner of the room.

A figure was there, hunched against the wall — and had a bow drawn — and Thane's body knew before Thane's mind did, because Thane was already throwing himself sideways when the bowstring sang and the arrow lodged with a meaty thump in the meat of his shoulder.

He grunted. He did not fall. He had been hurt worse in the alley behind the Smoking Boar.

He charged.

The figure was a female mongrel, and more than mongrel — the pale flesh below her chest split into a riot of additional spider-jointed limbs, a half-dozen extra legs sprouting from her hips and ribs, the half-arachnid heritage that some of these poor children of the deep dark carried in their blood. She skittered backward with a speed that was distinctly not the speed of a woman with two legs, and her bow was already nocking another arrow.

Thane closed.

His mother's dagger flashed and bit her in the upper arm. Blood — red blood, normal blood, the blood of a human child of human parents three generations back — welled and dripped. She hissed.

Korroc was at his side. The hammer came down. She bent her body in a way no spine should bend, and the hammer caught her shoulder a glancing blow rather than crushing her skull. Nageru came in from the other side with a fist that connected — Thane heard her ribs creak — and then the spider-girl rolled.

It was a thing Thane would remember.

She did not run. She did not duck. She rolled — every limb tucking in at once, the half-arachnid joints inverting in a way that made his stomach lurch — and she was behind them, across the room, in the corner opposite the one she had started in, all in the time it took Korroc to draw breath.

An arrow sprouted from Korroc's back.

She had reloaded while she rolled.

Caleth's hand came up and shimmered. The half-elf stepped forward and touched her — just laid his open palm against her shoulder almost gently — and the air cracked. Lightning danced from his fingertips and ran through her in a brief blue-white wash, and her eyes went very wide, and Thane saw in that moment that she was not very old. She might have been twenty. She might have been less.

He felt nothing about that. Or rather: he felt the small sad thing he always felt, the thing his mother had taught him to feel and to not let stop his hand. They chose. The deep dark is full of choices. She made hers.

He moved to her to finish it. His blade flashed — and missed, her spider-half snapping her sideways at the last moment.

Korroc bashed her with the hammer. She rolled again, into the room's center this time, and her bow came up again and the arrow took Caleth in the ribs.

Caleth grunted. Caleth did not fall.

Nageru was on her now that she was clear of the corner. Two fists came down. Her face came apart. She crumpled.

Korroc let go a healing wave that washed the room. Thane felt the shaft in his shoulder ease as the wound around it closed, the arrow now an inconvenience rather than a wound. He pulled it out with a grimace. He tossed it aside.

"That one was clever," he said, looking down at her body.

"Aye," Korroc said, quietly. "Clever in the wrong direction, lass."

The third door led down a corridor that smelled even worse than the rest of the den.

Thane had taken three steps into it when his stomach turned. He doubled over, hand against the wall, and emptied what little was in him onto the stone. Korroc was at his side in a heartbeat, hand on his shoulder.

"Lizard poison," the dwarf said, half-to-himself. "Aye, that bite was venomous. I should've known."

"I should've known," Thane gasped.

"Hush." Korroc was already digging in his pack. "Drink this."

A vial. Thick green liquid. The restoration potion they had pulled from the long-dead crusader's pack on the first day, the one Caleth had been hoarding for a true emergency.

"Save it. I'll ride it out."

"You'll ride it out bent over a wall. Drink, cousin."

Thane drank.

The potion went down warm and cold at once, a strange sliding sensation that ran down his throat and spread. The nausea did not vanish — Korroc warned him, briskly, that the potion would dull symptoms but not purge the venom, and that he would feel like death warmed over for the next day regardless — but the worst of the dizziness lifted, and Thane was able to stand straight again without the corridor swaying.

"Thank you, cousin."

"Aye. Don't get bit again."

"Doing my best."

He looked down the corridor. Refuse. A sloping floor. A mound of rotting garbage piled at the far end, dark and wet-looking.

He frowned.

"Caleth," he called over his shoulder.

The half-elf appeared. "Yes?"

"Can I borrow your glaive?"

"...for what?"

"To stab the trash."

There was a pause, and Caleth gave him the look — the gentle, pained, Spireborn-trying-very-hard-to-be-patient look that Thane had begun to enjoy provoking on principle.

"This place is full of stuff," Thane explained. "There might be a trash monster."

"There might be a trash monster."

"Aye."

Caleth, after a long-suffering moment, handed him the glaive.

Thane took it. He walked down the corridor, almost slipped on the grime, kept his feet by virtue of his short dwarven center of gravity, and reached the trash pile. He stabbed it experimentally. He stabbed it again. He withdrew the glaive.

A small glob of oozy ick clung to the leaf-blade.

It moved.

"Cute," Korroc said from behind him, in a tone that suggested he meant the exact opposite.

"I'll call it Squishy."

"I hate you, cousin."

"I love you too."

The ooze started to flow up the glaive.

Thane swung the glaive in a hard arc, trying to slap the ooze off against the wall — no, Korroc shouted, keep it still! — and Thane held still on instinct, and a small jet of fire shot from Korroc's outstretched fingertips and engulfed the ooze in an orange flash.

Nageru was up at the end of the glaive in a heartbeat, fist coming down. The ooze didn't stop. It surged the rest of the way up the haft and onto Thane's hand, tiny pseudopods slamming his wrist with a stinging cold-burn.

"Hold still!" Korroc shouted again, and brought his hammer down — a glancing blow, beautifully judged, the flat of the head striking the ooze and barely brushing Thane's knuckles. Nageru followed the lead with another careful glancing strike. The ooze gave up on cohesion and dropped off Thane's hand to the floor, where it spread out into a sad gray puddle and stopped moving.

Thane stared at his hand. His hand was, blessedly, still attached.

"Squishy is no more."

"Squishy was a stupid name."

"Squishy was a perfect name."

Caleth, retrieving his glaive with a small affronted air, said: "I would prefer if my weapon were not used to stab refuse heaps in future."

"Noted, paladin."

"Thank you."

The next door opened onto a room that immediately went on Thane's mental list of places that had been recently abandoned.

Shelves lined the walls, crowded with preserved small creatures — cave-fish in jars, dried bats hanging in strings, a row of strange yellow eyes floating in a clouded jar that Thane chose, on principle, not to think about. A bedroll lay against the far wall. A second door at the back was just easing shut as Thane stepped in, and he caught the shadow of someone running in the half-second between his arrival and the door's closure.

He did not pursue. Patience, his mother used to say, is the third dagger you carry. The other two get noticed. The third one wins.

Caleth came in behind him and looked around. "Anything?"

"Someone was just here. Ran out as I came in."

"Through that?"

"Aye."

Caleth crossed to the door. Beyond it was a small alcove with a rope ladder rising into a hole in the ceiling.

"Up?" Caleth said.

"Up. But not yet. Let's see what else is here first. I don't like leaving doors at my back."

Caleth nodded and let the door close.

Thane crossed to the door they had not yet tried — the one beside the trash hall — and crouched in front of it. He listened. He looked. He touched. The lock was better than the spider-girl's. Someone had spent coin on this one. He drew his picks.

The first pick snapped.

Thane swore. Loudly. In Dwarven.

Anevia was at his shoulder a moment later. She did not say shh or easy now or any of the patronizing things a partner who didn't know her business might have said. She simply unstrapped a small leather pouch from her belt and handed it to him.

He opened it. Better picks than his. Watch-issue, by the look of them.

"You came down here with a thieves' kit?"

"I came down here for a holy-day festival, dwarf. The kit just happens to live in my belt."

He looked at her for a moment. She looked back, mild-faced and patient.

"Thank you."

"Don't break these."

"Wouldn't dare."

The lock yielded to the second pick on a slow careful turn. Click.

He pushed the door open.

Beyond was a narrow passage that opened almost at once onto another vertical hole — a knotted rope dangling down through it into darkness.

Nageru went down first, because Nageru always went down first. The aasimar dropped lightly hand over hand into the dark, his soft voice floating back up.

"Forty feet... water below me. There's... a rock in the middle. I can drop to it."

A pause. A small splash.

"Fish in the water. Big ones. I'll come back up."

He climbed back up, reported. The group conferred. The rope ladder in the previous room — the one going up — was the better lead. Up was the direction of the surface. Up was the direction of home.

They moved to the ladder.

Nageru went first again, hand over hand, climbing into the dark above. Thane watched him go, and then watched the empty rope twitch as the aasimar paused near the top.

A long pause.

Then a quick scuffle, and Nageru came back down the rope much faster than he had gone up.

"Rats," the aasimar said calmly. "Big ones. Up there. Came at my face."

Thane snorted. "Wonderful."

Nageru looked up at the dark hole. "They are not coming down. That, at least, is something."

"That's much," Caleth said dryly.

Korroc cracked his knuckles. He touched the silver scale at his belt with reverent care, and then he rose — slow, deliberate, a dwarf in full plate ascending into a dark hole as if the laws of the world had simply decided to grant him an exemption today. Shield and hammer in his hands. The scale's gift left a cloudy, silvery trail of mist behind him as he climbed.

Nageru went up next, fast, using the lingering mist of the scale as concealment — Thane saw the cleverness of it, the aasimar's monk training making instant use of the cover Korroc had unintentionally provided. Nageru vaulted over the lip of the hole and Thane heard the wet sound of a fist meeting rat in the dark above.

Thane touched the scale at his own belt and ascended.

He came up into a low chamber that smelled. Dear gods, it smelled. The reek of rat — sour piss and matted fur and old droppings — was so thick he had to breathe through his mouth. Korroc was on his feet, shield raised, a rat already dying at his boots. Nageru was a blur of fists in the gloom. Three rats — no, four, there was another by the wall — the size of small dogs, all of them, with red-bead eyes and yellow fangs.

Thane stepped to the lip and stabbed down — a rat had been about to leap at Korroc's neck. The dagger went in. The rat shrieked. He kicked it off the blade.

Below them, Caleth had started to climb the rope and fallen — Thane heard the half-elf's grunt of surprise from forty feet down — but there was no time for laughing about that. A rat lunged at Korroc's leg and bounced off greaves. Another bit Nageru, and Nageru did not seem to notice, his fists already coming down on a third.

Korroc's hammer fell. A rat squealed and was no longer a rat.

Nageru's fists fell. Another rat was no longer a rat.

Thane stabbed. Another. Another.

By the time Caleth's head finally appeared over the lip of the hole — climbed at his second try, dignified despite the slip — every rat in the room was dead.

"No more?" Caleth said hopefully.

"No more," Thane confirmed.

"Wonderful."

"Mind the smell."

Caleth pulled himself up, took one breath, and his face went exactly the color a half-elf's face should not go. "Saint's mercy."

"Aye."

The second barricade came as no surprise.

Thane had been listening the whole way down the new corridor — listening for the small giveaway sounds of waiting men, the shuffle of feet, the small wet sound of mouths breathing through nose because someone had told them not to talk — and he had heard them three turns before they came into sight. He gave Korroc the small sign for contact ahead, multiple, and Korroc, who had picked up a few of the signs over the years from Thane's mother of all people, grunted and tightened his grip on the hammer.

This time the Vescavor were ready.

Or thought they were.

Thane slid forward and stabbed the first one over the barricade before the man had finished registering that the fight had started — a rear-hand strike, in over the shoulder, blade angled down to find the lung — and the mongrel fell back gurgling. Korroc was right behind him, hammer up, hammer almost down, almost missing as the second mongrel ducked behind the wood. Caleth's glaive swept between them and also missed as the same mongrel danced sideways.

Then the mongrel danced back the other way and his blade caught Caleth's ribs.

Caleth grunted.

Another Vescavor stabbed at Thane and caught him in the shoulder — same shoulder, same place the spider-girl had shot him, the wound reopening with a hot bright flare of pain. Thane snarled and stabbed back and missed. Nageru tumbled through the line of combat with the easy grace of his order, came up behind a Vescavor, and put a fist through the back of the man's skull. The Vescavor folded.

It was ugly close work. The barricade made it worse — too narrow for proper formation, too crowded for the longer weapons. Thane felt himself slipping into the Mendev camp drills his father had paid an old veteran to teach him — short blade in close, find the seams of the leather, don't try to hit the chest, hit the joints — and his daggers were bright and quick and his blood was in his ears, and somewhere distantly he was aware that two Vescavor had managed to flank Nageru and one of them had hit him hard.

A blade found Korroc's armpit. Korroc grunted — a real grunt, the grunt of pain, not effort — and Thane saw blood bloom on the dwarf's surcoat at the underarm.

Thane stabbed the man who had done it. The blade went in. The blade came out. The man fell.

Korroc touched his own chest and golden warmth pulsed through the room. Thane's reopened shoulder eased. Nageru's wounds closed. The dwarf-paladin's reserves of grace were running thinner with every passing hour, and Thane saw it on Korroc's face — the faint tightness around the eyes, the slight slowness of the next swing — but the wave came when it was needed, and the wave was enough.

Two more Vescavor went down to Korroc and Nageru working in tandem.

The chamber went still.

Thane wiped his daggers. He did not enjoy what he had just done — none of these mongrelfolk had been good people, by any reading of their dens or their tactics, but they were also the unwanted children of a hundred years of demonic taint and the indifference of the surface world, and a man with any wit at all could see how a tribe like this came to be — but he had done what needed doing. The Vescavor had thrown in their lot with the Templars, and the Templars served Baphomet, and Baphomet was the same chain that had pulled his father into the dark.

He tightened the chain by every Vescavor he killed.

That was how it worked.

That was how it had to work.

The next door was locked too, and Thane picked it cleanly with Anevia's better picks, and the room beyond was —

Wrong.

He felt it before his eyes confirmed it. A draft of cold, moving air came up from the room beyond — air that should not have been moving, in a sealed cave-system this deep — and Thane's hand stayed on the door for a long second, listening.

Behind him, Caleth said quietly: "Thane. Can you smell it?"

"Aye. Like wet stone."

"Like running water. There's drainage somewhere ahead."

Thane pushed the door fully open.

The chamber beyond was huge. Ten times the size of any room they had been in since the rift, a great vaulted space whose far walls were lost in the lamplight's edge. It had been a cistern, once — Thane could see the old waterworks at the high edges, the channels and the sluice-gates, the bricked-up lower ports where flow had once entered. Old Kenabres, his mind supplied. This was part of the city's sewer system, before the Worldwound, before the wardstone, before everything. They had been climbing closer to the surface than any of them had realized.

But someone had built in the cistern.

Walls of new brick — newer than the cistern's, anyway, though still old by any honest measure — rose from the cistern floor to the cistern ceiling, dividing the central space into a complex of corridors and dead-ends. The brickwork was crude, mortared with hands that did not love their craft, but the arrangement —

The arrangement was a maze.

Thane stood in the doorway and felt the small cold weight of professional dread settle on his chest. Mazes inside enemy territory served exactly one purpose, and that purpose was to separate the intruders. Whoever built this had wanted small groups of attackers to come down those narrow brick corridors one at a time, lost from sight of their fellows, ambushable from any of the cross-passages.

"This is bad," Thane said.

Caleth, behind him, was very quiet for a moment. Then he said, in a voice Thane had not heard from him before: "It's worse than bad."

Thane glanced back. The half-elf's face had gone pale. His hand had drifted to the silver scale at his hip without seeming to know it had moved.

"What is it?"

"The air in there. Can you taste it?"

"I taste damp brick."

"Underneath the damp brick. There's a flavor. Like —" Caleth searched for the word. "Like meat that's been left sitting in the sun. Sweet and wrong."

"Aye. I taste that now you mention it."

"That's an Abyssal stain, Stonevein. There are demons in there. Or there have been, recently, and the air remembers."

Thane's blood went very still.

He looked back into the maze. He thought about turning around. He thought about closing the door and barricading it and telling the others to find another way. But there was no other way — and the surface had to be on the other side. Whatever waited in those brick corridors, it was waiting between him and his city.

He drew his daggers.

"All right, half-elf," he said softly. "Stay close. Stay closer than close. If something comes around a corner I want a glaive past my shoulder before I finish my turn."

"Understood."

"Korroc. Heavy at the back. We'll need your shield to fall back to."

"Aye."

"Nageru. You're our second knife. Anything that gets past me, you finish."

"As you say."

He stepped into the maze.

The first three turns were nothing.

The brick corridor was narrower than Thane would have liked — perhaps four feet across, just enough for a dwarf in plate to pass without scraping shoulders, too narrow for two people to fight side by side. The mortar was crumbling in places. The floor was slick with old runoff. Thane moved on the balls of his feet, his daggers held low and ready, his eyes scanning every junction before he committed to a turn.

Twice he doubled back. The maze had been designed by someone with some small cunning — there were dead-ends meant to draw the eye, false continuations that led nowhere, a single stretch of corridor that circled back to the entrance if you took the wrong fork. Thane caught each trick and adjusted. Father's lessons. Walls lie. Floors don't. The runoff on the floor told him which corridors saw real traffic. He followed the wear-marks.

He was three turns in, four, five — he had stopped counting — when he saw the movement.

Just a flicker. The corner of something pulling back around an intersection ahead, vanishing before he could fix the shape. Maybe twenty feet away. Maybe less.

Thane stopped.

He did not call out. He did not gesture. He simply stopped, and his stillness was its own warning, and he heard behind him the small change in Caleth's breathing that meant the half-elf had read the stillness right. The party halted in the corridor at his back.

Thane waited.

His mother had taught him this, too. Patience is the third dagger. When you knew a thing was coming around a corner, you did not chase it. You let it come to you. Things that were lying in wait did not like being made to wait themselves; they grew impatient; they moved first if you outlasted them.

He waited.

Three breaths. Four. Five.

The thing came around the corner.

It was — flesh. That was the first thing his mind reached for. Flesh in excess, hung in folds and quivering pads of blubber over what looked like dense knotted muscle beneath, the whole frame of it the size of a large man but somehow more than a large man, as if the flesh of two had been packed into the skin of one. Its skin was the color of curdled cream. Its head was a low-set, heavy-jawed thing without much of a face — small eyes, no nose to speak of, a wide grinning mouth full of teeth that did not match each other in size or angle. Long arms ended in claws that had been grown rather than made, hooked black talons that came to wicked points.

It saw him.

It threw back its head and howled.

The howl was wrong. It was wrong in the way the air had been wrong, in the way the smell had been wrong — a wrongness that was not loudness but weight, a sound that pressed on the listening mind and tried to plant something there. Thane felt the cold finger reach for his heart. He felt the small foul suggestion climb the back of his neck: run. run. run. you cannot hurt this thing. you will die here. run.

Thane bared his teeth. Aye, you'd like that, wouldn't you.

His father had not run from the abyss. His son was not about to start.

He shrugged the fear off — physically, his shoulders rolling once — and the thing seemed almost surprised, its small eyes narrowing.

Then it charged.

It moved fast. Far faster than that quivering bulk had any right to move. One moment it was twenty feet down the corridor, the next it was on him, claws coming around in a scything double-strike. Thane got his left dagger up to parry and his right dagger up to strike and his parry was too late. The talons raked down his forearm and across his ribs in a single hot bright slash that opened him from elbow to hip.

He stabbed back. His right blade went into the thing's chest — solidly, properly, a strike that should have sunk to the hilt and pierced its heart.

The blade went in two inches. And stopped.

Thane had a half-instant to register the sensation — the wrongness of meeting flesh that did not yield as flesh ought, of having his mother's good steel turned aside by something that should have parted before it — and then Nageru was past him, the aasimar slipping in along the wall in the cramped corridor, fists already moving.

Nageru struck twice. Hard. Each blow would have dropped a strong man. The thing rocked and grinned and did not stop.

A second creature came around the corner behind the first.

Identical. Same curdled flesh. Same wrong-weight howl. Same hooked claws. It came at Nageru's flank and raked him with one set of talons and bit him with that mismatched mouth, teeth closing around the meat of Nageru's shoulder. Nageru grunted — the first sound of pain Thane had heard from him all day — and his blood ran red and bright down the white wraps of his tunic.

Thane stabbed his creature again. Twice. Both blows went in and pulled out as if he had stabbed thick rubber. The wounds closed around his blade as he withdrew it. He stared.

"Caleth!" Thane shouted over his shoulder. "What ARE these?"

Caleth's voice came back from behind him, tight and grim and certain — the voice of a Spireborn who had finally placed the smell of something he had been schooled to recognize.

"Dretches! Lesser demons! Steel barely touches them, Thane — they need holy or magic to die!"

Thane swore. In Dwarven. Loudly.

Korroc's hammer came down from behind him — the dwarf had pushed up the corridor, shouldering past Caleth — and that should have ended it; a holy paladin's warhammer driven by a dwarf's full weight should have caved the dretch's skull. The blow landed. The dretch staggered. The dretch did not fall.

Nageru's free hand moved to the silver scale at his belt.

Thane saw the gesture and understood it before the aasimar had completed it. Terendelev's gift. The dragon's grace. Each scale held a single charge of something — Korroc's had been levitation, Caleth's had been — well, Caleth was hoarding his still — and Nageru's was —

A soft silver light flared from Nageru's belt and ran up his arms to his fists, and the light settled there, a faint pearl-colored sheen along his knuckles, and Thane understood. Divine power. The dragon's holy grace, channeled through the aasimar's strikes. Now his fists could hurt these things.

The dretch on Nageru raked him again, both sets of talons, four parallel bleeding lines opening across his chest. Nageru's knees almost buckled. He did not buckle. He stayed up by some celestial discipline Thane did not have a name for, and his next strike came up and around in a short tight uppercut, and the dretch's head snapped back, and Thane saw the silver fire of the strike sink into the wound in a way that Thane's steel had not.

The dretch recoiled. Smoke rose from the wound. It did not close.

"Now we're getting somewhere," Korroc growled.

Thane stabbed his dretch in the throat. The blade went in. The wound closed. Worthless. Worthless. He swore and pulled back, stepping aside to let Caleth's glaive thrust through over his shoulder — and Caleth's strike missed, the half-elf's leaf-blade scraping the brick instead of the demon, and Thane heard Caleth swear in a soft shocked voice that was the closest the half-elf had come all day to losing his composure.

Korroc reached past Thane's shoulder and touched Nageru's back. Golden warmth poured out of the dwarf's palm into the aasimar's wounds. The deepest of the rakes closed. Nageru breathed out. He brought his fists down again and the demon's grin twisted into a thing that was almost a wince and his fists came down a second time, the silver fire flaring brighter, and the dretch's chest caved, and the demon collapsed into a heap of curdled flesh that began at once to steam and to come apart.

The other dretch — Thane's dretch, the one that had been clawing him and that Thane could not touch — saw its companion fall and focused.

It came around at Nageru.

Both sets of claws, in a rhythm Thane realized was deliberate — high-low, high-low, like a boxer working an opponent's guard apart — and then the wide grinning mouth coming in for the bite. Nageru took the first set of claws across the shoulder. Took the second set of claws across the thigh. Took the bite on the meat of his upper arm.

His knees did buckle this time. Just a little. Just for a moment.

Caleth was on him in a heartbeat. The half-elf's hand came up to Nageru's chest, glowing soft and gold — lay on hands, the half-elf spending his own paladin's reserves on the man taking the fight none of them could otherwise carry — and the worst of the wounds eased.

Korroc's hammer came down on the dretch from behind. Solid hit. The thing staggered. Nageru struck once — connected — and then struck again — and the dretch dodged, its wide grinning head twitching aside on a neck that bent in too many directions, and Nageru's second blow whistled through empty air.

Caleth healed him again.

The dretch bit hard into Nageru's arm and Thane heard the crunch of teeth on bone and Nageru's blood ran in a sudden hot torrent down to his elbow, and Korroc was already reaching, golden palm coming up, the dwarf's healing pouring through the aasimar's wounds like water poured through a sieve — the dwarf was nearly dry, Thane realized with a small horrified jolt, he was burning his last reserves to keep Nageru on his feet because Nageru is the only one of us who can hurt this thing —

Nageru's fists flashed.

One. Two. Three. The silver fire on his knuckles burned a little brighter with each strike, as if the dragon-grace was answering the desperation of the moment, as if Terendelev herself was leaning in through the gift she had left behind.

The dretch staggered. Smoke rose from three new wounds. It grinned still, the wide wrong grin of a demon that did not understand fear, but its grin was a slower thing now and its claws came up a half-beat too late.

Nageru struck a fourth time.

The dretch dropped to the brick floor, twitching. The grin went slack. The flesh began to come apart in the same wrong steaming way the first had.

The maze was silent.

Thane stood in the corridor with both daggers out and his arm bleeding and his side bleeding and a small unfamiliar feeling sitting under his ribs that took him a moment to identify.

It was fear.

Not fear of the demons themselves — that, he had shrugged off when the howl had tried to plant it, and he had not let it come back. This was a different fear. Fear of his own irrelevance. Fear of what it had been like to stab a thing with his mother's good steel and have the steel turn aside as if the steel were nothing. Fear of a fight in which Thane Stonevein had not, in any meaningful sense, been able to fight.

He had thrown his body in. He had taken the wounds. He had drawn the demon's attention while Nageru circled. Useful, he told himself. Useful work. The work of a knife is not always to kill. Sometimes the work of a knife is to be the thing the enemy is looking at while the hammer comes around.

He told himself this. He believed it. He still felt the small cold thing under his ribs.

Caleth was beside him, hand on his shoulder, the half-elf's healing flowing into the rake-wounds along his ribs. The bleeding stopped. The flesh closed. Thane let out a breath he had not known he was holding.

"Thank you, paladin."

"You did well."

"I did nothing."

"You stood. With these things, on a day this long, with the dwarf nearly dry and the aasimar nearly down — standing was much."

Thane looked at him. The half-elf's face was calm. The half-elf meant it. Caleth had his own private griefs about the moment, Thane suspected — the spell-misses, the missed glaive thrust — but the half-elf was, in the very particular Spireborn way of his, refusing to let either of them sit in shame about a fight that had simply been bad odds against the wrong opponent.

Thane huffed a short breath. "Aye. Thank you."

"Always."

Korroc walked past them to the dissolving heap of the second dretch. The dwarf-paladin spat on the floor beside it — not a great spit, a small efficient working spit, the spit of a dwarf clearing his mouth of bad-tasting work — and looked back at Thane.

"Demons in the cistern, cousin."

"Aye."

"In our sewers."

"Aye."

"Templars feeding them. Templars summoning them, like as not. The ritual chamber we just passed wasn't for show."

"No, it wasn't."

Korroc shook his head once, slowly. "Kenabres has a bigger problem than any of us thought."

"Aye, cousin."

"And we're going to be the ones who tell them."

"Aye, cousin. We are."

Nageru came up beside them, his white wraps scarlet now from collar to elbow, his face still calm in that infuriating way. He laid a hand briefly on Korroc's shoulder.

"You spent much for me, brother."

"Aye. You earned it."

"My thanks."

"Don't get raked again."

"Doing my best."

A small laugh, half-spent, ran through the four of them. The kind of laugh four men share after a fight that nearly went the wrong way. The kind of laugh that doesn't do anything except put the floor back under your feet.

They moved on through the maze.

Thane took point again, because that was what he did. The bleeding under his chain shirt was a low steady ache rather than a danger now, but every step pulled at the half-closed wounds, and every breath of the wrong-air in the maze reminded him what they had just put down. He kept his daggers out. He doubted very much that they would help against the next thing, if there was a next thing. But the daggers in his hands felt better than the daggers at his belt, and feelings counted for something even when they did not count for everything.

The maze unwound itself slowly. Three more turns. Five more. A long stretch of corridor that ran straight for sixty feet without a junction. At its end, a brick archway, and beyond the archway a passage of older masonry — proper Kenabres city-stone, cut and laid by surface-dwarves a hundred years before the wardstone was ever set in the Kite.

Thane stopped at the archway.

He looked down the long, long hallway beyond.

Old. Old. Cut by hands that had assumed they would always have a sky over them. The walls were stained with the long slow tide-marks of a hundred years of seasonal flow. The air that came down it carried, faintly — faintly, the way a half-remembered song carries on a wind from an open window — the smell of outside.

Of city.

Of woodsmoke and wet stone and far-off cooking and the green wet mineral tang of a river running between bridges.

Of Kenabres.

Thane stood in the archway with his bloody daggers in his hands and breathed in the smell of his city for the first time in two days, and his throat did something complicated, and his eyes did something complicated, and the small unfamiliar thing that had been sitting under his ribs since the dretch had turned his blade — that eased, a little.

Because his blade had not turned for nothing.

His blade had drawn the demon's attention while the hammer came around. His blade had let the holy fists do the work that needed doing. And that work had been done in a maze in a sewer beneath a city that was, somehow, still standing, still breathing the air it had breathed before the rift opened, still waiting for the men and women under it to come home.

Thane turned to the others.

"Sewers," he said, his voice steady. "Old ones. City ones. Ours."

Caleth came up beside him in the archway. The half-elf breathed in, breathed out, and Thane saw a small light come into the Spireborn's eyes that he hadn't seen since the morning of Armasse.

"That's Kenabres, Stonevein. That's home."

"Aye, half-elf. That's home."

Korroc's hand came up and clapped Thane's shoulder hard enough to rock him a half-step forward.

"And cousin?"

"Aye?"

"I'm out of spells."

Thane looked at him. Looked at the lines of exhaustion under the dwarf's eyes. Looked at the wound at the armpit that had finally stopped bleeding only because Korroc had spent the last of his grace on it, and not because it was healed. Looked at the careful way Korroc was holding his shield arm, favoring it.

"Aye, cousin. I see it. We'll find a hole to rest in."

They went back the way they had come.

The decision was made in the archway and confirmed in the corridor and not really debated, because there was nothing to debate. The surface was close — they could smell it now, the wet-stone and woodsmoke breath of Kenabres coming down the old sewer hall — but close was not the same as clear.

They re-traced the corridors. They came back, eventually, to the rat-room.

Korroc was already at the rope ladder. He hauled it up hand over hand and let it coil onto the floor of the chamber, putting a vertical drop's worth of dead air between them and the den below. Then he dragged a fallen chunk of masonry across the lip of the hole, and another, until the opening was capped by enough weight that nothing was coming up through it without making a great deal of noise first.

Nageru was at the door, doing the same kind of work — wedging the door fast with two of the dead rats' larger bones jammed against the hinges, then bracing a fallen beam across the inside. The aasimar moved slowly. Thane saw it. The aasimar would not say it.

"The rats stink," Caleth observed.

"The rats stink," Korroc agreed.

"The rats are also the reason no one came up that hole the whole time we were gone," Thane said. "I'll take stink over a Vescavor with a knife. Lay out the bedrolls."

They laid out the bedrolls.

Anevia had cold rations split among the company — hard bread and harder cheese, dried mushroom from Sull's people, a strip each of something that was probably cave-fish — and they ate sitting against the walls in a rough circle, none of them speaking much. Caleth lit a cantrip-flame in a dish of grease so they could see one another without having to see much. The little flame threw the chamber in soft yellow shadows and made it almost bearable.

It was Caleth who said it, eventually.

"We have to go back down."

He was looking at the sealed hole as he said it, not at any of the others. He said it the way a man says a thing he has already settled with himself and is now bringing to his companions for confirmation rather than debate.

"Aye," Korroc said.

"That ritual chamber," Caleth went on, still to the floor. "The dretches. Whatever's behind that last locked door. There were demons being summoned in the sewers under our city. Today. Today. If we walk out that hallway tomorrow and tell whomever we meet on the surface that there are demons in the cistern, and we don't tell them who, and we don't tell them how, and we don't bring back the evidence —"

"Then it'll be cleaned up before anyone surfaces to look," Thane finished. "Aye. They've had time to scrub it before."

"Sull asked us to take down what Vescavor we could," Nageru said quietly. "We told him we would. We have not yet."

Korroc grunted. "And there's whoever ran out the back door earlier. They're warning someone. The longer we leave it, the worse the welcome behind that locked door is going to be."

Thane looked across the dim chamber at the others. He realized, with a small clean surprise, that none of them were looking at him for the call. They were just talking it through, the four of them and Anevia listening and Aravashniel with his half-restored eyes half-open, and the answer was assembling itself in the room without anyone having to argue for it.

The answer was that they were not going home after rest.

The answer was that they were going back down.

"All right," Thane said. "All right. Then we sleep. We sleep hard, and we go down at first light — or whatever passes for first light in this hole — and we finish what's behind that locked door. Then we come back here, collect the others, and walk out the sewer."

"Aye."

"Aye."

Nageru nodded once. "As you say."

Anevia, from her wall, said: "I'm staying with Horgus and Aravashniel. The four of you can move faster without us, and someone needs to watch this room."

"Are you sure?"

"I'm sure. The leg's not for stairs and locked doors yet, dwarf, no matter how much I'd like it to be."

Horgus, from his corner: "I am also staying."

"Yes, Horgus. Thank you, Horgus."

"I am simply being clear about it."

"Yes, Horgus."

They drew watches. Nageru took first — the aasimar slept little anyway, Thane had noticed, and his meditations served him for most of what other men got from sleep. Korroc would take second. Caleth third. Thane fourth, which suited him; the hour before waking was the hour his mother had taught him to keep, the hour when the careless and the criminal were both equally at their most exhausted and a watchful blade earned its keep.

He laid himself down on his bedroll.

The chamber's stone was cold through the thin wool. The smell was bad. The wound under his arm pulled when he turned. His leg from the lizard's bite still throbbed dully.

The chamber was dim and quiet and bad-smelling and the sounds of distant Vescavor somewhere far down in the dens echoed up through the stone every now and again, but they did not come up, and the rope was up, and the door was wedged, and Korroc snored, and Anevia kept her bow across her knees in the dark, and the small flame in the grease-dish guttered low and finally out.

Thane Stonevein slept.

He slept hard.

He woke before dawn — before dawn in a place that knew no dawn, but his bones knew the hour anyway — and he woke ready.

He felt it as he stepped in. The room had a purpose. It was not a den. It was not a barracks. It was a chamber.

A natural cavern, but not left natural — three copper bowls hung from the ceiling on chains, each at a different height, each tarnished green with age. A great stone table dominated the center of the room, scattered around it half a dozen crude wooden chairs. The table's surface bore a wide dark stain that Thane did not need to lean down to identify. The smell told him.

A killing-table, he thought. Used recently.

He froze in the doorway.

Two Vescavor stood inside, but the other two figures —

The other two figures were human.

They wore well-kept armor. They carried wicked-looking glaives. Their bearing was military, not feral — the stance of trained sword-arms, weight evenly distributed, weapons held in the ready guards of professionals.

Thane's blood went very cold and very clear in the way it had used to when his father's old sergeant had drilled him.

Templars.

Templars in the chamber.

The contract.

Thane did not move. He did not have to — the others, watching his stillness through the doorway, had read his stillness as warning, and none of them moved either.

The lead Templar — a man with close-shaved hair and a cold thin smile that did not reach his eyes — raised a hand and spoke a word.

A wave of foul magic rolled over the doorway.

Thane felt it pass through him. It tasted of bile and old blood and the small wrong things at the bottom of dreams. He clenched his teeth and waited for it to take. It did not take. He felt the others behind him shrug it off as well — Korroc's faith, Caleth's training, Nageru's celestial blood, his own simple stubborn dwarven contempt for any magic that thought it could touch him.

A Vescavor lunged and missed his face.

The Templar gestured again. Another spell — fear, this time, Thane recognized it from the back-alley horror stories of cultists his Watch-handlers had drilled him on — and the spell touched him this time, a cold finger reaching for his heart, but his heart was full of his father and his father had not run from the abyss and Thane shrugged the spell off too.

He stabbed the lead Vescavor. He took a half-step back into the doorway.

Nageru moved up beside him, blocking the doorway with his bulk, which was useful and also infuriating — the others couldn't get in. The first Vescavor swung a club at Nageru. Nageru struck back. The Vescavor fell.

Nageru moved into the room, stepping over the body, and Korroc poured in after.

Thane saw, in the brief instant when his line of sight cleared, the door at the back of the chamber open. A woman stood in it.

She was tall and dark-haired and her face was sharp-boned and beautiful in the cold way that statues were beautiful, and her hand was raised in a casting gesture, and her eyes were on Caleth.

She spoke a word.

A spell lashed across the room toward Caleth — Thane saw the half-elf's body brace — and Caleth's training threw the spell off, the words sliding past him as if he had not been the one they were aimed at.

"Don't engage her last," Thane heard himself snap. "She's the caster."

Korroc was already moving.

The chamber became a storm.

Thane lost the fine grain of it — the way one always loses the fine grain in close work — but the broad shapes stayed with him. Two Templars closing on Nageru and Korroc with their glaives. Nageru stepping inside the reach of one and pummeling him twice with fists that did not stop coming. Korroc deflecting a glaive-thrust with his shield, the steel ringing. Caleth gesturing and throwing — a snowball of pure cold magic that struck the back-room woman full in the chest and she gasped and staggered, frost climbing her armor.

The other Templar stabbed Caleth. The half-elf's blood ran. Caleth did not stop casting.

Thane closed on the last Vescavor and stabbed him through the throat. The mongrel fell with the small surprised expression that the dying so often wear.

Nageru was bleeding — a glaive had opened him along the chest and his tunic was scarlet — and he stepped back from the line and pressed his palm to his own wound and the wound closed. Lay on hands. Aasimar discipline. Nageru did not even break breathing rhythm.

Korroc stepped into the gap Nageru's retreat had left and braced, shield up, forming a wall — and reached behind him to lay a healing palm on Nageru's shoulder. Selfless, that dwarf, always selfless, healing the man behind him while another man's glaive was an inch from his face.

Caleth gestured and his hand crackled. The half-elf moved forward and touched the Templar leader who had stepped out of the back room — it was the same gesture Thane had seen him use on the spider-girl, the same shocking grasp infused with paladin's smite — and the Templar leader screamed and crumpled. Smoke rose from the woman's chest.

The other female Templar turned her glaive on Korroc and found a seam in his armor. Korroc's blood ran. More of Korroc's blood ran. Thane had never seen Korroc bleed this much in a single fight before, and the sight of it lit a small clean fury in him that he stored carefully for use.

Thane moved to flank her. To stab her from the side. To end this woman, this Templar, this agent of the chain that had killed his father —

She spun before his blade reached her.

It was not a normal spin. It was the spin of a trained glaive-fighter who had been listening for footsteps and had timed the turn to exactly the wrong moment for the man behind her. Her glaive's leaf-blade came around in a horizontal arc that Thane could not duck because there was nowhere to duck to in the cramped space, and the blade went in above his belt and through his chain shirt and out his back, and Thane saw, very calmly and very distantly, a foot of bloody steel sticking out from the small of his back.

He thought, with a strange small clarity: Father, I'm sorry.

And the world tilted, and he went down on his side on the bloody flagstones, and Korroc was roaring, the sound of a dwarf's grief and rage compressed into a single bellow, and the hammer came down on Hosilla's chest with a sound like a cooper's mallet hitting a barrel and Thane felt the impact through the floor.

Caleth was over him.

Caleth's hands were on his side.

A wash of warm gold poured into him — lay on hands, the half-elf's reserves spent on him as Korroc's had been spent on Nageru — and the worst of the bleeding stopped, but Thane was still down, the wound was too deep, it was through and through, and the half-elf's healing had brought him back from dying but not back to fighting.

He lay there. He listened.

He heard Korroc's hammer ring twice more.

He heard Nageru's tumble — the soft scrape of a monk's body rolling along stone — and the wet finishing crunch of a fist coming down where it needed to.

He heard the woman fall.

He heard the room go still.

He heard Caleth, very close to his ear, say: "Stay with us, Stonevein. Stay with us."

He breathed. He stayed.

A second wave of healing rolled through the room — Korroc, spending the last of his grace, the dwarf's well finally running dry — and Thane felt the wound in his side close another fraction. It would still scar. It would still hurt for weeks. But it would not kill him today.

He sat up. Slowly. With Caleth's help.

He looked across the chamber at the dead woman.

She looked back, in the way the dead do — eyes half-open, jaw slack, the cold beauty of her face emptied now of whatever had filled it. He felt nothing about her death. He felt — something about what she had been. About what she had served. He did not have the words for the something yet, but it sat in his chest like a banked coal.

His father had died fighting these.

His father had died fighting this.

And he, Thane Stonevein, son of Thorek the Skyguard, had just put one of them down.

He did not feel triumphant.

He felt steady.

It was, he thought, perhaps a better feeling.

They searched the bodies.

It was Thane who found the letter. He patted Hosilla's belt-pouch first — always the belt-pouch, that's where they keep what they're afraid to lose — and his fingers met folded parchment. He drew it out. He smoothed it open against his thigh. He read.

He read it twice.

Then he looked up at Caleth, who was sitting beside him cross-legged on the bloody flagstones with a healer's patient watchfulness in his eyes, and he handed the letter over without a word.

Caleth read.

Caleth's eyes widened.

"Saint's mercy."

"Aye."

Korroc was already over. "What've you got, cousin?"

Caleth read it aloud, his voice careful:

Silence.

Thane felt the shape of it settle into his bones. The wardstone was not destroyed by chance. The wardstone was destroyed on purpose. The Storm King's strike was the visible part of a long careful plan. The Templars had been in place for months — possibly years — and they had known what was coming, and they had prepared safe houses, and they had moved relics, and they had set their pieces.

And the woman in front of him had been one of those pieces.

And she had held the letter that named the others.

"Three safe houses," Thane said quietly. His voice was perfectly steady. He was almost surprised by how steady. "Names. A passphrase. A timeline. Cousin. Cousin. This is the whole rotten thing. This is what I was hired to find."

Korroc looked at him. The dwarf's brow furrowed. "Hired by whom?"

"By the people who needed to know."

"...the Watch?"

"Aye. Among others. Three months ago. They suspected the Templars were inside the city. They wanted ears in the gutters. I've had ears in the gutters." He looked down at the letter. "They never had this. Until now. Nobody had this. This is the whole picture of an enemy operation in our own walls."

Caleth was looking at him with new appraisal. Not surprise — Caleth had probably guessed, or been told by his Spireborn instincts — but appraisal.

"You're a contract agent."

"I'm a dwarf with daggers and a grudge. I take work that lets me work the grudge. They had work. I took the work."

"And Anevia?"

"Same arrangement. Different angle. She handles the soldiers and the marketplace. I handle the gutters and the warehouses." He met Caleth's eye. "We're partners, half-elf. If you've been wondering. Partners. She's a friend. A good one. Nothing more, nothing less."

Caleth, very carefully, did not smile. "I had not been wondering. But thank you for clarifying."

"Hm."

A small thing. A clarifying thing. Good. He had wanted that on the table at some point. He filed away the relief of having it said.

He turned back to the letter. He touched the signature with a fingertip. S. V. Two initials. A leader. Someone who commanded Drezen, or expected to. Someone who had given Hosilla a pet name in the salutation — Hosilla with no rank or title attached — which meant they were close, perhaps lovers, perhaps long-time co-conspirators. Someone with the authority to assign safe houses across an entire city.

Someone who was going to find out, one of these days, that the woman they had written this letter to was dead.

Someone who was going to find out, one of these days, that Thane Stonevein had taken the letter from her cooling hand.

He folded the parchment with great care along its original creases and slid it into the inside pocket of his chain shirt, against his skin.

He stood up.

His leg hurt. His shoulder hurt. His side hurt worst of all. He stood up anyway, because that was what one did.

"There's a chest," Korroc said from the back of the room.

Thane looked. There was. The dwarf-paladin was already kneeling in front of it, brushing dust from the lid, his blunt fingers finding the latch. Thane drifted over and watched him work it open. It was not locked.

Inside, wrapped in linen, lay a sword.

Thane saw it and knew it before Korroc had pulled the linen back. He had grown up on these stories. Every dwarf-child in Mendev had grown up on these stories.

A longsword. The blade pale and clean as if it had been forged that morning. The crossguard a sweep of silver-and-gold Iomedaen sunwork. The pommel a small carved sun that caught Caleth's lamp-light and reflected it back as a different kind of light entirely — a soft, holy, living glow that made Thane's eyes water.

"Radiance," Caleth breathed.

"Yaniel's sword," Korroc agreed, his voice gone very quiet.

"Stolen from the museum," Thane said. "The letter mentioned it. The Templars salvaged it. Wanted to corrupt it."

Thane reached out with his bloody hand and laid his fingers against the blade's flat. He half-expected to be burned. He was, instead, met by a faint warmth, the way a hearth-stone is warm after a winter day's fire — a friendly warmth, an acknowledging warmth, the warmth of a long-loyal weapon recognizing a hand that had not come to harm it.

He withdrew his fingers reverently.

"It's the sword, isn't it. From the Fourth Crusade."

"Aye." Korroc's voice was thick. "Yaniel was a paladin of Iomedae. She fell at the gates of Drezen. Her sword was thought lost for half a century until a salvage team brought it home to the Kenabres museum twenty years ago." He swallowed. "The museum was hit two months back. The thieves were never caught."

"They were never caught," Thane said grimly, "because they were Templars, and the museum guards were Templars, and the report I was hired to write was about how the thieves were Templars. This is the missing piece, cousin. This is the missing piece."

Thane lifted the sword carefully from its linen with reverence, with the quiet awareness that this was not his sword and might never be. The blade caught the light and the light caught the blade and for a moment the chamber was less dark than it had been since they had fallen from the world.

"We get this to the surface," Caleth said softly. "We get this back."

"Aye."

"Along with the letter."

"Aye."

The room had no further doors. No more passages. No more Vescavor. No more Templars, alive at any rate. They walked the chamber once more end to end and confirmed it: a dead end, in the very particular sense that everything useful in it was dead. Hosilla's belt-pouch yielded a pouch of mixed coin. The dead Templars yielded weapons of crusader make and a wand of cure light wounds Caleth pocketed for the company. The chest yielded only the sword.

But the information — that was the prize.

Thane walked the chamber a final time with the letter in his pocket and Radiance laid carefully in his hands, he would carry it for now, and he felt, for the first time since the rift had opened beneath him in Clydwell Plaza, that he was winning something.

His father had died fighting these.

His father had died and his body had not come home, only the sky-steel insignia of a Skyguard who had once dreamed of Khar-Zadûn.

Thane had spent his life telling himself, with the grim half-comfort of the bereaved, that his father's death had meant something — that his father had bought a few extra hours for the line, a few extra paces for the retreat, a few extra Mendevian children one more night of being able to call themselves alive.

Thane had never quite believed it. Not fully. You couldn't, when you'd been the child who had to be told.

But here, in this chamber, with a Templar's letter against his skin and Yaniel's sword in his companion's hands, Thane felt — for the first time in twenty-some years — that perhaps his father's death had not been only a holding action. Perhaps there had been a thread of consequence running from his father's last moments through the long crusade-years to this, this small dim cavern under a broken city, this dwarf with daggers and a grudge who had been hired to listen and had listened and had now, by sheer dwarven stubbornness and good companions, walked face-first into the answer.

His father had died fighting Baphomet's chain.

His son had just broken a link.

Not a big link. Not the master link.

But a link.

Thane felt the small clean fury in him bank itself down to a small clean flame — the kind of flame a dwarf can carry in his chest for a very long time without being consumed by it, because dwarves are made for the carrying of long flames — and he turned to his companions.

"We're done here," he said. "Let's get back to the others."

"And then?" Caleth asked.

Thane patted the letter against his chest.

"And then we find Irabeth. And then we burn three safe houses to the ground. And then we find out who S. V. is."

He looked at Caleth.

He looked at Korroc.

He looked at Nageru.

He smiled — a small, sharp, Stonevein smile, the smile he had inherited from a father he barely remembered — and his cousin saw the smile and matched it with his own, and Caleth's mouth quirked, and Nageru gave the small inclined-head bow that Thane had come to recognize as the aasimar's expression of profound approval.

"Then we walk," Thane said, "until we find a sky."
