# The House That Beauty Built
*Chapter Ten — Stone in the Wound*

The Worldwound did not look like a wound. That was the first lie it told.

Caleth had expected something theatrical—a gash in the earth weeping black, a sky stitched shut with lightning. Instead the land west of the Sellen tributary was simply *wrong* in a way that took a craftsman's eye to name. The river branch had run dry, but the bed of it kept the memory of water: smooth stones, undercut banks, a meander that belonged to a living current. Only there was no current. The stones were dry as old bone and the grass on the banks grew in the wrong direction, leaning away from a sun that hadn't moved.

He rode near the middle of the column, where a man could see both ends of two hundred soldiers and the wagons strung between them like beads on a fraying cord. He had a habit of counting. Not the men—the men he trusted Irabeth to count—but the angles. The lines of sight. Which wagon would burn first if it caught, which slope a flanking force would choose, where the column thinned enough that a clever enemy could cut it like a ribbon.

*Saint's mercy*, he thought, *I have become a man who looks at a meadow and sees ambush.*

There had been a time, not so many years past, when he would have looked at the way the morning light caught the dust kicked up by the column and thought only that it was beautiful—a gold haze, soft at the edges, the kind of thing a painter spent a career failing to capture. He still saw that. He simply saw the rest now too, layered underneath, the way an old fresco showed the sketch beneath the paint when the colors thinned with age.

Beauty first. Then the arithmetic of who would die.

He had learned to hold both. He held a great many things.

---

The wagon broke at midday.

He heard it before he understood it—a sound like a green branch giving way, then the long groan of a heavy thing settling wrong, and a cascade of curses from the teamsters. The third supply wagon had dropped its rear corner into the dry riverbed, the wheel canted out at an angle no wheel was meant to make.

Caleth was off his horse and walking toward it before he'd decided to. The teamster was already on his knees in the dust, swearing at the axle, and Horgus Gwerm was bearing down on the scene with the particular fury of a quartermaster watching his inventory tilt toward ruin.

"Broken axle," the teamster said. "Just went. These things happen, my lord, on roads like—"

"This isn't a road," Caleth said mildly. He crouched. Ran two fingers along the break.

He had spent enough years among artisans to know how wood failed. Wood that broke under load splintered—it tore along the grain, fibrous, a wound with a thousand small mouths. This break was clean across two-thirds of its width, smooth as a planed board, and only the last third showed the ragged tear of a thing that had finally, suddenly, given up.

Someone had cut it most of the way through. Then let the weight and the road do the rest, miles from where the knife had done its work.

"That didn't break on its own," he said.

Horgus stopped mid-stride. "Say that again."

Caleth stood, brushing the dust from his knees. He kept his voice low, the way he kept most things. "It was sawn. Three-quarters through, then left to finish itself. Whoever did it wanted it to break out here, where it would cost us a day and look like an accident." He glanced down the column—two hundred soldiers, a dozen wagons, faces he didn't know wearing tabards he trusted. The arithmetic shifted under his feet. "It looks like an accident. That's the point of it."

Horgus's face went the color of old tallow. "In my supply train."

"Double the guard," Caleth said. "On every wagon, not just this one. Pair men who came up through different commands—don't let anyone watch a wagon alone, and don't let two men who already trust each other watch it together." He paused. He had nearly said more. He filed the rest away behind his teeth, where he kept the cards he wasn't ready to show. "And Horgus—say it was a precaution. The roads, the demons, ordinary caution. Don't say *sabotage* where the column can hear it."

"Why in the Nine—"

"Because if it's one of ours," Caleth said, very quietly, "I'd rather he didn't know we'd noticed."

Horgus looked at him for a long moment. Then he nodded, once, and began barking orders, and Caleth went to find the others, and underneath the calm he wore like a second skin, something cold had begun to turn over.

*Someone in our company wants us slowed. Or stopped. Or dead.* He had spent his life reading rooms, reading faces, reading the small honest betrayals of body and breath. And he had walked the whole length of this column a dozen times and seen nothing.

That bothered him more than the saboteur did.

---

The wagon would take hours. Anevia caught Caleth's eye across the chaos of levering and swearing and said, "We should scout ahead while we're stuck. Want to send Thane with me?"

Caleth nodded. There were worse uses of hours than knowing what sat above the road you were marching.

Anevia and Thane slipped out of the column together, north along the dry riverbed, and were gone before most of the soldiers had noticed the wagon was broken.

They weren't gone long.

Anevia came back alone, moving fast, the particular stillness on her that scouts wore when they'd seen something worth seeing.

"There's a building," she said. "Top of a cliff, half a mile north. Old. Stone. Stairs cut into the rock going up." She wiped grit from the corner of her mouth. "Thane's staying on it—watching. He said to tell you there are bones in the courtyard." A pause. "Could be nothing. Could be exactly the kind of nothing a man cuts a wagon axle to give us time to walk into."

"Or the kind of place a clever army uses to watch a road," Caleth said.

"That too."

So the three of them went—Caleth, Korroc, Nageru—and they found Thane where he crouched at the base of the cliff like a stain on the rock, twin daggers sheathed, eyes up.

"It's a building," Thane said by way of greeting. Dry, flat, shorter than the situation strictly required. "Nobody's come out. Nobody's gone in. Bones in the courtyard, though. I can see them from here." He tilted his chin at the stairs. "Those are going to be a problem."

They were. Caleth saw it the moment he looked.

The stairs had been cut into the living rock generations ago, switchbacking up the cliff face, and the Worldwound had been at them ever since. Whole treads were gone. Others hung from the rock by a habit of inertia, cracked through, waiting for an excuse. There was no rail. There had perhaps never been a rail. The drop on the open side went down to the dry riverbed in a way that promised a man wouldn't need to fall far to stop being a man.

Nageru went first, because of course he did.

The aasimar took the broken stair the way water takes a slope—he simply *flowed* up it, bare feet finding purchase where there was no purchase to find, white wraps catching the strange motionless light, that subtle gold about him like a candle seen through fog. He was at the first switchback before Caleth had finished assessing the second. He didn't even look winded.

"Show-off," Korroc muttered, with enormous affection.

The dwarf considered the stairs for exactly as long as it took to decide he wasn't going to dignify them. He set his jaw, and the air around his boots shimmered, and Korroc Stonevein simply rose—the silver scale's gift, levitate, the dragon's blessing turning a broken climb into an indignity the stairs could keep to themselves. He drifted up past the first switchback at a stately, slightly smug pace, adamantine warhammer across his back, Torag's hammer-and-anvil on his shield catching what little light there was.

"That's cheating," Thane observed.

"That's *winning*," Korroc said.

Thane went up the old way—daggers away, hands and feet, the careful economy of a man who'd climbed worse. He made it three treads past the first switchback before one of them betrayed him. The stone simply wasn't there anymore; it crumbled under his weight with a sound like a cough, and Thane dropped.

He caught himself—barely—on the lip of the next tread, hung for one ugly heartbeat over the drop, and then the air shimmered around *him* too. His own silver scale. He rose the rest of the way with his mouth set in a thin line, refusing to look pleased about it.

Which left Caleth.

He looked at the stairs. The stairs looked back, broken and patient.

*The world folds,* he reminded himself. Dimensional slide, the wizard's elegant theft of distance, a clean step from here to there with the world buckling shut behind. He didn't need the stairs at all.

He took the stairs anyway, for the first dozen feet, because some stubborn part of him objected on principle to a thing as simple as a climb defeating him.

The principle lasted exactly as long as the first broken tread.

His boot came down on stone that had been waiting all afternoon for precisely his boot, and the stone went, and Caleth went with it. He got a hand on the cliff face—palm scraping rock, the sharp bright pain of skin opening—and stopped himself, half-sprawled across the ruined stair, dignity scattering down the drop ahead of the dislodged stone.

"You all right down there?" Korroc called, from a height that suggested the answer didn't much matter.

"Fine," Caleth said, in the measured tone of a man who was not fine.

He stood. He brushed himself off. He took three careful steps up.

The fourth tread was a lie too. He went down a second time—harder, an elbow this time, a long graceless slide that ended with him on one knee with both hands flat on the broken stone and a fine ringing in his ears.

Above him, on the cliff top, he could hear Nageru saying something quiet and meant to be kind, and Thane saying something that was not meant to be kind at all, and Korroc laughing the big open laugh he never bothered to hide.

*Saint's mercy.* Caleth closed his eyes. *I am a Knight of the Fifth Crusade. I carry a blade an angel left for the worthy. I have looked the Storm King's works in the face and not flinched.*

*And I cannot climb a staircase.*

He stopped fighting it. He reached for the spell instead, and the world folded—a half-second of nothing, of being neither here nor there, the particular cold non-sensation of distance simply *ceasing* to apply—and he stood blinking on the next switchback. Then he folded it again, and the world buckled and reknit, and he was at the top, on flat stone, on his own two feet, with three faces wearing three different shades of amusement turned toward him and a smear of his own blood drying on his palm.

"That bad, eh?" Korroc said.

"The stairs," Caleth said, with all the dignity he could reassemble, "are structurally unsound."

"Aye," said Korroc. "That's why I flew."

---

The courtyard told him what the building was before his mind finished forming the question, and the answer landed in his chest like a stone dropped down a well.

He kept his face still. He had a great deal of practice keeping his face still.

It had been a chapel. He knew the bones of the architecture the way he knew his own hands—the proportion of the courtyard, golden and deliberate, built to draw the eye upward and inward at once; the way the broken arch over the entrance had once curved with the particular grace that meant a craftsman had loved the curving of it. Smashed statuary lay everywhere, marble gone gray with the Worldwound's patient corruption, and even broken he could read what the figures had been. A woman with a songbird. A man with a brush. Lovers, their faces struck off but their hands still reaching. The iconography was unmistakable to anyone who knew it.

*Hers.*

This had been a chapel of Shelyn.

He did not say so. He would not say so. The tabard he wore was a crusader's tabard, plain, no god's symbol on it—and every man who saw it assumed Iomedae, because most crusaders served her, and Caleth had never corrected them. That was the point of it, the whole long careful point of it. His goddess was a private goddess, kept close, worn inward where no one looked. *Saint's mercy* was the only sign of her he let the world see, and even that the others took for a soldier's mild oath.

But this had been her house. Someone had raised these walls to beauty and love and the making of beautiful things, out here, in the worst place in the world, as an act of faith so stubborn it bordered on madness. And something had come, and smashed the statues, and let the courtyard fill with debris, and—

"Bones," Thane said, crouching by the far wall. "Human. Picked clean." He turned one over with a dagger point. "Tooth marks. Something's been snacking."

The artist in Caleth saw the courtyard as it had been—the gold proportion, the lovers reaching, the songbird's lifted wing. The paladin in him saw it as it was. And for one terrible moment the two images lay over each other like a fresco and the ruin it had become, and something in his chest cracked clean across, smooth as a sawn axle.

He let none of it show. He breathed. He counted the exits—three; one collapsed; the door Thane was already drifting toward, lockpicks appearing in his hands as if conjured.

When he spoke, his voice was steady. "Watch the roofline," he said. "Anything that smashed statuary this size didn't do it from the ground."

Nageru looked up.

"Did that gargoyle just move?" he asked, in the mild, formal way he asked everything.

Korroc squinted at the broken parapet, where the stone figures crouched against the dead sky. "You seeing things?"

The gargoyle moved.

---

They came off the roof like the sky had decided to fall in pieces—four of them, stone wings cracking open, four shapes that had been crouched among the chapel's broken angels and were not stone at all, or not only stone. They dropped fast and silent and they came, almost all of them, for *him*.

*Of course they do,* some cold part of him noted, even as he moved. *No armor. They read me as the soft one. They're not wrong.*

The first hit the air in front of him and Caleth had Radiance in his hands before he'd thought to draw it.

The glaive came around in a flat singing arc—he had never carried it as a sword, it lived as a glaive in his grip, long and bright and *right*—and he caught the gargoyle across its diving shoulder, turned it, slammed it down out of its line. It hit the courtyard stone hard enough to crack the flag beneath it.

Nageru was there before it could rise. The aasimar's fist came down like a smith's hammer, knuckles into the thing's stone skull, and a spray of grit flew where the blow landed.

The second gargoyle got through.

Caleth felt the claws before he saw them—a line of fire across his ribs, his mage armor flexing and failing to hold, that invisible second skin parting under stone talons. Then teeth, a grinding pressure on his shoulder, and he wrenched away with a hiss, blood warm down his side. The wound had weight. They always did. 

A third dropped toward Nageru. A fourth came for Caleth and he got Radiance up—caught it, the haft jarring in his hands—but stone is patient and the thing's claw still found his arm before he turned it aside.

"*Caleth!*" Korroc, charging, the warhammer already swinging. The dwarf hit the gargoyle that had been chewing on Caleth's shoulder and the adamantine head took it square; the stone *cracked*, a great branching fracture running up through the thing's flank, and it shrieked—a sound like a millstone splitting.

Nageru destroyed his.

Caleth saw it from the corner of his eye, the way you see a thing you'll remember later: three blows, faster than three blows had any right to be, the gold-aura'd fists driving in—chest, throat, skull—and the gargoyle simply came apart, chips of stone flying out across the courtyard like spat seeds, the body losing the argument with the idea that it had ever been alive.

Korroc's gargoyle turned on him, teeth and claws raking—and found only shield and the Stonelord's hardened skin, stone meeting stone, finding no purchase. Korroc didn't even grunt.

Thane appeared at Caleth's flank—when had he closed the distance?—both daggers going into the gargoyle worrying at Caleth's side, mother's blade and father's blade, quick and economical, and the thing staggered.

Caleth finished it.

He brought Radiance around and put the point through it, twice—once to open it, once to end it—and the gargoyle that had been chewing his shoulder folded down into so much rubble. He pivoted, ribs screaming, and caught the next one a hard raking blow across its wing.

Korroc closed and put three hammer-strokes into that one—*crack, crack, crack*, the patient methodical violence of a dwarf who'd decided a thing should stop existing—and it stopped existing.

One left. Nageru hit it solid, square in the center of mass, and it rocked back into the path of Radiance, and Caleth swung, and the glaive bit deep, and the last gargoyle came apart against the courtyard stone and was still.

Quiet, after. The dead-sky quiet of the Worldwound, broken only by their breathing.

Caleth pressed a hand to his ribs. It came away red.

"Hold still," Korroc said, and a wand touched his shoulder, and golden warmth bloomed out from the point of contact—the particular sweetness of a healing spell, like stepping from cold rain into a warm room, the torn skin knitting, the deep ache going out of him. Korroc went down the line, touching each of them in turn, the warmth passing from man to man. "There. Anyone want to tell me what gargoyles are doing roosting on a ruin in the middle of nowhere?"

"Eating," said Thane, already back at the lock. "The bones outside. They were eating."

---

The first door opened on a dead end.

The ceiling had come down at some point—a slab of it lay across the passage beyond, sealing the way—but in the room before the collapse there was a pile of weaponry on the floor, heaped against the wall like driftwood left by a tide.

Most of it was ruined. Blades snapped, hafts rotted, leather perished to flakes. But Thane went through it with a craftsman's hands and came up with a masterwork battleaxe, the steel still sound; and then he found a longsword that hummed faintly against his fingers, the small particular thrum of a thing with enchantment in it; and there were weapons of cold iron, gray and heavy, the kind men carried when they expected to fight things that ordinary steel would only annoy.

"What are these doing here?" Korroc turned the battleaxe in the light. "Good steel. Cared-for, before it sat here."

"Different hands," Thane said. He'd crouched over the pile, sorting it the way he sorted everything, by the story it told. "Look. This is a courier's blade—light, cheap, for show. This is a soldier's. And this—" the longsword, the magic one "—this belonged to a knight. These didn't come from one place. They came from a dozen people."

"And nothing's attacked us yet," Korroc said slowly. "Except the gargoyles."

Caleth had already done the arithmetic. He didn't enjoy where it came out. "They didn't bring their weapons here," he said. "The weapons were *brought* here. After." He looked at the cold iron, at the courier's blade that someone had carried west with some errand still unfinished. "Travelers. People who came up that road and never came down it. The gargoyles, or whatever was chewing the bones—they took them. And piled what was left."

The room held its silence.

"Saint's mercy," Caleth said quietly, and meant it as the prayer it was. *Let me find who did this. Let me end it.*

The rubble blocked any further passage. They took the serviceable weapons and went back out into the dead light.

---

Thane picked the second door more slowly.

He worked the lock, then stopped—went still, the way he did, head tilted, two fingers rising to tap his own ear. *Listening.* Caleth watched him weigh whatever he heard. Then Thane's mouth thinned, and he eased the door open, and stepped back, and let them see.

Caleth let out a long groan before he could stop himself.

This had been the sanctuary. Four angel statues stood at the cardinal points—or had stood; their wings were chipped to stumps, their faces hammered to ruin, every line of grace a hand had given them answered by a hand that hated grace. The walls were covered in runes and figures, and the figures were the kind of thing that lodged in the back of the mind and stayed: open mouths, teeth, hunger given a shape. And the altar—

The altar had been Shelyn's. He could see, beneath the filth, the place where her symbol had been carved, the songbird and the rose, the proportion of a thing built to be beautiful. And it had been *befouled*. Smeared, fouled, made into something his mind flinched away from naming, an act of deliberate desecration aimed at everything his goddess was, performed by hands that had wanted her unmade.

His goddess's altar. In his goddess's house. And he could not even say so.

He felt the anger come up in him, and it was a stranger. Caleth did not do anger. Caleth did the cold thing, the measured thing, the arithmetic. Anger was a luxury for men who didn't need to see the board. But standing in the ruin of the one house in this whole poisoned land that had been raised to love and beauty, looking at what hate had done to it—

Something showed on his face. He felt it. A scowl, rare as a comet.

There were nine of them inside. Robed, shuffling, the wrong color, the wrong smell.

"Zombies," Nageru began, "the undead—"

Caleth fireballed them.

He did not decide to. The spell was simply in his hands and then it was *there*, a bloom of fire in the desecrated sanctuary, light and heat and a roar that ate the dead air, and the robed things caught and screamed and burned. Seven of the nine came apart in the fire, ash and worse drifting down through the holes in the ruined ceiling.

Two survived it.

Korroc charged the nearer, warhammer up. Caleth folded the world—a dimensional slide, here to there in the space between heartbeats—and came up beside the other and put Radiance through it twice, fast, the glaive opening it and ending it, and it fell.

Nageru kicked the last one twice, hard, driving it back. Thane slid to the door, covering the way they'd come. The thing had a flail—it came around in a clumsy arc and the head of it caught Caleth across the shoulder, a heavy graceless blow, and he stepped back out of the reach of its teeth and its claws, ceding the ground rather than the limb.

Korroc swung. Missed—the hammer whistling past—but the *threat* of it drove the thing back, right into Caleth's reach. He took it, twice, and the last of them fell.

"Ghouls," Korroc said, looking down at it, breathing hard. "Not zombies. Ghouls." He nudged the body with his boot. "Might have been them chewing on the bones outside."

Caleth was already looking at the runes. Aravashnial's lore had stayed with him; he'd made a point of learning the shapes of the Abyss the way he'd once learned the shapes of letters. These he knew. A gnashing, devouring sigil, a name written in the grammar of hunger.

"Kabriri," he said. The word tasted foul. "He Who Gnaws. A demon lord." He looked at the two fallen ghouls. "Lords of the dead, those that feed on the dead. This was made his."

"The last two," Korroc said. "Priests, then."

"Priests," Caleth agreed.

And then he set Radiance against the wall, and rolled up his sleeves, and began to clean.

---

He didn't explain it. He didn't have to—or he hoped he didn't. Korroc looked at him for a moment, then put his hammer aside and joined him, and Nageru after, the three of them working at the fouled altar with whatever came to hand. If they thought it strange that the mage was the one who couldn't leave it filthy, they didn't say.

It was Nageru who found the button—a discoloration in the altar's base, a place where the stone had been worn by more than weather. He pointed it out to Thane.

Thane came over, crouched, ran a cantrip of water across the last of the filth to clear it, and checked the seam with the patient distrust he gave every hidden thing.

"No trap," he said. "Push it."

Caleth pushed it.

A compartment ground open in the altar's base. Inside, ranked and cradled and impossibly untouched by all the foulness above—dozens of vials, the soft answering glow of healing in every one.

"Saint's mercy," Caleth said, and this time the others took it for relief, and it was that too.

"Potions," Korroc breathed. "Cure potions. *Dozens* of them.

"We can pass these around the army," Caleth said. He was already counting—two hundred soldiers, the road to Drezen still long. "One for every other man, near enough. Horgus will weep."

"Horgus weeps over a chipped spoon," Korroc said.

---

The next door gave onto a dining room, and the dining room told a story Caleth would have given much to un-read.

There were straps fixed to the tables. Leather, new, added long after the tables were made—buckled restraints, sized for wrists, for ankles, for a throat. The ghouls had eaten here. They had not eaten quickly.

Caleth drew a knife and cut the straps into pieces, methodically, every one of them, until there was nothing left that could hold a living thing down. He did not say anything while he did it. Neither did the others.

The dining room let onto a kitchen, and the kitchen onto a passage, and the passage to a door that breathed wrong.

---

The bedroom held more ghouls—and something worse standing among them.

A demon. Red eyes in a narrow face, hands and feet that ended in claws, ears drawn to points and horns above them, bat wings folded tight against a too-thin body. It opened its mouth and began to shape something—a spell, the air thickening around it—and then nothing visible happened, the magic curdling into some effect Caleth couldn't see and didn't have time to parse.

Nageru went for it.

The aasimar simply *ran*—past the ghouls, between them, through a gap that wasn't a gap until he made it one—and his foot took the demon in the side of the head, snapping it sideways, and then both fists drove into its chest, *one-two*, the gold of his aura flaring with the strikes.

Thane tumbled past the ghouls low and came up behind one and put both daggers into its back.

A ghoul got its teeth into Nageru's arm; he didn't so much as turn.

Korroc took the doorway—choke the room, make them come to the hammer—and broke a ghoul across the skull. Another raked his arm and its claws found the Stonelord's hardened skin and *broke against it*, the stone in his veins answering stone with stone, and the ghoul recoiled with a ruined hand.

Caleth made a choice.

"Nageru—" He shaped the spell and threw it, and *enlarge person* took the aasimar and unfolded him, doubling him in the space of a breath, the white wraps straining, the gold aura swelling to match. A giant now stood in the bedroom, and the demon's calculus had just gone badly wrong.

The demon agreed. It *teleported*—gone from in front of the giant, the air clapping shut where it had been—and reappeared behind *Caleth*, and its flail came around before he could turn, the heavy head taking him across the back hard enough to drive the breath out of him. He staggered, ribs lighting up again, the world graying at the edges.

Behind him, enlarged Nageru turned and swept through the ghouls like a man through wheat—three of them gone in a single furious motion, bodies flung aside. Thane killed the last with a quiet efficient thrust.

Korroc's hand found Caleth's shoulder; golden warmth poured in, steadying him, the gray retreating from his vision.

And Caleth reached for the smite.

It was not Iomedae's smite, whatever the party believed. It was Shelyn's—the goddess of love and beauty, who taught that there was nothing more beautiful than the destruction of what hated beauty—and when he called on her, Radiance answered. The glaive blazed, white light pouring off the blade, bright enough to throw hard shadows across the befouled walls.

He folded the world and came up behind the demon, and put Radiance into it. Twice. Hard. The blade bit through the thin body and came away dark with blood, the light along its edge guttering where the foul stuff touched it.

The demon screamed and answered with its own filth—*unholy smite*, a wave of Abyssal hate that caught both Caleth and Korroc. Korroc shrugged most of it off, the stone in him refusing the corruption a foothold. Caleth was not so armored. It took him full, and a sickness closed its hand around his guts, a deep wrong nausea, his strength leaking out through a wound he couldn't see. He grimaced, kept his feet, kept Radiance up.

Then Nageru—enormous, gold-wreathed, *furious*—hit the demon once more, and it folded, and dropped, and was done.

For exactly one heartbeat, Caleth thought it was over.

Then he heard the wings.

A heavy beating of great wings overhead, and a *thud* against the ceiling that shook dust from every crack, and a roar—angry, vast, the sound of something that had been waiting and was now done waiting. A shadow fell across the holes in the ruined ceiling, blotting the dead light.

Thane drank a potion and vanished—gone between one blink and the next, invisible, only the faint disturbance of his passage marking where he moved.

Korroc poured more warmth into Caleth, then into himself.

And Caleth, sick to his bones and bleeding and absolutely certain it was the right call, went outside to meet it.

---

He left Korroc muttering something behind him about *waiting for the rest of us, you mad half-elf*, and stepped out into the courtyard, and looked up at the thing that had been chewing the bones.

It was huge.

Scaled in places, furred in others, with a wingspan that could have shaded the broken courtyard whole. Its mouth was a horror of teeth, far too many of them, and a long tongue lolled and tasted the air. Clawed hands, clawed feet, horns curving back over a skull crowned with broad pointed ears, a mane of shaggy matted fur. *He Who Gnaws* had sent his servant, and his servant had grown fat on travelers.

It looked at Caleth, and a wave passed off it—a cold pressure, a command aimed at his nerves, *be still, be meat, do not move*. Paralysis, the ghoul-touch writ enormous. He felt it reach for him.

He refused it. *No.* The wave broke against his will and slid off.

Beside him, Nageru, having followed him out—shuddered and shook it off too.

The fight came fast then, the way the worst ones did.

Korroc burst out of the chapel and set himself fifteen feet from the thing, shield up, planting his boots like a man putting down roots. Drawing it to him. *Hit me. Hit the stone.*

Caleth called a spiritual weapon into being—a blade of pure transparent force, conjured and commanded, and it struck the demon once, a clean blow that drew a line of dark fluid.

Nageru threw spears—two of them, both finding the mark, both flaring with smite-light as they hit.

The demon cast, and Caleth felt the spell pass over them like a cold draft, a draining thing, life force itself thinning in the air. Then it went for Korroc—claws and teeth, the great mouth closing, and blood came, real and red, and Korroc *grunted*, a sound Caleth had heard from the dwarf perhaps twice in their acquaintance.

But Korroc was Korroc. He pressed a glowing hand to his own wounds—lay on hands, the paladin's gift—and then layered a cure spell over it, and most of the damage closed. Then he set his feet wider and went *still* in the particular way he had, the way that meant he was drawing strength up out of the stone beneath him, the Stonelord rooting himself to the bones of the world.

Invisible Thane moved somewhere—Caleth felt rather than saw it, a wrongness in the corner of the courtyard repositioning.

And Caleth, of all the spells in his book, of all the holy fire he could have called, reached for something else entirely.

He shaped scorching ray—he felt the fire gather in his hands, the familiar structure of it, three lances of heat building behind his fingers. But something in him *knew*—the way he sometimes knew where a line wanted to go on a half-finished sketch—that this thing's heat, its furred and scaled bulk, its appetite, would not love more of what it already was. It would love the opposite.

So he turned the spell inside out.

The Exploiter's gift—the thing the Spire had never quite understood about him, the instinct that treated arcane theory as material to be *bent* rather than followed. He held the scorching ray in his mind and *inverted* it, peeling the fire back, folding the heat in on itself until what gathered in his hands was not fire at all but its absence—a cold so sharp it burned, three lances of killing frost where three lances of flame should have been.

He loosed them.

The rays struck the demon dead center—three lines of pure inverted cold, one after the other, hammering into the vast furred body—and the world stopped to watch what they did.

Ice bloomed across the demon. Not a patch of it. *All* of it—racing out from the points of impact in branching white crystal, sheeting up the wings, glazing the horns, sealing the too-many teeth in the open mouth, until the whole vast creature stood rimed and glittering in the dead light, beautiful in the way only terrible things frozen mid-motion can be. It stiffened. It snarled—a small strangled sound, the last warm thing in it. And then it collapsed, all at once, and lay unmoving on the broken stone, and did not rise.

Silence.

"You killed it," Korroc said, into the silence, "by turning a *fire spell* cold."

"It seemed cold-averse," Caleth said.

---

They cleared the rest of it after that, room by room.

A chamber of shattered stained glass, the lead come apart, the colored shards ground into the floor by passing feet—Caleth crouched among them for longer than the search required, turning a single surviving fragment of rose-colored glass in his fingers before he set it carefully aside, whole, on a windowsill, where the light could find it.

A room with shackles bolted to the stone wall, sized for wrists, the same grim story as the dining tables. He set his hands to them and *pulled*, and the spell of his strength and the leverage of his anger tore them out of the wall, and he carried them outside and threw them off the cliff, and watched them fall the long way down, and felt nothing he was willing to examine.

And then he went back to the cleaning.

The others helped where they could, but it was Caleth's hands that did the last of it—the altar scrubbed back to the stone, the songbird and the rose emerging from beneath the filth, the angle of the proportion reasserting itself the way truth does when you wipe the lie off it. When the foulness was gone, when nothing of Kabriri's mark remained but the scoring in the stone, he reached into his pack and drew out a scroll he had carried a long while without quite admitting why.

It wasn't a consecration, but a dispel magic. It worked for the situation. A scroll to drive the evil out of a place and give it back to the light.

He unrolled it, and read it, and as the words left him the chapel *answered*. The dead air moved. A warmth rose up through the floor that had nothing to do with any cure spell, the particular warmth of a place remembering what it had been built for, and for a moment—just a moment—the broken courtyard outside seemed to hold the gold proportion again, the lovers reaching, the songbird's lifted wing.

And on his back, Radiance flared.

He felt it through the leather of the harness—the glaive blazing where it hung, white light spilling, *answering*. He pulled it around to his hands, and the blade was lit from within, and when the light faded it *felt* different. Heavier in the right way. Stronger. As if his goddess's house, restored by his hands, had put something of itself into the weapon that an angel of her sister-faith had left for the worthy.

He stood there a long moment, in the cleaned chapel, with the blade warm in his hands, and let himself feel the whole of it—the grief and the rage and the long careful silence he'd kept, the private faith carried inward where no one looked, his goddess's one stubborn house of beauty in the worst place in the world, smashed and fouled and now, by his hands, *given back*.

He said nothing. There was no one he could say it to.

But he nodded, once, slowly. And he let himself smile—small, private, the kind of smile he never spent on the world.

Then he sheathed the blade, and rolled his sleeves back down, and went to tell the others it was time to go.

---

The army was on the road when they came back down—the wagon mended, the column moving, two hundred soldiers grinding west into the dead land.

Caleth put the case of potions into Horgus's hands and watched the quartermaster's face do something complicated and grateful and immediately mercenary.

"Pass them through the column," Caleth said. "Wounded first, then by lot. And keep a tally."

"Keep a—" Horgus was already counting. He had the case open and his lips were moving over the vials before Caleth had finished the sentence. "Forty-one. Forty-*one*. Saint's bones, where did you—forty-two—"

Caleth left him to it.

---

The unease found him again at evening, the way it had been finding him since the axle.

He was at the fire when he heard the curse—Thane, low, in Dwarven, the particular guttural shape of a man who'd found something he hadn't put there. Caleth crossed to him without seeming to.

Three vials, wrapped in cloth, sitting in Thane's open palm. The liquid in them was dark, with a faint oily sheen that caught the firelight wrong.

"These aren't mine," Thane said. "Found them in my pack. Bottom, under the spare cord. I didn't put them there." He looked up, and for once the dry guard was gone from his face. "What are they?"

Caleth took one. Held it to the light. He didn't need a spell to know it—he'd seen its like in the back rooms of better cities than any out here—but he checked anyway, the small reading-magic that named a thing, and it named what he already feared.

"Shadowblood," he said quietly. "A drug. Highly addictive. The kind a man pays anything for, once he's started, and pays with anything once the coin runs out." He handed it back. "Thane. If one of the paladins sees this—"

"They can try something." Flat. Cold. The guard back up like a portcullis.

"I know." Caleth kept his voice even. "I get it. I'm not saying you're using it. I'm saying—if they *see* it, they won't see a man who was framed. They'll see evil. The detection doesn't read intent. It reads the thing in your hand." He let that sit. "Someone put it there to be *found*. The same someone who cut the axle, maybe. I don't know. But this is meant to make you the problem."

Thane's jaw worked. Then he wrapped the vials and stood. "Korroc and Nageru should see this."

---

They did not destroy it. Not for lack of Korroc wanting to.

"Throw it in the fire," the dwarf said, the moment he understood. "Now. Tonight. There's no good reason that exists in our camp another hour."

"It's evidence," Thane said.

"Evidence of *what*? Whoever planted it is evil. We don't need proof a man's evil to deal with him—we deal with him."

"I'm about to start questioning a couple hundred knights," Thane said, "and I'd like to have the thing in my hand when I do it."

It went to a leadership meeting, in the end—Irabeth and Anevia and Aron Kir and Aravashnial and Horgus, the lamps low, the vials in the center of the table like a thing too dangerous to touch. And the four of them swept the camp, the way Caleth had known they would: Korroc, Nageru and Caleth with their paladin's sight, Thane with the cantrips that named corruption, walking the whole length of the column and back, more than two hundred souls with the workers and a dozen wagons and the picket lines and the latrines and the dark between the tents.

They found nothing.

Not a flicker. Not a stain. Not one soul in the entire company that read as anything but a tired crusader in a terrible place.

Thane stood at the edge of the firelight afterward with his fists closed and his face white with a fury Caleth understood entirely. To be hunted by someone you could not see—to know they were *here*, sharing your fire, eating your bread, and to look straight at them and see *nothing*—

"They're here," Thane said. "Someone cut that axle. Someone put that in my pack. They're standing in this camp *right now* and the sweep says they're as clean as a temple novice."

"I know," Caleth said.

"How? How do you stand in front of a man who's working to kill us and read him *clean*?"

Caleth didn't have an answer. That was the worst of it. He had spent his whole life reading rooms—the tilt of a head, the choice of a silence, the small honest betrayals of the body that a person could not fully govern. He held two cards close always; he assumed everyone did; and he had learned to count the cards in other men's hands by how they held them. It was the thing he was *for*. The cold arithmetic under the painter's eye.

And someone in this company had a hand he could not read at all.

The night wore on. Thane wanted to keep at it—to walk the column again, to start the questioning, to *do something*—and Caleth let him want it for a long while before he set a hand on the man's shoulder.

"It's late," he said. "We need to take our watches."

For a moment he thought Thane would refuse. Then the fury went out of him, not gone but banked, and he nodded, and turned toward the picket line, and the matter was, for tonight, set down.

---

Caleth took the first watch.

He sat at the edge of the firelight with Radiance across his knees—stronger now, warmer, his goddess's gift folded into an angel's—and he watched the camp breathe in the dark. Two hundred souls. Faces he didn't know wearing tabards he was supposed to trust. The wagons in their rows, double-guarded now, men he'd paired who couldn't watch alone.

Somewhere out there in the dark, one of those faces had cut an axle and planted a poison and walked through a sweep of four servants of the light and come up clean.

He had restored a chapel today. He had given his goddess's house back to her with his own hands, and felt the blade answer, and let himself smile the private smile he never spent on the world. It had been the best thing he'd done in a long while, and it sat warm in his chest still, a small light he meant to keep.

But out here, at the edge of the firelight, the cold arithmetic ran on underneath the way it always did. And it kept coming back to the same unbalanced sum.

He could see the whole board. He prided himself on it. He had built a life on it.

And somewhere on the board was a piece he could not see at all.

*Saint's mercy,* he thought, and it was not quite a prayer this time, or not only one. *Let me find you before you find us.*

He kept his watch. The dead sky did not move. And the Worldwound, patient as a broken staircase, waited for someone to put a foot wrong.
