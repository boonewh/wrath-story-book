# Above the Sunken Sky
*Chapter Four — The Smith in the Ruin*

The light at the end of the corridor was the first thing Korroc had seen that was not made by Caleth's hand in four days.

He walked toward it the way a man walks toward water in a desert. Slowly. Trying not to want it too much. The air grew warmer with each step — not the warmth of a fire, which a dwarf knew, but the warmth of an open sky, which a dwarf had begun to forget — and it carried smells he had not let himself imagine since the rift had slammed shut above his head.

Woodsmoke. Wet stone. Far-off cooking.

Kenabres.

His city.

He kept walking. Behind him, the others followed in the order they had walked all morning — Caleth at his shoulder with his glaive at low ready, Thane a half-step behind that with one dagger out and the other in its sheath because Thane was always halfway to a fight even when there wasn't one, Anevia limping but upright on her cave-pine crutch, Aravashniel with his bandaged half-eyes fixed on the light ahead, Horgus muttering. Nageru brought up the rear.

The aasimar had been very quiet for the last hour.

Korroc had noticed. He had not asked. Nageru's quiet was a kind of weather, in Korroc's experience — it came when something was being weighed inside him, and it lifted when the weight was settled, and a friend's job in either case was to walk beside him until it did. So Korroc had not asked. But he had watched.

The light brightened. The corridor narrowed at its end, choked by a half-collapse of old brick and newer rubble, and Korroc set his shield against the wall and braced his shoulder and pushed.

Stone shifted. Light flooded in.

The stone shifted *for him*, the dwarf-paladin noticed, with the small distant noticing of a man too tired to follow the thought. He set it aside for later.

He climbed out into Kenabres.

---

He did not, at first, know where he was.

That was the strange part. He stood in the wreckage of what had been a sewer grate, in what had been a courtyard, in what had been a city he had walked his whole adult life — and for a long quiet moment his eyes did not find a single landmark they recognized. He turned slowly. Caleth came up beside him and turned too. Behind them Thane was helping Anevia clear the grate.

The cathedral.

That was how he found it. He looked for the great steeple of Saint Clydwell, the way a sailor looks for a star, and when his eye landed on the place the cathedral should have been, he found only a column of dark smoke rising into a sky that should not have been the color it was.

The cathedral was gone.

The Kite — to the west, where the wardstone fortress had been, where the sky had been clean for a hundred years — the Kite was gone too. Nothing in its place. A jagged absence, scarred black around the edges, with a low column of smoke standing over it like a tombstone.

Half of the skyline was missing.

The other half was on fire.

Korroc stood with his hammer hanging from his fist and did not say anything for a long time. The smell that had been woodsmoke and cooking now resolved itself into what it actually was, in this much volume — burned buildings, burned bodies, burned wood that had been someone's roof and was now the open thing demons had eaten through. Above the city wheeled fat black birds Korroc had never seen in Mendev before. The clouds were the wrong shape. The light was the wrong color. The very air had a smell underneath the smoke that was not the smell of his city at all — sweet, sick, abyssal — and Korroc's tongue knew it from the underdark, from the maze where the dretches had come for them, and his stomach turned.

The Worldwound. The Worldwound was *here*. The Worldwound had come *in*.

A small dry voice spoke at his elbow.

"Southeastern Gate district," Aravashniel said. The old wizard's half-restored eyes squinted at the skyline. "We came up well east of the cathedral. The Gate is — was — two streets that way."

Korroc could not have placed it. He had not recognized a single building. Without Aravashniel's careful pages-rustling voice he would have stood in his own city like a stranger for an hour.

"Aye," he managed. "Aye. The Gate. I know it."

He didn't know it. He nodded anyway, because a dwarf-paladin in plate did not stand in the wreckage of his own home and weep where his companions could see, and so he locked the weeping behind his teeth the way a smith bars the door of a forge against the wind, and he looked at the others.

Caleth's face had gone still. The half-elf was cataloguing — Korroc had come to recognize that small narrowing of the eyes — every detail of what was in front of him, the way Caleth catalogued everything, and storing it for later. His mouth was a thin line.

Thane was already moving. He had unslung his bow and was up on a piece of broken wall, low and quiet, scanning the streets. A scout's work. The work that came easier than the looking. Korroc was grateful for it.

Anevia was looking at the same skyline Korroc was. Her face had gone the color of bone. She had walked these streets longer than Korroc had worked his forge. He saw her see what he had seen, and he saw her not say it.

Nageru had not turned to look at anything in particular. The aasimar stood with his head bowed, his bare feet planted on the broken stone of the courtyard, his hands open at his sides. He was breathing slowly. His eyes were almost closed. Korroc, watching him, had the sudden unsettling sense that Nageru was *listening* to something — not with his ears, but with whatever it was in him that was older than him. Whatever the gold in his blood had inherited.

The aasimar's quiet had not lifted. It had deepened.

Korroc did not, in that moment, have time to wonder what Nageru was hearing.

A sound from a heap of rubble across the courtyard. A shifting of stones. A wet snuffling breath.

"Above," Thane said softly from his perch.

Korroc raised his shield.

---

The thing came over the rubble in one long fluid leap.

It was huge, like a massive hunting dog and shaped like one, four-legged, low-bellied, with a long jaw and a longer tongue — but its hide was a dull boiled-leather hide that was not the hide of any dog Korroc had ever seen, and black quills ran in a stiff brittle mane from the crown of its skull down its neck and along its spine to the base of its tail. Its eyes were red. Its mouth was full.

It went for Horgus.

Horgus screamed — a high panicked scream from a man who had not signed a contract to be useful in a fight — and the thing was on him before any of them had cleared a weapon. The merchant's silks tore. Blood bloomed. Horgus went down on his back under a hundred pounds of quilled hide, his hands up trying to push the jaws away from his throat, and the thing snapped and snarled and bit and Horgus was making the sound now of a man who had finally understood what the world cost.

Korroc moved.

He moved without thinking. He moved the way a smith moves when a blade comes out of the quench too hot — not with calculation but with the small fast certainty of long training. Three steps. Shield up. He planted himself square over Horgus's body and brought the shield down on the thing's flank with the full weight of his stride.

The thing lurched off the merchant.

Thane's dagger was already in the air. The dwarf-rogue had jumped from his wall the instant the creature had moved, and the blade hit the thing's flank as it lurched — sank, twisted, came back red. The creature roared. It turned toward Thane.

Caleth came around Korroc's other shoulder, his hand already shaping a spell, and the snowball formed in his palm and flew —

— and missed. Skittered off the broken stone behind the creature with a sharp hiss of vapor.

"Saint's *mercy*," Caleth muttered, and went for his glaive.

The creature lunged at Thane. Claws raked. Thane danced back the way Thane danced — the small precise pivot, blade tracking — and his daggers came in on the recovery, twice, both finding meat. The creature bellowed. It turned for him again and swung its head, and the bone quills along its mane bristled and dropped, *the things were meant to embed*, Korroc realized too late, and Thane saw it the same instant and dropped flat to the stone and rolled. The quills passed over him in a soft brittle hiss and clattered against the wall behind.

Korroc swung.

His hammer came down on the creature's flank in the same place Thane's blade had bitten, and the impact rang through the haft and into his shoulders the way it rang when the hammer landed on cold iron — wrong, not yielding, *the thing was harder than meat had any right to be*. The creature staggered. It did not fall. Caleth's glaive came over Korroc's shoulder and the leaf-blade punched into the thing's side and held, and Thane was up off the stone with both daggers leading and went in low and stabbed up under the ribs.

The first dagger found the heart.

The creature died standing. It folded. It hit the broken courtyard stone with a wet sound and stopped being a creature and started being a body.

Quiet.

Korroc lowered his hammer. His arm was shaking, slightly, in the way his arm shook after a hard set at the forge — not weakness, just the muscles remembering they had been asked to work. He looked down at Horgus.

The merchant was still down. Bleeding from the shoulder and the chest. Conscious but not moving. His silks were torn open, his rings catching the wrong-colored sunlight at his clutching fingers.

Anevia was already there. She had limped to him faster than Korroc could believe, her splinted leg held off the ground entirely. Her hand was on Horgus's brow. She looked up at Korroc and her face was as careful as Korroc had ever seen it.

"He's hurt, bad."

"Aye."

Korroc knelt. His plate creaked. He set the hammer aside. He laid both hands on Horgus's chest, the warm callused palms of a smith on the soft expensive linen of a merchant, and he called up Torag's grace.

A wash of golden warmth flowed into Horgus.

The torn skin began to knit. The bleeding slowed. Korroc felt the small specific knit of the muscle that had been ripped open over the merchant's collarbone find itself and close. He felt the rib bone that had been bruised but not broken settle back into the right alignment. He felt Horgus's breathing, which had been short and hitching, even out and deepen.

Horgus opened his eyes. They were wet.

"Easy, merchant," Korroc said, more gently than he had ever spoken to the man. "Easy."

"I am — I am bleeding."

"Aye. Less now."

"I am — I am — there is a thing on the —"

"It's dead. Dead and done. You're alive. Eyes on me."

Horgus's eyes came to Korroc's. They held there. Korroc saw, in the small wet center of the man, something Korroc had not seen in him before — not the snobbish merchant, not the complainer, not the man who had wanted to leave Anevia in the dark. Something smaller. A frightened man on his back in a city that had unmade itself, who had been a dog's chew-toy for ten seconds and had survived only because dwarves had stood over him.

Korroc kept his hand on the man's chest a moment longer. Then he stood, slow with his plate.

"Up with you. You can stand?"

"I — yes. I — yes."

Horgus stood. He did so carefully. His face was pale beneath the dust. He did not complain.

Korroc turned to Thane. The dwarf-rogue had a long scrape down his forearm where a claw had caught him — shallow, oozing — and Korroc did not even ask, just laid his hand on it and let a smaller wave of healing pass through. Thane nodded his thanks. He wiped the daggers and sheathed them in the same motion, the right blade first, the left blade after.

"No Stonevein has ever sheathed a wet blade, cousin," Korroc murmured.

Thane's mouth quirked, just a little. "And no Stonevein ever will."

It was, Korroc would think later, the first thing in the morning that had felt like the morning.

---

They moved through the streets in a careful single file with Anevia in front, because Anevia knew the streets, and Korroc behind her with his shield up and his hammer in his hand, and Caleth behind that with his glaive low, and Thane drifting along the rooflines a half-step ahead of them when the rooflines still existed.

The streets were emptier than streets had any right to be.

It was that, more than the broken buildings, that put a cold weight in Korroc's chest. He had grown up in a dwarven hold on the edge of nowhere, where the population had been small and the silence had been the silence of a small people in a great cave. Kenabres had never been that. Kenabres at dawn had been carts and shouts and bells and the long groaning yawn of a city the size of a small country waking up to do its business. Kenabres in the middle of the day had been a wall of human noise.

This was not Kenabres.

There were sounds. Now and then. A scream, somewhere far off — quick and terrible and then gone. A long low groan from a building that had not yet finished settling into its own ruin. The deep distant booming that was either a demon walking or the ground itself remembering what had broken it. The wheeling sound of the black birds overhead, which had a register Korroc did not have a name for.

But the human sounds were gone.

Twelve thousand people, Korroc thought, looking down a street that should have held two hundred and held none. Maybe less. Maybe none at all. Where did twelve thousand people go in four days?

He did not let himself answer.

Anevia raised a hand. The party stopped.

Korroc came up beside her. She had stopped at the corner of a small open plaza — Korroc recognized it now, faintly, as a market square that had held a Sarenrae shrine in its eastern arch — and her free hand was at her mouth. Her eyes were on the shrine.

Or what was happening in front of it.

Three figures in tabards. Three figures in dented armor. A small fourth figure who was not a figure but a child.

The Sarenrae knights had her by the arms. One held her. One held her shoulders down. The third — a woman, the leader, by her stance — was speaking to her in a voice that was very nearly gentle and very nearly not, and her longsword was unsheathed and held tip-down toward the cobblestone.

The child was crying. Quietly. With the small worn-out crying of a child who had been crying a long time.

"Child," the woman was saying. "Your sacrifice will aid the cause. Hush, now. Hush."

Korroc's hand tightened on his hammer.

Beside him, Caleth had also gone very still. The half-elf's voice came in a murmur Korroc was meant to hear and no one else was. "Sun Lady's mercy. Korroc — *the legend.*"

Korroc had heard it. Years ago, in a tavern, from an old soldier who had laughed about it. *Sheath a holy sword in the body of a virgin and the blade will turn demon-killer for a year.* He had laughed too, at the time. He had not believed any sane crusader would credit such a thing, much less try it.

The crusaders in front of him were not sane.

"Aye," Korroc said softly. "The legend."

He stepped forward.

He did not draw breath to shout. He did not consider an approach. He simply stepped forward into the plaza with his shield up and his hammer high and he bellowed across the cobblestones in a voice that came up out of his chest from somewhere very old.

"HEY!"

The three knights froze.

"What in Torag's name are you doing?" Korroc called. "*Step away from the child.*"

Caleth was at his shoulder a heartbeat later, glaive lowered, voice as level as Korroc's was loud. "What is going on here?"

The leader — the woman — turned slowly. Her eyes found Korroc's. Korroc had seen the eyes of mad horses before. He had seen the eyes of mad dwarves who had drunk the wrong brew in the wrong cave. He had not seen the eyes of a paladin of a good god gone over. He saw them now. The whites were too clear. The pupils were too small. There was nothing of cruelty in them — that was the worst of it — only the burning fixed certainty of a woman who had spent four days watching demons unmake her city and had decided, somewhere in the second day, that the legend was true and she would prove it.

She drew the child back against her chest. Her longsword came up to the child's throat.

"This is none of your business," she said. Her voice was steady. "We have to slay the demons. The child will help us slay the demons. Walk on."

"Walk on," echoed one of her men. He sounded farther gone than she did.

Korroc did not walk on.

He walked *toward* them.

He did not run. He walked. The plate creaked at every step. His shield came up across his body. His eyes were on the woman's eyes and his hammer was raised and he spoke as he came, low and even, in the same voice he had used on Horgus.

"You'll do no demon-killing today, lass. You'll do none. If you touch that child, I will put you down myself."

She did not lower the blade. Her men spread. One stepped behind her to put the child and her body between him and Korroc. The other came toward Caleth.

Thane had not made a sound. He had drifted to the left and was already gone from the corner of Korroc's eye, working his angle, where Thane always worked his angle. Korroc trusted him without looking.

The female knight's sword came around in an overhead arc at Korroc's head.

Korroc caught it on the shield. The impact rang. He did not flinch. He kept walking. He took another step — they were inside each other's reach now, plate to plate, the child between her arm and her ribs, the longsword caught wedged against his anvil-and-hammer sigil — and Korroc planted his shoulder under the shield and *pushed*.

He bull-rushed her.

He had not bull-rushed a creature his own size in twenty years. The technique had not left him. He felt the small precise transfer of weight from his hips through his shoulder into the shield-face, he felt the woman's stance break, he felt her foot scrape backward on the cobblestone as she gave ground, and he kept pushing. One step. Two. Three. Four.

Five.

He shoved her back ten feet.

Her grip on the child loosened. The child stumbled free with a small choked gasp. Korroc kept his shield up and his eyes on the woman and bellowed sideways without looking —

"*Run, lass! Run!*"

— and the child ran.

She ran toward the nearest cover, which happened to be Caleth, who had taken three long strides into the open at exactly the moment Korroc had bellowed and who had — Korroc registered it peripherally — *vanished*. The half-elf had blinked out of the air on one step and reappeared on the next, twenty feet away beside the running child, his hand coming down on her shoulder, his body turning to put himself between her and the knights.

"Come on, sweetheart," Caleth was saying, his voice as soft as Korroc had ever heard a paladin manage. "Come along. We're going to be safe over here, you and I. Come along now."

The female knight roared and stabbed at Korroc.

Korroc had been waiting for it. He had taken her shield, taken her balance, taken her weapon-arm out of her line — she was still going to come at him because madness did that, because once a paladin's oath had gone over the cliff there was nothing for them to do but fall — and the longsword came in low and fast and found the small gap at the bottom of his side-plate that no smith ever quite got tight, and Korroc felt the cold steel slide in.

He grunted.

He brought his hammer down on her sword-arm.

The arm broke. The longsword fell. The woman did not fall — she came inside the hammer's arc with a knee, slammed it into his thigh, took her broken arm back and brought her good arm up with a dagger he hadn't seen, and the dagger went for his throat above the gorget.

Korroc caught it on the rim of his shield.

The dagger skipped sideways.

He hammered her again.

This time she fell.

To his left he was distantly aware of Thane's daggers flashing — once, twice, in the small fast rhythm of the dwarf's blade-work — and a knight grunting and Thane stepping in and the knight grunting again and folding. Caleth, somewhere across the plaza with the child, had turned and stabbed the second man as he came at him; the man had stabbed back and caught Caleth in the ribs; Caleth had not gone down. Anevia, who had been holding the corner with her bow, had nocked and loosed an arrow that took the back-man through the shoulder a heartbeat before Thane finished him.

Korroc stood over the woman. She was still alive. She was looking up at him with the burning fixed eyes that had not yet realized they had been beaten. Her broken arm was at the wrong angle. Her good hand was scrabbling for the fallen sword.

She tried to stand.

He did not let her. He set his boot on the sword. He set his shield-rim, gently — gently — against her throat, just enough to hold her down. He did not bring the hammer down again.

"Lass," he said. "Lass. *Look at me.*"

Her eyes wavered. For one moment they were the eyes of a person.

"It was a lie," Korroc said softly. "Whatever told you that legend was true was a lie. The demons fed it to you. Do you hear me? *They fed it to you.*"

Her face crumpled. The burning went out of her eyes.

For an instant — a clean instant — Korroc saw what she had been before four days had broken her. He saw the paladin of the Dawnflower who had volunteered for the Mendev crusade because the war was real and the wound was real and her god had asked it of her. He saw her face the way her mother had seen it once, the day she had first put on Sarenrae's tabard, smiling and afraid and certain.

Her good hand groped sideways and found his boot. Her fingers closed weakly on the leather above his greave.

"Brother," she whispered.

"Aye, sister."

"I — I —"

"Aye."

Her eyes closed.

The breath came out of her and did not come back in.

Korroc stood over her for a long moment. The plaza was very quiet now. The two men were on the cobblestones, dead. Thane was wiping his blades. Caleth was thirty feet away with the child pressed against his side, his free hand around her shoulders, his face turned away from the bodies.

A small voice. Thane.

"She'd have gone for the child again, cousin. The minute you turned your back."

"Aye."

"You did her a mercy."

"Aye. I know."

He stood there a moment longer.

Then he laid his hammer down on the cobblestone — carefully, the way a man sets down a tool he loves — and he knelt and began to drag the bodies together.

---

He did the work himself. He would not let anyone help him.

He laid the three of them in a row near the eastern wall of the shrine, where the rising sun would touch them when it touched anything else in this district. He crossed their arms on their breasts. He closed their eyes. He arranged their tabards so that the gold Sarenrae sunburst lay flat over each chest, as straight as he could make it.

He stood over them, his hammer at his hip, and he prayed.

He prayed in old Dwarven first, because that was the language his mother had prayed in when she had prayed over his father's empty grave forty years ago. Then he prayed in Common, because the dead in front of him had served a human goddess in a human tongue, and a paladin's last courtesy was to send the dead off in the tongue they would have wanted.

"Torag, Father of Forges. Sarenrae, Mother of the Dawn. Hear me. These were yours. They did not know what they did. Their desperation to do right drove them mad, and they died with their hands on a wrong they meant for a right. They served you long before they failed you. *Remember the long service.* Remember the volunteer-day. Remember the tabard freshly sewn. Remember the long road from the dawn that called them. Forgive them what they would never have done in a kinder world."

He paused.

"Forgive me," he added, quieter, "for what I had to do to stop them. I bear the weight. I do not put it down. Anvil and hammer. So be it."

He bowed his head a moment.

Then he turned away.

The others were waiting. Caleth stood with the child, his cloak around her shoulders against the cold of her shock. Anevia was a few paces off, her face very still. Thane was a watchful shape at the plaza's edge.

Nageru was just behind Caleth.

The aasimar had crossed the plaza without anyone noticing — even Thane had not noticed, Korroc was certain of it — and was standing now perhaps three paces behind the child, his hands open at his sides, his head bowed. He was not looking at the bodies. He was not looking at Caleth. He was looking at nothing, or at something only he could see, and his breathing had the slow deep rhythm Korroc had come to recognize as Nageru's prayer.

He had stood that way for the whole of the last rites.

Their eyes met for a moment. Nageru inclined his head a fraction. Korroc did not have a word for the look that passed between them, but he felt it. *Brother. Done well.* The aasimar had been holding the moment for him from the outside.

Korroc nodded back.

The child spoke for the first time.

Her voice was very small and very dry. "Are they dead?"

Caleth knelt to her level. "They are dead, sweetheart."

"Are they going to come back?"

"No, sweetheart. They aren't."

"Good." Her face did something complicated. "Are you going to kill me too?"

Caleth's expression broke. For one heartbeat Korroc saw the Spireborn discipline slip and the half-elf's face show what it was actually feeling — anger and grief and a fierce protective tenderness Korroc had not seen on Caleth before. Caleth covered it quickly. He took both her small hands in his.

"No, sweetheart. We're not going to kill you. We are not going to let *anyone* kill you. Will you tell me your name?"

A long pause.

"Klarah."

"Klarah. I am Caleth. This is Korroc. This is Thane. This is Anevia. This is Aravashniel. This is Horgus. And the tall one behind you is Nageru. We're going to take you somewhere safe. Will you tell me where your parents are?"

A longer pause.

Klarah did not answer. She turned and pointed down a side street.

They walked her there.

The house had been a small one. The roof had gone through the upstairs floor. There were two bodies in the rubble of what had been the kitchen — a man, a woman, both of them still in their nightshirts. They had been sleeping when the roof came down. They had not woken.

Klarah said, in her small small voice: "They've been sleeping. They won't wake up."

Korroc could not make himself speak.

It was Caleth who knelt beside her, and Caleth who said, with great patience, "They aren't going to wake up, sweetheart. I'm so sorry. They aren't going to wake up. I'm so sorry."

Klarah cried.

She cried into Caleth's shoulder for a long time. Korroc stood at the door of the ruined house with his shield on his back and his hammer at his hip and did not say anything. Nageru stood beside him. Anevia stood on his other side. They did not say anything either.

When the crying slowed, Korroc turned to Thane.

"Cousin."

Thane looked up at him. The dwarf-rogue's face was a hard quiet shape. "I know what you're going to say."

"She's coming with us."

"I know."

"You good with it?"

"Aye, cousin. I'm good with it."

Thane swore in Dwarven once, softly, and then he walked over to Klarah, knelt to her eye level the way Caleth had, and held out one weathered dwarven hand.

"Klarah," he said. "I'm Thane. I'm going to help carry you when you get tired. That's my job. Will you let me carry you when you get tired?"

She nodded, very small.

"Good." Thane stood. "Come along, then. We've a long walk yet."

Anevia scooped the child up. Klarah let herself be lifted, her face in Anevia's shoulder, her small fingers tight in the lapel of the woman's coat.

They walked on.

---

The walk to Anevia's home took the better part of an hour, and they did not speak more than a dozen words the whole way. Korroc did not have words. He kept seeing the Sarenrae woman's face at the end. He kept seeing Klarah's parents in the rubble. He kept seeing the column of smoke where the cathedral had been.

He prayed, silently, as they walked. He prayed to Torag in the small steady way a smith prays at the bellows. He prayed for the dead. He prayed for the city. He prayed for himself, briefly — the prayer of a tool asking the Smith to keep its edge through what was still coming — and then he stopped praying for himself, because that was always the prayer that ran the well dry the fastest.

The stone under his boots had a different feel today than it had ever had.

He had not, before today, thought of city-cobbles as *stone.* They had been pavement. They had been the path. You walked them; you didn't think about them. But today, climbing up through the broken Gate district behind Anevia's lead, Korroc could feel each broken slab of cobblestone the way he could feel the temper of a blade through his hammer. *This one cracked when the rift moved. This one was lifted and dropped by a demon's footfall. This one — this one is still sound, sound and good, my city is still sound under the wound —*

The stone in him was registering the stone of his city. It was, he realized with a small distant noticing, the first time it had ever done that aboveground.

He set it aside. Later. There would be time for later.

Anevia stopped.

"That's it," she said quietly. "The one with the red door."

The door was not red anymore. It had been red once. Now it was scorched and split halfway down its length, hanging from one hinge. The little house behind it stood, mostly — the roof had survived, the walls were intact — but the door spoke. Someone had come through that door not gently.

Anevia did not wait for the others.

She limped to the doorway and pushed the broken door open with the end of her crutch. She called, before anyone could stop her, in a voice that broke at the edge of itself:

"Irabeth! Irabeth, where are you?"

Korroc, three paces behind her, felt the world catch.

*Irabeth.*

The name Anevia had given them as the contact they had to reach. The Watch leader. The one above ground who had to know what they knew. The name had been a name, until now. The way Anevia had just said it was not the way one calls a name.

It was the way one calls a name.

Korroc looked sideways at Caleth.

Caleth was looking at Anevia with the small still face that meant the half-elf had just understood something he should have understood days ago. Their eyes met. Caleth nodded once, very small.

"Irabeth!" Anevia called again, deeper into the house. "Irabeth, are you here?"

No answer. The little house was quiet.

She moved through it, calling. Korroc and Caleth and Thane went in after her. Aravashniel and Horgus and Nageru held the door — Nageru carrying Klarah now, the child wrapped against his shoulder and quiet in a way Korroc could not read.

They went through the front room and into the small kitchen. They went through the kitchen and to the inner door.

Anevia opened it.

She made a strangled noise and backed out fast, and a beetle the size of a house cat came skittering out of the storeroom after her.

"Fire beetle," Thane snapped — he had identified it before Korroc had even raised his hammer, by something in its carapace or its eye-shine — "but its not right. It's been touched by the lower planes."

The beetle came at Anevia low and fast. Thane stepped past her in a single tight pivot and his right dagger went down into the thing's back at the seam between thorax and abdomen. The beetle convulsed. Caleth's glaive came over Korroc's shoulder and the leaf-blade punched straight through the beetle's body and pinned it to the wooden floor.

The thing twitched. Stilled.

Anevia patted herself down as if to brush something off. Her hand was shaking, slightly. "Filthy thing."

"You all right?" Thane asked.

"Aye. Fine. Filthy thing."

Korroc was already past her, into the storeroom, looking for what she had been looking for. A note, he assumed. A sign. Anything.

He turned to ask Anevia where Irabeth might leave a message, and saw it.

A shimmer in the air, behind her.

His shout did not make it out of his throat.

The ranseur crashed into Anevia from behind. Its long polearm-head took her in the side under her ribs. She buckled — her knees almost went — but she did not fall. The blade came out red. The half-orc holding it was suddenly visible, a tall pale-skinned half-orc in dark layered leathers, his tusks chipped, his eyes a flat unpleasant yellow.

He shouted something in Orc and stabbed again.

Thane was on him already. The dwarf-rogue had moved the moment the ranseur had appeared, and his dagger went up under the half-orc's ribs in a strike that should have ended the fight then and there — but the half-orc was armored under the leather, plate-vest at the chest, and Thane's blade skipped sideways on the metal.

Caleth's glaive came up. Caleth thrust. The thrust went wide.

Korroc came in.

He came in low and hard and brought the warhammer down in a two-handed strike from his hip, the kind of swing that broke armor and the bones beneath, and the half-orc — Korroc would have to grant him this — *dodged*. The half-orc was fast. The half-orc was faster than half-orcs ought to be. He twisted sideways inside the hammer's arc, his free hand already shaping a glyph, and the spell came off his fingers.

Three darts of pale violet light.

One took Korroc in the chest-plate. One took Caleth in the shoulder. One took Thane in the arm.

Magic missile.

A real caster. Korroc's stomach turned. The half-orc was not a thug who had been sent to ambush them. The half-orc was a trained operative.

Korroc slapped his palm against his sternum and let healing roll out in a wave around the four of them. The dart-burns eased. Anevia gasped — Korroc saw the ranseur-wound at her side begin to knit — but the wound was deep, and he did not have the grace today to close it fully. He closed what he could.

The half-orc cast again. Korroc felt the cold finger of fear reach for his chest — the spell he had felt a hundred times in the underdark, the spell every cultist tried — and his oath swallowed it, the way his oath had always swallowed it, and he came in again.

The half-orc dodged again. Korroc's hammer crashed into the storeroom door behind where he had been. Wood splintered.

"He's not standing still!" Caleth snapped. "He'll wear us down. *Cage him.*"

Korroc understood. He shifted, took a step left to block the half-orc's retreat path toward the back room. Thane shifted right to block the kitchen door. Caleth held the center with his glaive at threat-range, forcing the half-orc to commit to either dwarf if he wanted to break the line.

The half-orc cast a third time. A summoning. Korroc saw the somatics — the small cupping gesture, the dark word — and knew what was coming a heartbeat before it came.

A dretch.

Behind him.

Korroc felt the small wet shift of air and the foul smell at his neck and he ducked on pure instinct as two clawed hands closed where his head had been. The dretch had appeared in the storeroom doorway *behind* him. The half-orc had put a demon at his back.

He spun.

The dretch's claws raked across his back-plate — found no purchase — and one talon found the small gap at the lower edge of his cuirass and bit into the small of his back. Korroc felt the cold wrong slide of demon-claw on his skin. He felt the dretch's grin, somehow, in the air around him.

Caleth shouted something that might have been "Korroc!" The half-orc loosed a scorching ray at the half-elf and Caleth grunted and the smell of burned cloth filled the room.

Korroc reached down inside himself for the second answer.

He could feel the stone — the stone of his city — under the floorboards of Anevia's house. He could feel it the way he had felt it walking the broken streets. *Stone in his veins. Stone in the floor. Stone calling to stone.* He pressed his free hand flat to the wooden floor and reached *through* the wood, and the stone beneath answered.

The hammer came up on the recoil and came down on the dretch's chest with the weight of his arm and the weight of the *floor* and the weight of the deep stone of Kenabres, the city that did not belong to demons.

The dretch caved.

The hammer-head went through it and into the wood beneath and stopped. The dretch's body separated around the strike in two foul steaming halves. Korroc had not known he could do that. Korroc had not known the stone would *answer* him so directly. He stood for a half-second with his hammer wedged in the floor, panting, the dretch dissolving around it, and then he yanked the hammer free and turned for the half-orc.

The half-orc was already going down. Caleth and Thane had closed on him while the half-orc was watching Korroc — Caleth's glaive in the chest, Thane's dagger in the throat — and the half-orc folded in a clatter of plate.

Quiet.

Korroc looked at the wreckage of Anevia's storeroom floor. He looked at the foul puddle that had been a dretch. He looked at his hammer, which had a few foul shreds clinging to its face.

He looked at Caleth.

Caleth, leaning on his glaive, was looking at the smear on the floor with a careful expression that Korroc could not quite read. The half-elf had seen Korroc hit it. Caleth had seen what Korroc had done.

"Cousin," Caleth said quietly. "That was —"

"Aye." Korroc was breathing hard. "Aye. I — aye."

"Was that the —"

"The Stonelord's strike. Aye. First time it's — first time it answered like that."

Caleth let out a breath that was nearly a laugh. "Saint's *mercy.*"

"Aye."

Anevia limped past them. She was bleeding. She was pale. She did not stop to be examined. She went straight to the inner storeroom, the one the fire beetle had come out of, and she crouched in a corner and dug in a small alcove behind a row of clay jars.

She came out with a pouch.

She came out with a note.

She unfolded the note. She read it. Her hand went to her mouth.

"What?" Thane said.

Anevia did not answer for a long moment. When she did, her voice was steadier than Korroc would have expected.

"She left a note. She's alive. She made it out. She left a note."

"What does it say?"

"It says, *Meet at the Defender's Heart.*"

Caleth's eyes closed for a second in something that was almost a prayer. Korroc, with no preamble, walked over and laid his hand on Anevia's shoulder.

She turned her face into his palm for a moment.

"Aye, lass," Korroc said quietly. "Aye. She's alive. We'll find her."

"She's — Korroc."

"Aye."

"She's my *wife.* I should have told you. I should have said. I —"

"You don't owe us anything you didn't owe yesterday. We'll find her. You hear me? *We will find her.*"

Anevia nodded. She nodded again. Her face stayed in his hand a moment longer. Then she straightened up and was Anevia again, sharp and direct and on a broken leg, and held up the pouch.

"Potions. Three. She left them for us. She knew I'd come back here."

Caleth took the pouch. He tested them by resonance — Korroc had come to know that small narrowing of the half-elf's eyes — and nodded. "Healing. Two healing. One that's — one that's good, I'm not sure what. We'll keep them close."

Korroc looked at Anevia again. "Wife," he said.

"Aye."

"It suits her, having you for one. I don't know her. But it suits her."

Anevia laughed, just once, raw and short. "Get me to that inn, dwarf."

"Aye, lass."

"Get me to my *wife.*"

"Aye."

---

It was Horgus, of all people, who suggested they not run for the inn.

He had come into the storeroom at the end of the fight, white-faced again but unhurt, and he looked at the wreckage and the bleeding Anevia and the half-elf with the burn-mark down his shoulder, and he said in a voice that was almost not snobbish:

"This is no place for us. They'll raze this place tonight. The Defender's Heart is halfway across the city. We need to go to my home. It's bigger. It's defensible. It's an hour from here. We can rest. We can be safe for a night. Then in the morning, we make the longer journey."

Caleth looked at Korroc.

Korroc looked at the man who had wanted to leave Anevia in the dark four days ago. Korroc looked at the man who had been a dog's chew-toy at the morning. Korroc looked at the man who had just, for the first time on this whole impossible journey, opened his mouth to *offer something* instead of complain.

"Aye, merchant," Korroc said. "We'll come to your home."

Horgus blinked, as if he had not quite expected to be agreed with.

"Right," he said. He cleared his throat. "Right. This way."

He led them through the streets to Gwerm Manor.

---

The manor was a miracle.

Korroc would not have believed it possible — and he had said his prayers for the day already — but the manor was entire. The front door stood wide open. Inside, looters had been through; the silver was gone, the tapestries were gone, the carved chairs were gone. But the structure was whole. The walls stood. The roof was on. The kitchen was unburned. The cellar was sealed.

Horgus walked the manor in a slow careful sweep, room to room, his face working through expressions Korroc had not seen before. When he came back to the great hall he was very quiet.

"My guards are gone," he said. "My servants are gone. They fled. I cannot blame them. The looters did not bother with the bottom of the house. My cellar stores are — my cellar stores are intact."

"Aye," Korroc said.

"There is food."

"There is."

"There is — there is space. There are beds. The shutters can be closed. We can keep light out. We can look empty from the street. They will not look in if we look empty."

"Aye, merchant. That's the way of it."

Horgus nodded, twice, and looked at the floor.

"Welcome," he said, very stiffly. "Welcome to my home."

Caleth bowed to him. It was a small bow, but it was the bow the Spire taught for elder peers, and Korroc saw Horgus's mouth move a moment without sound before the merchant turned and went to organize the kitchen.

They closed the shutters. They lit no fire — Caleth set out one cantrip-flame in a copper dish on the kitchen table — and they sat at that long pale table and ate whatever Horgus brought from the cellar. Hard bread. Aged cheese. Cured sausage. A jar of pickled cave-mushroom that Korroc thought he recognized as Sull's people's work — Horgus had been buying from the underdark trade routes for years without knowing it.

Korroc made a slow round of the company. He laid healing on Anevia's ranseur-wound until it knit fully. He laid healing on the burn on Caleth's shoulder, and on the small cuts along Thane's arms, and on the deeper wound under his own armpit that he had been ignoring since the morning. Klarah he did not touch directly — she was not hurt, only stunned — but he laid the smallest possible hand on her brow and let a thread of grace pass through, just enough to ease the breathing. She did not flinch. She did not look up. She was sitting in Nageru's lap, with the aasimar's arm around her shoulders, and she was sucking the corner of a piece of bread.

Nageru had been silent through the whole meal.

When Korroc passed close to him, the aasimar caught his eye. He inclined his head, fractionally.

"Brother," Nageru said, very quietly. "Sit with us a moment."

Korroc sat.

The aasimar looked down at Klarah. Klarah was almost asleep against him. Her bread was forgotten in her hand.

"She will need a name," Nageru said.

"She has one. Klarah."

"She will need another. A name we use for her. A small name, kind." A pause. "A name a parent uses."

"Aye." Korroc felt his throat catch. He had not thought of that. "Aye, brother. You're right."

"It does not have to be soon. It will come."

"Aye."

A long quiet.

Then Nageru said, in a voice softer still:

"I am sorry I have been quiet today."

Korroc looked at him. "You've nothing to be sorry for."

"I have been —" Nageru paused. He did not often pause. He paused now. "There is much in this city that — that *speaks* to what I am, brother. Too much. I have been listening. I have not been entirely with you. I am sorry for it."

"What does it say?" Korroc asked, because the answer mattered more than the asking did.

"It says many things," Nageru said. "Mostly it says that there has been a great breaking of order here. Doors that should not have been opened. Walls that should not have come down. The thresholds of this city have been violated past counting. My blood — my blood feels each one."

Korroc nodded slowly. He did not entirely understand it. He understood enough.

"Will it ease?"

"It must ease. I will ease it." Nageru smiled very faintly. "But for today, brother — for today, my fists are slow and my mind is half a step behind itself. Forgive it."

"There is nothing to forgive."

"Then thank you."

Nageru bowed his head. Klarah, asleep against him, did not stir.

Korroc stood and went to take first watch.

---

He woke before dawn — before dawn, in a city that had not yet decided whether dawn still meant anything — and the manor was quiet in the way of a house full of people who had not slept enough.

He prayed his morning prayers in the kitchen. Torag answered them, quietly, the way Torag had taken to answering quietly now that the world had broken. His grace returned. The well filled. He felt the small clean fullness of a paladin restored, and he stood in the cold kitchen for a moment to feel it.

Then he turned and went to wake the others.

He stopped in the doorway of the great hall.

Aravashniel and Caleth were already up.

They were standing at the far end of the hall, by the cold hearth. Aravashniel had his bandaged hand on Caleth's sleeve. The two of them stood close, the way two men stand who are saying something that no one else is meant to hear, and their faces were tilted slightly toward each other and their voices were low enough that Korroc, twenty feet away, could not catch a single word.

He stopped.

He had walked in on a private moment.

He stepped back into the hallway and waited. He waited the way a man waits at the door of a forge when another smith is doing something delicate that should not be interrupted.

He could see them, dimly, through the doorway. Aravashniel's bandaged hand was on Caleth's sleeve. Caleth was listening. Caleth was very still. Aravashniel said something short. Caleth nodded, once. Aravashniel said something else. Caleth nodded again. Then Caleth said something quietly back, and Aravashniel — Aravashniel laid his other hand on Caleth's shoulder, briefly, and there was a thing in the gesture Korroc would not have a name for.

A long quiet between them. Then Caleth turned and walked toward the doorway where Korroc stood waiting.

Korroc met him there.

"Korroc," Caleth said. The half-elf's face was composed but very still. "We have another stop to make."

Korroc looked at him.

He looked past him at Aravashniel, who was standing alone by the cold hearth, his half-eyes closed.

He looked back at Caleth.

Caleth, who had carried the silver scale and the unholy symbols and the small careful Spireborn discretions that Korroc had come to trust without ever quite understanding. Caleth, whose word on a thing of magic was the word Korroc took. Caleth, who had stood beside him in the underdark and the maze and the wound-city and had not once asked Korroc to trust him without earning it first.

Korroc nodded.

"Is it on the way?" he asked.

"It is."

"Aye, then. We make the stop."

He did not ask what stop. He did not ask why.

He turned and went to get the others ready.

---

The three of them left the manor in the second hour after dawn. Korroc, Thane, Caleth. Horgus stayed with Klarah in the great hall — Horgus had said, gruffly, that someone had to make sure the child ate, and Korroc had heard the small embarrassed kindness under the bluster and had thanked him for it. Aravashniel stayed too, for this first errand — the recon was light, the company small, the elder wizard's strength still half-restored. Anevia held the back door with her bow across her knees.

Nageru stayed.

He had risen before any of them and he had taken up a position by the inner door of the great hall, his fists open at his sides, his head lowered. When Korroc had passed him on the way out he had said only "I will hold the manor while you walk, brother." Korroc had clapped him on the shoulder and gone.

The walk to Nyserian Manor took them less than half an hour, through streets that were quieter than the morning streets had been. The looters were still mostly asleep. The demons were elsewhere. They moved fast and they moved low and they did not stop.

Nyserian Manor, when they reached it, was a ruin.

A great track of broken cobbles ran straight through the front facade — Korroc, who had seen the path Thane had described from the rooflines, recognized it as the wake of a demon the size of a barn — and the front of the manor was simply *gone*, replaced by a heap of shattered marble and timber and the wallpaper of what had been an entrance hall. The roof had collapsed. The remaining walls leaned in at angles a builder would not have stood under for love or coin.

Thane stopped Caleth at the property line with one quiet hand on his sleeve.

"Look at it, half-elf. A strong wind blows that over."

Caleth nodded slowly. He stood for a long moment, his eyes moving across the wreckage in the careful cataloguing pattern Korroc had come to recognize. Then he let out a breath that was almost a laugh, the dry laugh of a man finding the wrong answer to be also the answer.

"One safe house off the list, then."

"One off the list, aye," Korroc said.

"S. V. is going to have a thinner net than they planned for."

"Aye."

They walked the property's perimeter once anyway, looking in through what had been windows, in case some Templar with more patience than sense had thought to bury something in the wreckage. There was nothing. The demon's path had not been delicate. Whatever Nyserian had held was gone.

They turned back for Gwerm Manor.

---

It was Aravashniel who spoke when they returned.

The elder wizard was sitting at the kitchen table with Klarah opposite him, drawing slow patterns on the wood with a finger. He looked up when they came in. He looked at Caleth. He looked at Korroc.

"And?"

"Gone," Korroc said. "Demon's path through it. One safe house off."

"And the other stop?"

It was Caleth who answered, after a small pause. "Still ahead. We're going to make for the inn directly — by way of the library, since it's on the road."

Aravashniel's face went very still.

"The Blackwing."

"Aye."

"It is —" Aravashniel's voice caught. He recovered it. "It is good of you. Thank you."

Caleth nodded once.

Korroc, watching, did not understand the weight in the elder's voice. The Blackwing Library was a library. He knew it from passing — a long low building down toward the river, with bronze doors and an ill-tempered keeper who had not let dwarves past the threshold without three forms of identification. He had no love for the place. He understood that scholars loved it. He understood that Aravashniel, a scholar, would grieve to see it harmed.

He did not understand the weight he saw on the old man's face.

He filed it.

They ate again. They prepared. Korroc topped up every healing he had on every wound he could find — none of them were truly hurt now, but he wanted every reserve full. Anevia tightened the strap of her splint and slung her bow. Aravashniel stood up with the small straight-backed pride of a man who had decided to walk to a thing he loved and not be carried there.

Horgus came out to the front steps as they left.

"The child is sleeping," he said. "I will not let her wake alone."

"Aye, merchant," Korroc said. "We know it."

Nageru remained at the inner door. He had not moved since the morning. He raised his eyes to Korroc as the party assembled in the front hall.

"Brother," he said.

"Aye."

"Walk well."

"Aye. Hold the manor."

"I will."

The aasimar bowed his head. Korroc went out.

---

The streets were busier than they had been at dawn.

Not with people, mostly — with things. Lesser demons crawling along walls in places they should not have been, easily avoided if you saw them in time. A pack of fiendish rats that scattered into a sewer grating at Thane's whistle. A long-fingered thing perched on a chimney that watched them pass without coming down. Caleth had been mapping the route they would take with Aravashniel's quiet guidance, and the route they took was a route that bent and zagged through alleys to avoid the open boulevards where the larger things were known to walk.

They were perhaps half the way to the library when they came on the barricade.

Wagons had been pushed together across the mouth of an avenue, three deep, lashed with rope and chocked with stone. Behind the wagons, perhaps a dozen people in mismatched armor — laborer's leather, a dented helm, a smith's apron worn over a wood-axe — stood with weapons at the ready. They were filthy. They were bloody. Their faces had the flat exhausted hardness of people who had been awake for too many days.

Korroc raised a hand as the party approached.

"Friends!" he called. "Friends, we mean no —"

The faces behind the barricade did not change. They did not call back. They watched.

Above the barricade, three small black shapes detached themselves from a broken cornice and dropped into hovering flight ten feet up. Bat-winged. Fork-tailed. The size of large cats. Their faces were small wrinkled malevolent things and their eyes were yellow.

"Quasits," Caleth said quietly. "*They're controlling the people, Korroc. Don't hurt the people.*"

Korroc's stomach dropped.

He understood. He had seen it before in the underdark — the small wet attachment of a lesser demon's will on a wearied mortal mind — but he had not seen it on this scale. Twelve people. Twelve frightened tired Kenabres folk who had built a barricade to be safe behind, and three small black things had come and made the safety into a trap. The men and women behind the wagons were not enemies. The men and women behind the wagons were *prisoners* of the small things flying above them.

"Hey!" Caleth shouted. He stepped forward into the open with his hands up. "*Leave these people alone!*"

The quasits chittered. The men and women behind the barricade hefted their weapons and began to climb over the wagons.

Their faces were the worst of it. There was no anger in them. There was nothing in them at all. The first one to come over the barricade — a thin woman in a baker's apron with a meat-cleaver in her hand — looked at Caleth with eyes that were entirely empty, and she came at him.

She swung. The cleaver caught Caleth on the shoulder. He grunted but did not hit her back.

He blinked instead. The half-elf vanished from the spot he had been standing and reappeared past the line of advancing innocents, ten feet up the avenue, his glaive already coming around in a thrust at the nearest quasit.

The blade took the small demon in the wing. The thing shrieked. It buzzed sideways and clawed at Caleth's face. The other two banked.

Thane was already moving. The dwarf-rogue had gone wide to the left, slipping along the side of a half-fallen building, taking the long route around the controlled innocents to come at the quasits from below. He gestured to Korroc with two fingers: *I'll work the flank. Go through.*

Korroc went through.

He charged.

The innocents were between him and the quasits. He did not slow. He did not strike them. He shouldered the baker-woman aside as gently as a charging dwarf in plate could shoulder anyone — she stumbled and stayed up — and then he was through the line, his hammer already coming around in an overhead arc at the quasit Caleth had wounded.

The hammer hit. The quasit *cracked*. Its wing folded. It dropped to the cobblestones spitting and clawed at Korroc's greaves with its small wicked talons.

Korroc felt poison. He felt it bright and sharp the way one feels a hornet sting, but the oath swallowed it the way the oath had swallowed the fear-spell, and the poison did not take. He brought the hammer down a second time. The quasit stopped being a quasit.

The other two were on Caleth. The half-elf was holding them off with the glaive's reach, fending strikes, but one had gotten inside the blade's arc and was raking at his face. Thane came up under it with one dagger and the quasit thrashed and spun and Caleth's glaive caught it on the recovery, pinned it, and Thane's other dagger finished it.

The third banked away from Korroc and tried to climb. Korroc had it at maximum reach.

He hefted the hammer in one hand and threw.

It was not, by any technical measure, a thing one did with a warhammer. But the dwarf in him had thrown a hammer before — at a runaway bellows-cord, at a stuck door, at his brother once when they were boys — and the small precise twist of the wrist that put the head over the haft came back to him from a place beneath thought. The hammer turned once in the air and the head took the quasit square in the chest.

It dropped like a stone.

Korroc walked over and picked his hammer up.

The innocents had stopped moving the moment the third quasit had fallen. The bloodless empty look had gone out of their faces. The thin baker-woman stood blinking at her own cleaver as if she had never seen it before, and her hand opened and the cleaver fell to the cobblestones with a hollow clang.

She looked up at Korroc. She made a sound that was not a word. She started crying.

"Easy, friend," Korroc said. "Easy. You're back. You're back now."

The others were coming down off the barricade. Some sat down on the cobbles where they had been standing. One man, an older one in a smith's leather apron, walked up to Korroc and tried to speak and could not.

"I know, smith. I know."

The smith touched Korroc's vambrace once, lightly, and then turned away and walked back to his neighbors and began, in a thin shaking voice, to organize them. *Find a building. Stay together. Stay off the street. Move.* They moved, eventually, in twos and threes. The baker-woman went last, looking back at Korroc with eyes that were her own again, and lifted one hand in something like a wave.

He waved back. He did not trust his voice.

They moved on.

---

The Blackwing Library had been a low long building of pale stone roofed in green-patinated copper. Korroc remembered it from passing — bronze double doors with a sun carved into each leaf, a flight of broad shallow steps, two stone griffins on the corners of the front facade.

They came up to it through what had been a small square that fronted the river.

The doors were gone. The roof was gone. Half of the long pale building was gone. One hall stood — the western wing, by Korroc's reckoning — and that was all. The rest had been opened to the sky in a way that suggested something very large had walked across the top of it and let it fall in. One of the stone griffins lay broken at the foot of the steps. The other was gone entirely.

Aravashniel made a sound.

It was a small sound — not a cry, not a word — but it was the sound of a man whose chest had just been split open without warning, and Korroc turned at it. Aravashniel had stopped at the edge of the square. His half-eyes were fixed on the ruin. His bandaged hand had come up to his mouth. His legs were giving.

Caleth caught him.

The half-elf was beside the elder wizard before Korroc could move, one arm under Aravashniel's shoulder, holding him upright with the easy competent strength of a man who had been waiting to do exactly this since the morning. Aravashniel sagged against him. He did not weep. His face did something worse — it went flat, became the face of a man who could not hold his face — and his free hand came up and gripped Caleth's sleeve very tightly.

He said something Korroc did not catch. Caleth bent his head close. Caleth said something back, quietly.

Aravashniel sank to his knees on the cobblestones.

Caleth knelt with him.

Korroc looked away. He looked at Thane. Thane's face was very still. Korroc looked at Anevia. Anevia was looking at the ruined library with the careful watch of an agent, but her eyes flicked to Aravashniel and softened, and then flicked back.

Korroc watched asThane slowly moved to the steps. He had to do the job.

He climbed the broad shallow steps. He stopped at the threshold. The one standing hall opened off to the west, and from inside that hall, faintly, voices could be heard, even from where Korroc stood.

Voices and the small sound of books being moved.

Thane ghosted through what had been the doorway. He was gone for perhaps a minute. He came back and his face was a tight grim shape.

"Three," Thane said. "A human in armor. Two tieflings. They've got four — four people tied up. Librarians, looks like. They're stacking books around them."

"Stacking?"

"Aye." Thane's mouth was a hard line. "Stacking. Like — like a bonfire."

Korroc looked back at Caleth, who was still kneeling with Aravashniel at the base of the steps. Caleth's eyes met his. The half-elf understood without needing to be told. Caleth helped Aravashniel to a sitting position against the broken griffin and stood, glaive in hand, and came up the steps.

Anevia followed, bow nocked.

Korroc set his shield. He took three slow breaths.

"Right, then," he said softly. "Let's clean this place out."

They went in.

---

It went wrong faster than Korroc had imagined it could.

Thane went first because Thane always went first into a room. He was three steps into the standing hall when his boot caught a small stone — one small stone, in the dust, in the rubble, the only stone in a thousand stones that he would have caught — and the small stone skittered ahead of him across the flagstones with the sharp clear sound of a coin dropped in a chapel.

Every face in the hall turned.

The human in armor — a knight of some kind, plate-armored, mace at his hip, beard streaked with soot — spat a word. The two tieflings moved. They came for Thane in a fast practiced pincer, blades out, faces hard.

Caleth blinked.

He vanished from beside Korroc and reappeared ten feet into the hall, past the tieflings, his eyes already on the bound librarians. Korroc saw the half-elf's face change — the small specific narrowing he made when he had just seen something he had not expected to see — and then the knight in armor was on Caleth, mace coming around in a brutal overhand.

The mace caught Caleth on the shoulder. Caleth grunted and stumbled. Blood ran down his arm.

The knight hit him again before he could recover. Caleth's eyes rolled back. He dropped.

He dropped *all the way.*

Caleth lay on the flagstones and did not move and Korroc heard himself make a sound that was not a word, not a prayer, not anything that could be written down. A roar from his throat that came up out of his belly the way a roar comes up out of a smith who has just seen a finished blade shatter on the quench.

He charged.

He charged *past* the tieflings, who were grappling with Thane, who were not his problem. He shouldered through the gap between them with the full weight of plate and dwarven fury and one of them got a stab on him as he passed — a deep stab, into the gap below his shoulder, blood, *blood* — and Korroc did not feel it because Korroc was already past them and on the knight with the mace and the hammer was coming down in a strike that would have ended a horse.

The knight parried it.

The knight had — somehow, somehow — gotten the mace up in time, and the hammer-haft cracked against the mace's haft and the strike went sideways, and Caleth was on the flagstones at Korroc's feet and not breathing.

*He's not breathing.*

Korroc dropped to one knee. The knight stepped back, raised the mace for another strike — Korroc would have to take the strike, he had no choice, the choice was take the strike or let Caleth die —

He took the strike.

The mace came down on his pauldron. The pauldron dented. Pain bloomed in his shoulder. He did not move. He pressed his free hand flat on Caleth's chest. He called up everything he had.

And the thing inside him that had been a Stonelord's stone-strike that morning in Anevia's house — the thing that was beneath that, deeper, older, the thing that was the Life Oracle's gift he had been a Life Oracle for fifteen years and had never once seen *do this* —

The thing answered.

He felt his body change.

His skin lit. His armor went hot — went bright — went *transparent*, almost, the plate ghosting around a body that was no longer a body. His outline blurred. His arms were arms but they were not flesh anymore. They were — they were *fire.* Golden-white fire. The fire of a forge at its cleanest, the fire of the moment a blade comes out true. It did not burn him. It did not burn his armor. It was him and the armor was him and the fire was him.

The fire was *life.*

He laid both burning hands on Caleth's chest and the life poured out of him into the half-elf the way water pours into a dry basin. He felt Caleth's breath come back. He felt the half-elf's heart pick up. He felt the cracked rib that the mace had broken find its place and seal.

Caleth's eyes opened.

"Saint's *mercy,*" Caleth whispered, looking up at him.

"On your feet, half-elf."

"Korroc — what are you —"

"On your *feet.*"

Caleth stood. He stood on shaking legs but he stood. His glaive came up.

The knight stared at Korroc.

It was the first time, Korroc realized later, that he had seen anything like fear in the man's eyes. Whatever foul certainty had carried the knight through the morning — whatever broken oath had set him to walling living people in with books for a burning — had not prepared him for a dwarf made of forge-fire standing over the man he had just dropped.

The knight swung at him anyway.

The mace took Korroc — took the *fire-form* of Korroc — square in the chest, in what should have been the place of his sternum. There was a crack, and a flare, and Korroc felt the fire-form *spend itself* in the place the blow had landed, the way a hot wire shorts when something cold touches it.

The fire-form collapsed.

He was a dwarf in plate again, kneeling on the flagstones, with the dent in his cuirass smoking faintly and his ears ringing and his side bleeding hard.

The knight raised the mace again.

Caleth's hand came up *over* Korroc's shoulder and he touched the knight's chest. Caleth's hand was glowing — the small specific glow of his smite-and-shocking-grasp signature — and the air around the knight flashed white and the knight's plate seared and the knight rocked backward.

He did not fall.

Behind them, Thane had finished one tiefling and was working the second. The second tiefling was already stumbling — Thane was that good, Thane was always that good in a room this tight — and Thane's blade went under the ribs and the tiefling folded.

A small voice from across the hall.

"Drop."

The four bound librarians, who had been bound only loosely against the stacked books, had not been bound at all. Korroc would understand it later — *they had untied themselves while the knight was busy with us* — and one of them, a slight figure in scholar's grey, had raised both hands and a spell came off her fingers and the knight in front of Caleth caught fire.

The knight howled. The knight swung wildly. The mace came down on Caleth again. Caleth did not fall.

But the knight did not fall either. He shook the burning hands off himself, somehow, and brought the mace around at Korroc — still kneeling, still half-dazed — and Korroc saw the strike coming and could not get the shield up in time.

A new voice. A new spell.

A wash of golden warmth flooded through Korroc — *not his own*, this one — and his head cleared, and his shield came up, and the mace bounced.

Korroc looked up.

One of the librarians — not the one who had cast the fire, an older man, white-bearded, half his face bandaged — was lowering his hand. He had cast a cure-light-wounds across half the hall, into Korroc, without ever quite looking at him.

The older librarian's eyes met Korroc's for a heartbeat. They were sharp eyes. Scholar's eyes.

He nodded, once, to Korroc.

Korroc had no time to nod back. He came up off his knee with the hammer in both hands and brought it around in the lateral sweep that came up out of the hip, and the warhammer caught the knight in the small of the back and the knight folded with a sound like wet rope snapping.

Caleth's glaive came down on top of him.

The knight stopped being a knight.

Quiet.

Korroc stood, breathing hard. Caleth stood beside him, breathing harder. Thane was walking across the hall, blades out, checking each tiefling body in case. Anevia was at the doorway, bow nocked, scanning the empty bookshelves beyond for anything else.

The four librarians stood in a small huddled knot in the center of the hall, surrounded by the books they had been stacked among. The slight grey-robed one was crying, very quietly. The white-bearded older one was straightening his robes. He looked old, but his back was very straight.

Aravashniel had come up the steps without anyone noticing. He stood now in the doorway of the hall, leaning heavily on Anevia, his half-eyes wide and wet.

He looked at the white-bearded librarian.

The white-bearded librarian looked at him.

Something passed between them. Korroc, watching from across the hall through the haze of pain and adrenaline and the small distant noticing that he had just transformed into a being of pure life-energy for the first time in his life, did not catch what passed between them. He saw the recognition. He saw both old men's faces change, very briefly, in a way Korroc could not read.

Then Aravashniel walked into the hall, slow, leaning on Anevia, and laid a hand on the white-bearded man's shoulder.

The white-bearded man laid his hand over Aravashniel's.

They did not speak. They stood there a moment.

Caleth, beside Korroc, was very still.

"Aye," Korroc said quietly, mostly to himself. "Aye. Friends, then. He has friends here."

He turned and walked, slowly, to a fallen bookshelf and sat down on it.

He did not stand up again for a while.

He looked at his hands.

He turned them over, slow. He half-expected them still to be light. They were hands. Smith's hands, callused and dust-bloody, the small puckered burn-scar on his right wrist where he had been splattered with hot pig-iron at sixteen and still bore the mark.

Just hands.

The fire was gone. Whatever had answered him had gone back to wherever it lived, and it had left him standing in his own plate again in a ruined library on a ruined day.

Outside, somewhere in the broken city, a building came down with a long slow grinding sound that ended in a soft cough of dust. Inside the standing hall, the dust from the fight had not finished settling. It drifted slowly through the shaft of wrong-colored light from the broken ceiling, falling on the dead, on the scattered books, on the four librarians who had not yet moved from where they had stood up out of their ropes.

Aravashniel and the white-bearded librarian still stood together. They had not spoken yet. They might not, for a long time.

Caleth came over without saying anything and sat down on the flagstone beside Korroc. The half-elf's hand came across and laid itself on Korroc's pauldron — gentle, brief — and then withdrew. Caleth set his glaive across his knees and looked at nothing.

Thane finished his sweep of the hall and came back. He did not say anything either. He sat down on a fallen stone block and began, with great care, to wipe each of his daggers — the right blade first, the left blade after — on a strip of cloth he had cut from a dead tiefling's cloak. *No Stonevein had ever sheathed a wet blade*, and no Stonevein ever would.

No one spoke.

Anevia held the doorway with her bow still nocked, her face turned out toward the broken square they would have to cross to get back to anywhere, watching the wrong-colored sky.

Korroc looked down at his hands again. He laid them, slowly, palms down on his knees. He felt the stone of the flagstone through the leather of his greaves. He felt the deep stone of Kenabres under the flagstone. He did not reach for it. He did not have anything to ask of it yet.

The dust kept settling.

Outside, the city was burning still.
