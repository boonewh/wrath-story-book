# The Vision Made Flesh
*Chapter Six — The Blade That Chooses*

The Defender's Heart smelled of woodsmoke and wet wool and the particular sourness of too many frightened people sleeping in too small a space, and Caleth, who had slept four nights in it now, had stopped noticing all of it but the woodsmoke. The woodsmoke he kept. It meant a roof. It meant the city had not, despite everything, gone entirely to ash.

He sat at a corner table with his spellbook open and a cup of something the innkeeper called tea cooling at his elbow, and he watched the common room fill and empty and fill again with the slow tidal rhythm of a refugee house at dusk. Crusaders off the wall, faces grey. Children who did not cry, which was worse than children who did. A woman mending the same sleeve she had been mending for three days, because the mending was a thing she could finish, and almost nothing else was.

Four days of this. Four days of carrying water, of digging, of hauling the dead from cellars and the living from worse, of standing in line behind Korroc while the dwarf laid his hands on burned men and gave them an hour they would not otherwise have had. Useful work. Honest work. The kind of work that let a man feel, briefly, that the world could be set right one small stone at a time.

Caleth knew better. The world could not be set right one stone at a time. But the men around him needed to believe it could, and so he carried water with the rest, and he was careful never to let the arithmetic show on his face.

Across the room, Thane came down from the loft with a long shape wrapped in oilcloth across his arms, and the arithmetic stopped.

Caleth closed his spellbook.

---

The dwarf had stowed it days ago and not touched it since — the sword they had carried up out of the dark, the holy blade with the dead saint's name. *Radiance.* Thane had wrapped it in oilcloth the way a man wraps something he does not want to think about, and had pushed it under the loft bedding, and had not thought about it. Caleth had noticed. Caleth noticed most things Thane tried not to think about; it was one of the small currencies of their friendship.

Now the dwarf laid the bundle on the table across from him and stood looking at it with his arms folded.

"You're going to look at that thing eventually," Caleth said.

"I'm looking at it now."

"You're looking at the cloth."

Thane grunted and unwrapped it.

The sword came out of the oilcloth and the lamplight came off it, and Caleth's eye — the eye his foster parents had trained on iconography from the day they first put a brush in his small hand — moved over it the way it moved over anything made with love. A longsword of good steel, plain in the fittings, no jewels, no gilt, nothing that announced itself. The kind of blade a smith made when the smith cared more for the edge than the eye. There was a fuller down the center cut so true it might have been ruled. The pommel was a simple disc. The grip was wound in leather gone dark with old sweat — *her* sweat, Yaniel's, dead these long years, a hero of a crusade three before this one — and Caleth found himself reluctant to let his gaze rest there too long, the way one is reluctant to read another's letters.

It was a beautiful object. It did not need to be told so.

"May I see it?" he asked.

Thane shrugged and turned it, offering the hilt. "I don't use swords. Feel free."

Caleth took it.

He had handled a hundred swords. The Spire had drilled the blade into him before they ever trusted him with the glaive he preferred — *know the weapon you will not carry, so you understand the man who carries it* — and his hand knew, the instant the grip settled into his palm, that this was a good one. Balanced a thumb's width above the guard. Alive in the wrist. He shifted his stance out of old habit, dropped his back foot the way the spire-masters had taught, and brought it up in a slow testing arc.

And the steel woke.

Light ran the length of the blade — not lamplight, not a reflection, but a clean white radiance that rose from inside the metal like dawn coming up behind a hill, and the grip in his hand went suddenly *warm,* warm the way the scale at his hip was warm, warm the way a thing is warm when it is alive and pleased.

Caleth went very still.

He had heard, somewhere in the long catalogue of things the Spire had poured into him, that holy weapons of this kind were *particular* — that they were forged to a purpose and a person and that they slept until the right hand woke them. He had never expected to be a hand that woke one. He stood frozen with the white light pouring up the steel and his breath stopped in his chest, and then —

The hilt moved.

He felt it before he saw it. The metal beneath his fingers *flowed,* not melting, not breaking, but lengthening, the way water lengthens when you tip a glass, and the warmth ran out along the changing steel, and when it settled he was no longer holding a sword.

He was holding a glaive.

A glaive of pale gleaming metal, the haft sized exactly to his grip, the leaf-blade at its head curved in precisely the line his own weapon took — the weapon slung across his back this very moment — as though the holy steel had reached into his hands and read what they knew and *become it.*

Caleth stared.

He was aware, distantly, that the common room had gone quiet around them. He was aware that Thane was saying something. He could not entirely hear it over the single clear thought that had risen in him and filled him to the brim and left no room for anything else.

*A blade wreathed in radiance.*

The Lantern Seer's second vision. The old figure in the back room in Kenabres, the one some called a fraud and some crossed the street to avoid, who had looked at a boy with green eyes and seen too far — *three things will organize you, child, three rivers in the dark.* A city burning under a bleeding sky. That had come at noon on Armasse with the wrong sun in the wrong west. A blade wreathed in radiance.

It was in his hands.

He had known, dimly, that they had recovered Radiance — had helped pull it from the ruin where the Templars had cached it, had watched Thane wrap it in oilcloth — and some small careful part of him had wondered, in the way he wondered about all the Seer's words, whether *this* was the blade, whether the river had reached this bend. But knowing a thing might be the vision and feeling the vision *arrive* in your own grip, the warmth and the light and the steel reshaping itself to the shape of you — those were not the same knowing at all.

The visions did not frighten him. They never had. They organized him. Each one that came was a piece of a board only he could see settling at last into its square, and a man who could see the board could *plan.* He felt the old cold pleasure of it move under the awe — *one more piece placed, one more line of the future become the present* — and he was careful, very careful, to let none of it reach his face.

He did not announce it. Of course he did not announce it. A card revealed was a card spent, and this was not a card he would ever spend cheaply.

"Saint's mercy," he said instead, quietly, which was the truest thing he could say aloud.

"It does that," Thane said, deadpan, as if a holy weapon reforging itself in a man's hands were a mild inconvenience, like a door that stuck. "It was a sword for the saint. Apparently it's a glaive for you."

"Apparently."

Nageru had drifted up from somewhere — the aasimar had a way of being suddenly present, bare feet silent on the boards — and was looking at the glaive with his still dark gaze. Caleth, recovering himself, turned the haft and offered it across.

"Try it."

Nageru took it. He held it a moment, weighed it in both bronze hands, and gave it a single slow turn through the air.

The light flared. The metal flowed.

When it settled it was a longsword again — Yaniel's plain good longsword, exactly as it had come out of the oilcloth.

Nageru regarded it without expression, then handed it back. "I am not a weapon user," he said, as though declining a second helping at table.

"You hit things very hard with your hands," Thane observed.

"My hands are not a weapon I draw."

Caleth took the sword back, and it became a glaive again the instant his fingers closed, the warmth returning like a dog returning to a known hand, and that small fact lodged in him and would not be dislodged. *It knows me. Among the four of us, it has already chosen, and it is only being polite about letting the others ask.*

He kept that thought, too, off his face.

"Let's see what it makes of Korroc," he said.

---

They found the dwarf at the far end of the common room, where he had spent most of the evening with his sleeves rolled and his hands on the wounded, and where he was now sitting heavily with a bowl of stew he was too tired to lift. Korroc paladin of Torag, Korroc the Stonelord, Korroc who carried the company's healing on his broad back and complained about every climb and held every door — he looked up at the three of them with the flat patience of a man who has guessed he is about to be asked to do something.

"What," he said.

"Swing this," said Thane, and Caleth handed it over.

Korroc took the glaive in one thick hand, and the moment his fingers closed around it the light did not merely wake — it *blazed,* fierce and white, the brightest Caleth had seen it, bright enough that men three tables down turned to look — and the metal ran and folded and shortened and thickened, and where there had been a glaive there was now a warhammer. A dwarven warhammer, the head broad and blunt and brutal, the haft sized to a dwarf's grip and a dwarf's reach, as though the holy steel had taken one look at the man and known him to the bone.

Korroc grunted. He turned the hammer over, examined the head, gave it an experimental half-swing that the nearest crusaders prudently leaned away from.

"Well," he said. "That's interesting."

He looked at Caleth. There was a question in the look, and something else beneath the question — the particular wary respect of one paladin recognizing that a thing of the gods had just behaved as though it had opinions.

Caleth nodded back and said nothing. He had learned long ago that the most useful thing a man could do, in the moment after a wonder, was let the wonder finish speaking before he spoke over it.

Korroc turned the hammer over once more. Then, unexpectedly, he was quiet for a while, and when he spoke it was slow, the way the dwarf spoke when he had thought a thing all the way through before he opened his mouth.

"I'll tell you what I think," he said. "I use my hammer often enough now. I like the weight of it. But." He set the warhammer down on the table between them, and the light in it gentled, dimmed, banked itself like a fire being covered for the night. "If we're to be fighting demons — and we are, that's the whole shape of the days ahead — then I think you'll need me doing more mending than killing. Most fights, I'll be the one keeping you all on your feet. That's the position I have to hold. Not the one where I'm swinging a weapon and hoping someone else keeps *me* up. The one where you all depend on me to keep you breathing."

"Aye," said Thane, who had cause to know.

"So." Korroc nudged the hammer a half-inch toward Caleth with one blunt finger. "You use that glaive well, half-elf. I've watched you do it. You take the seam, you take the angle, you put the point where it needs to be and not a hair off." He shrugged his great shoulders. "Seems to me the saint's blade has gone looking for a hand. And it keeps finding yours."

He pushed it the rest of the way across.

Caleth looked at it. On Korroc's surface this was the plainest practical reasoning in the world — the healer should heal, the glaive-hand should hold the glaive, distribute the tools to the hands that use them best. That was Korroc all over: the forge-logic, the smith dividing the work. Caleth could have laid the whole argument out himself in those terms and it would have been entirely true.

But he heard it differently, and he could not help hearing it differently, because the warmth of the thing was still on his palm.

*It found my hand. Korroc said so himself. It keeps finding mine.*

He reached out and took it.

The warhammer became a glaive the instant he lifted it — the white light running up the haft, the steel flowing out long and bright and his — and Caleth's eyes did not leave the weapon as it changed, and changed, and settled into the shape of him. Some part of him, the cold part that ran the arithmetic of every gift before he accepted it, noted that he had just acquired a holy weapon of significant power and that it would smite as a paladin smote and reach as a glaive reached and that this materially improved the company's odds in the days to come. That part of him was satisfied.

The rest of him, the part that had stood three feet from a dragon and been smiled at, was very quiet.

*The blade wreathed in radiance.* The river had reached the bend. One more piece had found its square. He had been living *ahead* since the day the Seer first spoke to him — living in the future, measuring it, paying for it in advance — and now the future had walked up to him in a refugee inn at dusk and put itself in his hand, warm and patient and certain, and asked nothing of him but that he carry it.

"Thank you," he said to Korroc.

"Don't thank me. Thank the saint. I just did the sums."

"You did the sums," Caleth agreed, and slung Radiance across his back beside the pale-wood glaive that had served him so long, and felt, absurdly, as though he had two weapons that were one weapon now, and the warmth of it settled against his spine like a hand laid there in passing.

He did not say *be the kind one.* He did not say it aloud and he did not let it show. But the dragon's words moved in him as he settled the strap — *even when the others choose the hammer* — and he thought, with something that was almost a smile and that he kept entirely off his face: *Korroc chose the hammer, and the hammer gave itself to me as a glaive. There is a joke in that somewhere. She would have liked it.*

---

The papers changed the evening's weather.

They had recovered them in the last of the safe houses — a packet of letters and ledgers and one folded missive sealed with a sigil Caleth had not wanted to look at too long — and they had carried the packet without quite finding the moment to give it weight. Now Caleth crossed the common room and laid it on the table where Irabeth Tirabade sat going over a roster by lamplight, and the half-orc looked up at him, and he watched her read.

He always watched people read. It was the most honest a face ever got — the small involuntary tightenings, the place where the eyes stopped, the breath that changed. Irabeth's broad shoulders were squared in the worn breastplate she never seemed to take off, the green-dyed gambeson showing at the collar, and she read the first page with the flat attention of a soldier, and the second with a small line deepening between her brows, and the third —

The third page she read twice. Caleth saw her read it twice. Then she set it down very carefully, as if it might break, and she said a word under her breath that no priest of any god would have blessed, and she shook her head.

"I need to talk to the other leaders about this," she said. She was already rising. She did not look at him as she said it; she was looking at something past the wall, some shape that had assembled itself in her mind out of those three pages, and Caleth knew that shape was bad because he had been watching her face when it arrived. "Stay close. Don't go far."

She walked away into the back rooms with the packet held against her breastplate, distracted, her stride gone short and hard.

Caleth let her go. Then he turned and looked for the other man he had been meaning to find.

---

Quednys was in the back, as Irabeth had said he would be when Caleth asked, and Caleth found him at a table half-buried in lists — supply tallies, casualty counts, the grim ledgers by which a dying city was kept barely alive. He was a middle-aged man with ink on his fingers and grey coming in at his temples and the particular hollowed look of someone who had not slept properly in a long time and did not expect to soon.

Aravashniel had asked after this man. Days ago now, in the Blackwing Library, the elder wizard had said the name *Quednys* and asked the company to find him if they could, and Caleth — who carried more reasons than the others to do as Aravashniel asked, and carried them privately — had kept the name. He crossed to the table and gave the small respectful half-bow of the Spire.

"You are Quednys. Aravashniel asked us to find you."

The man's head came up. Something crossed his face at the name — relief, and under it a grief he did not bother to hide. "Aravashniel lives?"

"He lives. He is at the Blackwing."

"The Blackwing." Quednys breathed it. "I thought — we all thought the library was lost. Burned, or taken, or both. I thought —" He stopped. His ink-stained hand came up and pressed flat against the tabletop, as if steadying himself on it. "Tell me."

So Caleth told him. The library besieged, the librarians bound and waiting for the knife, the fight in the stacks, the four scholars they had pulled out alive. He told it plainly, the way he had learned to tell Sull things plainly in the dark — *some men need beauty and some men need bluntness, and this is a man who needs the truth handed to him whole.* He left out what he could not say. He did not say *the library was a Riftwarden stronghold,* because Quednys already knew that. He did not say that the order's working library of demon-lore in all Golarion had nearly been put to the torch. But he saw, as he spoke, that Quednys grieved as though he half-knew anyway. And Caleth filed that away — *this man cared for that library more than a clerk cares for books* — and did not pull on the thread.

When he finished, Quednys was silent a long moment. Then he reached across the table and gripped Caleth's shoulder, hard, and Caleth let him, though he was not by nature a man who liked to be gripped.

"I regret that I was not there," Quednys said. His voice was low and rough. "I should have been at the library. I had the chance and I —" His jaw worked. "The city needed holding. If I had left, the lines might have come apart. I made the choice and I have lived with it every hour since, and it was the right choice, and I hate it." He met Caleth's eyes. "Thank you. For being there when I was not. I will send what soldiers I can spare back to the Blackwing. I'll bring those people home."

"They will be glad of it."

"It is the least I can do. It is, in fact, exactly the least I can do, and I am ashamed it is all I have." He released Caleth's shoulder and sat back, scrubbing a hand down his tired face.

Caleth left him to his lists. As he walked back toward the common room he turned the man over in his mind the way he turned over a face he meant to draw later, and he understood, by the deference the others gave Quednys as he passed and by the weight of the ledgers and by the simple fact that the man had stayed at his post when his heart had screamed at him to go — he understood that this was one of the lynchpins. One of the few hands holding the survivors of Kenabres from scattering into the dark like spilled beads. The city had not dissolved into chaos, and men like Quednys were the reason. It was a quiet kind of heroism, the kind no song would find, and Caleth's Shelyn-trained heart marked it and would not forget it.

---

Irabeth came back for them within the hour, and gathered the company, and led them into a back room with a map on the table and a closed door, and told them what the three pages had said.

She did not soften it. Caleth respected that.

"What we have been through," she said, "the burning of the Kite, the death of the wardstone, Terendelev murdered, the city torn open — we have been calling it the worst thing that has ever happened to Kenabres. It was not the worst thing. It was the *opening.*" She set her broad hands flat on the map. "The Worldwound is moving in force, more concentrated than anything since Drezen fell a hundred years ago. The Lord of the Locust Host is finally making his move. And the end of this war — the whole hundred-year war — is going to be decided here, in the next days, by whoever holds the field. The Templars of the Ivory Labyrinth are scattered. But the ones who serve the Locust Host directly are gathered in Old Kenabres, behind a barricade, and they are *waiting* for something." Her eyes moved across the four of them. "From what we've pieced together — including what you recovered — it appears that what they are waiting for is *her.* Areelu Vorlesh is coming to Kenabres."

Caleth knew the name the way one knew the name of a plague. The witch who had opened the Worldwound. A century of crusade and the wound never closed, and the hand that first cut it still walked the world.

He kept his face still and listened.

"When the Storm King destroyed our wardstone," Irabeth went on, "it didn't simply *fail.* It exploded — it took the Kite with it — but the field along the whole border didn't go out. It guttered. It's still there, weak and thinning, holding the demons back from a full crossing." She tapped the map, the long line of the border. "I believe that's because a large fragment of the wardstone *survived.* And I believe the cultists have it. They've carried it to the old garrison in Old Kenabres — the Gray Garrison — and they're working there to do something I would not have believed possible. They mean to *reverse* it. To take the field that has been our greatest shield for a hundred years and turn it inside out. To make our own defense the weapon that kills us all."

Someone breathed out. It might have been Korroc.

"The attack on the city did exactly what it was meant to do," Irabeth said, and now there was iron under the grief in her voice, and something close to self-disgust. "It pulled the crusade to the border to defend it. Everyone asks why the demons haven't come across in greater numbers, and I'll tell you why. Because Vorlesh *wants* us massed on that line. If she can corrupt the wardstone field, she strikes one blow and breaks the whole crusade at once. We've lined up at the wall like cattle at the rail, and we've done it gladly, and she's been counting on it from the first." She straightened. "She is seeking some object of Abyssal power to do it — a Nahyndrian crystal, named in the missive you brought up. With that, she turns the source of our hope into a nightmare none of us will survive. We cannot pull the crusade off the border. They would not leave the line even if we told them all of this — they would call it a trick, and they would be wrong, and we would all die proving it."

She looked at each of them in turn.

"But we don't have to move the army. We only have to take away her key. Someone has to get into the Gray Garrison, find the wardstone fragment, and destroy it. A few people. Not an army — an army can't get in. A few."

The room was quiet.

And then Quednys, who had come in behind them and stood near the door, stepped forward holding a long dark rod.

He came to Caleth — to Caleth specifically, and Caleth noted that, and noted that the others noted it — and held the rod out across both hands the way one offers a sword to a king.

"A rod of cancellation," Quednys said. "I dug it out of a wizard's shop that the fighting brought down. There was a wizard under the shop, too, but the rod outlasted him." His mouth twisted. "If you put this against the wardstone fragment, it will sever the fragment's last tie to the field along the border. The connection breaks. The link in her armor that she needs — gone. She won't be able to turn the wardstone into a weapon. Whatever else she does, she won't do *that.*"

He pressed it into Caleth's hands. The rod was cool and heavy and dead — a thing made to unmake other things, and Caleth's arcane sense recoiled from it faintly, the way the tongue recoils from biting tin. He took it anyway. Of course he took it. He used whatever resources came to hand and saw no shame in it, and this was a resource that might save a hundred thousand lives, and his fingers closed around it without hesitation even as the wizard in him flinched from what it was.

Quednys looked at him gravely, and then around at all four of them, and then back to Caleth, eye to eye.

"Do this," he said, "and she cannot use our own hope to murder us. Do this, and we have a chance."

Caleth inclined his head. "We will do it."

Irabeth looked at them, and Caleth — who watched faces the way other men watched the sky for weather — saw her come to the edge of tears and stop there by main force.

"A small force has a better chance than an army at this," she said. "And the heroes of Kenabres are dead, or captured, or simply gone. We don't know where half of them are. We can't find them." Her voice thickened. She mastered it. "Considering what you four have already done — the dark, the Templars, the library, all of it — your group is the best chance this city has. Maybe the only chance." She drew a breath. "Everyone is behind you. Understand that. Whatever happens out there, you go with the whole of what's left of Kenabres at your backs."

Caleth looked at the company.

Korroc met his eyes and nodded once, slow and certain, the nod of a man setting a stone in a wall. Thane met his eyes and nodded — quicker, sharper, already three moves ahead into the doing of it. Nageru met his eyes and inclined his head, the still water taking the weight without a ripple.

No one said anything. There was nothing that needed saying, and they were not, any of them, men who wasted words on a thing already decided. Caleth had learned the shape of these three in the dark below the city, and he knew this silence for what it was: not hesitation. Assent. The whole of it.

He turned to the map.

---

"Here is where the demons have massed," Irabeth said, and her finger came down on Old Kenabres, on the maze of the ruined quarter beyond the broken wall. "Thick. Too thick to walk through. So I'll give you a way through." She traced a route. "I can pull together some of the mercenaries still in the city, and what's left of the Watch, and I'll start hitting the edges of Old Kenabres — here, here, here. Feints. I'll make enough noise in enough places that they'll come boiling out to meet me, and the streets you need will thin. There's a break in the wall here." Her finger stopped. "That gap will put you closest. You go through it while they're looking at me."

"And the garrison itself?" Caleth asked.

"Across the old Cathedral square. Or what was the square." Her finger rested on a mark Caleth's eye had already found and already understood. "You'll have to cross open ground. There's no way around it that I know. Rest tonight. At first light, we begin."

Caleth looked at the mark on the map where the Cathedral of Saint Clydwell had stood, and the painter's eye in him laid the place over the memory without being asked — the stage, the old straight-backed inquisitor climbing it, the woman in grey standing too still at the edge of the crowd, the wrong sun, the silver shape erupting from the press of bodies and climbing the broken sky. He had not been back to that square. He had fallen through the world a hundred feet from it and come up four days later on the far side of the city, and he had not stood again in the place where he had watched a legend die.

He would, tomorrow.

He kept his face still and said nothing, and went to rest.

---

In the grey before first light Irabeth went over the plan a second time, point by point, the way good commanders did — *say it twice and a man remembers it once; say it once and he remembers none of it* — and as she finished, a figure came in through the inn's door from the cold.

Aravashniel.

The elder wizard had come from the Blackwing on his own feet, leaning on the staff Caleth had cut for him weeks ago in the dark, and his half-restored eyes found the company and warmed, and his ruined face — older than it had been, much older, the price of the dark and the demons paid out in lines and hollows — broke into a smile.

"My friends," he said.

He went to each of them. A word for Korroc, a nod for Thane, a long quiet look for Nageru that Caleth could not read and did not try to. And then he came to Caleth, and the look he gave Caleth was different, and only Caleth knew why it was different, and Caleth gave nothing back but the same respectful bow he gave any elder, because that was the whole of the agreement between them and neither of them had ever needed to speak it aloud.

*Riftwarden elder.* The word lived in Caleth and nowhere else in this room. The same order his blood-parents had served and vanished serving. He had known it since the whispered conversation at Gwerm Manor, and he had told no one, and he would tell no one, and the not-telling had become one more card held close in a hand already crowded with them.

"I cannot come with you," Aravashniel said. "I would only slow you, and the dark has taken more from these eyes than it has given back. But I can give you this." He came to the map and bent over it, the half-sight squinting, his finger hovering and then settling on a street in Old Kenabres not far from the gap in the wall. "There is a cache here. Hidden. Equipment — supplies, the kind of thing that has been laid down quietly over years against a day of need. If it is still there, and if you can reach it, it will help you. Potions. Arrows. Such things." His finger traced the way to it. "Here, and then here, and the cache is set into the cellar of the third building, behind a false wall on the north side. If it has not been found. If it has not burned."

He straightened, and looked at Caleth, only at Caleth, for the briefest instant.

"It has been there a long time," he said. "Longer than the cultists. Longer than this war's worst years. Laid down by careful hands, against exactly such a day as this."

Caleth understood him perfectly. The others heard *a cache of supplies,* useful, practical, welcome. Caleth heard *the order put this here.* He heard that the secret guardians who watched where reality thinned had, in some quiet year, foreseen a day when Kenabres would need a hidden hand reaching up out of the past, and had laid that hand down in a cellar, and waited. The Riftwardens had not been only Aravashniel and a burned library. They had been *here,* in the bones of the city, all along.

He let none of it show.

"Thank you," he said. "We'll find it."

Aravashniel smiled at him — the small smile of two men who share a thing no one else in the room can see — and stepped back, and leaned on his staff, and let them go.

---

They went out at first light through the break in the wall, and the broken city took them in.

Old Kenabres in the grey dawn was a thing Caleth's eye could not refuse to read even when he wished it would. The buildings stood like the carcasses of buildings — walls without roofs, doorways without doors, a child's shoe in a gutter, a shop sign hanging by one chain and swinging in a wind that smelled of old smoke and the sweet-rot underneath it that he had learned, these four days, to stop naming. The painter in him catalogued it all and the calculator in him laid the catalogue over the map, marking cover, marking sightlines, marking the places a thing could come from that he could not yet see.

They found a solid structure near the gap — a counting-house, by the iron in its door and the heavy shutters — and slipped inside, and waited.

Caleth crouched at a shuttered window with Radiance — a glaive again, the moment he took it up — laid across his knees, and listened.

It did not take long. From off to the east, faint and then less faint, came the sounds of fighting. Steel and shouting and the particular shriek that demons made when they died, which was not like any sound a living throat should make. One of Irabeth's groups had found the enemy, or the enemy had found them.

"It's begun," Thane murmured.

They moved out into the street and went quietly, hugging the walls, Thane a little ahead with his sharp watchful eyes reading every doorway and every gap, and they had gone perhaps a hundred paces toward the next break in the inner walls when Thane's hand came up and they all froze.

Down the street, between them and the gap they needed, stood cultists. A large knot of them — twenty, perhaps more, Caleth's eye counting and discarding the count as too many to fight and too tight to slip past — clustered around something Caleth could not see, robes the color of old blood, a low ugly chant rising off them like steam.

Too many.

Caleth did the arithmetic and did not like the answer, and was reaching for the cold practical question of *which way around* when the cultists, all at once, came alert.

It was a strange thing to watch. The whole knot of them stiffened in the same instant, heads turning the same way, like a flock of birds taking alarm from a sound below human hearing, and then they *broke* — surged off down a side street toward the east, toward the noise, toward Irabeth's feint, their chant dissolving into shouted orders and the slap of running feet. Within a dozen heartbeats the street was empty.

The ruse had worked.

"Saint's mercy," Caleth breathed, and meant it as thanks, and moved.

They went quickly then, through the gap and through the next, slipping along the seams of the broken city while the demons and cultists ran the other way toward the woman who was bleeding to draw them off. The arithmetic, which had been so grim a minute before, tilted in their favor with every empty street, and Caleth let himself feel, briefly, the cold clean satisfaction of a plan working — *she is paying for this passage in her own blood and the blood of her mercenaries, and we had better be worth it.*

And then they came around a corner and the broken city opened, and Caleth knew where he was.

---

He stopped without deciding to stop.

It was the square. Or what the square had been. The wide paved plaza of Clydwell, where the feast of Armasse had been celebrated, where the whole city had gathered under the noon sun to honor the dead heroes of old crusades and bless the living swords of this one. Where the stage had stood, and the old inquisitor had climbed it with his straight back and his too-old face. Where a woman in grey had stood at the edge of the crowd, three feet from a half-elf who had thought she had nice eyes.

There was almost nothing left of it.

The Cathedral of Saint Clydwell was gone. Not damaged — *gone,* reduced to a vast tumbled mound of broken stone where the great façade had been, the saints fallen from their niches and shattered to gravel, the whole proud weight of it come down in a single afternoon when a dragon and a balor had plummeted from the sky locked together and struck it like the world's heart breaking. Where the nave had stood there was now a jagged rift in the ground, deep and dark and silent, as if the earth itself had cracked open to receive what had fallen.

Caleth's eye moved across the ruin and would not stop, cataloguing it the way it catalogued everything — the angle of the collapse, the place where the rose window must have been, a single carved hand of a single fallen saint lying palm-up in the rubble as though asking. And then his eye came to the center of the square, to the open ground before the rift, to the place where he had last seen her.

It was empty.

He had known it would be. He had heard the rumors in the inn, traded low among the refugees — that the demons had carried her body away, dragged the silver wyrm's corpse off into the Worldwound for some foul purpose no decent mind would name. He had heard it and set it aside and not let himself dwell. Now he stood at the edge of the place where Terendelev of Kenabres had come down out of the sky with her head struck from her shoulders by a demon lord's burning sword, and there was nothing there. No silver. No bones. No grave. Only swept stone and the dark mouth of the rift and the cold grey light.

They had taken even that from the city. They had not even left her body to be mourned.

Caleth did not say anything. He was not a man who wept in front of others, and he was not a man who spoke his grief aloud, and this grief was not even fully his to speak — he had stood three feet from her and been smiled at, and he had carried her last gift warm against his hip ever since, and not one of the men beside him knew it. He could not stand in this square and say *I knew her. She saw me. She chose me.* The words would have meant nothing to them and everything to him, and so he kept them, the way he kept all his cards.

But he let the absence land. For one breath, two, he let himself simply stand in the empty place and feel how empty it was. The silver scale was warm against his hip, warmer than usual, or perhaps that was only his own blood. *Get them out,* it had said to him in the dark, a hundred feet below this very square. He had got them out. And now he had come back up to the place where she fell, and she was not even here, and the only monument to the kindest thing he had ever witnessed was a swept patch of stone before a hole in the world.

"I am still trying," he said, too quietly for any of them to hear. "I will keep trying."

Then Thane, ahead of him, went still in a different way, and held up a hand, and the moment closed, and the work resumed.

---

Thane waved them up, and they came, low and quiet, to where the dwarf crouched at the corner of a half-fallen building, and they all saw it at once.

It was tearing a shop apart.

A demon — Caleth's eye took it in and his memory supplied the shape before Thane named it — half-rat and half-man, hunched and ropy and wrong, standing upright on bowed hind legs with a long naked tail lashing behind it and a snout full of yellow chisel-teeth. It was *destroying* the shop, methodically, with a kind of furious appetite — ripping shelves from walls, smashing what could be smashed, and at the center of its ruin it had hold of a book, and it was *chewing* on it, tearing the pages with its teeth, grinding the words to pulp in its jaws with an expression of vile satisfaction.

"Demon," Thane breathed. "Abrikandilu."

The name came up out of the catalogue in Caleth's mind with the rest of it. *Abrikandilu. Mirror-breakers.* The demons that hated beautiful things — that destroyed mirrors and art and craft and every made thing of grace, that fed on ugliness, that could not bear a lovely object to exist in the world without unmaking it. And here was one, in a book shop, eating the words off the page, fifty paces from the place where a dragon had given her life in the single most beautiful act this ruined city had ever seen.

The irony was not lost on him. It was never lost on him. The Worldwound had sent its mirror-breakers to gnaw the books in the shadow of Terendelev's empty grave, and there was a kind of black perfection to it that the painter in him registered even as the calculator began counting exits.

Thane was already moving. The dwarf drew a small clay potion from his belt, thumbed the stopper, and drank it down in one motion — and faded, edges going soft and then gone, the air closing over the place where he had been. Invisible.

Caleth crept forward. The Abrikandilu did not look up; it was wholly absorbed in its ruination, in the small sounds of paper dying. He went low and careful, glaive ready, taking the angle the way the Spire had taught — *do not approach a thing head-on if you can come at its seam* — and behind him he felt the others moving up: Thane somewhere ahead and unseen, Nageru drifting up on the soft soles of his bare feet, and then Korroc's heavy quiet hand touching his back from behind.

Warmth poured through him. Not healing — a shield. *Shield of faith,* Korroc's gift, a faint pressure of grace settling over him like a held breath. Caleth gave the dwarf the smallest backward nod and kept moving.

He had closed to within a few paces when a head came around the doorframe.

A second Abrikandilu. It had been inside the shop, and now its snout poked out into the grey light, and its small red eyes found Caleth, and it grinned — if a thing with that many teeth could be said to grin — and it spoke.

It spoke in Abyssal.

Caleth understood it perfectly. He had learned the tongue the way he had learned all the old dangerous languages, as if remembering rather than studying, the Spire's arcane drills laid over something older in his blood, and the harsh syllables resolved in his ear into clear and ghastly words.

*"Ooo,"* the demon said, looking him up and down with its red eyes, looking at the fine bones and the glaive and the made things he carried, *"more pretties to eat."*

Caleth smirked.

He could not have said, afterward, why it amused him. Perhaps because of course it did — of course the mirror-breaker would name him a *pretty* and mean it as both a compliment and a menu, of course the thing that hated beauty would look at a half-elf paladin of the goddess of beauty and see only something delicious to ruin. There was a terrible logic to it that his strategic mind appreciated even as his oath-bound heart recoiled. And of course he, Caleth Aranor, would be the one man in the square who understood the insult, and of course his answer would be dry.

"You'll eat the tip of my blade," he said.

The demon came at him.

It came faster than anything that shape should move — a blur of ropy muscle and chisel-teeth, crossing the ground between them in a single bound — and Caleth got the glaive up but the thing was inside the reach of it, both clawed hands raking. The first claw he turned on the haft. The second got through. It opened a long line of fire across his forearm and he felt the warmth of his own blood and set his teeth and did not give ground.

Ahead, the invisible Thane stopped being quiet.

The dwarf erupted out of nothing beside the first demon, the one with the book — one dagger going in low and hard, then the second, twin punches of steel into the ropy flank. The Abrikandilu shrieked, a sound of pure outraged surprise, and spun, and there was Thane, visible now, his face flat and his daggers wet, dancing back out of reach as the demon clawed twice at the place he had been and found only air.

And then a third came out of the shop at Nageru.

Caleth caught it at the edge of his sight — the aasimar drawing up, and the demon on him, claws raking down, and then the snout darting in to bite, teeth closing on bronze flesh — and Korroc was already moving to Nageru's side, the dwarf's instinct for the wounded faster than thought.

Caleth turned back to his own. He gripped Radiance two-handed and drove the leaf-blade into the rat-demon's chest, a clean hard thrust off his back foot, and the holy steel went in and the white light flared along it and the thing screamed.

A book hit Thane in the head.

Caleth saw it happen out of the corner of his eye and his mind reported it before he could quite believe it: a fourth Abrikandilu had appeared at an upper window of the shop, and it had picked up a book — a heavy one, a folio by the size of it — and it had *thrown* the book at Thane, and the book had caught the dwarf squarely above the ear with a flat meaty *thwock,* and Thane staggered.

In any other moment Caleth would have laughed. He nearly laughed now. The mirror-breakers had run out of things to smash and had begun, in their hatred of all made things, to throw the made things at their enemies — to weaponize the very books they had come to destroy. There was a joke in it as black as the rift behind them, and some cold part of him, even in the middle of the fight, *enjoyed* it, the way he enjoyed any pattern that resolved itself cleanly. *They hate the books so much they'll throw them. Saint's mercy.*

Thane shook off the blow and snarled something about libraries.

Nageru struck. His fist came down on the demon savaging him and Caleth saw the flesh of the knuckles go dark and wet — *acid,* some monk's gift, the bronze hand dripping — but the Abrikandilu shrugged the burning off as if it were rain, its hide too foul to feel the bite, and Nageru's brow drew down a fraction at the failure.

Thane shifted. Caleth watched him do it with a professional's appreciation — the dwarf stepping deliberately so that the demon in front of him stood between his own body and the book-thrower at the window, turning the enemy into his own cover. The next book sailed wide. The dwarf's daggers went in once, twice, and the demon he was fighting wheeled and bit, and this one *connected,* the chisel-teeth tearing a hideous ragged wound in Thane's shoulder that made even Caleth wince.

Across the fight Nageru took claws across the ribs and did not fall.

Korroc looked around — Caleth saw the dwarf take the whole shape of the fight in a single glance, the wounded counted, the arithmetic of survival run behind the broad blunt face faster than anyone gave him credit for — and Korroc lifted his hand and golden light spilled out of it, a wash of warmth rolling across all of them at once, and Caleth felt his torn forearm knit and the ache in it ease as the wave passed through him.

He stabbed at the demon in front of him and missed. The thing had ducked low and his leaf-blade went over its back.

At the window, the book-thrower wound up again and let fly, and the book sailed wide once more — Thane had kept his demon between himself and the window, and the dwarf's geometry held, and the folio sailed past his head and shattered against a wall in a flutter of dying pages.

Nageru struck again, and again, a flurry of bronze fists, and the demon in front of him *folded* — its ropy body crumpling under the assault, collapsing into the rubble it had made.

Caleth's demon hit him. A claw and then a bite, the teeth scoring his hip just above where the scale rode warm, and he grunted and held his ground and drove Radiance into it again. Beside him Thane stabbed twice more, and when his demon tried to bite back the dwarf simply *was not there,* stepping inside the strike, letting it close on nothing.

Korroc's eye found Caleth and found the blood on him, and the dwarf stepped over and laid a hand flat against Caleth's side, and direct healing poured into him — not the broad wave this time but the close, fierce, full warmth of a paladin's touch — and the bleeding wound at his hip closed in an instant, the torn flesh drawing shut, the pain gone as though it had never been.

"My thanks," Caleth said.

"Keep your blood inside you," Korroc grunted. "It's tidier."

Reinvigorated, Caleth stabbed his demon again and left another bloody ruin across its flank.

The book-thrower at the window ducked down and vanished behind the wall, and the angle closed, and Caleth lost it.

And then Nageru, with the demon before him dead at his feet, found himself for a moment standing alone — and the aasimar did a thing Caleth had not seen him do before. He reached over his shoulder, drew a javelin from the bundle on his back, and threw.

The javelin crossed the shop's interior in a flat hard line and took the demon inside — somewhere Caleth could not see — and a shriek of surprise answered it, high and ugly.

Thane's demon clawed him twice. Caleth saw the dwarf take both raking strikes and absorb them without a sound, his daggers already coming back. And Korroc moved up — over the rubble, hammer in hand — and brought the warhammer down on the demon that had been savaging Caleth, a single brutal overhand blow that staved its skull, and the thing dropped.

The book-thrower stayed hidden. The window stayed empty.

Then Nageru moved, quick and certain, and was suddenly *between* the demon on Caleth and the demon on Thane — taking the flanking angle alongside Caleth without a word, the two of them bracketing the same enemy — and the aasimar's fists came down on it in a hammering flurry. The demon did not like being flanked. It turned from Caleth to Nageru, all its fury rounding on the nearer threat, and it raked the aasimar twice with its claws.

Korroc saw the strikes land and sent the golden wave out again, healing rolling across all of them, and Caleth felt Nageru's wounds close in the same warmth that eased his own bruises.

Caleth drove Radiance into the flanked demon and opened it from collar to hip. Nageru's fist came down once more and the thing collapsed. And the aasimar did not even pause — he simply pivoted, took one gliding step, and brought a single descending fist down on the demon that had been fighting Thane, and that one fell too, and the square went suddenly, ringingly quiet.

The last one outside was down. The shop was still.

Thane moved up to the window, daggers ready, expecting the book-thrower to be crouched below the sill. Caleth came up beside him, Radiance level, and they looked in together.

The shop was empty. At the back wall there was a ragged hole punched through into the next building, the edges still trickling dust.

"He's gone by now," Thane said, and frowned, and wiped both his daggers clean on a fallen scrap of curtain — the blades wiped before they were ever put away, always, the habit that was also a rule.

Caleth nodded. He looked once more at the chewed book lying ruined in the rubble, its pages ground to pulp, and at the scattered folios the demons had thrown, and at the small dead made things smashed across the floor of a place where a bookseller had once stood behind a counter and loved his trade. Then he looked through the broken window, past the shop, to where the empty square lay under the grey light with its swept stone and its silent rift.

Mirror-breakers in the shadow of her grave. He let it land, and then he let it go.

"The cache Aravashniel mentioned is just down this street," he said.

---

It was where Aravashniel had said it would be — the third building, the cellar, the false wall on the north side. Caleth found the seam in the stonework with his fingers while the others kept the street, pressed where the elder had described, and the false wall swung back on a hidden pivot with the soft grate of a thing that had not been opened in a long time.

Behind it sat a crate.

Caleth knelt and lifted the lid. Inside, packed in straw gone brittle with age, lay potions — he counted them by their resonance, healing and more besides — and bundled arrows, and a sheaf of javelins, and the small careful surplus of people who had planned for a bad day and laid by against it.

"Good," said Korroc behind him, peering over his shoulder. "Saint's blessing on whoever stocked it. We'll be glad of those."

"We will," Caleth agreed.

He let his hand rest on the edge of the crate for a moment longer than he needed to.

The others saw potions and arrows. Caleth saw something else, and kept it where he kept everything. The order had laid this here. The Riftwardens — the secret guardians who watched where reality thinned, the order his blood-parents had died serving, the order Aravashniel led and the burned library had served — they had reached forward out of some quiet year into this exact terrible morning, and they had set down a hand to help, and the hand had survived. The library was ash. The order's heart in Golarion had nearly gone to the knife. And here in a cellar in the ruined city, against every odd, one small piece of it had endured intact, waiting in the dark for someone of the order's blood to come and find it.

Something in him that was usually very quiet went quieter still.

*It survived,* he thought. *Something of theirs survived.*

He did not say it. He gathered the potions into his pack, and distributed the arrows to Thane and the javelins to Nageru, and closed the crate, and stood.

"Let's go take her key," he said.

---

The going was easier after that. Irabeth's feints were working better than Caleth had dared hope — every street they came to was thinner than it should have been, the demons and cultists drawn off east toward the noise like iron filings to a lodestone, eager, it seemed, to go and fight something rather than stand and guard. Twice they heard combat off in the ruins, the half-orc's mercenaries paying in blood for every empty street Caleth's company walked. He marked the debt. He intended them to be worth it.

So they came, sooner than he had expected, close to the garrison, and Thane led them up into a burned-out tangle of rubble across from its front — a long buckled stretch of broken ground where some great building had been thrown down. Caleth crouched in the wreckage and looked across, and understood that they had arrived back at the Cathedral of Saint Clydwell after all, by another road.

The Gray Garrison stood across from where the cathedral had stood.

The cathedral itself was the mound of rubble he had seen from the square's far side, and the rift was the deep jagged dark scar in the ground before it, and across that ruined holy place squatted the old garrison — a grim grey blockhouse of a building, square and heavy and built to be held, with its doors shut and its walls sheer. Two figures stood before the doors, each holding a scythe.

Caleth's eye went over the approach the way it went over everything. *Open ground. Sheer walls. No second way in that I can see but a straight climb, and a climb under those scythes is a climb under death. The doors, then. It has to be the doors.* The arithmetic was bad, but it was the only arithmetic on offer.

And then Thane, beside him, went rigid, and his eyes widened, and he drew back from the edge of the rubble and turned to the company with a face Caleth had not seen on him before.

"Two nobles of Kenabres," Thane said, low. "Standing at the doors."

They moved up to look. And Korroc, beside Caleth, cursed under his breath — a deep dwarven curse — and said, "Something's been done to them."

Caleth looked.

The two at the doors were men, or had been. The painter's eye in him read them even as the rest of him recoiled. The one on the left was vast and soft and rotund, swollen past any natural fatness, and Caleth knew the face beneath the bloat — Kandro Nyserian, the owner of the Nyserian Manor that had been a Templar safe house, the ruined estate they had picked through days ago. Nyserian had been a portly man in life. This was past portly. This was a man inflated, a man filled with something that was not flesh.

And the other —

"That's Hulrun," Korroc breathed.

It was. Or it had been. Lord Hulrun, the leader of Kenabres, the old straight-backed inquisitor Caleth had watched climb the stage at Armasse — *older than he had any right to look, an inquisitor who had outlived three wars and was halfway through a fourth, but his back was straight.* The back was not straight now. The man at the door was a bloated, sagging ruin of the man Caleth had seen on the stage, swollen and slack and wrong, the proud hard face buried under a mask of distended flesh. But the bones of it, under the bloat, were Hulrun's bones.

*But Hulrun was a righteous man,* Caleth thought, and the thought would not fit the thing at the door. *Whatever else he was — and he was hard, and he burned heretics, and half this city feared him — he was a righteous man. This makes no sense.*

"Wait here," he murmured.

He went forward alone, low and quiet, around the edge of the rubble until he could study the thing that wore Hulrun's shape from close. He drew on the small clean sense his oath gave him — the discernment a paladin carried, the reading of a soul's weather — and let it touch the figure at the door.

The figure at the door turned its bloated head, and looked directly at Caleth where he crouched in the wreckage, and smiled.

Caleth's blood went cold. The thing had no business seeing him. He was in shadow, in cover, behind broken stone. It should not have known he was there. It had known anyway, and it had smiled, and the smile was a wrong and knowing and hungry thing on the ruin of Hulrun's face.

He drew back and went to the others and crouched among them.

"They radiate a strong evil," he said quietly. "Both of them. There's no doubt. Whatever they were, they're steeped in it now."

Korroc's jaw worked. He looked across at the two bloated figures by the doors, and the dwarf's face went heavy with the particular grief that Caleth had come to recognize as Korroc's alone — the grief of a man who could not, ever, quite give up on a soul.

"Most likely corrupted," Korroc said. "Twisted into this against their will. If they were in their right minds —" He shook his head. "They'd want to be put out of it. Any decent man would. We'd be doing them a mercy."

He looked at Caleth, asking.

Caleth nodded slowly, and gripped Radiance, and felt the holy steel warm to a glaive in his hand.

And then Thane, who had been very still, did not wait.

The dwarf had already drawn one of the cache's arrows and nocked it while the rest of them talked, and now he loosed — a single clean shot across the open ground — and the arrow took the thing that had been Hulrun square in the chest. The bloated figure rocked back a half-step and did not fall, and Caleth saw, in the place where the arrow stood, no blood. Only a thin dark weeping, and a wrongness, and the thing's smile did not change.

So much for surprise. So much for the careful approach. Caleth felt the plan tilt and slide, and adjusted, the way he had learned to adjust — *the board has moved, count it again, move with it.*

Nageru flowed up to where Caleth was, drawing even with him, bare feet silent on the rubble. The two scythe-bearers at the door did not move.

And Korroc — Korroc, who could not help himself, Korroc who tried every time, walked out into the open.

He walked out across the buckled ground in his full plate, plain in the grey light, and he cupped a hand to his mouth and called out across the dead square:

"Lord Hulrun! Is that you?"

Thane muttered something that included the words *thick head* and *always tries too hard,* and shook his own head in disgust, and Caleth heard him and could not entirely disagree. He muttered something of his own under his breath as he rose to follow the dwarf out into the open — something that was not quite a word, a sound that meant, in the private grammar of his own exhaustion, *this is going to be messy, and he is going to make it messier, and I love him, and Shelyn give me patience* — and then he was moving out across the open ground to catch up with Korroc, because if the fool dwarf was going to stand in the open and shout at a demon, then he would not stand there alone.

Nageru came too, fast and quiet, and the four of them were suddenly out from cover.

The thing that looked like Hulrun answered the dwarf's question.

It raised one bloated hand, and Caleth felt the spell gather an instant before it broke — the cold gathering wrongness, the pull of something that fed on living things — and then a wave of negative energy washed out across all of them at once, a tide of grave-cold that passed through flesh and through the warmth of life and tried to put it out. Caleth gritted his teeth against it and felt the others do the same beside him, the cold dragging at something below the skin, and rode it out.

That answered Korroc's question better than any word could have.

*Not corrupted men. Not men at all. Whatever is wearing Hulrun's face, the man is already gone.* Caleth had been wrong — not in the direction he had feared, the fear that these were innocents twisted past saving, but in another direction entirely. There was no one in there to save. There never had been. He set the last of his hesitation down where he set everything he no longer needed, and lifted Radiance, and the glaive blazed.

The thing that had been Nyserian came off the door.

It moved on Korroc, scythe rising, and the blade came down — and it came down *true,* a savage perfect arc that found the seam of the dwarf's plate at the shoulder, and Korroc grunted, a deep pained sound, as the scythe bit into him. But the dwarf did not give. Korroc set his feet and brought the warhammer around and slammed it into the creature wearing Nyserian, a blow that landed with the flat heavy *crunch* of metal meeting something that was not bone, and the thing rocked.

Thane was there — out of nowhere, the way Thane was always suddenly there — driving a dagger into the Nyserian-thing from behind.

Nageru went for Hulrun. The aasimar crossed the open ground to the door and brought a single hard fist down on the bloated figure, and the thing that wore Hulrun's shape fell backward against the doors and did not move.

Caleth had a half-instant to think *too easy* before the dead man's skin began to writhe.

The thing that had been Nyserian shuddered, all over, and *shed.* It split. The bloated human skin tore open from within and the man-shape that had worn it stepped out of it the way a moth tears out of a chrysalis — except that there was nothing of moth or grace in it, only a grotesque eruption of writhing and wet sound, the flesh-suit collapsing into an empty sack of skin as the thing inside it stood revealed. A fat pale body, hunched and wrong. And where the head should have been there was no head at all — only a thick worm-like stalk crowned with a gaping circular maw, four fleshy petals peeling back around a wet ringed mouth, like some obscene flower grown in a cellar that had never seen the sun.

Caleth's eye took it in and his oath recoiled and his mind, cold and steady beneath both, filed it: *demon. The skin was a costume. There was never a Nyserian to save — there was only this, walking around inside a dead man's hide.*

The petaled thing lunged forward and its ringed maw closed on Korroc, biting deep, and the dwarf bellowed.

Caleth drove Radiance at it and missed — the thing swayed aside, faster than its bulk promised — but Korroc's hammer came down on it in the same instant, a solid ringing blow, and the creature reeled.

By the doors, Nageru had begun to turn back toward the main fight, the Hulrun-thing fallen and still behind him. And then *it* began to shudder. The skin of the thing that had been Lord Hulrun split and tore and erupted in the same grotesque unmaking, the bloated human hide collapsing as a second petaled demon stood up out of it, the same worm-stalk head, the same wet ringed maw, the leader of Kenabres revealed at last as a costume a demon had been wearing at the door.

But Nageru was already there, and already moving, and his fist came down on the thing as it rose, a single descending blow with the whole still weight of him behind it, and the demon that had worn Hulrun shuddered and stopped and did not finish standing. It sagged back against the doors, twitching, and went still.

The Nyserian-demon was the last. Korroc's hammer rose and fell and the thing twitched under the blow, and from behind it Thane's dagger drove in low and hard, and the petaled creature shrieked through its ringed maw and folded down into the rubble beside its empty skin and lay still.

Silence came back to the dead square.

---

Caleth stood among the wreckage with Radiance — a glaive, gleaming, gone dim now that the killing was done — held loose in his hand, and breathed, and let his heart slow.

Korroc moved heavily across the broken ground to where the two demons lay among the collapsed ruins of the skins they had worn. He knelt, with the slow care of a man whose plate had taken a scythe-blade, over one and then the other, and he turned the empty hides with the haft of his hammer, and he looked at what was left.

"It's the men," he said. His voice was thick. "Their skins. The faces are right — that's Nyserian, that's Hulrun, the faces are the very men. But there's nothing under them. No skeleton. Nothing." He turned the empty face of Lord Hulrun over with the hammer-haft and let it fall. "Demons. Literally wearing their skins. Walking around in the hides of the men they killed, standing guard at the door wearing the leader of Kenabres like a coat."

He spat, and shook his head, and rose.

Caleth looked down at the slack empty face of Hulrun, the old inquisitor he had watched climb a stage at noon on a feast day, the proud hard righteous man whose city had feared him and whose city had needed him, reduced to a discarded skin at the threshold of a fortress. There was nothing to bury. There never had been, not since whenever the demons had taken him. Like Terendelev, whose body the demons had carried off into the Worldwound. Like the cathedral, ground to gravel. The enemy did not even leave the dead their dignity. They wore them. They ground them up. They threw them.

He let it land.

Then he raised his eyes to the doors of the Gray Garrison.

The doors were shut. Behind them, somewhere in the grim grey heart of the blockhouse, lay the wardstone fragment, and Areelu Vorlesh's terrible purpose, and the thing they had crossed a ruined city and a dead square and the empty grave of a dragon to do. The rod of cancellation was a cold weight in his pack. Radiance was warm in his hand — *the blade wreathed in radiance, the second vision arrived and made flesh and serving him now.* The silver scale was warm against his hip. *Get them out,* she had said. He had got them out, down in the dark. Now there were a hundred thousand more along the border, lined up at the rail like cattle, and the only thing standing between them and the slaughter was four tired strangers who had become something else, and a closed grey door.

He looked at the company.

Korroc, hammer in hand, blood at the seam of his shoulder-plate, the conscience of the company who had walked out into the open to call a demon by a dead man's name because he could not bear not to try. Thane, daggers wiped clean and ready, watchful eyes already reading the doors, the seams, the wear-patterns in the grey stone, looking for the way in. Nageru, bronze and barefoot and still, his hands open at his sides, the patient thunder waiting to break.

His friends. The four of them who had fallen through the world together and climbed back up through the dark and come, at last, here.

Each of them met his gaze. No one spoke. There was nothing that needed saying, and there was no time, and they were not, any of them, men who wasted words on a thing already decided.

They turned to the doors of the Gray Garrison.

They gripped their weapons.

And they looked at each other one last time, in the grey light, before the dark behind the doors.
