# Beneath a Sunless Sky
*Chapter Two — The Glaive in the Gentle Hand*

The shrine was behind them within the hour, but the silver of it stayed.

Caleth had not slept well. He never did, in places that had once been holy and were holy again — the resonance was too clean, too wakeful, like trying to rest with a struck bell still ringing somewhere just beneath your hearing. He had spent the small hours sitting against the basalt wall with his glaive across his knees, his spellbook open to a page he could not really read in the gloom, his fingers tracing the diagrams of cantrips he had known since he was a boy in Kenabres. Tracing them not to learn. Tracing them the way a man worries a knot of polished wood in his pocket. Steadying himself.

Because the silver had not left him.

He had picked the scale up in the first cavern with everyone else, and he had felt what the others had felt — that vast gentle dying will, that live, fight, get them out. But underneath the scale's pressing gift there had been a second sensation, a private one, one he was almost certain none of the others had felt and would not have understood if he tried to explain.

He had felt recognition.

The dragon had known him.

He had been standing not ten paces from her, in the crowd at Clydwell Plaza, when the wrong sun rose in the wrong west.

She had been a woman, then. He remembered her perfectly — he remembered everything, it was a Spireborn's curse and a Spireborn's tool both — a tall, plain-featured human woman in plain traveler's grey, standing alone at the edge of the press of bodies, watching the stage with an expression of polite, attentive boredom that Caleth had, in the moment, taken for the face of some merchant's wife indulging her city's piety. He remembered thinking she had nice eyes. He remembered, with a small flick of half-elven amusement, thinking she stood very still for a human, almost too still, the way a heron stands when it is waiting on a fish.

He had been three feet from a silver dragon and he had thought nice eyes.

When the light came from the wrong direction and Hulrun's shadow stretched long across the cathedral, Caleth had turned, like everyone, toward the west. And then, when the sound came — when the world tore — he had turned, like a man whose paladin's instincts had finally caught up with him, toward the danger. Which meant he had turned back. Which meant he had been looking directly at her at the precise instant the woman in the grey traveler's cloak had ceased, in a single breath, to be a woman.

He had seen her change.

He had seen the cloak lift away on a wind that came from inside her. He had seen her arms unfold into wings, the bones lengthening, the fingers becoming pinions, the silver scales crawling up her skin like frost crawling up a window-pane in the first hard cold. He had seen her face, for a single half-instant before she launched, lift toward him — and meet his eyes.

And smile.

A small, apologetic, here we go smile, the smile of a woman caught at her tea-table by an unwelcome guest, and then she had been gone — vast and silver and roaring, climbing the broken sky to meet the Storm King. And Caleth had stood there with his mouth open, the air still moving where she had been, and a single, ridiculous, useless thought had filled his head like a struck bell:

That was Terendelev. I have been standing next to a legend.

She had looked at him, and she had known him for what he was, and she had still smiled at him as if he were a friend she might have liked to share a longer afternoon with, in some other world.

In this one she had had three minutes to live.

He had not told the others. There had been no moment for it, between the rift opening at his feet and the dust still settling on the dead in the first cavern. And he was not sure he would tell them now. It felt, somehow, too private. Like a letter someone had pressed into his hand on a battlefield and asked him to carry. The scale at his belt was not just a scale. It was a correspondence. And Caleth, who had been trained for half his life to hold his own counsel, knew how to carry a letter without opening it in front of strangers.

But he could feel her. As they marched out from the consecrated shrine in the morning-that-was-not-a-morning, with Anevia limping on Thane's arm and Aravashniel's restored half-vision fixed on the back of Caleth's cloak like a man following a guide-rope through fog — he could feel her, faintly, in the silver at his hip. Pressing. Patient.

Get them out, Spireborn. Get them out.

"My lady," he murmured, too quietly for the others to catch, "I am trying."

The passage opened, after half an hour of marching, into a cavern that took his breath away.

Not for its beauty. For its grief.

Statues. Dozens of them. Standing in the cavern as if they had walked into it from somewhere else and then stopped, frozen mid-step, mid-prayer, mid-cry. Crusaders, every one — Caleth's eye, trained on iconography from the day his foster parents had first put a brush in his hand, catalogued the gear at a glance. The tabards were of a cut three generations out of date. The blades were the long straight cavalry swords of the First Crusade. The shields bore the sun-and-sword of an Iomedaen order he half-remembered from a manuscript in the Spire's lower archive.

But it was the faces that arrested him.

Each one had been carved by someone who had loved the original. Caleth could see it. The artist's hand had lingered on the curl of a beard, the line of a jaw, the small exhausted hollow under a young woman's eye. These were not generic memorials. These were portraits. Stone portraits of stone people, made by stone hands.

Not stone. Flesh, once, he thought. Flesh that became this.

Aravashniel, beside him, was leaning on a staff Caleth had cut for him from an old crusader's spear and breathing through his teeth. The half-restored eyes squinted, drank, drank again. The old wizard had been silent most of the morning. Now he spoke for the first time, his voice the dry rustle of pages.

"Caleth. Look at the workmanship."

"I see it."

"No. Look." Aravashniel raised a trembling finger and pointed at a knight whose hand was reaching forward as if to bless something — or to ward something off, Caleth could not tell which. "These are not statues that were carved. These are statues that were grown. These men and women were the carvers' families. The carvers were —"

He did not finish. He did not have to.

"The First Crusade," Caleth murmured. "The ones who were taken."

"The ones who were touched. The ones who could not go home." Aravashniel's voice was a thin thread. "Some of them came down here. To live out their lives in the dark, away from the eyes of the surface. And to raise their children. And their children…"

He trailed off again.

Children of the touched. Children of the children of the touched.

He looked again at the carved faces in the cavern. Each one a portrait, each one made by the hand of someone who had loved the dead. He felt his throat close.

"Shelyn," he said softly, "have mercy on them."

He was about to turn and say something to the others — to Korroc, perhaps, who would have a paladin's word for this — when the first one dropped.

It came down from the cavern roof on a whisper.

That was what saved Thane from worse than what came: that Caleth had heard the whisper before he had seen the shape, that the half-elf's hand had jerked toward his glaive on instinct a half-instant before his eyes confirmed what his ears had reported. He turned. He shouted.

"Above us!"

A pillar of pale flesh dropped from the dark and struck the stone three paces from Thane.

Caleth's eye, again, catalogued before his fear could. Pillar of flesh. No proper face. A ring of writhing tentacles around what passed for a mouth. A skirt of grey membrane around the base, drifting like a jellyfish's veil. He did not know the species. He had never seen one in any bestiary. They were not the sort of creature that lived under sun.

Thane was already moving. The dwarf-rogue's reflexes were the reflexes of a man who had spent years on the roofwalks and learned to assume that anything coming from above wanted you dead — he ducked, drew, and slashed all in one motion, his off-hand dagger raking across the thing's pulpy flank. Caleth saw the cut land. Saw the strange, almost milky liquid spurt.

Saw the tentacles come around Thane's chest like a striking serpent.

The slam of impact was enormous. Caleth heard the crack of it — the wet, wrong sound of weight meeting bone — and Thane simply folded. His knees gave. His daggers fell from his fingers with a small chiming sound that was, Caleth would think later, the worst sound he had heard all day. The creature gathered him up into the membrane of its skirt the way a fisherman gathers a fish into a net, and Thane vanished inside the writhing mass.

Korroc roared.

A second one dropped.

This one fell at Caleth's own feet, and a third half a heartbeat later by Nageru, and the cavern was suddenly full of the things, their tentacles questing, their pillar-bodies pulsing.

Caleth's training took him before his fear did. He dropped his back foot, brought the glaive up in the long high guard the spire-masters called the heron's throat, and thrust. The leaf-blade punched into the soft pulpy flesh of the creature in front of him with a horrid yielding sensation, like driving a knife into a wineskin.

The thing did not bleed. It embraced him.

He had time to register that the tentacles were stronger than they looked — much stronger — and then they were around his arms, around his chest, around his throat, and he could not get his glaive back, could not get his hands free, could not get air.

He saw Korroc, peripherally, charging the thing that had Thane.

He saw Nageru, peripherally, settling into a crouch.

He saw, peripherally, his own breath leaving him.

His left hand spasmed — and the wrist-sheath he had built for himself as a young man, the one his teachers had laughed at and then quietly approved, clicked. A dagger dropped into his palm. He did not need to see what he was doing. He had practiced this draw in the dark for ten years. He drove the dagger up under the crushing band of tentacles and into the thing's bulk, hard, twisting.

It squeezed.

Caleth heard himself make a sound that was not a word.

His ribs creaked. His vision flowered black at the edges. He had time, in the long slow second before unconsciousness, to think I have not even told them about her yet, and then he was aware of the world only in pieces.

A flash of green-acid from Aravashniel's hand, missing.

The wet crunch of Korroc's hammer caving in the creature on Thane.

The sensation of being lifted — the thing was climbing, climbing the wall with him pinned in its skirt like a wasp's prize.

A sudden, impossible, warmth.

Korroc's healing wave rolled over him like a wash of summer water and he came back into himself with a gasp, his lungs filling, his vision clearing. He was twelve feet up the cavern wall, wrapped in the clutching membrane of a thing that was hauling him toward a dark crevice in the stone, and his glaive was somewhere on the floor below.

He still had the dagger.

He stabbed it. He stabbed it again. He had no leverage, no breath for a real strike — the dagger went in to the hilt but the angle was wrong and the thing barely shuddered. Then the constriction came back, worse this time, and he felt his sword-arm pinned against his ribs and his other arm pinned across his throat —

A war-cry from below — a dwarven war-cry — and Korroc was at the wall.

Caleth would remember, after, the look on Korroc's face: head tilted back so his beard pointed at the ceiling, both hands wrapped around his hammer's haft, his short legs braced against the cavern wall itself for purchase. The hammer came up overhead in a two-handed swing whose arc was beautiful in a way Caleth's painter's eye would not let him forget. It was the swing of a man who had spent his life at a forge. It was the swing of a man who meant it.

The hammer hit the creature square in the bulk of its pillar-body and pulped it.

Caleth dropped.

Twelve feet to stone, with the wet remains of the thing slapping down around him. He landed hard on his side, the air going out of him for the second time in a minute, and he lay there blinking up at the cavern roof and at the stone-faced crusaders ringed around him in their long sad vigil and thought, with strange clarity, They saw this once, too. They saw their own people taken.

A second wave of healing rolled through him — Korroc again, the dwarf was spending spells like a drunk spending coins — and Caleth dragged himself upright in time to see Nageru bring both fists down on the last of the things in a single descending blow that dropped it twitching to the cavern floor.

Silence.

The kind of silence that comes after sudden violence in a cavern, where the echo of your own breathing seems an obscenity.

Caleth wiped foul ichor from his face with the back of his sleeve. He looked across the cavern at Korroc, who was already moving toward Thane to check him, and at Nageru, who was standing with his hands open and his head bowed in some brief private prayer of thanks, and at Aravashniel, who was leaning hard on his staff and breathing as if breathing hurt.

He looked at the statues.

The crusaders looked back.

"My thanks," Caleth said quietly, and was not entirely sure who he was speaking to.

Aravashniel found his voice again, after a moment. He did not raise it. He did not need to. The cavern carried it.

"They came down here," the old wizard said softly, looking around at the carved company, "because they could not bear to be looked at any longer. And they raised children. And the children were what the touch had made them. And the children raised children. And no one ever came down to find them."

Caleth said nothing for a long moment. He bent and retrieved his glaive from where it had fallen and wiped it carefully on the hem of his cloak.

"Until now," he said at last.

The next cavern smelled of woodsmoke.

Caleth, in front because his half-elven eyes drank the dim light better than the others', slowed and raised a hand. The smoke was recent. So was the small orange flicker he could see further in — a campfire, banked but still alive, its light playing over the sides of a cooking-pot and the rumpled folds of a bedroll laid out beside it. Utensils, too. A tin cup. A wooden spoon worn smooth with use.

Someone lived down here.

Or had, until very recently.

"Smoke," he said softly.

Nageru drifted up beside him. The aasimar's expression was unreadable in the gloom, but his nose wrinkled fractionally. "Fresh."

"Aye," Korroc said behind them, and Caleth heard the dwarf shift his shield. "And the smell of a trap."

"Possibly a trap," Caleth corrected, mildly. "Possibly someone's home."

He took a step further into the cavern's mouth, glaive lowered to a non-threatening angle, eyes scanning. The bedroll. The cup. The pot. The —

The air at the edge of the campfire's light folded.

Caleth had a sliver of a half-instant — the trained Spireborn instinct that registered displacement before sight, the small wrongness of an outline that should not be there — and then a dwarf was suddenly visible where no dwarf had been a heartbeat before, his fingers already shaping the final glyph of a spell.

"Down!" Caleth shouted —

— but a streak of pale violet light was already lancing across the cavern, and it struck Korroc in the breastplate with a thump and a flare, and Korroc grunted as if punched.

Magic missile.

A real mage, then. Not some hedge-conjurer. A mage who had been here lying invisible by his own fire, waiting.

The dwarf rose into the air.

He simply rose, his feet leaving the cavern floor in a smooth twenty-foot ascent that took him up out of melee reach, and as he rose his hand was already shaping the next spell. Caleth read the somatics from across the chamber and his blood went cold.

Color spray.

He had a half-instant to shout warning. He used it. "Eyes!"

He tore his own eyes away. He saw, in his peripheral vision, Korroc and Thane and Nageru all do the same — every paladin's instinct, every monk's discipline, every veteran's reflex — and the prismatic burst that erupted from the floating dwarf's hand washed over them and did not take. Caleth heard the slight, irritated grunt from above. The mage had expected at least one of them to drop.

Caleth slung his glaive across his back in a single motion. Wrong tool for this. He pulled the shortbow from his shoulder instead, nocked, and loosed in one practiced flow. The arrow streaked up into the air —

— and struck.

A flesh-shot, not a kill, but the dwarf jerked in mid-hover. Caleth heard him swear in Dwarven. Behind Caleth, Anevia had unslung her own shortbow despite her broken leg, her face white with the pain of standing on it, and her arrow followed Caleth's a half-second later and also struck. Aravashniel's hand moved in the small precise gestures of an acid dart and a hissing droplet of green crossed the air and bit the floating dwarf in the shoulder.

The mage was bleeding, now, in three places. He hovered, snarling, recalculating.

Korroc flew.

Caleth would never quite get over it. The dwarf in his full plate, hammer in hand, simply rose into the air — the silver scale at his belt catching what little light there was — and ascended toward the floating mage with the slow inevitable dignity of a man who has decided that he, personally, is going to end this. Nageru's javelin hummed past him as he climbed, taking the mage in the thigh, but the mage stayed aloft.

Korroc reached him.

The dwarven warhammer came up — up, Caleth realized, an underhand strike, brutal and short — and slammed into the floating mage's chest with a sound Caleth would carry with him into other deeper caves. There was a crack and a strange wet pop, the sound of a ribcage giving up its argument with the world, and the mage flew backwards through the air and struck the cavern wall and slid down it leaving a long red smear and lay in a heap on the stone, and did not move again.

Korroc descended.

He landed by the body and stood over it for a long moment, his hammer hanging loose at his side, his expression unreadable behind the beard.

Caleth, glaive once more in hand, came up beside him.

"Why?" Korroc said quietly, almost to himself. "Why's a lone dwarf-mage down here, in a cave he doesn't live in, waiting invisible?"

No one had an answer.

Korroc shook his head. "Walk away, lad. There's no understanding it."

Caleth nodded slowly. But he made a mental note to draw the dwarf's face in his journal at the next rest — every detail he could remember — because his Spireborn memory would not let him forget a face he had seen, and the face of an enemy was a coin you could spend later.

The far end of the cavern rose in a steep, broken incline.

Pitons studded the wall — generations of them, some rust-eaten almost to powder, others surprisingly fresh — and old hand-cut grips had been chiseled into the stone where the natural holds gave out. A practiced climb, the kind that cave-explorers leave behind for those who come after.

Nageru went first. The aasimar climbed the way water flows — Caleth, watching from below, realized he had never seen a man move with so little wasted motion. Nageru did not climb so much as he rose, hand and foot and hand and foot, and he was at the top before Caleth had finished framing the praise he wanted to offer.

Thane turned to Anevia, who was leaning hard on him, her splinted leg held off the ground.

"How are we getting you up?" Thane said, pitching the question to the room because he did not, Caleth saw, want to ask it directly to her face.

The look Anevia gave him would have melted steel.

"Watch me," she said, and hobbled to the wall and put her hands on the first holds.

She climbed. Slowly. Painfully. Her good leg did most of the work, finding holds her bad leg could not, her hands taking the slack, her teeth set so hard that Caleth could see the muscle flex in her jaw from twenty feet below. Twice she paused, breathing through the pain. Twice she found new holds and pushed on. She reached the top in, all told, perhaps four minutes, which was not bad for a woman with a broken leg climbing a forty-foot ascent in the dark.

When she reached the lip Nageru took her wrist with both hands and lifted her over as if she weighed nothing.

Caleth filed that away. A useful woman, Anevia Tirabade. A very useful woman. He could already see why Thane loved her.

Korroc looked at the climb and his shoulders sagged.

"I hate climbing," the dwarf muttered to no one. "I hate climbing so very much."

He climbed. He got halfway up. He was reaching for a hold that an unkind piton had loosened over the years, and the piton came out in his hand, and the dwarf came off the wall like a sack of bricks and hit the floor with a tremendous clang of plate on stone and an explosion of profanity Caleth only half-followed because had only learned some of the curse words since knowing Korroc.

Caleth, despite himself, laughed.

Korroc lay on his back in the rubble of his own dignity and glared up at him.

"Not a word, half-elf."

"Not a word, paladin."

"Not. A. Word."

Korroc rolled to his feet, brushed himself off, hefted his hammer, glared at the climb as if it had personally insulted his mother, and started up again. The second time he made it. He complained about the height the entire way. Caleth watched him the whole climb and did not laugh again, though it was a near thing.

Caleth himself went up last, after handing his glaive and the gear up to Nageru on a length of rope. The climb was easy enough. Half-elven balance, what training he had gained in his season at the Spire, the cliff-work he had drilled against salt-wind with the sea booming below. He found himself thinking, with an odd quiet pang, of those teachers — of the long patient climbs, the senior brother shouting holds from above, the cool indifferent stone under his fingers. He wondered if any of them were still alive. The Mordant Spire kept its own counsel and shared little with the wider world; he had not heard from his sister-instructor in seven years.

He topped the lip and rolled to his feet beside Nageru.

"Thank you," he said.

"You climb well."

"I had patient teachers."

Nageru looked at him for a moment. The aasimar's gold eyes had a way of holding a gaze just half a beat longer than was comfortable, the way of a man who had been raised to see.

"You hold much you do not say, Caleth."

Caleth smiled, crookedly, the way the Spire had taught him to smile when something true had been said about him.

"I am Spireborn, my friend. It is what we do."

Nageru considered this. Then he inclined his head — a small, formal, accepting bow — and turned away to scout the next passage, and Caleth was quietly grateful for the courtesy.

Korroc's hand came up suddenly and they all froze.

"Voices," the dwarf whispered.

Thane was already moving. Caleth had, by this point in the day, formed a quiet appreciation for how Thane moved — the dwarf was not graceful, exactly, but he was exact, every footfall placed like a dagger thrown at a target. He ghosted along the wall to the bend in the passage and peered around it for a long moment, his head perfectly still.

He came back and signed two, fingers held up and pointed, sign for targets, ahead, my count, no more.

Caleth looked at Korroc. "What now?"

Korroc grunted and pointed at his own breastplate. I'll go.

Caleth pointed at him in return. "You have the most armor."

Korroc grunted again and walked into the cavern.

His plate clanked. Of course it clanked. Caleth, watching from concealment, saw Thane shoot the dwarf a look of such pure disgust that Caleth had to clamp a hand over his own mouth to muffle his laugh. The voices on the far side of the cave had already gone quiet — they had heard Korroc coming the moment he set foot on the stone — and Caleth caught Nageru's eye and shook his head in fond exasperation. Subtlety is not a virtue of the dwarven peoples.

Thane, having abandoned hope of a stealthy approach, slipped forward along the cavern wall to find a vantage from which to see what they were walking into.

Caleth quietly cast mage armor on himself — a slow shimmer of blue that wreathed his unarmored body and faded into the gloom — and stepped up to the corner.

Korroc's voice rang out, gruff and deliberately friendly. "Hello?"

A pause.

A second pause.

Then Korroc, more loudly: "Hello? We're lost, we need help."

And that, Caleth thought, was actually rather well done. Whoever was on the far side of the rubble would be calculating, in this very moment, whether four-or-more people who announced themselves and asked for help were more likely to be friend or foe. Korroc had put a thumb on the scale of the answer.

Caleth came around the corner with his hands open and visible.

He saw the collapsed tower first. A round watchtower of mortared rubble, perhaps forty feet at its base, half-fallen across the floor of the cavern in a great ruin of stone and dust. Recent, his trained eye said at once. Within the last day. There was no patina on the broken edges. The cracks were sharp and pale.

Then he saw the people.

Two of them, standing back from the rubble, hands raised cautiously. One was a woman whose left arm seemed to grow into her side at the wrong angle, her face heavily marked with knotted growths. The other was —

Caleth's breath caught.

The man was, in the wrong half of his face, beautiful. The high cheekbone and the long black eye and the elegant line of jaw of an elf of old and pure blood — a face Caleth had seen, in less ravaged forms, on his own teachers in the Spire. A face that could have walked out of an illuminated manuscript. The other half was reptilian, scaled and ridged, with a single curving black horn rising from above the right brow.

Beautiful and terrible in one face.

Mongrelfolk, Caleth thought, and for a moment the word — which he had heard all his life used as casually as farmer or fisherman or thief — sat on his tongue as a lie. That is not a mongrel. That is a man whose grandparents were the carved statues we just walked past. That is what the touch did to the children of the touched. That is —

Aravashniel's words from an hour before came back to him: no one ever came down to find them.

Caleth swallowed.

The half-elven half-saurian man spoke. His voice was beautiful — a low, formal voice with the careful diction of someone who had taught himself language from books because there was no one above the surface he was permitted to speak with.

"If your intent is evil, I ask you to move on and leave us. But if you are here on good terms, we could use help. As you can see, ill fate has befallen us."

Caleth's heart broke a little.

"We are here on good terms," he said, before anyone else could speak. He stepped forward, glaive lowered, and bowed — not deeply, but properly, the small respectful bow of the Spire. "I am Caleth. We came down from Kenabres, not by choice. We were caught in a rift when the wardstone fell."

The man's good eye widened. "The wardstone fell?"

"This morning. Or what we are calling morning."

A muffled cry came from inside the rubble.

All thoughts of explanation went out of Caleth at once. There was someone under the tower.

The woman — Dyra, he would learn — was already at the rubble, scrabbling at the smaller stones with hands gone white at the knuckles. Thane was at her side a heartbeat later, and Korroc beside him, and the rest of the company moved up.

Horgus, at Caleth's elbow, made a sound of disgust. The merchant's lip curled and he hissed, just loud enough for those nearest to hear: "Mongrel men. Don't trust them. They cannot be trusted."

Anevia, leaning on her makeshift crutch, whirled on him. "Horgus Gwerm, hush."

Horgus opened his mouth.

Caleth looked at him. He did not say anything. He simply looked, the long level look the Spire had taught him to use when a younger student spoke out of turn — a look that contained no anger and no contempt and no insult, only a quiet I am paying attention to you, and I will remember this.

Horgus closed his mouth.

Caleth filed that away too. The merchant could be steered. Good. He had worried that Horgus would prove an actual problem rather than a mere irritation, and it was useful to know that the man could be managed by a gaze.

He turned and went to help with the rubble.

It took hours.

Caleth catalogued the work even as he did it. We have eight able bodies if you count Aravashniel and Anevia, neither of whom should be lifting. Six, then. Two are dwarves built for stone-work. One is an aasimar with the strength of two ordinary men. One is a Spireborn paladin whose chief contribution is going to be steady patience. One is a noble whose chief contribution is going to be supervising. One is —

He looked at Lann, who was straining alongside Thane at a stone the size of a sheep, his half-elven half-saurian arms corded with effort, his beautiful ruined face streaked with sweat.

One is a man who is afraid for his friend.

He stepped in beside Lann and put his shoulder to the same stone. They moved it together.

For a long time they worked in silence, broken only by grunts and the scrape of stone and Anevia's quiet, patient voice from the side calling out warnings about loose pieces. Aravashniel sat by, unable to lift, but his half-restored eyes scanned the rubble with the trained focus of a structural engineer — every now and then he would call out, "Not that one yet, take the wedge first," or "That stone is bracing the next, leave it a moment," and the work would adjust around his guidance.

At some point in the second hour, Caleth realized that Nageru had drifted away from the heavy work and was talking quietly with Horgus at the cavern's edge.

He watched out of the corner of his eye.

He could not hear the words. But he could see Nageru's body language — the aasimar standing easy, hands relaxed, head slightly inclined, the posture of a man who was not lecturing but asking — and he could see Horgus's face, which started in its usual sour curl and slowly, over the course of perhaps three minutes of gentle aasimar conversation, changed. Not into kindness. Caleth was not sure Horgus was capable of kindness. But into a kind of grudging decency, the decency of a man who has been quietly reminded that he has a face and that the face is being looked at.

Horgus walked over to the rubble and put his hands on a stone and pushed.

Caleth caught Nageru's eye across the work and inclined his head a fraction. Well done.

Nageru returned the bow with the same tiny inclination. It was nothing.

It was not nothing. Caleth filed that, too.

After a while the work narrowed to a single great problem: a boulder, half the height of a man, that lay across the chest of the trapped figure. They had cleared everything else. The boulder was the wall between life and death.

Aravashniel limped over and laid his hands on it. He frowned, his half-restored eyes squinting.

"Can you get it?" Caleth asked quietly.

The old wizard shook his head. "Not by myself. Not in this state. But with all of us, and a lever, and perhaps — perhaps a levitate spell to take half the weight while you all push…"

"Show us."

Aravashniel showed them. It took another hour to construct the lever from a fallen rafter and the proper fulcrum from a smaller stone. It took more time for Aravashniel to gather enough focus to pull the levitate spell out of his half-blind memory. But when at last they were ready — Korroc and Nageru and Lann at the lever, Caleth and Thane and Dyra at the boulder's far side, Horgus and Anevia coordinating from the corners and even Aravashniel adding his small wizardly weight — the boulder moved.

Six inches. Then a foot. Then enough.

Dyra was on her knees in the gap before the boulder had fully shifted, her hands diving into the dust, and a moment later she was dragging out a third figure — another mongrelfolk, a man whose deformities were more dramatic than the others' but whose chest was, blessedly, still rising and falling.

Lann's good eye filled with tears.

He did not let them fall. He did, however, turn to Caleth and reach for his hand, and grip it hard, with both of his.

"My thanks," he said. His voice was thick. "My eternal thanks. I am Lann. This is Dyra. The man you have just saved is Crel. We are of Nathholm. We were assigned to this watchtower. There was an earthquake — the tower came down — we could not — we could not —"

"There was a fight," Caleth said gently. "Above the city. A demon and a dragon. The Storm King and Terendelev. The earthquake was the wardstone going down."

Lann's face did something complicated. The good half went pale. The saurian half flushed an unhealthy purple.

"The wardstone."

"Yes."

Lann turned to Dyra, who had not heard, who was bent over Crel and weeping silently into the dust of his cheek. He laid a hand on her shoulder. He spoke to her in a language Caleth did not know — a tongue with the cadence of old Elven but the consonants of something else, something deeper, something that had grown in the dark.

She looked up. Her face went very still.

Lann turned back to the company.

"You must come to Nathholm," he said. "You must speak with our chief. Sull must hear this. If the wardstone is gone, if the surface is — if the surface is what you are telling me it is, then everything has changed for us as well. Will you come?"

Caleth looked at Korroc, at Thane, at Nageru. He saw three nods.

"We will come," he said.

The chasm caught them an hour later.

Caleth had been expecting something like it. He had felt, walking the new corridor, the small wrong tilt in the angle of the floor that meant they were walking a passage that had been recently twisted. Cracks in the walls were too clean. Dust on the floor was too thin. The Worldwound's tremor had reached down here and moved things.

When they came around a bend and the corridor simply ended in a ten-foot chasm dropping seventy feet into the dark, no one was surprised.

Lann swore. Dyra swore in the deeper tongue. Crel, leaning between them, swore in both.

"Nathholm is past this," Lann said grimly. "If this much damage is here — gods, we don't know what we're going home to."

Nageru drifted to the chasm's edge and looked across. "Ten feet."

"For you," Korroc muttered. "For you, ten feet is a yawn."

Caleth was already pulling his coil of rope out of his pack. He grinned at Korroc. "Anyone else have rope? Better to have two."

Three more coils came out of three different packs.

Caleth laughed — a real laugh, the first one of the day that came easy — and the laugh seemed to ease something in the cavern. Even Lann smiled, a little, his ruined beautiful face splitting in a way Caleth would have liked to draw if there had been time.

Nageru took the end of the strongest rope, backed up three steps, and jumped.

The aasimar cleared the chasm with two feet to spare and landed in a soft crouch on the far lip. He turned, found a sturdy outcropping of stone, and made the rope fast. Caleth secured the near end to a similar projection on his side. They strung a second rope above the first as a handline.

One by one the company crossed.

Korroc, complaining bitterly, dragged himself across hand over hand and arrived on the far side with his face the color of old wine. Thane and Anevia crossed together, Anevia clinging to Thane's back, the dwarf moving with the steady careful confidence of a dwarf who had crossed worse on rooftop work. Aravashniel, frail as he was, made it across by the slow patient method of looping his belt around the upper rope and shuffling along it inches at a time, his half-eyes squinted shut.

Horgus —

Horgus slipped.

He had been doing well, in fact, until his foot caught a loose flake of stone three-quarters across, and his hand jerked off the upper rope, and he was suddenly hanging only by his other hand from a line that was already starting to slip out of his soft merchant's fingers.

Caleth, on the near side still, started forward — too far, he was too far —

Nageru moved.

The aasimar had been kneeling at the far anchor point. He came up off the stone in a single coiled-spring motion, took two strides, and threw himself flat on his belly at the chasm's edge. His arm shot down. His hand closed around Horgus's wrist the instant before the merchant's grip on the rope went.

For a moment Horgus simply hung there, two hundred and fifty pounds of silk-clad merchant dangling sixty feet over nothing, held by a single aasimar wrist.

Nageru did not strain. His face did not change. He simply pulled, a long smooth pull that brought Horgus up over the lip and onto the safe stone.

Horgus lay on his back, white as paper, his chest heaving, his rings glittering uselessly on his clutching fingers.

Nageru sat back on his heels and folded his hands in his lap.

After a long moment — a very long moment — Horgus rolled his head sideways and looked up at the aasimar who had just saved his life.

"Thank you," he croaked.

It was, Caleth thought from across the chasm, the first genuine thing Horgus Gwerm had said all day.

Nageru bowed his head fractionally. "It is what I am here for."

Caleth crossed last. He gathered the gear, threw the smaller coils across to be caught, paused at the chasm's edge with the heaviest pack on his back, and considered. He could climb the rope. It would take a minute, maybe two. He was tired. The day had been long. The half-elven part of him was beginning to feel it.

He shrugged.

He backed up four paces, took three running strides, and jumped.

In the air, mid-leap, he reached for the small clean spell that lived in the back of his memory and pulled it forward. The world folded — that small cool sensation of a half-second of nowhere — and his feet came down on the far lip of the chasm with no shock at all, as if he had simply walked the last step.

Korroc, watching, threw up his hands. "Of course he can do that. Of course. Why would he climb the rope. Why would anyone climb the rope. I could have climbed the rope, but the half-elf — "

"Peace, dwarf," Caleth said, smiling.

"No peace."

The next cavern's mouth was where Lann stopped them.

"Spore cougher," Lann said quietly, gesturing ahead. "A fungal creature. It's been here a while. With this many of us, it'll likely wake. You look like you can fight it."

Caleth rolled his shoulders and nodded. "We can probably take it."

They moved into the chamber — a circular cavern, perhaps sixty feet across, with passages branching off in every direction. Lann pointed to one across the way: "That one goes up. Everything else goes down. We don't go that way."

"And the spore cougher?"

"By the up-passage. The ropy thing. With the bodies."

Caleth saw it. Saw them.

A fungal growth, ropy and grey, sprawled across the wall beside the upward passage. Beside it, two human bodies, sprawled in the stone, weapons fallen from their hands. The bodies looked recent. A day. Two at most. The fungus appeared to be inactive, but —

"Let me try," Nageru said quietly. "I will walk close. If it does not stir, we are safe. If it does, I am the fastest of us."

Caleth nodded. He did not like it. But Nageru was correct.

The aasimar walked, soft-footed, to within ten feet of the spore cougher.

It did not move.

He motioned Korroc up. The dwarf joined him.

Together they walked to the bodies. Caleth followed at a careful distance, glaive ready, eyes on the fungus.

The fungus, it transpired, was simply dead. Long dead. Its ropes were dry and dusty. Whatever battle had played out here, the fungus had won and then died of its own age days later, which struck Caleth as a kind of cruel parable.

Nageru knelt by the bodies. "Looks like a fight no one survived."

The rest of the company came up.

Caleth bent over the second body. There was something clutched in the dead hand — the rigor had locked the fingers around it. He gently pried the fingers open.

A holy symbol fell out into the dust.

A familiar holy symbol.

Black iron. The bull's head. The long horns.

Baphomet.

Nageru's brow drew down. "Heretics?"

Caleth, mind racing, picked the symbol up between two fingers. "Are you sure? Maybe it's a warning. Maybe they pulled it from someone they killed, and then died of their own wounds before they could carry it back."

Nageru, kindly: "Their weapons are not the longswords of Iomedae. Strange?"

Caleth turned to argue — they were crusaders, they bore the tabards of crusaders, every part of his Shelyn-trained heart wanted them to be honest dead — and saw Thane crouched over the first body with his face gone hard.

"I don't think they're crusaders," Thane said.

Korroc looked at him. "What are you thinking?"

Thane peeled back the edge of the dead man's tabard. Beneath it, on the bare skin of the chest, was a brand. Ridged scar tissue worked into the unmistakable horned mark of Baphomet. Old. Healed. Made on purpose.

Caleth looked at it for a long moment.

"Now that is evidence," he said quietly.

He stood up. His knees were not entirely steady. He had been prepared to be wrong, prepared to defend the dead even from his own companions' assumptions, because that was what Shelyn asked of him — see beauty in the broken thing, see honesty in the doubted thing — but the brand on the man's chest was a thing made deliberately and worn deliberately, and the mongrel tribes had not put it there.

These men had been crusaders, once, perhaps. Or had pretended to be. Or were currently pretending to be.

Lann spoke, hesitantly. "There have been men down here lately. More than usual. They've been working with… a bad tribe. The Vescavor."

"Bad tribe?" Caleth asked.

"The tribe works with evil things. They're violent. They're — they're not like us." Lann's good eye dropped. "They are what we could have been, if we had given in to what is in our blood."

Thane stood up. His face was iron.

"Templars of the Ivory Labyrinth."

He said the name like a verdict.

"There have been rumors for half a year now. They've been infiltrating — guard companies, crusader orders, even priesthoods. Wearing the colors of Iomedae and answering to Baphomet. We thought they were a problem of information — spies and counter-spies. We did not think they were a problem of —" He gestured at the dead. "— of boots on stone. They've been moving down here. And if they're working with a tribe of mongrelfolk who serve the demon-lords — "

He did not finish.

Anevia, who had been silent through all of this, spoke up. Her voice was steady.

"We need to get to the surface. We need to find Irabeth. She has to know about this."

Thane tore a strip from the dead man's tabard and used it to wrap the unholy symbol carefully — touching it only through cloth — and tied it tight and put it in an inside pocket of his cloak where it would not lie against the silver scale.

He did not want those two things touching, even through wool.

He looked up at the company. He looked at Lann, Dyra, Crel — the three mongrelfolk standing apart from the dead, their ruined beautiful faces watching the company with a wary mix of hope and grief.

"We will get this to the surface," Caleth said. "And we will get you home, first."

Lann bowed his head.

Nathholm, when at last they came to it, was a wonder.

A great cavern, two hundred feet across, lit by a hundred small lanterns and torches whose light played over a lake — a still black mirror of a lake, in the cavern's center, with a rocky island rising from its middle. On the island were buildings. Stone houses, square and squat and patient, lights warm in their narrow windows. Rafts of mismatched timber bobbed at the lake's near shore.

Caleth's painter's eye drank it.

It was beautiful. It was a home. A century of mongrel hands had built it in the deep dark, and a century of mongrel hearts had lit the lanterns. He thought of the carved crusaders in the cavern with the spore creatures, the portraits made by hands that had loved their subjects, and he thought: This. This is what they made instead of dying. This is the children's children's children's answer to being unlovable.

The mongrel sentries at the great wooden doors questioned Lann briefly, in the deeper tongue, and let them through.

They were taken across the lake on rafts — Caleth balancing his weight carefully, Korroc gripping the raft's edge with both hands and muttering something about how no proper dwarf would build this far from a forge — and led up a stone path to the largest of the island's buildings.

There they were brought before Sull.

Chief Sull was a massive mongrel, bloated and hunched, with one eye gone milky-white and the other small and shrewd. His features were a riot of mismatched parts — a human nose, a goblin's lower jaw, a dwarf's heavy brow — and he looked at the company with an expression Caleth had a moment of trouble reading. Anger? Suspicion? Pain?

All three. All three at once.

Sull spoke without preamble, his voice gravelly and tired. "Lann. Why are outsiders in the cavern."

Lann began. "Chief, they —"

"I am asking them."

Sull turned the milky eye on Caleth.

Caleth — who had been raised by foster parents who did not waste a word on a man who would not earn it, and who had been a paladin of Shelyn long enough to know that some men needed beauty and some men needed bluntness — chose bluntness.

"We fell from the surface," Caleth said. "Hard. The wardstone of Kenabres is destroyed. The dragon Terendelev is dead. A balor lord called Khorramzadeh attacked the city at noon during the Armasse festival. We have been making our way down through the caverns for what we believe to be most of a day. Your people found us in a collapsed watchtower, and we helped them dig out their friend. They asked us to come and speak with you. Here we are."

A long silence.

Sull's small shrewd eye searched his face.

"You are a paladin," Sull said at last.

"I am."

"What god."

"Shelyn."

The milky eye blinked. "Huh."

Sull was silent for another long moment. Then he turned slightly, taking in Korroc, Nageru, Thane.

"Tell me everything," he said.

So they did.

Caleth led the telling, with Nageru and Korroc and Thane filling in details — the rift, the falling, the camp of the long-dead crusader, the fly cavern and the consecrated shrine of Torag, the statue cavern of the old First Crusade dead, the Templar in the cave with the magical fire, the second pair of Templars dead with their bull-symbols clutched in their fists. Caleth made a careful point of unwrapping the symbol Thane had taken, showing it to Sull, and explaining their conclusion: that the Templars of the Ivory Labyrinth were moving in the Underdepths in numbers, working with a hostile tribe of mongrelfolk whom Lann had named the Vescavor.

Sull listened. He did not interrupt. When Caleth finished, the chief sat for a long time looking at the bull-headed symbol in Caleth's open palm.

"The Vescavor," Sull said at last. "Yes. They have been bolder in the last months. We have lost three hunters to them. We thought it was the usual quarrel. We did not know they had brought these into their counsel."

He looked up.

"Will you carry a message for me to your surface?"

Caleth nodded. "We will."

"Tell whomever you can reach — your Watch, your crusader-marshals, your priests of the white sun — that Nathholm stands. We have always stood. We will stand now. We will not let demon-tainted men hide in our caverns and stab the Mendev that — that has been what it has been to us." A bitter line moved in Sull's heavy face. "We have grievances. We have many. But the Worldwound is not a grievance we share with the demon. We will share what we know. We will harry the Templars where we find them. We will hold the lower passages."

Caleth's chest tightened.

"That is more than the surface deserves of you," he said quietly.

Sull's milky eye shifted to him.

"That is for the surface to one day deserve," he said. "Not for me to give as if it is owed."

Caleth, after a moment, bowed.

It was a deeper bow than the one he had given Lann. It was the bow the Spire taught for elder peers.

Sull saw it. The chief's small shrewd eye narrowed in what might have been satisfaction.

"There is one more thing," Sull said. "The Vescavor. If you find any in your way as you climb — any of them — kill them. They will not parley. They have set themselves against us and against you. Every one of them you put down is one fewer the Templars can use." He paused. "I will give you what aid I can in return."

He gestured to a side-room. An attendant — a younger mongrel, less ravaged than most, with merely a cleft lip and an extra finger on the left hand — emerged with a small carved wooden tray. On the tray were potions. Caleth counted nine. He could see, by their resonance, that six were healing and three were restoration.

"For you," Sull said. "And a meal. And beds. You have been long in the dark today. Sleep with us. Eat with us. Climb tomorrow."

Caleth took the tray with both hands and bowed again.

"Chief Sull," he said, "we will not forget Nathholm."

"See that you do not."

That night Caleth did not sleep at first.

The mongrelfolk had given them a long stone-walled room with woven-reed mats on the floor and small clay lamps in the wall-niches, and the others had eaten their fill of fish-stew and dark cave-mushroom bread and had fallen asleep one by one. Korroc was snoring within minutes. Thane lay curled near Anevia, who had taken the longest to settle, her broken leg propped on a folded blanket. Aravashniel slept sitting up against the wall as old men do. Nageru had folded himself into a meditative pose in the corner that Caleth could not quite tell was sleep or not. Horgus, after some grumbling about the bedding, had also gone under.

Caleth sat near the lamp by the door with his journal open on his knee and the silver dragon-scale in his hand.

He turned it slowly. The lamplight caught it. The scale was warm. It had been warm since he had picked it up.

He thought about the woman in grey.

He thought about her here we go smile.

He thought about how she had stood in the crowd watching Hulrun on the stage with the polite attentive expression of a citizen at her own city's holy day, and how that meant — and he was only now putting this together — that Terendelev had been attending Armasse. Not as a dragon. Not in her power. As a member of the city. As one of the people. As a participant.

She had been worshiping with them.

That single small fact rearranged something in him that he had not realized needed rearranging. He had been schooled in Spireborn doctrine — that the great immortal beings of the world were other, that their concerns were not the concerns of folk, that to encounter one was to encounter a force of nature with a face. And here was the most powerful being in the entire region of Mendev, a silver wyrm older than the city itself, standing in the crowd in a borrowed body so she could attend the festival as a neighbor.

He thought about how, when the world tore, she had not even hesitated. She had not weighed her chances against the Storm King. She had simply gone up, because that was what you did when your city was attacked, even if you knew you would die for it.

He thought about the smile.

The smile had said: Oh, hello. Yes. You see me. Don't worry about it. Watch a moment.

The smile had said, in the last second of her woman-shape: I have been seen by a half-elf paladin of beauty. Good. That will serve.

And then the scale.

Caleth turned it in his fingers again, and felt the patient gentle dying will press on his — get them out, get them out, get them out — and he understood, in that small lamp-lit stone room two hundred feet under a city that was burning, that this scale had not fallen to him by chance.

She had given it to him.

Specifically him.

A Spireborn paladin of Shelyn whose particular gift was seeing, whose half-elven memory would hold her face for the rest of his life, whose Spire-trained discretion would carry her last word without spilling it to the wrong ears. A glaive-hand who had a wizard's mind and a paladin's oath and an artist's eye and a foreigner's distance from the politics of Mendev. A half-elf who had stood three feet from her in the crowd and thought she has nice eyes.

She had picked him.

He set the scale carefully on the journal page. He took up his pen. He drew her face from memory — the woman's face, the borrowed face, the here we go smile — and beneath it he wrote a single line in the small careful Spireborn hand his masters had taught him.

Terendelev of Kenabres, last seen alive at the noon of Armasse, 16 Arodus. She was kind. She was watching us. She knew me before she died. I will get them out.

He blew gently on the ink. He closed the journal. He laid the scale beside his hand and lay down and at last, at last, allowed sleep to take him.

He dreamed, briefly, of a woman in grey.

She was standing on a cliff above a sea he had never seen. Her hair was the color of a winter sky. She turned when she felt him and smiled — that same small here we go smile — and reached out a hand that was not quite a hand and brushed his cheek.

"Carry it," she said.

"I will."

"Even when it is heavy."

"I will."

"There is one of yours coming who will need you to be the kind one. Be the kind one, Spireborn. Even when the others choose the hammer. Be the kind one."

"I will."

She turned and looked out at the sea.

"There is a great deal of dark in front of you," she said, almost to herself. "I am sorry."

"I am not," Caleth said, surprising himself.

She glanced back. The smile widened a fraction.

"No," she said. "You wouldn't be."

The sea brightened. The cliff faded. The smile was the last thing to go.

He woke to the smell of cave-fish frying, and the sound of Korroc complaining about cave-fish, and Thane laughing for the first time in two days, and Anevia's voice answering from somewhere near, sharp and warm at once.

Caleth sat up and stretched and felt the silver scale, warm against his hip beneath his cloak. He laid his hand on it for a second.

Then he picked up his glaive, and his journal, and his quiet held heart, and went to break his fast with his companions before the long climb began.

Above them, somewhere very far above, a city he had not yet seen the truth of was waiting.

And down here, in a stone room in a mongrel village in the dark, four strangers who had become something else were finishing their bread and standing up to walk.
