# The Sound the Thunder Makes

*Chapter Thirteen — What Breaks the Stillness*

---

Nageru had sat through many arguments in his life, and he had learned to hear them the way his masters heard weather.

This one had been building since dusk. The command tent held it poorly — canvas holds heat and voices both, and gives neither back — and the argument went around the map-table in slow circles, gathering, the way a storm gathers over dry hills. Bridge first, or cemetery first. Move the army west to the Ahari crossing and plant it there, or clean out the dark place first and move after. Korroc and Caleth stood on the one side of it. Thane stood on the other. And Nageru stood where he usually stood, which was slightly apart, listening to the shapes under the words.

"You put the army at that bridge and you've *done* something," Korroc was saying, blunt hands flat on the map. "Ground taken is ground held. We sit here another day and we're a foothold that forgot what feet are for."

"The crossing is the whole campaign." Caleth's voice, measured as ever, laying his reasons down like cards. "Every day the army is not at the Ahari is a day the citadel spends preparing for us. We move, we set the camp, we let the engineers look at the span. Then the cemetery, from strength."

"And when the citadel sees two hundred soldiers walk west on the other side of their riverbed?" Thane, dry and flat, arms folded. "You think they'll wait politely while we make camp? You move the army before we're ready and you *invite* the thing we're not ready for. The cemetery is now. It's quiet, it's close, and it's at our backs. You clear the dark behind you before you walk toward the dark in front. Any door-guard knows it."

Nageru listened, and his own unease sat in him with a shape he could name. It was not the bridge. It was not even the citadel.

"The south is not finished," he said, when a gap opened for him. He said it quietly, and the tent quieted to hear it, which was a thing he had never asked for and had stopped remarking on. "We hold a hill and a bridge. Everything else on this side of the riverbed is unread. If we walk away from it, we leave it at our backs — and the things at a man's back do not stay still because he is busy." He paused. "I am with Thane. The cemetery first."

The argument went around twice more anyway. Arguments do. Irabeth let it run the way a good commander lets a fire burn down to where the coals show, and then she put her hand flat on the table.

"Enough. We vote."

Nageru watched the vote travel the table, and watched the arithmetic of it arrive at Korroc last of all, and watched the dwarf see the arithmetic too. Thane: cemetery. Nageru: cemetery. Caleth: bridge. Two and one, and Korroc standing there with his vote in his hand like a stone he did not want to throw. If he said *bridge* — the thing he believed, the thing he had argued for half the evening — the table locked, two against two, and the night would be spent arguing instead of sleeping.

Korroc stood and frowned into his beard. The frown went on a long time.

Then the dwarf let a breath go through his beard, long and grudging, the sound of a man setting down a load on the wrong side of a door because carrying it further would break something better than his back.

"Cemetery," Korroc said. "I won't lock us up."

Nageru inclined his head, a fraction, where no one was looking. He had watched men win arguments all his life. It was rarer, and worth more, to watch a man lose one on purpose.

It should have ended there. It did not. Aron Kir came forward over the map with his flat spent voice and began laying out problems — approaches, sightlines, what a burial ground that ate its visitors might do to a party of four, whether it should wait for daylight — and the problems were real, and the talk began to circle again, and Nageru felt the tent fill back up with weather.

Thane shook his head once, turned, and walked out.

No one else marked it much. Thane came and went; it was his nature and his work. But Nageru had spent two years learning the difference between a man leaving a tent and a man *leaving*, and this was the second thing. The set of the shoulders. The particular economy in the step. A man who had stopped waiting for the talk to finish because he had already decided what the night was for.

Nageru gave it the space of five breaths. Then he slipped out after him.

---

The dark outside the camp was cold and moonless, and Thane was already gone in it.

Gone to most eyes. Nageru stood at the picket line and let his own eyes soften the way his order taught — *do not hunt the shape, let the shape come to you* — and found what he knew to look for: an absence moving, a place where the night was arranged slightly too well. Westward. Toward the riverbed. Toward, if a man drew the line long enough, the cemetery.

*Alone. Of course alone.*

Nageru followed.

He did not announce himself, and he did not hide, exactly; he simply went quietly, well back, matching his breathing to his feet the old way, and he was perhaps forty paces behind when the absence ahead of him stopped, and resolved into a dwarf crouched at the lip of the dry riverbed, and one hand came up, palm back. *Hold.*

So Thane had known he was there. Probably the whole way. Nageru came up beside him without a word and crouched, and did not defend the following, and Thane did not ask him to. They were past that, the two of them. A watcher understands being watched.

Below them, west along the riverbed's dark throat, was the Ahari Bridge. And under the bridge, something was breathing.

Nageru heard it before he saw it — a low wet enormous breathing, many lungs, slow. Then the shapes came clear against the paler stone. Oxen. His mind offered the word and then withdrew it, ashamed, because they had the shape of oxen the way the vrock had had the shape of a bird. They were huge — shoulder-high to the bridge's stone piers, four of them, and wrong in every line, horns spiraling out too far, hide moving where hide should not move, a heat shimmer over them in the cold night. Each one was chained. Great black links, thick as a man's wrist, ran from iron collars to the pillars of the bridge itself.

Not tethered to posts. Tethered to the *bridge*. To the piers that held the span up.

Nageru read it, and the reading arrived all at once, cold and complete, the way a strike arrives. He slid closer to Thane's ear.

"If those things get the mind to run," he breathed, "they'll pull down that bridge with them."

Thane nodded, slow, his eyes never leaving the dark below. "And the army has to go down into the riverbed and come up the other side to approach the citadel." A pause, flat and grim. "It would be like another wall."

That was the whole of it. The enemy had not fortified the Ahari crossing. They had made it a hostage. Four living engines chained to the piers, and somewhere a hand that could spook them at the hour of its choosing, and the bridge would come down into the riverbed and the Crusade's road west would become a cliff. Nageru looked at the great breathing shapes and felt a thing he did not often permit himself, which was anger — not at the beasts, who had been made into this, but at the mind that had done the making. Somewhere, something had looked at a living creature and seen a demolition charge.

*Order is owed to the innocent,* the old cord in him said. *Even the innocent with horns.*

Thane pointed once more, across the span. Shapes on the far bank, and on the bridge deck above — tiefling guards, a lazy scatter of them, the watch of soldiers who believe their work is being done by something bigger than themselves.

Nageru nodded. They had seen what there was to see. The two of them slid back from the lip and went home through the dark, carrying the bridge's secret between them, and neither of them said the obvious thing aloud: that the vote had been righter than anyone knew. The army could not go west tomorrow. Not until the beasts were dealt with. And the beasts could wait one more night, because the cemetery could not.

---

Caleth and Korroc were readying their gear by lamplight when the two of them walked back into camp, and Caleth looked up, and read them, and set down the strap he was buckling.

"You went and looked at something," the half-elf said. "Tell me."

They told him. Korroc's face darkened through it, and at the word *chained* his hand found his hammer's haft without his seeming to notice. Caleth listened the way Caleth listened, gaze fixed on a middle distance where Nageru suspected the map of the city hung permanently drawn.

"Beasts first light," Caleth said at last. "After the cemetery. Agreed?"

"Agreed," Thane said. "Cemetery now, while the dark's ours."

*The dark is not ours,* Nageru thought, but did not say, because it was not a useful thought, and because they went anyway. That was the work. You went anyway.

The cemetery announced itself before they reached it.

It began as a wrongness in the air, faint as the smell of rain. Nageru felt it the way he had felt the broken thresholds of Kenabres — a pressure against the blood, his ancestors leaning forward, attention arriving from somewhere high and orderly and old. By the time the ground opened out into rows of leaning stones, the pressure had teeth in it. This was not the flat hollow of an abandoned place. This was a place *occupied* — occupied the way a wound is occupied by infection.

The graves themselves were old and mostly seemly. Crusader graves — he could read the shapes of the markers, the sword-and-sunburst worn shallow by seventy-five years of wind — the dead of the first city, laid down in hope, before the hope fell. Rank upon rank of them in the dark. The dead did not trouble Nageru. The dead were the most orderly of all things, when they were left to their order.

These had not been left.

"Look," Korroc said.

The dwarf had stopped, and his voice had gone low and flat, and his hand was up, pointing across the leaning rows to the heart of the ground, where the largest structure stood — a mausoleum, stone-built, broad-shouldered, the tomb of someone the first city had loved. Even in the dark Nageru could see what Korroc was pointing at.

The shadows on it were moving.

Not shadows cast by anything. Shadows *resident* — a slow rippling dark that crawled across the stone facing and around the corners of the building like oil moving on water, thickening and thinning, never still. Nageru watched a band of it pour itself around a carved pillar and re-emerge on the other side slightly wrong, and the pressure against his blood sharpened until it sang.

"I can feel that from here," Korroc said.

"As can I," Nageru said quietly.

They moved closer, careful among the stones, and the feeling worsened with every rank of graves they passed — and Nageru marked, with the strange doubled awareness the oath gave him, that it was worst in the three of them who had knelt and sworn. Korroc's jaw had set to iron. Caleth had gone pale around the mouth in the way he did when something offended more than his senses. Three paladins of three different vows, and one desecrated building singing the same wrong note into all three.

Caleth stopped at the edge of the open ground before the doors, and his eyes went over the carved facing, the worn iconography, the sword and the sunburst and the rayed crown above the lintel.

"Iomedae markings on it," the half-elf said. "But without a doubt, it's been desecrated."

*Desecrated.* The word was smaller than the thing. Nageru looked at the shadows crawling on a tomb built to honor the goddess of the crusade, in a burial ground full of her own dead, and felt the full shape of the offense the way his blood insisted he feel it: a threshold violated. A door that had been holy, made into a door for something else. Somewhere behind that stone, order had been inverted — not broken, which is mere ruin, but *turned*, made to run backward, sanctity spent as fuel for its opposite.

This was why no one who entered came out. He was suddenly sure of it, sure in the blood. The cemetery was not dangerous the way a lair is dangerous.

It was dangerous the way an altar is.

---

The doors were stone, tall as two men, and they were locked.

Thane went up the steps to them like a tradesman arriving at a job, and knelt, and studied the lock in the dark for a moment with his fingers rather than his eyes. Whatever he found amused him, in the dry interior way that passed for Thane's amusement.

"Big doors, common lock," he muttered, and had his picks out, and Nageru stood watch on the steps below and listened to the small patient metal sounds behind him, and to the large patient silence beyond the doors, and of the two of them it was the silence he watched. It was not empty. Nothing about this building was empty.

*Click.*

The door swung open on dark.

They went in with Caleth's conjured light held low, and the first room took shape around them — a broad antechamber, stone-floored, colder inside than the night had been outside, which stone should not be. Dust lay thick and wrong, disturbed in paths Nageru's eye could not immediately read. A second door stood in the far wall, larger and heavier than the first, shut. And against the base of a pillar near the middle of the room, slumped like a tired porter, sat a dead man.

Very old, by the clothes — a style Nageru had seen only in paintings, a long coat of another century's cut gone colorless with age. The body had settled into itself the way the long-dead settle.

Thane moved past it without a glance, drawn to the far door, already reading its hinges. Caleth did not move past it. Caleth stopped, and tilted his head, and crouched down before the seated dead man with the particular frown he wore for a detail that would not resolve.

"Him," the half-elf murmured, mostly to himself. "A little fleshy, actually. First Crusade...?"

Nageru was watching from the second door, half his attention on the room's corners, when the dead man's hand came up and took a fist of Caleth's tabard.

The room went from stillness to motion in a single beat. Nageru crossed it — he was moving before he had chosen to move, the training moving him — and brought both hands down at the thing's grip, a breaking blow, and the thing was no longer where the blow went. It had surged upward against Caleth, all its dead weight suddenly quick, and Nageru's fists met stone floor with a crack that jarred him to the shoulders.

He had missed. He noted it the way his masters had taught him to note it — without heat, a fact for later — and was already resetting.

Caleth did not need the rescue. The half-elf had gone backward half a step with the thing hanging from his tabard, and his free hand came around and pressed flat to the gray skull — almost gentle, an anointing — and he spoke a word, and lightning went through the creature at the range of a caress. The dead man lit from inside. Every limb snapped rigid, the grip tore free, and it dropped to the stone and lay smoking and did not move again. Ghoul, Nageru's mind filed, catching the details at last through the after-image — the too-long tongue, the hooked nails. The fleshiness that had troubled Caleth's eye. Not a dead man of the First Crusade. A thing wearing one.

"Behind," Korroc grunted.

The dwarf kicked backward without turning, boot meeting something with a wet crunch, and stepped smartly off the spot — and Nageru saw the second one, crabbing out of the corner shadows where the dust-paths led, one clawed hand still reaching for the ankle it had missed. And a third, rising from behind the pillar on Nageru's own side, unfolding joint by joint, its tongue already out.

There was no artistry in what followed, and none was owed.

Korroc's hammer came around and down onto the crawling one, a short flat stroke, and the skull did not so much shatter as *disperse* — a burst of grimy dust and old bone across the floor stones. The third came at Nageru with its ragged mouth open, and Nageru let it arrive, turned its reaching arms past him with one forearm, and put his fist through its skull the way the forms prescribed: not at the target, *through* it. The thing came apart at the blow and rained down around his stance, and the stillness of the room came back down like settling dust.

They waited, all four, in the ready quiet. Nothing else rose.

"Ghouls in the anteroom," Caleth said, brushing at the char-mark on his tabard with distaste. "Guarding, or just denning. Either way." He looked at the inner door. "Deeper."

---

Thane worked the second lock, and this one he was slower about — not for its cleverness, but because he checked twice for the trap that ought to have been there and was not.

"Locked but honest," he reported, sounding almost disappointed in it, and — *click* — pushed the great door open.

Light met them.

Nageru had not expected light. It was thin and gray, the first weak filtering of dawn from somewhere above — vents, or a broken course of stones, some architecture of openings that let the sky reach in — and it fell across a wide chamber that had once been the mausoleum's heart. Sepulchers along the walls. An open floor for standing mourners. Carvings gone soft with age, and under everything, everywhere, that same wrong pressure — thicker here, close and sour, the source of the whole ground's sickness somewhere near.

Caleth put his head through the doorway and swept it once, wary. Nageru simply walked past him and in.

It was not recklessness. It was the plainer arithmetic that someone had to be first through, and of the four of them, he was the one built to survive being wrong about a room. He stood in the gray light and let the chamber tell him what it held.

It held a sound.

"I hear something," he said.

Small. Rhythmic. Off to his right, past a screen of carved stone where the chamber turned a corner into some annex or side-chapel — a wet, industrious, scraping rhythm, like a dog at a bone, if a dog were larger, and patient, and the bone were not a bone. He turned toward it. He squinted against the half-light and took three quiet steps, and the annex opened before him around its corner, and he looked at what was in it.

It was green-skinned and hunchbacked, crouched over something dark on the floor, and its wings were folded along its spine like a beetle's. Long ears tapered to points off the sides of a narrow skull. A tongue hung longer than a tongue should hang. One clawed hand was buried to the knuckles in whatever it had been digging into, working, and the sound Nageru had heard was that claw, going about its business.

It felt him there and turned.

For half a breath they looked at each other, the monk and the horror, and Nageru had time to mark the eyes — hungry and aware, nothing animal in them — and then the thing opened its jaw wider than its own skull and *screamed* at him, and came.

It was fast. Faster than the vrocks had been, faster than anything its crooked shape promised. It crossed the annex in a beat, claws leading, and Nageru's forearms came up on trained instinct and swept the claws aside — both of them, clean — and then the teeth were in his shoulder.

The bite went deep. He felt it grind on the bone, felt his own blood run hot down his chest under the wraps, and the pain arrived and he did with it what he did with all pain, which was to give it a place and refuse it the rest. His shoulder. Not his stance. Not his breath.

Thane was already there. He had come at the scream — quick as thrown water — and a dagger flashed in low at the thing's ribs, and the creature dropped off Nageru's shoulder and flowed sideways, and the blade cut air. Caleth loomed behind Thane and Radiance came over the dwarf's shoulder in a straight-armed thrust, white fire down its length, and the creature was not there either. Korroc had circled to Nageru's far side, hammer up, and brought it down in a stroke that should have staved the hunched spine, and the thing spun out from under the arc with an acrobat's contempt.

*It reads us,* Nageru thought, cold and clear, watching it move. *It has fought men before. Many times.*

It came back at him. Him again — it had chosen him, the way predators choose, marking the one it had already bled — and this time the claws beat his guard, one and then the other, twin lines of fire raked across his chest, and his blood spattered the old stone of Iomedae's dead.

He answered before the creature finished the stroke.

That was the gift the Sunken Fist had ground into him across twenty years: the answer that lives inside the block, the counter that needs no gathering. His fists went out from the center, one-two, short and total, and both landed — the first into the creature's ribs, the second up under the hinge of that too-wide jaw — and the horror rocked backward with a sound like a kettle taken off a fire.

Thane and Caleth came in on the opening. The creature, hurt now and knowing it, dodged them both — but the dodging cost it, Nageru saw; it was spending speed it no longer had to spare. Korroc reached down — Nageru felt it more than saw it, the dwarf's boots settling as though the floor had grown up around them, the borrowed patience of bedrock flowing up into his arms — and the hammer came around in a flat forge-stroke and *connected*, and the creature's whole frame shuddered under the weight of a hill. It writhed away from the second swing, which found only air.

And then it made its mistake.

Nageru saw the decision travel through the creature — saw it count four enemies with angles on it, saw it choose to break and reposition, saw it turn to give ground — and the turning turned its back to three of them at once. There are movements that offend the whole logic of a fight. His masters had a word for them, and the word meant *door*.

Three went through the door together. Nageru's fist, Korroc's hammer, the bright length of Radiance — no signal, no word, three strikes arriving in a single beat from three sides, and the green horror came apart in the middle of its retreat and hit the floor in ruin, and this time the stillness that came down held.

They stood over it, breathing.

"Berbalang?" Caleth said at last, looking down at it with his head tilted, a scholar cross-referencing.

Korroc came up beside him and looked, and nodded slowly. "First I've ever seen. But I've heard tales of them."

"The same," Caleth said.

Nageru pressed a palm to his torn shoulder and said nothing. Behind them, in the annex, lay the dark thing the creature had been digging at, and he looked at it long enough to understand it, and then chose, deliberately, to file it and not to dwell. Some of the missing of this cemetery had been found. That was as much of the thought as it was useful to finish.

---

The sickness of the place had a center, and they were standing in it.

Nageru felt it the moment the fight's noise drained away — the main chamber was the wound. The wrongness did not seep into this room; it seeped *out* of it, out through the walls and the soil and the leaning stones, the whole cemetery poisoned from this one turned altar of a room. Korroc walked a slow circle to the chamber's heart with his palm out flat, like a man feeling for heat above a stove, and stopped, and shook his head with his beard dragging his chest.

"I don't have the power to undo this," the dwarf said. The admission cost him; Nageru heard the grind in it. He turned to Caleth. 

Caleth frowned. It was not reluctance — Nageru had learned the half-elf's frowns, and this one was the calculating kind, a man checking his own reach against a load. "I can try," he said. "A simple dispel might do it. If what's holding it is a working and not a... resident." He did not finish the alternative. He stepped to the center, drew one breath, and began.

The incantation was quiet. That was the thing Nageru would keep. No thunder in it, no blaze — just a measured voice speaking the syllables of unmaking with a wizard's precision and a paladin's intent, one gesture folding into the next. Nageru stood in the gray light with his blood still pressed flat by the room's sour weight, and watched his friend work, and felt the exact moment the working found its grip.

The darkness did not flee. It *dissipated* — the truer word — like smoke when the door is finally opened, like a held note releasing. The pressure against Nageru's blood thinned, and thinned, and lifted, and the attention of his ancestors, which had been leaning hard against the inside of his chest since the first leaning gravestone, eased back into its usual high remove. The room became only a room. Old stone, gray light, the honored dead. Cold, still — but clean cold now, the ordinary cold of places built to hold the sleeping.

Korroc let out a breath and nodded to Caleth. "Aye," he said, and the word carried everything the dwarf was not going to say in longer form.

Caleth nodded back. Then his eyes went around the chamber — the scattered bone-dust, the berbalang's ruin, the fouled sepulcher lids, the disorder that the desecration had made and the fight had worsened — and something in his face set.

"I think we should clean up a little," he said.

"Aye," Korroc said again, in an entirely different tone, and took off his shield.

So they cleaned. Knights of the Fifth Crusade, in a tomb an hour past dawn, doing servants' work with their own hands — righting what could be righted, gathering what the horror had scattered, laying the disturbed dead back into their alcoves with the shapes of reverence four different faiths agreed on. Three of them knelt to it. Thane did not kneel. Thane took the door — set his shoulder to the jamb where he could hold the chamber, the anteroom, and the gray morning beyond it all in one unhurried gaze, and kept the watch while the paladins worked. Nageru marked it and understood it was not abstention. The dwarf's quiet god was a keeper of thresholds; a door well watched over the honored dead was his verse of the same prayer, offered the only way Thane offered anything — standing up, facing out, saying nothing. Nageru carried and knelt and set things straight, and did it as his order had taught him to do all such work, as a prayer in motion — *this is also the fight; this has always also been the fight* — and marked, without comment, that no one had suggested it but Caleth, and no one had questioned it at all.

Restoration, not rage. It was the oldest lesson he owned, and he watched a wizard-paladin of another god entirely enact it with a straw broom found behind a sepulcher, and was, for a quarter of an hour, wordlessly and completely content.

When they came out of the mausoleum the sun was fully up, and the cemetery lay silent around them.

It was a changed silence. They walked the grounds once, the four of them spreading out among the stones, looking for whatever else the place might hold — and there was nothing. No movement, no wrongness, no pressure against the blood. The leaning stones leaned in the ordinary way of old grief. The dead of the First Crusade slept the way the dead should sleep, in ground that was only ground again.

*Everything was suddenly very quiet,* Nageru thought, and then thought: *No. Everything was finally very quiet.* The difference was the morning's whole work, and it was worth what it had cost.

---

They gathered at the cemetery's north edge, and Caleth looked down the fall of the land toward the dry riverbed, and said what they were all thinking.

"We'll move north from here — down to the riverbed, and try to come at the beasts unseen." His eyes went along the line of the gorge, toward where the Ahari span stood against the morning. "Before the day gets older and the handlers get lucky with those chains."

They all nodded. No one proposed going back for the army first, and Nageru understood why without it being said: a hundred soldiers cannot sneak, and the beasts were a thing that had to be done quietly or not at all. Four could do it. Four would.

Getting down into the riverbed was simple enough — the bank fell away in broken shelves, and they went down it one at a time and gathered in the channel's shadow. But as they worked east along it and the bridge rose ahead, Thane's hand came up, and they eased to the cover of a great fallen slab and looked, and the picture had grown worse since the night.

There were tieflings *under* the bridge. Among the beasts. Nageru counted them at the pillars — red-skinned figures moving between the vast chained shapes with the particular ease of familiarity, checking chains, hauling at feed troughs, one of them slapping a beast's flank as it passed and stepping away from the answering toss of horns with a laugh Nageru could not hear and did not need to.

"Handlers," he said, low.

Which changed the arithmetic entirely, and everyone behind the slab knew it. Beasts alone might be freed, or calmed, or at the very worst simply killed at whatever pace the work required. Handlers meant the enemy could *spend* the beasts — one shout, one goad, four maddened engines pulling four ways against the piers, and the bridge would come down while the Crusade watched from a quarter mile away. The beasts had to die or be loosed before any handler got the order out. Quietly. Quickly. Now.

Caleth motioned them close, close enough that his breath barely reached them.

"Thane first," the half-elf whispered, and touched the dwarf's shoulder and spoke a word, and Thane was not there anymore. The absence that remained shifted; a pebble clicked softly, moving east.

Then Korroc's armor creaked.

It was a small sound. In any fight it would have been nothing. In the held silence of the riverbed, in the shadow of the slab, with the nearest handler forty paces off, the creak of dwarven plate settling was a *bell*. Nageru saw two of the tiefling heads come up and around, saw a hand drift toward a horn at a belt, saw the whole delicate arithmetic of surprise begin to tip —

Caleth was already casting. Not the invisibility — no time to hide four — the other thing, the folding thing, his hands carving it fast and sure, and Nageru understood the shape of the gambit in the half-beat before it resolved: *let them look here. We will be there.* The handlers were turning toward a noise in the west shadows.

The world folded, and the two dwarves and Caleth were gone.

Nageru did not wait to watch the handlers stare at the empty slab. He was already running.

The spell had not included him — a narrow spell, three and no more, and he was the one of them who did not need it. He came around the slab at a dead sprint, bare feet silent on the dry stone, the channel unrolling under him, and this was its own discipline and its own joy, the body doing wholly what the body had been trained for — forty paces gone before the nearest handler's head had finished turning the wrong way. Caleth and the dwarves reappeared beyond the beasts, on the handlers' blind side. The tieflings were still looking west at nothing.

Nageru arrived.

The first handler never saw the fist. It took him under the ear as Nageru passed — passed, not stopped, the strike flowing out of the stride the way water leaves a wheel — and the tiefling's blood hit the stones a moment before the tiefling did. The nearest beast started at the smell and the fall, its great head slewing around, and it led at Nageru with its horns, a ton of fused hate moving quicker than anything that size had a right to move. He read the line of it and was not in the line. The horns went past with a wind like a door slamming.

Across the beast's flank he saw the blood-spatter of Thane's work appear out of empty air — one dagger-flash of red, then another — and a second handler folded down onto the stones with no visible cause. Two down. The camp under the bridge was waking to catastrophe in the wrong order, handlers dying before sentries had found anything to shout at.

Then the bridge deck above them woke too.

A tiefling leaned over the parapet with a crossbow, took one look at the bronze figure standing over a dead handler, and fired. Nageru heard the string more than saw it, and the bolt came down at him with that flat vicious speed that was almost — *almost* — too fast.

The Sunken Fist spent years on this. Seasons of a boy standing in a courtyard while masters threw first beans, then stones, then blunted arrows, and the lesson was never *be faster than the arrow*; no one is faster than the arrow. The lesson was *be earlier*. Read the string, the stance, the eye. Begin before the shot begins.

His hand went up and the bolt was in it.

The smack of it against his palm was total, stinging his whole arm to the elbow, and the shaft's whine died in his fist, and for one long half-breath the crossbowman on the parapet stared down at him — at the bolt, caught, whole, in the hand of the thing he had shot — with an expression Nageru had seen before on men discovering that the world held more law than they had planned for.

Nageru threw it back.

He put his hips into it, the whole uncoiling line from heel to fingertip, and the bolt went back up quicker than it had come down. The crossbowman was still staring when it took him. He sat down against the parapet with his own bolt in him, out of the fight, and Nageru was already turning to the next thing, because the next thing was always arriving, and wonder was a luxury for men on walls.

The next thing was Korroc, and the first chain.

The dwarf had not gone for the handlers at all. He had gone for the *problem* — straight to the nearest pillar, straight to the great black links bolted to it, and his adamantine hammer came around and up and struck the chain where it stood taut, and the chain, forged to hold a maddened giant, met the one metal in the world that does not negotiate. It shattered. Links sprang apart in a bright ringing spray, and the nearest beast staggered sideways, suddenly answering to nothing, free.

*One,* Nageru counted. *Three chains to go.* And the whole bridge above them boiling awake.

---

The bolts came down like the first fat drops of a storm.

One struck short of Nageru and skipped away down the channel. The second he did not read in time — it came from behind, from the far parapet, and buried itself in his back below the shoulder blade, a deep bright bloom of pain that staggered him half a step. He gave it its place. *The shoulder blade. Not the stance. Not the breath.* Another bolt spat off the stones at Caleth's feet, and across the channel Korroc's shield came up and took a fourth out of the air with a clang, the dwarf swatting it down the way a man swats a wasp.

And Nageru felt, distantly, along the thread he had carried since the bridge fight two days gone, a familiar easing — a portion of the bolt's fire drawn quietly out of his back and away, into the chest of a dwarf forty feet off who had promised to take half of everything and had never once been known to break a promise. *Brother,* Nageru thought, with the pain-lightened clarity the link always brought, *one day I will find the way to carry a share of yours.*

Caleth moved. The half-elf had been working the western shadow, and now he slid under the span itself, through the forest of piers, until he owned an angle on the far-side handlers that the bridge deck could not see or stop. He raised one hand. Fire gathered along it — and inverted, the way it did in no other hands in the world, heat curdling into its opposite — and two rays of killing cold lanced out from his fingers across the channel. The first handler they found simply toppled backward, frost across his chest, dead with so little ceremony that his fellows did not immediately understand they had lost him.

The beasts, loose and chained alike, had gone mad by now.

One of the still-chained ones lunged the full length of its links at Korroc, jaws first — an ox does not bite; whatever lived in this one had other opinions — and the dwarf got his shield into its mouth, and the sprung horns came around the shield's edge and caught him along the side. Nageru heard the grunt. He was already at the flank of the nearest beast himself, and he put his fist into it and learned the second fact of the day's fighting: the hide was armor. His knuckles met something like layered horn under the bristled skin, and the blow skidded, wasted.

He adjusted. That was the whole of his training in one word — *adjust* — no frustration, no force; the target was wrong, so the target changed. Second knuckles, driving angle, the seam behind the shoulder where the armor of the hide creased to let the leg move. His next two strikes went in hard, and the beast felt them both, bellowing, and its horns came around in a scythe that he stepped back from, out of the arc, unhurried.

Thane materialized in his effect if not his person — the beast worrying at Korroc suddenly grew three wounds in its neck, one-two-three, dagger-work from nowhere — and the great thing shuddered and went down like a dropped wall, and Korroc, freed, was swinging before its knees finished buckling. Not at a beast. At the next *chain*. The hammer rang, the links burst, and the second beast stood suddenly loose, tossing its horns at nothing, unclaimed by any order.

*Two,* Nageru counted. Bolts spanged off Korroc's plate — one, two, a loud ringing clang and a whine away into the channel — and a third found Caleth under the span and stuck in him, and Nageru marked the half-elf's hiss without being able to reach it.

Then the world went white and hot.

The fireball landed among them without warning — a bloom of orange fire filling the underspan, stone-shadow and beast and man all lit at once, the concussion slapping down the channel. Nageru took his share of it across the back and shoulders, wraps scorching, and came out of the roll it taught him with his ears ringing. Through the after-glare he saw Korroc — beard singed, a fresh bolt sunk into his shoulder in the fire's wake — patting at the smoldering braids of his beard with an expression less of pain than of profound personal offense, muttering something in Dwarven that Nageru elected not to translate.

Caleth looked up. Nageru watched the half-elf's eyes trace the fireball's path backward, up over the parapet, to its source — and find it. A tiefling stood above on the bridge deck, hunched and small in robes cut for a larger frame, hands still smoking with the cast. A caster. The bridge had a caster, and the caster had the high ground and the range, and there was nothing under the span that could reach him.

Nothing except his own fire, returned with interest.

Caleth's answer gathered in his palm — the same spell, the *very same*, Nageru understood with something adjacent to awe, the man taking the enemy's own working and folding it inside out as he built it — and the ball that leapt from his hand and shot upward over the parapet was white, not orange. It burst on the bridge deck in a soundless flower of ice. The cold rolled out across the stone, and the hunchbacked caster froze mid-gesture — frost-locked, rimed, done — and three of the crossbowmen near him were caught in the same white bloom and dropped where they stood.

One crossbowman remained upright on that stretch of deck, staggering, wiping frost from his eyes.

And then Caleth was standing beside him. The world had folded; the half-elf had stepped from the underspan to the deck in the space of a blink, and Nageru — who could not see over the parapet, who only saw the top half of what followed — saw his friend appear at the tiefling's shoulder wearing a look he had rarely seen on that composed face. Grim. Angry. The paladin's anger, the kind that keeps accounts. The crossbowman had time to turn.

Nageru returned his attention below, where it was needed. What happened on the deck took only a moment, and Radiance came back over the parapet's edge red.

---

Below, the fight had narrowed to beasts and chains, and the beasts were losing patience with the concept of masters altogether.

One of them bellowed — a sound with two voices in it, the ox and the thing fused through the ox, neither happy — and came at Nageru with everything, and this time the horns found him. The gore took him in the side and threw him, a white shock of it, ribs bending in a way ribs should not bend, blood hot down his hip, and half of it — half, exactly, faithfully — flowing away along the thread to a dwarf who was even now shattering a third chain with his hammer's downstroke. Nageru landed, rolled, rose. The beast was already on him again.

He answered it from the rising. Fist to the skull-boss, the whole body behind it, and the beast's charge died in its front legs; it wavered, knelt, and teetered over sideways with a crash that shook the channel.

He stood over it, breathing carefully around the wrongness in his ribs, and felt warmth arrive.

It came the way Korroc's healing always came, gold and sure — but from *distance*, which was new. He looked and found the dwarf fifteen feet off, one hand still on his hammer, the other flung out toward Nageru open-palmed, and the healing crossed the gap between them like a thing thrown — a smith tossing a tool to a workmate, easy and exact — and landed in Nageru's body as though the hand itself had been laid on him. The bent ribs eased. The gore-wound in his side drew closed. Since the wardstone's golden fire, all four of them had carried things they had no names for; this was one of Korroc's, and Nageru accepted it as he accepted all grace, with a slight bow across the fifteen feet, which the dwarf, already moving, entirely missed.

Korroc was going *under* the bridge, toward the far side. And past him, Nageru saw why, and the why redoubled him.

The last two beasts — one loose, one still chained to the final pier — had stopped fighting anything mortal and begun, in their two-voiced madness, to simply *pull*. Away from the noise, away from the pain, wherever their fused spirits drove them — and the chained one's links stood taut as a harp string off the last pillar, and the pillar was answering. Nageru heard it. A grinding, deep in the stonework. A sifting of mortar-dust, sparkling down through the morning light under the span.

The bridge. The whole day's reason. It was beginning, by inches, to lose.

Nageru went past Korroc like wind through a doorway — the dwarf was moving with all a dwarf's planted purpose, but purpose is not pace — and reached the loose beast as it wheeled to add its weight to some new panic, and struck it, hard, at the seam behind the shoulder, pulling its madness onto himself, which was where madness could be managed. Thane's work bloomed red along the chained one's flank, the unseen daggers finding the softness under the armor plates of hide. The beast screamed at wounds it could not see the maker of.

Korroc pressed on for the last pillar, where the final chain stood singing.

The loose beast wheeled on Nageru as he harried it — one last two-voiced lunge, horns first — and he was not where the lunge arrived. He swayed off its line, and his fist met the great head as it went past, the whole body behind the blow, and the beast's charge carried it three more strides on momentum alone before the momentum understood that the life had left it. It came down in a sliding ruin of dust and did not rise. It had spent half of itself already against chains and panic and his first strike at the shoulder-seam; the blow only finished what the morning had started. Nageru turned to the arithmetic that remained: one beast alive, one chain unbroken, one pillar still answering the strain.

A handler — the last of them on this side, who had spent the fight discovering that his beasts were dying to an enemy he could not count — found the blood-marks of Thane's passage and made the desperate deduction, and went to the empty air where the wounds kept happening and swung his short sword through it in great wild arcs. The air declined to be found. And then Caleth stood beside him — the fold again, deck to channel, unannounced — and Radiance went through the handler with the economy of a full stop at the end of a sentence. The tiefling buckled and was done.

The final beast hauled at the last pillar with all its maddened weight, the links singing, the stonework grinding out its slow complaint overhead. Korroc — who had waited out the worst of the thrashing beside the pillar with the patience of the stone he was named for — measured the chain, and swung once.

*Four,* Nageru counted, at the burst of links, *and done.* The ringing faded down the channel, and the beast, answering to nothing at last, staggered sideways under its own unspent fury, wheeled, and blundered away from the pillar — free, riderless, alive. Nageru let it go, and no one raised a hand to stop it. It had been a victim before it was ever a weapon; if it fled this place and its two voices with it, the day asked nothing further of it. The work had never been the beasts. The work was the bridge.

The bridge stood. Above them, the piers ticked and sifted and held.

Nageru let his breath go all the way down for the first time since the riverbed. Three beasts dead, and the last one loose and wandering the channel like a bad dream with nowhere left to be dreamed. The handlers dead. The deck swept. The chains — every chain — broken off the piers, no living engine left tethered to the Crusade's road west.

It was at exactly that moment, with the arithmetic finally clean, that Nageru saw Caleth go still and look north — past the bridge, over the ruins, toward the broken silhouette the maps called a cathedral — and Nageru turned to follow the look, and so did Korroc, and so, presumably, did the unseen Thane, because what was coming was for all of them.

It came down out of the north sky and landed in the channel a stone's throw away, and the ground took its weight badly.

It was huge. That was the first fact, and the mind could not immediately get past it — a beast the size of a laden wagon, wings folding along its back with a sound like sails. Then the mind reached the second fact and stopped altogether.

It had three heads.

A snow leopard's, pale and black-rosetted, lips already back off the fangs. A mountain goat's, heavy-horned, its slotted eyes utterly wrong in a face that large. And between them, riding the longest neck, a dragon's head, white-scaled, frost smoking off its jaw in the morning air.

The dragon head rose, and roared, and the other two heads opened with it — and all three breathed as one.

---

The world turned white.

It was not fire's opposite; it was worse than that, a *cutting* cold, a storm-blast of wind packed with ice like flung knives, and it filled the channel wall to wall and took all four of them at once. Nageru got his forearms crossed before his face and felt the ice shear along them, felt the cold reach through the wraps and the skin and lay its hand directly on the bone. His blood — poison ran slow in it, heat and cold broke on it gentler than on other men — even his blood *rang* with it, a struck-bell shock from crown to heel.

He came out of the blast upright. He heard the last beast go down somewhere behind him — the freed one, the one he had let go, caught in its own side's breath and killed by the thing that had come to command it; there was a lesson in that about the Abyss, and no time to read it.

And he heard Korroc.

The sound was small. That was what made it terrible. A dwarven grunt, bitten down to almost nothing — and Nageru turned and saw the wall of their company halfway to its knees. Ice sheathed the dented plate. Frost whitened the singed beard. Korroc was a child of the forge; the fire in him ran so hot that cold found him the way water finds a crack — Nageru had known it as a fact the way one knows a comrade's facts, and was seeing it now as a truth, the strongest of the four of them bent over his hammer's haft, gritting through a cold that had bitten him twice as deep as any of them, forcing his own knee, by main will, not to touch the stone.

Caleth was already casting. Nageru heard the incantation come quick and clipped behind him and knew the working as it flew — a ward against cold, cast wide, laid over every friend the half-elf could see: himself, the dwarf, Nageru. Nageru felt it settle over his own shoulders like a cloak, a dry steady warmth rising between his skin and the air's lingering bite, the breath's cold easing back a pace from his bones. Thane, twice-hidden, was beyond even a friendly spell's finding. It was the right spell. It was the obvious spell. It was what any of them would have spent, seeing what stood in front of them and what it breathed.

Korroc lifted his head, found the half-elf, and shook it. Once.

No words crossed the channel, and none were needed. Nageru read the whole of it in that one small motion, and the reading reached him a beat behind the warmth on his own shoulders: the same ward that had settled over him and Caleth like a blessing had settled over Korroc and found nothing it could hold. Whatever ran crosswise in the dwarf — the forge-heat that was his gift, and the price the heat exacted — it ran deeper than any warding could reach. A cloak does not help the man whose winter is already inside the house. The spell that sheltered two of them slid off the third like water off hot iron, and Korroc had known it would, and had spent no breath explaining. The beast was still moving. Explanations were for the living to trade at dinner.

And then Nageru felt the thread let go.

There was no violence in it. That was the thing he would remember. One moment the quiet channel between them stood open, as it had stood for days — his own aches lessened, drawn steadily away into the dwarf's stubborn keeping — and the next it was simply shut. Closed from the far end. And its meaning arrived in Nageru whole, and it was the worst news of the morning, worse than the wings and the three heads: *he has nothing left to carry with.* The dwarf who took half of everything, who had drunk the party's wounds through every fight since the wardstone, had done his own arithmetic in the ice-fog and found that one more shared blow would drop him — and so he had let go of them. To keep standing. Standing, and alone in his body for the first time in days, with the worst thing they had ever faced already turning toward him.

Korroc reached down — Nageru saw it through the thinning white, the familiar settling, the stone rising up through the dwarf's boots to root him where he stood, one hand pressing gold light into his own side.

And the three-headed thing chose him.

Of course it chose him. Predators choose the wounded; three heads read weakness three ways. It came up the channel at the rooted dwarf with a speed nothing that size should own, and the leopard head and the goat head came down on the shield — twice, the impacts booming off the steel like rams at a gate, Korroc's braced arm giving and holding, giving and holding — and then the dragon head, patient, riding high on its long neck while its siblings hammered the guard flat, darted in over the shield's rim and *bit down* on the dwarf, and Nageru saw the white teeth close on plate and flesh, and saw his brother's face grind sideways with it, and heard him grimace out a sound he had no spare strength to swallow.

Something happened in Nageru then, and he did not resist it, and this too he would remember: that he did not resist it.

A growl came up out of him, low, from a place beneath the training. His whole life was a discipline of the shed heartbeat — read, yield, redirect; the still water, the patient thunder; anger mastered a thousand times in a courtyard until mastery was nature. But the water had a floor, and the floor was this: the one who carries everyone was in a monster's teeth, and *had no one carrying him.*

His fists caught fire. The smite rose through him unbidden — his blood and his oath agreeing without consulting him, celestial light hammering up his forearms and off his knuckles in cold-gold flame — and Nageru left the ground.

The leap covered the channel. He rose over the shield-line and the boom of the two hammering heads, over his brother in the dragon's teeth, and he came down on the beast with everything the Sunken Fist had ever poured into him and everything it had never taught him at all, and he heard his own voice at last, and it was loud, and it was one word, and the word was the whole law:

"*NO!*"

The blow landed like a falling tower. Light burst where it struck. The chimera's three throats screamed together — the dragon head tearing free of Korroc to scream, which was the point, which was worth it all by itself — and the great body staggered sideways off the rooted dwarf, wings flailing for balance, frost and blood flying.

But it stood. It absorbed a strike that had ended demons outright, and it screamed, and it *stood*, and beneath its rage Nageru's ear caught something that chilled him lower than the breath had: the screams were not only pain. The thing was affronted. It had understood the blow — understood the light in it — and its hate had a mind.

Caleth cast. Nageru heard the incantation land and could not find its work anywhere — until he looked for Korroc and Korroc was gone. Not fallen: *hidden*, the air holding a dwarf-shaped nothing where the bitten wall had stood. The half-elf had spent his moment making the beast's chosen prey vanish from under its noses — three sets of noses, all of them suddenly bereft — and the chimera's screech this time was pure frustration, three-throated, ringing down the channel.

Its heads swung. Hunting. And found the caster.

It went for Caleth with the same total speed, and Nageru, still recovering his stance from the leap, was too far, a fact he took in with cold completeness even as he moved. The dragon head struck first — and something met it. Not steel. A pressure. A shape in the air between the fangs and the half-elf, arriving from the direction of an invisible dwarf with an outflung unseen hand — Korroc, spending what he did not have, reaching across the space to put one more wall where a wall was needed. The bite came through it — the beast was too strong, the wall too dearly bought — but it came through *slowed*, blunted, and that was the margin.

Caleth used the margin the way only Caleth moved. He swayed backward out of the closing arc — not a stumble but a fencer's retreat, precise as handwriting, the leopard's claws and the goat's horns cutting the air where his chest had been a half-breath prior — and as the dragon head overreached past him he turned the retreat into a thrust and drove Radiance up into the white-scaled jaw. The blade went in burning. The head recoiled with white fire in its mouth.

Gold flared twice in the empty air — Korroc, unseen, healing himself, and then a second warmth crossing to Caleth, closing the worst of what the slowed bite had done. The wall, still walling. With nothing left, from nowhere, as ever.

The chimera took one more prey.

It came back at Caleth faster than the fencer's grace could answer twice. The dragon head feinted; the whole vast body followed; and the bite that landed, landed *hard* — Nageru heard it land, heard the breath go out of his friend all at once — and Caleth crumpled to the channel floor and did not move.

The world narrowed. Nageru was moving; Thane was closer.

Thane had been nowhere all this while, which meant Thane had been exactly where Thane goes. Behind it. Under the wings, in the blind angle three heads somehow share, unseen twice over — and as the chimera reared over the fallen half-elf, the daggers went in. Nageru did not see the blades. He saw the *result* — the great body seizing, arching, every line of it converting in one instant from triumph to catastrophe — mother's knife and father's, he would learn afterward, driven both together into the one seam where the three lives of the thing ran in a single channel.

The chimera screamed. This one was different. Every fight has a last sound, and everything in the channel knew this was it — a three-voiced death-scream that broke apart into its separate throats and failed, one head at a time, and the enormous body toppled sideways, wings loose, and came down on the stones with a boom that rolled away east down the riverbed and did not come back.

Silence, then. The ringing kind.

---

Nageru reached Caleth before the echo died.

He knelt in the spreading dark of the half-elf's blood and laid both palms to him — chest and ruin — and poured the grace through, matter-of-fact as a punch, which is to say: with everything he had, delivered exactly. The torn flesh drew. The faltering breath caught, steadied, deepened. Nageru watched the pale face for the eyes and did not permit himself any feeling that would not be useful until he had them.

Caleth's eyes opened slowly.

The half-elf lay still a moment, taking inventory from the inside. Then, with great dignity: "Damn, that hurt."

Nageru grinned at him — it arrived on his face without permission, warm and entire — and nodded, because it was the truest report anyone had made all morning and deserved ratification.

Gold rolled over them both; Korroc, visible again and gray-faced and upright, had planted his hammer and sent one more wave of healing out across the channel, wounds closing across all four of them at once, the wall doing wall's work to the last of its mortar. Nageru pressed a second healing into Caleth on top of it — his own, from the oath's deep well — and the half-elf breathed all the way down, and sat up under his own power, and looked at the mountainous dead thing that had nearly kept him.

It had never breathed its ice a second time. Nageru marked that in the quiet ledger a fighter keeps for afterward — that the thing had spent its storm once and then trusted its teeth, and that the trusting had been their fortune. Against teeth there had been answers: a shield, a ward flung across a channel, a fencer's grace, two daggers in the dark. Against a second storm of cold, three of them could have been sheltered. The fourth had no shelter that any magic could give, and every one of them had fought the rest of that fight knowing it, and none of them had said it aloud. Some knowledge works best as weight, not words.

They stood in the channel, the four of them, bleeding and breathing, and around them lay the morning's whole ledger: four great beasts dead, their handlers dead, a caster and his crossbowmen frozen and fallen on the deck above, a chimera cooling into the stones — and the Ahari Bridge standing over it all, chains broken from every pier, holding up the sky the way its builders had meant it to.

"The bridge is cleared," Nageru said, to no one, confirming it to the morning.

"Then let's get back," Thane said — Nageru had barely registered he was visible again — wiping each dagger with long even strokes, mother's then father's, "and tell Irabeth she can bring the army over."

The walk back into camp, Nageru would think later, was its own small chapter in the company's legend, and none of them planned it. They came in through the pickets in the full light of morning, and they were — he inventoried it honestly — appalling. Blood dried black on white wraps, on dented ice-scarred plate, on a tabard torn by teeth the size of forearms. Korroc's beard was singed at the braid-ends. Caleth had been dragged to the very edge of the dark, less than an hour ago, and was walking as straight as a spear anyway, at cost only Nageru — who walked behind him — could see. And the camp turned to stare, cookfires and sentry posts going quiet in a spreading wake, and the four of them simply *walked*, unhurried, as though returning from an errand of no particular note.

No one performed it. That was what made it work. Nageru watched the soldiers watch them, and understood what the soldiers were writing in their own minds, and let it be written, because an army marches on its stories as much as its feet.

By afternoon the army was moving. Irabeth heard the report standing, asked the right questions in the right order, and gave the order before the telling was fully done; and the column came off the foothold ground and swung west, and by evening the Fifth Crusade stood camped at the south edge of the Ahari Bridge, engineers already down among the piers with lanterns and plumb-lines, crawling over the stonework the beasts had strained, arguing happily about loads and shoring the way engineers argue, discussing how to make the wounded span carry an army.

The road west was open. The city lay ahead.

---

Aravashnial found the beasts before dusk, and Nageru found Aravashnial.

The wizard was moving among the great carcasses in the channel with his book out, sketching in quick strokes, peering at the fused wrongness of them with his restored eyes. Caleth had wandered down as well. Nageru drifted after, because something in him wanted the day *named* — it was the way his mind closed accounts — and the elf was the namer.

"Aurochs," Aravashnial said, as Caleth came up beside him. He said it and then he *spat*, an emphasis Nageru had never once seen the dry elf spend on anything. "In this case — an animal fused with an evil spirit. Often the result of a botched summoning, or a botched exorcism. Wretched things. The beast is in there the whole time, you understand. Underneath."

Nageru thought of the two voices in the bellow, and was quiet.

Then the wizard turned to the other carcass — the mountain of it, the three ruined heads lying loose-necked on the stones — and the professional briskness went out of him.

"But that one?" Aravashnial shook his head slowly. "No idea why that one is so different. So strong." He walked its length, and stopped at the dragon head, and looked at it a long moment, and when he spoke again his voice had lowered as if the thing might hear. "It seems *enhanced*. Fortified past its nature." He looked up at them then, and his new-healed eyes went from Caleth to Nageru to the dwarves, one to the next, deliberate. "In the same way the stone affected you."

The whole group had gathered by then, drawn in the way people are drawn to the naming of things. Nobody spoke. Nageru looked at the chimera — at the size of it, the speed of it he had felt in his ribs, the strike it had eaten and screamed at and *stood through* — and set that beside the golden fire that had filled the four of them under the Gray Garrison, the power that healed their wounds and quickened their hands and had carried a heal-spell across fifteen feet of open air that very morning. The same. In the same way. On the other side.

His blood was very still. His ancestors were paying attention.

Somewhere behind them the engineers' lanterns bobbed under the span, and the sound of an army making camp drifted down the evening — orderly, human, alive; two hundred soldiers and two hundred freed souls and a road west finally open — and ahead in the dark the citadel sat over it all, holding its banner and its traitor and, now, this new fact, this worst fact, patient as everything else it held.

The breath went down.

"I don't like the sound of that," Nageru said, low.

No one answered him. There was no answer. The gods had marked the four of them; something in that citadel was marking its own; and the still water lay quiet in him around the shape of what was coming, and did not pretend the quiet was peace.
