# The Voice That Answers
*Chapter Nine*

The city was breathing differently.

Nageru noticed it the way he noticed all things — not first in the eyes, but lower, in the place where the breath went down. He stood barefoot at the edge of the northern wall in the gray hour before the sun had committed to rising, the worn prayer-beads dark about his wrist, and he let the city move through him, and what moved through him had changed.

For weeks Kenabres had breathed like a wounded man: shallow, guarded, each inhalation taken as though the next might be the one the ribs refused to allow. He had learned the rhythm of it. He had matched himself to it the way a man matches his breathing to a sleeper he does not wish to wake.

Now there was an army camped against its walls, and the city breathed deeper, and the deeper breath hurt worse.

He had thought, before, that hope would feel like relief. It did not. It felt like the moment a numbed limb begins to wake — the blood coming back, and the blood bringing pain with it, because the limb had been spared its own truth while the cold held it. The crusaders had come up the road with their sword and their rose and their hammer, and the people of Kenabres had climbed to the ramparts to see them, and Nageru had stood among those people and watched their faces, and he had understood that the city was not whole enough to hold what it was being given.

They were so few.

That was the thing the army's arrival had made plain. Stand a thinned people against a great host and you could finally count them, the way you could finally count the trees of a forest after a fire. Barely half of Kenabres remained. He had known the number as a number for weeks. He had not known it as a thing you could see in the gaps between the cheering — the woman alone where a family should have stood, the long stretches of ruined street where no one came out to look at all because there was no one left in them to come.

Klarah stood against his leg.

She had come to the wall the way she came everywhere now, in the gray hour, without being told and without being sent away — her small shoulder set to his knee, her brown head turned toward the road and the standards as though they were a thing she was deciding about. Over the weeks of the breathing space she had drifted to him the way water finds the low place. Not only to him. But to him as much as anyone, and in the early dark more than to anyone. She did not speak often. She had not spoken now. She had only found his leg with her shoulder and stood, watching the army that had come, and Nageru let her stand, and matched his breath down to hers without making her feel it.

He thought of where she had been found, and of the two who had been found in their nightshirts in the rubble, asleep when the roof came down, and did not say it.

She was the gap he had been counting. She was the child held by a stranger — except the stranger had a name she had begun to use, and the strangers had become, by some arithmetic he had not agreed to and would not have refused, a thing close enough to hers that she stood at his leg in the cold and watched help come up the road.

But hope shone in the gaps too. He saw that. A man with a bandaged head had lifted a child onto his shoulders to see the standards, and the child had pointed at the sword-and-sunburst of Iomedae and laughed, and the man had wept under the child without letting the child feel it. An old woman had pressed her palm flat to the cold stone of the wall as though to tell the wall: *hold a little longer; help has come.*

The breath went down.

The breath came up.

*This,* Nageru thought, *is what the breaking spared and the mending will cost. They will have to feel it now. They will have to be alive enough to grieve.*

He bowed his head a fraction, to no one, and went to find his brothers, because the trumpets had been answered and the road was full of crusaders, and the work — he could feel it gathering, the way he had felt the storm gather at the Sunken Fist — the work was about to ask for him again.

---

The summons came at midday.

The army had spent the morning making itself at home in a city that had no home left to offer. Road-worn crusaders moved through the streets in companies, battle-scarred, gray with the dust of the long march, and they did not rest. They set to the walls. They raised barricades that Kenabres had not had the hands to raise. They carried timber and stone and the dead, and they buried the dead with rites that the city had been too overwhelmed to give them, and Nageru watched a column of paladins kneel in the rubble of a collapsed chapel and sing the office of the fallen over bones that had lain a year without it, and something in his chest eased that had been clenched since the day they climbed out of the dark.

Order, returning. In small pieces. A rite said. A wall raised. A grave dug straight.

Then a messenger found them — found each of the four, separately, the way a thing is done when it is meant to carry weight — and the message was the same to all of them. *Report to the Defender's Heart.*

They went together.

Nageru felt the change before they reached the door. The street outside the inn had always been busy, but it had been the busyness of survivors, the milling of people who had nowhere better to be. Now it was the busyness of a command. Crusaders in clean tabards stood post where there had been refugees. Horses were picketed in ordered ranks. And as the four of them came up the steps, the people in the doorway moved aside.

That was the thing he marked. They moved aside.

Not in fear. In something closer to deference, and the deference was harder to carry than fear would have been, because Nageru did not know yet what they thought they were deferring to. He kept his eyes level and his step even and he let the moving-aside happen around him without acknowledging it, the way still water lets a current pass.

There were guards at the council-room door he did not know — crusaders, hard-faced, the badges of an army that had marched a long way to be here. They saw Korroc's shield, the new hammer-and-anvil of Torag bright on the dented plate, and one of them gave a small nod and stood away from the door.

Inside, the room was full of voices, and then the four of them entered, and the voices stopped.

Nageru took the room in the way his order had taught him to take a room — not feature by feature but as a single held breath, reading the postures, the small motions that came before intent. Irabeth, near the table, tall and steady, but with a tension across her shoulders he had not seen in her since the day Thane came home half-dead. Quednys, the old wizard, his hands folded, watchful. Aravashniel, off to one side, and the wizard's stillness was its own kind of wound — a man holding himself very carefully, the way a man holds himself when something in him has cracked and he is afraid that movement will show it. Anevia, sharp-eyed, reading the room as Nageru read it. Horgus, near the back, arms folded, watchful in his own heavy way.

And a figure at the far window, facing away from them, looking out at the broken city in the gray noon light.

Full plate. The kind a smith made once in a lifetime and a soldier earned over a longer one. The line of the shoulders said *commander* before the figure had moved at all — said it the way a wall says *wall*, with the simple fact of standing.

The figure turned.

A woman. Not young. Not old. A face that had spent decades doing the thing the body in the armor was built to do, and the doing of it had carved her down to essentials, the way wind carves stone — nothing soft left, nothing wasted, only the shape that endured. Her eyes moved across the four of them slowly, weighing, the way a quartermaster weighs goods he has heard about and now must judge for himself.

Nageru knew her at once, though he had never seen her face. He had seen her shoulders. He had seen them at the head of the army, coming up the road against the gray edge of the world.

She had ridden first. Of course she had ridden first.

Quednys broke the silence. His old voice was dry but it carried.

"Your Majesty," he said. "These are the four men you requested to see."

He gestured to each in turn, and named them, and Nageru heard his own name placed last and felt nothing about the placing. "Korroc and Thane Stonevein. Caleth Aranor. And Nageru."

The Queen's gaze went to each name as it was given. It lingered, Nageru saw, on the two dwarves — went to Korroc's shield, to Thane's watchful stillness, and held there a half-breath longer than it held on the others. He could not read what the holding meant. He filed it. Some weights belonged to other men, and a man did not pry into them uninvited.

Then she inclined her head, the smallest measure, and named herself.

"Queen Galfrey of Mendev."

The room did not bow, because there was no room to bow in, and because she was plainly not a woman who required it. But something in the room lowered all the same.

Irabeth stepped forward, and her voice came quick and proud, the way a soldier's voice comes when she has carried good men's deeds a long way and wants them set down at last in front of someone who can honor them.

"These are the men that destroyed the last of the ward—"

The Queen raised one gauntleted hand.

Irabeth stopped.

"I would like," the Queen said, "to hear it from them."

A silence. Nageru felt the others' attention shift the way he had felt it shift once before, in a back room of this same inn, with a different audience and a smaller world. Korroc looked at Caleth. Caleth looked at Nageru. There was the faintest dry pressure at the corner of Thane's mouth — the look of a man recognizing, again, that the watch had fallen to the one most likely to draw it.

Nageru understood that he was being asked to speak.

He did not mind it now. The first time, in this room's neighbor, the words had felt like a tool he had not held in years. They felt like his own hands now. He had used them since. He set his breath, and he found the shape of the thing, and he told it.

He told it in order. He told her of the Gray Garrison and the deep room beneath it, of the wardstone fragment with its veins of wrongness, of the strange events that had come over the four of them as they worked to unmake it — the light that had risen out of the stone and out of them, the way the room had gone gold, the way the world had folded and unfolded. He did not embellish. He did not understand it well enough to embellish, and he had learned long ago not to dress a thing he could not name. He told it as he had lived it, plain, up to the moment Irabeth had shaken them awake on the bloodied floor with the demons already burning in the streets.

He stopped.

The Queen was quiet a long moment. Then she asked one question, and the question was precise, the question of a commander who had read a hundred reports and learned to find the one fact under them all.

"What exactly happened," she said, "when you destroyed the stone?"

So he told her that part again, slower, the part he understood least. The light. The folding. The sense — he gave her this carefully, because it was true and he would not withhold a true thing from her — the sense that something had passed *through* them, and not back out.

When he finished she did not answer at once. She studied him. She studied all four of them. And when she spoke, she spoke as one who had already decided what she was going to say and had only been waiting to look at the men she was going to say it to.

"While there are some narrow-minded souls among the crusade who blame you for the destruction of the wardstone border," she said, "I do not begrudge you for what you did. Far from it."

Nageru held still.

"From the sound of it, you saved entire legions of crusaders from a most vile fate by preventing Vorlesh from transforming them, and the blast of energy gave us the time we needed to regroup and prepare for what will certainly come next." Her gaze moved across them, level, weighing nothing now, only telling. "But you understand the implications of what you've done, I'm sure. The wardstones are gone." A pause. "And Iomedae has seen fit that you will be their replacement."

The room held it.

Caleth was the first to break — and Caleth breaking was a small thing, a single sentence, but Nageru had stood beside the half-elf long enough to know how rare even that was. "Wait. What?" The measured voice had slipped its measure. "*All* of the wardstones are gone?"

The Queen nodded, once.

Korroc made a low sound in his beard, the grumble of a dwarf who had been handed something heavier than he agreed to carry. "We were following orders," he said, to no one, to the floor.

The Queen's mouth moved. She hid the smile well, but Nageru saw it go across her eyes and out again, the way light goes across deep water.

Thane said nothing for a moment. Then: "Wardstone's *replacement*?" — flat, dry, the question of a man checking whether he had heard a thing he very much hoped he had not.

The Queen let the words sit. She did not soften them. Nageru thought better of her for it. A commander who softened the weight before a man had lifted it only made the lifting harder later.

"The demons are regrouping," she said. "And so are we. There is an opportunity in this moment that there will not be again." She moved to the table, where a map was spread, and set one finger to it without looking down. "Aponavicius — a marilith, a general of the Worldwound — has taken the bulk of her army out of Drezen. She moves it east, toward us, toward what she expects will be the next battle. Which means Drezen, for the first time in living memory, is only moderately protected."

Nageru watched the two dwarves.

He had been watching them since the Queen named Drezen, because he had felt the change in them before she finished the word — felt it the way he felt the small motions that came before a strike. Korroc had gone very still. Thane had glanced at Korroc, one short glance, and Korroc had not glanced back, which was its own kind of answer.

*Drezen.* Nageru knew part of what that word carried. He had stood in the gold with them, hung in the wardstone's dying light, and seen what all of them had seen — the burly dwarf branded by the holy stone, the chase across the Kite, the name laid into their minds like a date stamped in metal. *Staunton Vhane.* The traitor who had crippled Irabeth and fled north on a fiend's wings to Drezen. S.V. — the initials from the letter had a face now, and the face had a destination, and the destination was the place the Queen was asking them to go.

But there was more in the two dwarves than the vision explained. Nageru could see it — the way Korroc's stillness had gone from attention to something harder, the way Thane's glance at his cousin had carried a weight that did not belong to a traitor's name alone. He knew their fathers had fallen in the Fourth Crusade. He knew Drezen had been a dwarven citadel before it fell. He did not know why the name *Staunton Vhane* sat in these two men differently than it sat in him — heavier, closer, more personal — but he could feel that it did, the way a fighter feels a shift in the room's whole balance before the first blow lands. Whatever it was, they had not shared it, and Nageru did not ask. A man's grief was his own house. He read what he could read. He did not break the door to read the rest.

"Drezen," the Queen went on, "was the first city to fall after the First Crusade. When it fell, we lost more than a city. We lost the Sword of Valor." Her finger left the map. "A banner. A magical standard, created by Iomedae's own hand and carried by Her during the Shining Crusade. It is, we believe, still within the citadel — kept as a trophy, the way such things are kept by such creatures. And while it remains in their hands, it works against us. It hinders divination. It blinds us to the very place we most need to see."

She straightened. She looked at the four of them, and the weighing was gone from her eyes entirely now, replaced by the thing that came after weighing — the asking.

"A small group of capable heroes," she said, "with a modest army, could take it."

She let that stand.

"Are you willing," she said, "to try to take the citadel of Drezen?"

Nageru did not look at the others.

He had spent his life being the one who waited — the still point, the one whose breathing did not change while the world tilted around him. He had waited through the underdark. He had waited through the manor and the library and the long siege-quiet of the breathing space. Waiting was the discipline of his order; it was a prayer the way breath was a prayer, the way standing still long enough for a hard man to find his own goodness was a prayer.

But there was a thing his masters had taught him that he had been slow to learn, and the Worldwound was teaching him now, fight by fight, threshold by threshold. *Stillness is not the absence of motion. Stillness is the readiness for it.* The thunder is patient. The thunder does not stay patient forever.

The breath went down.

"Yes," he said.

He said it first. He felt the others register that he had said it first — felt Korroc's weight shift, felt Caleth's quick glance — and he let it stand, because it was true, and because some part of him had understood, standing on the wall in the gray dawn, that the waiting was over and the answering had begun.

Thane spoke next, and there was something underneath the dryness now, something banked and hot. "We have business there," he said. "Gladly."

Nageru heard the word *business* and understood it was not the Queen's business Thane meant. There was something personal in the dryness — something that had been there since the wardstone's gold, since the name *Staunton Vhane* had landed in all of them. Whatever the dwarves carried about that name, it was more than Nageru carried, and it made the word *business* mean a thing Thane was not saying aloud.

Korroc nodded. One nod. It was enough; it was a Stonevein's whole speech.

Caleth was the last. He stood impassive a long moment, his green eyes on the map, on the place where the Queen's finger had been. Nageru watched the calculation move behind the half-elf's face — the futures running one against another, the way they always ran behind Caleth's stillness. Then the calculation resolved, and Caleth nodded too.

"Yes," he said. "Saint's mercy. We will try."

The Queen looked at the four of them a moment longer.

"Good," she said.

---

The crowd outside the Defender's Heart had grown.

Nageru did not understand, at first, why. The four of them came out into the gray noon and the street was full — full of crusaders and townsfolk both, packed close, a murmur running through them that rose as the door opened. Word had moved ahead of them, the way word does in a place where everyone is starved for something to say that is not a death.

The Queen came out behind them. And she drew her sword.

It came out of the scabbard with a sound like a held breath let go, and the crowd went quiet, and Nageru understood then what was happening, and a strange stillness took him — not his own stillness, the disciplined kind he made for himself, but a thing arriving from outside, the way the Archon weight sometimes arrived, settling into his ribs.

"To make things official," the Queen said, and her voice carried across the silenced street the way a bell carries, "I dub each of you Knights of the Fifth Crusade."

She came to Korroc first.

The dwarf knelt before the blade reached him — went down on one knee the way a smith sets a thing where it belongs, without ceremony and without doubt, because for him there was nothing to weigh. The Queen set the flat of the blade to one shoulder and then the other, and spoke the words, and Korroc's iron-banded beard did not move but his eyes did something Nageru had not seen them do — went briefly, fiercely bright, the brightness of a man whose father had been a crusader and whose father's father had been a crusader, hearing himself named into the line at last by a queen's own blade.

Then Thane. And here Nageru read the body the way he read a body across a sparring floor — the small things, the weight, the breath. Thane did not go down. A beat passed, and then another, a beat too long, and there was a stiffness in his stance that had nothing to do with his knees, the stillness of a proud man who had bent to dwarven kings and to no one else and felt the difference in his bones. Then Korroc, still kneeling beside him, said something low — three words, four, dwarf to dwarf, too quiet for the street, the kind of thing said into a beard and meant. And Thane knelt. He did it cleanly, but Nageru saw the cost of it travel through him as he went down, saw the proud thing yield to the larger thing, and he thought of the man in the culvert that Korroc had spoken of once, who had run alone through the broken land to bring this army home. *He earned this in the dark. The kneeling is the only part that costs him.*

Then Caleth. The half-elf knelt the way he did everything, precisely, as though there were a correct geometry even to this, and the Queen's blade touched him, and when he rose his face had gone still in the way that was Caleth's deepest feeling, the stillness he wore over the things that moved him most.

Then Nageru.

He knelt — and bowed as he knelt, from the waist, low, the bow of his order, the bow he gave to a master or a god or a thing that deserved more than courtesy. He felt the flat of the blade come to rest on his right shoulder, cool through the white wraps, and then the left. He heard the words. He let them settle into the place where the breath went down. *Knight of the Fifth Crusade.* He had crossed a thousand miles from the Sunken Fist toward a storm he could feel but not name, and he had not known what shape his role would take in it. He felt the shape arrive now, with the cold weight of the blade on his shoulder. Not the whole of it. A piece. The next piece.

The breath went down.

The breath came up.

When he straightened, the Queen was already turning, and Nageru saw with a quiet gladness who she turned to.

"Irabeth Tirabade," the Queen said.

The half-orc had been standing to the side, and at the sound of her own name her face did a thing that broke Nageru's heart a little — a flinch of disbelief, quickly mastered, the look of a soldier who had given everything for a cause and never once expected the cause to turn around and name her. She came forward. She knelt. The blade touched her shoulders, and the Queen named her into the order, and when Irabeth rose there were tears standing in her eyes that she did not let fall, and at the edge of the crowd Anevia was looking at her wife with an expression Nageru recognized from a doorway weeks ago — *love survives, it is one of the things we are for* — and he bowed his head a fraction, because some moments deserved a small bow even from a stranger.

Then came the medals.

A page brought them on a cloth, and the Queen took them up one at a time, and Nageru watched her give them with the same precision she gave everything.

To Korroc, and to Thane, and to Caleth: the Righteous Medal of Vigor — a hexagon of white enamel, a castle worked upon a field of red, the whole bordered in gold. *For those who hold,* Nageru understood, watching her pin it. *For walls that do not fall.* It suited the two dwarves entirely.

And the Righteous Medal of Clarity, circular, blue, a pair of gray eyes worked at its center beneath a silver bolt struck in a ring — given for sight, for the seeing-true that a battle needed as much as it needed steel. Caleth took it without expression and Nageru thought it suited him too, the half-elf who saw angles where other men saw only the fight.

Then the Queen came to Nageru with the last one, and it was not like the others.

A triangle of dark iron. Upon it, a demon's face — a snarling, horned thing — surrounded by three crossed swords. She held it a moment before she pinned it, and she looked at him, and she spoke quietly enough that the words were for him and not the crowd.

"The Righteous Medal of Agility," she said. "It is given rarely. It is given to a soldier who delivers a death-blow to a demon before the demon is given the chance to act at all." Her eyes were steady on his. "I am told you have done this. I am told you will do it again."

Nageru inclined his head.

"I will do what must be done," he said.

She pinned the iron triangle to the white of his wraps, over the place where the breath went down, and she stepped back, and the crowd surged in.

He had not expected the surging. They came in around the four of them all at once — hands at their sleeves, faces close, voices overlapping, the people of Kenabres pressing toward the four strangers who had walked out of the Garrison and the four knights the Queen had just made of them, as if some of the hope might rub off on their own hands by the touching. A woman caught Nageru's wrist and blessed him in the name of Sarenrae. A man wept and could not say why. A child — not Klarah, a smaller one — held up a wooden toy as though to show him something important, and Nageru crouched, and looked at the toy with full attention, and the child went away satisfied.

He let it happen. He received it the way he received most things, with a small bow and few words. But under the receiving, the Archon weight had come up behind his ribs and would not settle, and he understood why. *This is the weight of what we are about to carry,* it said, in the way it spoke, which was no words at all. *These hands. This child. The road to Drezen runs through every one of them. Do not forget whose hope you spend.*

He did not forget.

He was still in the press when Quednys reached him — the old wizard moving through the crowd with the patience of a man parting reeds — and put a hand briefly on Nageru's arm and spoke low under the noise.

"Drezen," Quednys said. "That's a tall order." His old eyes searched Nageru's face. "Are you sure you're ready?"

Nageru did not have to think about the answer. He had given it already, inside, on the wall at dawn.

He nodded.

Caleth had drifted close, and he heard, and he answered for both of them in his measured voice. "We are."

Quednys held them a moment longer in his gaze. Then he let go of Nageru's arm and stepped back, and something in him seemed to decide to trust them, the way an old man decides to set down a weight he has carried as far as he can.

"Then take friends," he said. "And prepare."

---

The Queen's camp had risen against the northern wall the way a city rises in a dream — overnight, complete, ordered.

Nageru walked through it in the late afternoon with the others, and his Archon blood, which had spent weeks pressed flat by the wrongness of broken Kenabres, found in the camp a thing it had not felt in a long time: the clean weight of order made by hands. Tents in ranks. Fires at measured distances. Horse-lines dressed. Sentries posted where sentries should be posted. It was not the order of his monastery — it was rougher, an army's order, made for killing and not for contemplation — but it was order, and his blood drank it the way a parched man drinks.

The Queen's own tent stood at the camp's heart, large enough to hold a council and holding one now. Some two hundred soldiers stood within it and around it — paladins, mostly, the badges of the Mendevian host on their tabards, the hard quiet faces of people who had marched a long way and knew there was a longer way still to go. The four of them came in, and Irabeth with them, and Anevia, and the soldiers made room.

The Queen was waiting. She did not waste their time. Three figures stood near her, and she named them as the four approached.

"You will not march alone," she said, "and you will not march only with the men you already trust. I am giving you three who know things you will need to know."

The first she named was a man Nageru's eye had gone to before she spoke — because the man was still, and Nageru noticed stillness, and this man's stillness was not the disciplined stillness of an order. It was the stillness of a man who had stopped expecting anything good and had made his peace with the stopping. Lean. Plain. A face that had lost too much and had quit being surprised by the losing.

"Aron Kir," the Queen said. "He will advise you on movement and tactics. Listen to him. He has survived things that should not be survivable, and he survived them by being right."

Aron Kir gave the four of them a single nod and said nothing, which Nageru respected.

"Sosiel Vaenic," the Queen said, and a younger man stepped forward — and Nageru saw Caleth go very still beside him.

It was a small thing. Anyone not standing where Nageru stood would have missed it. But Nageru had spent weeks reading the half-elf's stillnesses, and he knew this one was different from the others, knew that Caleth had seen something on the young cleric — a symbol, a sign, something at the man's throat or breast that meant a great deal to Caleth and that Caleth would not name. Nageru let his own gaze go to it: a holy symbol, the rose and songbird, the sign of Shelyn. The young cleric's faith, then. A god of beauty and love in a war of demons.

Nageru did not pry into the half-elf's stillness. He filed it the way he filed all such things, gently, in the place where another man's house was another man's house. *The half-elf knows this faith,* was all he let himself think. *Or it knows him.* He let it lie.

"Sosiel will see to the spirits of your soldiers," the Queen went on, "and to their wounds. An army marches on its heart as much as its feet. He keeps both."

Sosiel inclined his head, and his face was open in a way the other two faces were not — a young man who had not yet been hollowed by the war, or who had decided, against the evidence, not to let it hollow him.

"And Nurah Dendiwhar," the Queen said.

The third figure was small, and Nageru's eye had nearly passed over her — which, he thought a half-breath later, might have been the point of how she stood. A halfling woman, unassuming, plainly dressed, the kind of person a room forgot it had seen. She gave the four of them a quick bright nod.

"A historian," the Queen said. "A chronicler of the Worldwound. She knows the land between here and Drezen better than anyone living who is not currently a demon. She knows what stood there before, and what stands there now, and the difference between the two has kept armies alive." The Queen's mouth moved in the not-quite-smile. "Use her."

Nageru inclined his head to each of them in turn. "We are glad of you," he said, and meant it.

The Queen turned then to the two hundred soldiers ranked around the tent.

"These are yours," she said simply. "Two hundred. To command, to spend wisely, to bring as many home as the war allows." She looked at the four knights she had made that morning. "And take whoever else you think will help. The choosing is yours. Choose well; the choosing will be the half of it."

---

The choosing fell to them in the cool of the evening, around a fire at the edge of the camp, and Nageru found that the others looked to him for it more than he had expected.

It was Caleth who began. "Who should we take with us?" the half-elf asked, and his green eyes went, as they often did now, to Nageru.

Nageru considered. He had been considering since the Queen said *the choosing is yours,* turning the company over in his mind the way he turned a strike over before he threw it, looking for the line.

"Irabeth," he said. "To command the soldiers. She has done it through the worst weeks this city has known, and they will follow her because they already have." He paused. "And Anevia. For the scouting, and the seeing. There is no one who reads a marketplace or a road as she does, save Thane, and Thane cannot be in two places."

Caleth nodded slowly. "Yes," he said. "Both." He looked at Nageru. "Ask them."

Korroc spoke next, the firelight catching the sharp watchful planes of his face. "Horgus," he said. "For the logistics. The man can count, and he can find, and he can make a supply-train out of nothing. We saw it. He fed the whole city out of a hole in the ground." A pause. "I'd rather have him counting our grain than have us run out of it three days from Drezen." 

Thane's mouth did a dry, complicated thing at the name *Horgus*, but he did not object.

"Yes," Caleth said again. "Horgus."

"And Aravashniel," Caleth said, after a moment, and his voice when he said the wizard's name was carefully level — too level, Nageru thought, the levelness of a man holding something in place. "He knows things we will need. Demon-lore. The old crusades. He should come."

Nageru nodded. He did not know the whole of what stood between Caleth and the wizard — there was a thing there, a closeness, the careful orbit of two men who shared something the rest of the party did not — but he knew Aravashniel's worth, and worth was enough. "Yes," he said.

The choosing was made. Then came the asking, and the asking, by the shape of the evening, fell to Nageru.

He found Irabeth at the horse-lines, checking the picket the way a good officer checks everything twice. She heard him coming — barefoot as he was, she heard him; she was that kind of soldier — and turned.

"Knight Nageru," she said, and there was a dry warmth in it, the warmth of someone who had been knighted that same morning and found the title absurd on both of them.

"Commander," he said, and watched her flinch.

"I am not a commander," she said.

"You have commanded the watch of this city through a siege and a breaking," Nageru said. "You commanded it when there was no army and no queen and no hope. You commanded it alone." He let the words stand a moment in the cool air. "We march on Drezen with two hundred soldiers and four knights who have never commanded so much as a squad between them. We need someone who has done the thing. We need your experience, Irabeth. Not your sword only. Your experience."

She looked at him a long moment, and he saw the refusal rise in her and saw it meet the thing he had said and falter against it.

"I am better in the line than at the back of it," she said. "I lead by being where the worst of it is."

"Then lead by being where the worst of it is," Nageru said. "But carry the rest of them in your head while you do. You have been doing both for months. You only lacked anyone to say it aloud." A pause. "I am saying it aloud. We need you to command."

The half-orc was quiet. The horses shifted in the lines. At last she let out a long breath, and it had a roughness in it, the roughness of someone setting down a thing they had refused to pick up.

"Reluctantly," she said.

"Reluctance is the right way to take a command," Nageru said. "The eager ones spend men they should not spend."

That earned him a short startled laugh — the first he had heard from her — and she shook her head and said she would do it, and Nageru bowed and left her to the horses.

Anevia he did not have to persuade at all. He found her sharpening arrows by a fire, and he told her the company, and when he came to Irabeth's name a thing went across the scout's hard face that she did not bother to hide from him, because she had stopped hiding things from him somewhere in the broken weeks.

"Irabeth signed on," she said. Not a question.

"She did."

"Then so did I," Anevia said, "an hour before you asked," and went back to her arrows, and that was the whole of it.

---

Horgus was the one Nageru had thought might be hard, and Horgus surprised him.

He found the nobleman in a tent the army had given over to supply, and the tent was already half-ordered — crates stacked by Horgus's hand into the beginnings of a system, the man moving among them with a tally-board in his fist. He stopped when Nageru entered. His heavy face was tired, and under the tiredness was the thing Nageru had marked weeks ago in the cellar of the Gwerm mansion — the frustration of a man who had lost everything and did not know what to do with the part of himself that was still good.

Nageru told him the company. Told him the road. Told him the count of grain and horse and men that would have to be made and remade between Kenabres and Drezen, and the man who would have to make it.

Horgus listened. When Nageru finished, the nobleman set down the tally-board, and for a moment he did not speak. When he did, his voice was flat and stripped, the voice he used only when he had stopped performing.

"I have nothing left here," Horgus said. "You know that. The estate is a ruin. The fortune is rubble and what I could carry. There is nothing in Kenabres that needs Horgus Gwerm." His jaw worked. "Are you sure you want me with you?"

Nageru did not answer quickly. He had learned, with Horgus, that quickness read as dismissal. He let the question stand a moment, the way he let all things stand, so that the man would know the answer had been weighed.

"We need your skills," Nageru said. "An army that cannot feed itself dies before it reaches the wall. You have already kept a city alive once. We are asking you to keep a company alive across a hundred miles of the Worldwound's edge." A pause. "There is no one I would rather have counting our grain. I have seen what you do with a hole in the ground and a list."

Horgus looked at him, and something moved in the heavy face — the same thing that had moved when he set the prayer-beads on the table, the look of a hard man being seen for the part of himself he had given up hoping anyone would see.

"You need my skills," Horgus repeated, slowly, as though testing whether the words would hold weight.

"We do."

The nobleman nodded, once, and turned back to his crates so that Nageru would not see his face, and his voice when it came was gruff and not quite steady. "Then I will see your company does not starve," he said. "Which is more than the Queen's own quartermasters can promise, I might add. Go away. I have work."

Nageru bowed to the nobleman's turned back, lower than courtesy required, and went away, and left him to his work.

---

The matter of Aravashniel's eye came up the next morning, and Nageru was standing near enough to see it begin.

Caleth had gone to the wizard at first light, and Nageru, passing, had marked the bad state the elf was in — not the body, the body could be mended; the spirit. Aravashniel had been carrying himself carefully ever since the Defender's Heart, the way a man carries himself when something in him has cracked, and the cause of it was the eye and was not the eye. The ruined eye was only where the larger grief had found a place to settle. Nageru understood that much without being told. A man could bear a great loss and break, in the end, over a small one, because the small one was the only piece of it he could hold in his hands.

Caleth called Korroc over, quietly, and asked him to look at the wizard's eyes again. Korroc came and looked — the dwarf had a healer's gift, the Life Oracle's fire that had risen out of him under the Garrison — and he was a long moment about it, his blunt fingers gentle near the ruined socket, his face working through something.

When he straightened he shook his head.

"His eyesight can be fixed," Korroc said. "But it's beyond me. Not the will — the *reach*. The eye is ruined past what I can call up. Useless. He'll never see from it again." A pause, the dwarf choosing the next words like stones. "Not as it is. But it could be regrown. Regenerated. That's a deeper magic than mine."

Caleth's face went still — the strategic stillness, Nageru saw, the one that meant the half-elf was running the futures and not liking the count. Doubt sat in him. The cost of such a magic, perhaps. The unlikelihood of finding it. The hundred reasons a man learns to expect a *no* before he has finished asking the question.

Nageru had been standing near, and the answer was so plain to him that he almost did not understand the doubt.

"We could ask the Queen," he said.

Caleth looked at him.

"She brought an army," Nageru said. "An army carries clerics, and clerics carry scrolls, and a queen who has just named us her own knights will not balk at the cost of one man's eye when that man is the demon-lore the company will need at Drezen." He let it stand. "It is the obvious thing. I will ask her."

Caleth still looked doubtful — not at the logic, Nageru thought, but at something underneath it, some old habit of not asking, of solving things in the dark by oneself. Nageru did not wait for the doubt to resolve. Some things a man solved by doing them.

"I will ask her," he said again, and went.

The Queen heard him out in the morning light outside her tent. He was brief. He told her the eye was ruined past common healing, and that the man it belonged to was a wizard of rare knowledge who would be an asset to the company she had sent toward Drezen. He did not embellish. She had not seemed a woman who needed embellishment.

She studied him a moment with the weighing look. Then she nodded.

"Give me an hour," she said.

An hour later a cleric of Iomedae came across the camp with two paladins at her shoulder, and a scroll-case at her hip, and she found Aravashniel where he sat, and she did not make a ceremony of it. She unrolled the scroll. She laid her hand near the ruined eye and spoke the words of a magic deeper than any in the company could call up, and Nageru, standing at the edge of it, felt the thing happen — felt the clean weight of high order moving through the air, the way his blood felt all true things, a restoration that ran inward instead of down.

Aravashniel went rigid. Then he blinked. Then he blinked again, and his eyes — both of them now, both of them clear — went wide, and a sound came out of him that was not a word.

"I can see," he said. "I can — I can *see* again."

His voice cracked on it. Tears stood in the newly-mended eye and the old one both, and spilled, and the wizard did not seem to know it. He turned his head this way and that as though the world were a thing he had been handed back after thinking it lost forever — which, Nageru understood, it was. He thanked the cleric, his dry pages-turning voice gone ragged with it. Then he turned and found Caleth and thanked him, with a look that held more than the thanks, more than Nageru could read. And then — Aravashniel turned to Nageru.

"You asked her," the wizard said. "He told me. You asked her, when I would not have, when I had stopped — " He stopped. He gathered himself. "Thank you," he said, and the simplicity of it was the whole weight of it. "Thank you, Knight."

Nageru bowed.

"You will see the road to Drezen," he said. "We will need a man who can."

---

They marched at dawn the third day.

Horgus had performed his quiet miracle by then — wagons procured, horses found, a supply-train assembled out of an army's stores and a nobleman's stubbornness, the whole of it organized and counted and counted again by the day's end before the day they left. Nageru had watched it come together and let it become one more piece of order in the world. The man worked, when he worked, the way a smith worked. With love he would not have admitted to.

The Queen had given them their road. East bank of the River Selen, north, to a place called Vilareth Ford. "There is a fort there," she had said. "I do not know how holds it now. Be careful at the crossing." And then she had clasped each of their forearms in turn, a soldier's grip, and wished them the favor of Iomedae, and turned back to the larger war that was hers to fight while theirs went north.

The army formed up in the gray. Two hundred soldiers, the supply-train, the four knights and their chosen company. And at the head of it, a small comedy that Nageru watched with a stillness that hid a great deal of warmth.

Korroc came to the horse they had brought him. He looked at the horse. The horse looked at Korroc. The dwarf's face went through a slow sequence of opinions, none of them favorable, and then he grunted — a single eloquent dwarven grunt that contained an entire essay on the relationship between dwarves and horses — and turned his back on the animal and climbed up into the driver's seat of the nearest wagon, where he settled himself with the satisfaction of a man who has solved a problem by refusing to have it.

Thane clambered up beside him a moment later, muttering. Nageru caught only the tail of it — something about how no Stonevein had ever trusted a creature that could not be reasoned with, and a horse could not be reasoned with, and that was the whole of the matter and he'd thank the world not to argue.

Caleth mounted his own horse with the same precision he brought to everything, and said nothing about the dwarves, and the corner of his mouth moved in a way that was, for Caleth, an outright laugh.

Nageru walked. He had a horse, and he gave it to a soldier whose feet were worse than his own, and he walked at the head of the column barefoot in the cold, because his feet were a prayer the way his breath was a prayer, and the road was a thing he wished to feel.

They moved out north, toward a place the maps called Valas's Gift.

---

Thane scouted ahead, again and again, all that first day.

It was the thing he did, and he did it well, climbing down from the wagon at intervals to go ahead on foot, reading the floor of the world the way only he could read it, and coming back with the small certainties that kept an army alive. *The road is clear to that ridge. There is old demon-sign at the ford of the stream, three days cold. The grass lies wrong in that hollow; we go around.* Nageru marked the rhythm of his going and returning the way he marked all rhythms. It was a good rhythm. It was the rhythm of a company that meant to live.

The land was quiet. Too quiet, the way the land south of Kenabres had been quiet, the quiet that was not peace but absence — a settled country gone hollow, farms standing empty, fences mended by people who had meant to stay and had not been allowed to.

At the end of the first day they came to Valas's Gift, and Thane and Anevia and Aron Kir went ahead to scout it, the three of them moving out into the gathering dark while the column held.

They came back to report it empty. Not held by the enemy — empty. A ruin. No living thing in it, the houses standing open to the sky, the wells gone foul, the silence absolute. Nageru, when they told it, felt nothing wrong with his blood — no wrongness, no demon-shape, only the flat hollow of a place that had been left. So the army moved in, and found food in the abandoned stores worth the carrying, and Horgus catalogued it before a single crate moved, and they camped amongst the ruined walls for the night.

Nageru took a watch. He stood barefoot at the edge of the empty town in the small hours and let it breathe through him, and it did not breathe at all, because it was dead, and the dead do not breathe. He thought of the people who had mended these fences. He prayed, in the way he prayed, which was no words — for the clarity to see the road, for the next strike to land where it must, for the line of order to hold one more morning over one more company of the living.

Another day and a half brought them within reach of Vilareth Ford.

And the quiet, on that last stretch, changed.

Nageru felt it before Thane reported it. The land had been quiet with absence; now it went quiet with presence — the held-breath quiet of a thing that knew it was being approached, or a thing that lay in wait. His blood did not ring with demon-wrongness, not yet, but it stirred, the way still water stirs before a stone breaks its surface.

"Too quiet," Thane said, coming back from one of his runs, his face grim. "The kind of quiet that means something's making the quiet. We're close. Let me get ahead and look before we walk into it."

Nageru nodded.

"Look at the floor first," he said, because he had heard the dwarf say it once, and Thane gave him a short surprised glance and then a shorter approving one, and went ahead into the failing light.

---

The ford was not empty.

Aron brought the truth of it back near dawn, coming in low and fast out of the gray. He laid it out for the leaders in quick clean strokes, drawing in the dirt with the point of a dagger.

"A hundred at least," he said. "Tieflings. Encamped around the fort and the crossing." He marked the ground. "Here's the good news and here's the bad. The terrain lets us get close — there's cover all the way in, broken ground and old walls, we can come up on them unseen. That's good." The dagger moved. "The bad: the ford's the only crossing. Water on both flanks, deep and fast. We can't pincer them. Whatever we do, we do it from the one direction."

He sat back on his heels.

"They're not paying much attention," he went on. "Lazy. They've held this crossing a while and nothing's come at them. But here's the thing." The dagger tapped twice. "Their commanders are *obvious*. Standing out in the open, giving orders, no thought to it. And they're vulnerable. You take the head off this fast, the body might not know what to do."

Nageru considered the dirt-map. He considered the one direction. He considered the hundred.

"Go back," he said to Aron Kir, who had carried the first word. "Have Thane watch them a while longer. Two hours. I want to know what they do when they think no one is looking."

Aron nodded and went.

The two hours gave them the thing that decided it.

Aron came back at a hard quiet pace, and his lost-too-much face had something in it that Nageru had not seen there before — not fear, the man was past fear, but a grimness gone personal. "They've dragged a prisoner out," he said. "A human. Out of one of the houses. Their leaders have him." A pause. "It looks bad."

Nageru stood.

He did not raise his voice. He never raised his voice. But he motioned, one short economical motion, and the army began to ready itself, because the watching was over.

Caleth had moved up beside Irabeth, and Nageru heard the half-elf give the shape of it in his measured voice. "Take the army and attack from the road," Caleth said to her. "Down the one direction, hard and fast — that is the body. Give us a little time to get into position first. We will go for the leaders, and for the prisoners."

Irabeth nodded. She was a commander now, reluctantly, and she commanded. "Give us a signal," she said. "When you're in position. A flaming arrow into the sky — I'll see it, and the column comes."

So it was set.

The army drew up and went forward onto the road, down the one direction, settling into the broken country short of the camp where the walls and the failing light would hide a column from lazy eyes. Nageru watched it go. Then he turned the other way, and he and Korroc and Caleth went into the broken ground on foot, low and quiet, working forward through the low rises toward the place where Thane had been watching since before dawn.

They found him where the cover ran out, lying along a fallen stretch of wall with his bow beside him, and he did not startle when they came up — he had marked them coming, the way he marked everything. He did not waste words. He pointed. The camp lay below in the gray, the tieflings moving slack and unworried about their fires. He pointed again, to the open ground at the center where the leaders stood plain as Aron had said, careless, and to the bound human shape on the ground between them.

"Still alive," Thane murmured. "For now."

Nageru looked, and let his breath go slow, and the still water in him went still around the shape of what was about to happen. He set himself in the broken ground at the camp's edge, where the run to the nearest officer was clean. Korroc settled in beside him behind the shield. Caleth found his angle. They were in position.

Nageru gave Thane the nod.

The arrow went up.

It climbed out of the gray into the lightening sky, trailing fire, and the tieflings in the camp saw it — and a confused murmur went through them, and they turned toward it, toward the arc of it, toward the place it fell, *away* from the road. Away from the army. A signal meant for Irabeth's eyes alone, and it moved the enemy's in the bargain.

Horns blew.

And the cavalry came down the road.

---

The charge hit the camp like a hammer hits a flaw.

Nageru heard it before he saw it from his place in the broken ground at the camp's edge — the rolling thunder of the column coming down the one direction, the only direction, the direction the tieflings had just turned their backs on. Paladins at the front, lances down, the standards snapping. The tieflings spun back toward the road and found death already arriving, and the confusion of it was total — a hundred creatures who had held a lazy crossing for weeks discovering all at once that the war had come to them.

That was when Nageru moved.

He came out of the broken ground at a flat run, barefoot and silent, the white wraps the only thing about him the gray light caught. An officer stood at the camp's edge with his back half-turned, shouting orders into the chaos, his attention all on the road and the cavalry and the ruin of his lazy command.

Nageru reached him before the man knew the ground had changed.

He left his feet in the last stride — a flying kick, the whole weight of him gathered behind the heel, every mile of the road and every hour of the Sunken Fist's training folded into the single line of it — and his heel took the officer beneath the ear, and the tiefling crumpled where he stood, dead or as near to it as made no difference, and did not get a word of warning out before he fell.

The Righteous Medal of Agility was iron over the place where the breath went down. *Given,* the Queen had said, *to a soldier who delivers a death-blow to a demon before the demon is given the chance to act.* The medal was barely two days old and Nageru had already earned it twice over.

The camp came apart into fighting.

Caleth was a half-step behind him, and Nageru felt the cold gather before he saw it — the half-elf raising a hand, the air going bitter and crystalline, and then the fireball that was not fire at all but its inversion, a roaring bloom of cold that took four tieflings clustered near a watch-fire. The heat went *out* of them; frost burst across them in a single white instant; two fell where they stood and the other two reeled away screaming with their hands gone the color of old bone.

A crossbow snapped from somewhere in the chaos and a bolt grazed Caleth across the arm — and Nageru, three strides on, saw the strange thing happen that he had seen before and would never quite be easy with. The wound on Caleth's arm closed even as it opened. And across the camp, Korroc, just emerging from the broken ground with his shield up, jerked as a matching line of red sprang open on his own forearm. The life-bond. The thread the dwarf carried in his chest, drinking his brothers' wounds into his own body. Korroc grunted, glanced at the new cut, and came on with the shield up and the hammer ready, the wound already nothing to him.

Thane was not in the press at all. Nageru caught one glimpse of him taking a circuitous route along the camp's edge — the knife's path, the path that put a man behind the fight rather than in it — and then the dwarf was gone into the cover and the chaos, where he did his best work.

The tieflings fought with trepidation. Nageru marked it even through the speed of his own body. They were not warriors of conviction; they were creatures who had held an easy crossing and had not expected to bleed for it, and the surprise had gone into their arms and made them slow and made them miss. Strikes came at the four of them and went wide — a sword that swung a foot short, a spear that skidded off broken stone, a dagger that found nothing but air. The enemy's fear was doing half the party's work for them.

But not all of it. One tiefling, steadier than the rest, came in low on Korroc and got his blade past the shield and struck the dwarf hard across the hip, a real wound, and Korroc staggered and set his feet and did not go down. The dwarf moved past him with the shield up, planting himself the way he planted himself, a wall in the chaos, drawing the enemy's eyes onto the one of them best built to take them.

A tiefling near Thane's hidden path saw the dwarf there in the cover — saw him, looked at him, and *discounted* him, the way the careless always discounted Thane, and turned away toward Nageru instead, which was the last mistake the creature's carelessness would let it make. Nageru took its attention the way he took all gifts, gratefully, and turned it into the line of a strike.

Caleth had Radiance in his hands — the glaive's silver length catching the gray dawn — and he stabbed, clean and measured, the patient weapon for the patient man, and took a tiefling through the chest. Another bolt found him, more solid this time, low in the side, and across the camp Korroc took the matching hurt without a sound and absorbed it into the wall that he had become.

Nageru flanked the army's leader.

He had found the man by the same sense that found all centers — the tiefling who stood where the others looked when they were afraid, a broad red-skinned creature with a heavy blade and a band of office across his brow. Korroc was already on him from the front, the hammer working, the shield catching. Nageru came up opposite, taking the second angle the way he and the dwarf had learned to take it without a word, and his fists came down on the leader's flank — once, twice — slamming home, demonic blood coming up where they landed.

The leader was strong. He stayed up. He turned on Korroc and brought his heavy blade around in a killing arc, and Korroc — Korroc did the thing he did, the thing that Nageru had watched a dozen times and not stopped admiring. He did not parry the blade with his hammer. He took it on the shield, a small precise turn of the wrist that knocked the sword aside and out of line, and in the instant the leader was open, off-balance, his weapon committed to a stroke that had gone nowhere —

Nageru came in with a kick.

It took the leader in the center of him, under the band of office, the whole gathered weight of the strike, and the broad red creature folded around it and crumpled and did not rise.

The fight went on a while after that, but the going-on was a formality. Caleth slid sideways through space in the way he did — there one instant, behind a tiefling the next, a dimensional step too quick for the eye — and put Radiance through the creature twice before it understood it had been flanked by a man who had not walked. Korroc laid a hand on his own wounds, the warmth running into the worst of them, and then turned and brought the hammer down on another. Thane's dagger flashed out of the cover and a tiefling collapsed clutching its throat, never having seen the knife that opened it.

Two tieflings flanked Korroc, one of them slipping behind him, finding his back — and Nageru was there before the blade could fall, coming in behind the backstabber, his fists hammering down on the creature's spine, dropping it. Korroc laid hands a second time, on himself, and then sent a wave of healing rolling out across the four of them, and the small fires of a dozen wounds dropped to aches and the aches to memory, and Nageru breathed the warmth in like hot broth on a cold morning.

Swords bounced off Korroc's plate. Caleth ducked under a wild swing and answered it. Nageru hit the backstabber a second time, both fists, and it collapsed and stayed down.

A tiefling tried to maneuver on Thane and found, too late, that it had stepped instead into the reach of Caleth's glaive, and Radiance took it before it could correct.

Then there was one on Korroc — and the dwarf's hammer came down and sent it sprawling — and one on Thane, daggers flashing at the dwarf and missing, and Nageru's fist connected with the creature's jaw, and Caleth stabbed over Thane's shoulder, and Korroc moved up absorbing the last of the group's wounds into his own indestructible patience, and Thane, finally, cornered the last of them against an old wall and finished it with both blades.

Silence, where there had been a hundred.

And the army's fight, Nageru saw when he turned to it, was already over. The paladins had overrun the tiefling formations in the first minutes — the surprise, the charge down the one direction, the enemy's eyes pulled the wrong way by an arrow. What remained was clean-up, the grim ordered business of an army that had won. Nageru stood in the quieting camp and let his breath come down and looked at the place where the leaders had stood, and where they stood no longer.

---

The buildings were locked.

Thane found the keys on the leaders' bodies — a ring of them on the broad red creature's belt — and worked through the doors one at a time, and Nageru went in to the first of them.

The dark inside smelled of fear and worse. A handful of survivors were chained to the walls — human, gaunt, wounded, the look on their faces the look of people who had stopped counting the hours they had left and were now being asked to start again. Nageru went to them and knelt and put his hands to the chains, and Thane came behind with the keys, and they got them loose, and the survivors wept or did not weep and told the four of them, in broken pieces, what had been happening here.

The attack had come at midnight, days ago. The leader — the broad red creature, a tiefling named Uthniel — had been taking them one at a time, one each hour, for sacrifice. The math of it sat in the room like a held breath. *One each hour.* The arrow Thane had loosed had not only moved the enemy's eyes. It had come in time.

"You can make it back to Kenabres," Nageru told them, when they were free and steadier. "We will give you food. The road south is hard but it is clear; the army before us cleared it." He paused. "Can you walk it?"

They could, they said. They would. And then the eldest of them, a woman with iron-gray hair and a soldier's old bearing under the wounds, caught Nageru's wrist before he turned away.

"They talked," she said. "The tieflings. They didn't think we understood enough to matter." Her grip tightened. "There's a bigger army north of here. Keeper's Canyon. *Bigger* — the way they said it, the way they laughed about it. Bigger than this. You should know, before you go on."

Nageru nodded slowly.

"My thanks," he said. "We will know it now. That is worth more than you can guess."

He filed Keeper's Canyon in the place where he filed all such things, and the Archon weight stirred at it behind his ribs. *These also,* it said, in the way it spoke, which was no words.

---

They buried their own dead with rites that evening, and burned the tieflings, and the smoke of the two fires went up separate into the gray, the way the two kinds of dead should always go up separate.

The leaders gathered after, over the Queen's map spread on a wagon's tail, and the talk turned to the thing the gray-haired woman had warned them of.

Aravashniel found it on the map first. The wizard could read a map again now, and he did it with the hunger of a man who had thought he never would, his finger tracing the line of their march and stopping on a notch in the hills to the north. "Keeper's Canyon," he said. "Here." His finger did not move. "That is in our path. We are heading directly for it."

"Of course we are," Korroc said, to no one.

Irabeth leaned over the map, the commander's frown on her. "We'll scout it when we get there," she said. "Properly, the way we did this one. But we lost men today — a dozen, near enough — and the soldiers are spent. We could use a rest before we walk into something the tieflings thought was worth laughing about."

Caleth had been quiet, studying the line of march. Now he spoke. "The army should stay here a full day," he said. "Rest, refit, bury the dead properly. But the scout team moves forward now. We learn what is at Keeper's Canyon before the column commits to it. We do not walk a tired army blind into a thing bigger than what we just fought."

Nageru nodded. It was sound. The waiting, and the moving, in the right order.

It was Horgus who surprised them again. The nobleman emerged from the supply-train's edge, hesitant, clearly uncertain whether his voice belonged in this council — and then deciding it did. "We have messenger pigeons," he said. "Part of the army's stores. I packed them; I pack everything." A pause; the old defensiveness, then past it. "We could send word to the Queen. Tell her what we found here. Tell her what may be waiting north."

Nageru nodded again, slower. The man had brought the company a thing none of the warriors had thought to bring. "Yes," he said. "A wise thing, friend."

"I'll write the message," Aravashniel said, and there was a brightness in the wizard's voice that had not been there before the morning his eye came back. "I'll start now, while the light holds. Two birds — one to carry the warning, one against the chance the first is lost."

Thane and his team left within the hour — Thane, Anevia, Aron Kir, north into the dusk toward Keeper's Canyon, the knife's path again, ahead of the army that would follow when it had slept.

---

They reached Keeper's Canyon early the next morning, and what Thane found there made his heart fall, and Nageru read it in the dwarf's face before a word was said when the scout came back to meet the column.

"Two hundred tieflings," Thane said. His voice was flat the way it went flat when the news was bad and he did not want the badness to leak into it. "And a hundred dretches with them. Demons. The real thing." He let that land. "And it's *organized.* This isn't a lazy crossing. They're arrayed. Someone's commanding them."

"Who," Nageru said.

Thane's jaw worked. "An incubus," he said. "A greater demon — a leader, not a foot-soldier. Out in the open, hanging back, the way the smart ones do." A pause, and the next part came harder. "And a human. In Iomedae's armor." His mouth thinned to nothing. "Full plate, the sword-and-sunburst, the whole of it. Walking among them like he belongs there. An imposter, a traitor, or worse — one of theirs, dressed in our faith to mock it."

A silence went around the leaders.

Nageru felt the Archon weight come up cold behind his ribs at *a human in Iomedae's armor.* It was the same wrongness he had felt at the Blackwing Library, weeks ago and a world ago — a man who had taken the shape of order to commit a chaos. There were few things his blood found more offensive. He let the offense settle into the place where the breath went down, and he did not let it become haste, because haste was how the offended died.

Aron Kir rode back hard to bring the army up. The column came on through the day, and in the afternoon they crested the last of the rolling ground, and Nageru saw, for the first time, the Worldwound itself.

It lay to the west.

He had crossed a thousand miles toward the storm it had loosed. But he had never stood and looked at the thing whole, and the looking stopped his breath for a moment in a way that few things did. The sky over it was red — not the red of sunset but a sick low red that did not move and did not mean weather. The land beneath it was barren past barrenness, a desolation that went to the horizon and did not soften, a wound in the world that had been bleeding for a hundred years. His blood, which had pressed flat under broken Kenabres and stirred under the quiet of the empty land, now *roared* — a single deep note of wrongness so vast that it was almost silence, the way the loudest sounds become a kind of silence at their center.

Beside him, Aravashniel had gone still, his newly-mended eyes fixed on the red horizon.

"I remember when that land was lush," the wizard said quietly. "And green. And full of wild magic." His face, which had been bright since the morning of his healing, grew somber, the brightness going out of it like a lamp shuttered against a draft. "I walked it, once. Before."

Nageru did not answer. There was no answer that would have been larger than the silence, and the silence was the right size for it.

Caleth met Aron first when the scout rode in, and took the report, and Nageru, near enough to hear, caught the half-elf's quiet oath at the end of it — *saint's mercy* — and then the thing Caleth said after, low, more to himself than to anyone, the thing that was the whole of their situation laid flat.

"Nothing we can do," Caleth said, "but move forward."

---

There was no easy way at Keeper's Canyon, and Thane was the one to say it.

"No advantage in the terrain," he reported, when the scouts and the leaders had gathered in the cover of the last ridge. "It's flat. Open. The moment the army moves up, they'll be seen — there's no coming up on this one unaware. And the canyon itself is a choke. One way through."

Anevia, lying flat at the ridge's edge with a small mirror in her hand, caught the morning light with it and flashed a signal back down the line — *halt* — and the approaching head of the column stopped where it stood, out of the enemy's sight by a margin Nageru did not like.

The leaders put their heads together in the cover.

"The dretches will shrug off a lot of what we throw," Irabeth said grimly. "Demon flesh. Common steel barely marks them." Then, a half-beat later, the commander's mind already turning toward the answer: "But our paladins carry cold iron. The smite will tell on them. The dretches won't be the problem the incubus is."

Thane was watching the enemy through a gap in the rocks, and Nageru saw his face do the thing it did when the news kept getting worse. "There's the incubus," the dwarf murmured. "Hanging back, like I said. Dretch guards around him." A pause. "This just keeps getting more and more fun."

"Look," Anevia said, low, at his shoulder. "The human."

Thane found him, and cursed — short, dwarvish, detailed.

Korroc turned to Irabeth. "Ideas," he said. It was not quite a question. It was a dwarf asking a commander to command.

But Irabeth's eyes went to Aron Kir, and so did Caleth's, both at the same moment, and Nageru understood why. The Queen had said *listen to him; he survived by being right.* Aron had been quiet through all of it, the way the man was quiet through everything, and now he spoke, and the company listened.

"Cold iron handles the dretches," Aron said. His voice was flat, the voice of a man who had spent all his feeling years ago and now had only the facts left. "Smite and steel — the paladins can manage them. So they're not the danger. The incubus is the danger, and not because of what it can do in a fight." He looked at each of them. "It's because of what it can do *out* of one. Do not let it teleport away. If it slips, it comes back at the worst possible moment, or it carries word of us somewhere we don't want word carried. That could be very bad. Pin it. Kill it where it stands."

He let that sit.

"And remember this," he went on. "Demons aren't soldiers. They're held together by whatever's leading them. Take the leader, and there's a good chance the rest just — leave. Back to the Worldwound, where they came from. A demon without a master has no reason to die for a crossing." A grim ghost of something crossed the lost face. "So the whole battle might come down to one death. Make it the right one."

Caleth nodded slowly, the strategist's nod, the futures resolving. "We can do that," he said. "The army makes itself a target — a section of it, here, at the mouth of the canyon. The choke works for us if we make them come into it. And while they're committed to the army, while their eyes are on the obvious threat —" his green eyes went to Nageru, to the incubus's distant position "— we move around. And we take the incubus."

It was set. The waiting was over, and the order of moving had been found.

---

The battle began with a section of the army showing itself at the canyon's mouth.

Nageru, moving with the others through the broken ground to the flank, heard the enemy answer it — the tieflings and dretches surging toward the visible threat, the canyon's choke doing exactly what Caleth had said it would do, funneling the demon-host toward the bait. The incubus hung back, as Thane had promised, dretch guards clustered around it, its attention on the army at the mouth.

A small detachment of paladins had come with the party — and split off now, peeling toward the dretches to draw their guards and keep the demon-flesh occupied while the four knights went for the head.

Thane went first, in his own way.

He rose from cover with an arrow already nocked, it held an enchantment — a spell laid along the shaft, a thing that made the head of it gleam with a cold killing purpose, demon slaying. He rose, and he drew, and he loosed it across the open ground at the incubus. Thane's own magic had imbued with a trueness that guided it to the target.

The arrow struck home.

The demon screamed — a sound that scraped at the edges of Nageru's hearing, the wrong-shape made audible. But it straightened under the scream, and Nageru saw the killing purpose of the arrow break against the thing's nature, the bane in it resisted, turned, only half its power finding purchase. The incubus was wounded. The incubus was not undone.

Caleth's hand came up, and the bitter cold gathered, and his inverted fireball roared out across the canyon floor and bloomed among the tieflings nearest the bait — two of them dropping where the heat was torn out of them, others reeling, screaming, frost-white.

Crossbows answered. Bolts came screaming across the open ground, past Nageru, past Caleth, the air around the two of them suddenly full of the snap and hum of them.

And Nageru moved.

He crossed seventy feet in what felt, to him, like a single held breath — the broken ground a blur beneath his bare feet, the canyon tilting past, the white of his wraps the only thing on him the red light caught. He had spent his life learning to be still. He had only lately learned what the stillness was *for.* It was for this — for the moment the patience ended and the whole of it released at once, the thunder that had waited finally arriving, all of it, in one direction, at one thing.

The imposter was in his path.

The man in Iomedae's stolen plate turned as Nageru came through — turned and struck, a mailed blow that caught Nageru across the shoulder as he passed, a line of fire across the meat of him. A grazing wound. Nageru did not stop. He let the pain pass into the breath and carry on through, because the imposter was not the target and the target was ten strides past him.

He reached the incubus.

And his fist came up.

There was no flourish in it. There was only the line — the single line his whole life had been training, the strike that was a prayer in motion, every mile of the road and every hour of the Sunken Fist and the cold weight of an iron medal folded into the one blow. His fist slammed into the center of the incubus, beneath the wound Thane's arrow had opened, and demonic blood splattered out across the red ground, and the thing — the greater demon, the leader, the thing Aron had said the whole battle would come down to —

The incubus collapsed.

Extinguished. Snuffed from the plane like a candle pinched between two fingers, the wrong-shape of it simply *gone* from Nageru's blood, leaving a clean hole in the air where a vast wrongness had been.

One hit.

Nageru lowered his arm and breathed, and the breath went down through the bronze of him into the bones, and the medal of Agility was iron over the place where the breath went down, and the Queen's voice came to him from two mornings and a lifetime ago: *I am told you will do it again.*

He had done it again.

But the imposter was not done with him. The man in Iomedae's armor had followed Nageru through, and the incubus's death had not stopped him — had only sharpened the fury in him, the personal fury of a creature that had chosen its evil rather than been born to it.

He hit Nageru hard.

The blow came in while Nageru's arm was still down from the killing strike — a heavy crashing impact that took him across the side and rocked him a full step sideways. The man in the holy armor was strong, and trained, and he had already tasted Nageru's shoulder once on the way past and was not afraid of the monk who had just dropped a greater demon in a single blow.

The battle closed around them.

A bolt found Caleth and the half-elf staggered and stayed up. Thane's dagger took a tiefling that had come too close, and the dwarf melted back into the chaos. Korroc charged in heavy and slammed a tiefling off its feet with the hammer, and planted himself, the wall arriving where the wall was needed.

A tiefling caught Thane a glancing stab and Thane swore and answered it. Another nicked Nageru across the arm and he absorbed it into the breath. Caleth slid sideways through space again, appearing behind a tiefling that had been working on Thane, and Radiance came down and the creature folded. A tiefling tried Korroc and found only shield.

And Nageru turned on the imposter.

He let his hands fill.

It was not the silver scale — that charge was spent, had been spent in the Garrison weeks ago. It was the deeper thing, the thing his masters had named and his blood carried, the conviction that order is owed to the innocent made manifest in the body. His fists kindled — a clean white-gold fire running up them from the knuckles, the celestial fire of the Archon line, the smite of a man sworn against exactly this. A man in the armor of the Inheritor, mocking Her, walking Her sign through a host of demons.

"Evil," Nageru said, "must die."

He did not raise his voice. He never raised his voice. But the canyon seemed to narrow around the three quiet words, and he stepped in past the imposter's guard, and his burning fist found the center of the man's chest — fist to plate, the holy fire going *through* the holy armor as though the armor were a lie, which it was — and the man in Iomedae's stolen plate sprawled backward onto the red ground and did not get up.

A tiefling sneaked behind Nageru in the instant after and stabbed him in the back — a real wound, deep, between the shoulder-blades, and Nageru's breath caught on it the way Thane's wound had caught on his breath in the back room weeks ago. He rode it. He did not turn yet.

Caleth turned for him. The half-elf took a step back and Radiance lashed out at the length of its reach and the tiefling at Nageru's back crumpled with the glaive through its ribs.

Then Nageru turned, and the world narrowed to the line of his strikes, and the fire was still on his fists.

He put two fists into one tiefling and it dropped. He pivoted on the same heel and hit another, and it dropped too. Thane connected out of the chaos, both daggers in, and a third tiefling fell. There was one left against Korroc, and the dwarf — Korroc set his feet and drew on the stone, the way he drew on it at the worst moments, the Stonelord's gift coming up through his bones into the haft, and the hammer came down, and there was a sound of bone giving way, and then there was no one left against Korroc at all.

The four of them stood in a sudden quiet, breathing, the red light over them.

And the wider battle, Nageru saw when he lifted his eyes to it, was breaking the enemy's way the moment the incubus had fallen. Aron had been right. The dretches, their leader gone, fought with no purpose and less heart; the army's cold iron told on them; and when the head of the host lay extinguished and the imposter sprawled and dead, the demons did the thing Aron had said they would do. They scattered. They turned west, toward the red horizon, toward the Worldwound that had birthed them, with no master left to make them die for a canyon. The tieflings ran. The paladins charged the breaking field and pursued, and the canyon that had been laughed about by a hundred dead tieflings at Vilareth Ford was theirs.

It had come down, in the end, to one death.

Nageru had made it the right one.

---

The aftermath had a question in it, and the question fell to Nageru.

Aron Kir, surveying the won field with his lost-too-much eyes, said the practical thing. "The camp should be looted," he said. "Stores, weapons, anything that can be carried. An army runs on what it can take."

And the paladins balked.

Nageru saw it ripple through them — the Mendevian host's own, the ones who had charged the field, the ones in clean tabards who had marched a thousand miles for the Inheritor. To loot the dead was a thing below their vows. They had crossed a continent to be better than the enemy, and looting a demon-camp felt, to them, like the first small step down toward the thing they had come to destroy. They went quiet and stiff, and the question hung in the red air, and it was a true question and not a foolish one, and Nageru honored it for that.

He moved up among them.

He did not raise his voice. He let the stillness he had carried his whole life stand around the words, so that the words landed in the quiet of it.

"There are needs in front of us," Nageru said. "There is a citadel to take, and a banner to recover, and a hundred miles of the Worldwound's edge to cross before we reach either, and we will cross it on what we carry. And there are needs behind us — in Kenabres, where half a city lives on a hole in the ground that will not last the winter." He looked at them, one face and then another, the paladins who had come so far to be good. "What lies in this camp was taken from the living. It does no honor to the dead to leave it to rot for the crows. It does honor to the living to put it to their benefit."

The paladins were quiet, hearing it.

"We will only do what we must," Nageru said, "to ensure that we can accomplish our mission, and take Drezen. No more than that. Not for greed. For the road, and for the city, and for the people who are counting on us to arrive." A pause. "There is no vow against feeding the hungry with what the wicked no longer need."

The paladins looked at one another. And then, slowly, they nodded — not grudgingly, but the way men nod when they have heard a thing they recognize as true and are glad to have had it said for them, so that the doing of it could be a thing of conscience rather than a thing of shame. They set to the work. They did it cleanly, and they did it for the road and the city, and Nageru watched them do it and let it become one more piece of order in the world.

The Archon weight settled in his ribs, at the bottom of him, the way a stone settles to the bottom of a riverbed when it has found its place.

He had spent his life being the still point — the one whose breathing did not change while the world tilted, the one who waited. He understood, standing in the won canyon with the red sky over him and the paladins bending to the work he had given them words for, that the waiting had become something else. He had spoken first to the Queen. He had recruited the company. He had asked for a wizard's sight to be returned. He had given the moral weight of the company a voice, and the company had answered to it. The still water had risen, and the thunder had spoken, and both of them had been heard.

*Stillness is not the absence of motion,* his masters had said. *Stillness is the readiness for it.*

He was ready. He had been ready for a long time without knowing it. He knew it now.

Irabeth came to him then, and to Caleth, across the field, wiping her blade. Her face had the grim satisfaction of a commander who had spent men and not spent too many.

"We lost nine," she said. "In all of that — the canyon, the incubus, the dretches. Nine." She looked out over the field, the breaking enemy, the pursuing paladins, the won ground. "I'd say it was a successful day."

Nageru nodded. He did not speak. He bent to the work, the way he bent to all work, and let the count settle into the place where the breath went down. Nine. Nine households that would hear the worst, somewhere, in time. Nine rites to say. He did not let it become small in him. But he did not let it become despair, either, because nine, against what they had faced, was the count of a thing done as well as such things could be done.

But Caleth stopped.

The half-elf had been searching a fallen tiefling, methodical as ever, and at Irabeth's words he straightened, and he turned, and he looked at her with an expression Nageru had not seen on him before — a kind of disbelief, but a soft one, the disbelief of a man who had braced for a far worse number and been handed a mercy he did not know how to hold.

"*Only* nine?" Caleth said.

And a small smile crossed the half-elf's measured face — rare, real, the smile of a man who had spent so long planning for catastrophe that a survivable day caught him off his guard. It was there and then it was folded away, the way Caleth folded everything away, but Nageru had seen it, and it was the best thing he had seen all that long red day.

Caleth bent back to his searching.

Nageru stood a moment longer in the won canyon, under the wrong red sky, with the Worldwound on the western horizon and the road to Drezen running north through all of it, and he breathed.

The breath went down.

The breath came up.

And somewhere ahead of them, beyond the red edge of the world, a dwarven-built citadel held a banner that had been Iomedae's own, and a marilith's absent army left it only moderately guarded, and the thunder that had learned at last what it was for turned over once more, and this time it did not go back to sleep.
