# Style Guide — Wrath of the Righteous Blog

This document defines the prose rules for the campaign blog. Read it before writing any chapter.

## Core Style Identity

The prose blends:

- **Brandon Sanderson's** detailed worldbuilding and character interiority — internal monologue that reveals how the POV character sees the world, with specific concrete metaphors drawn from their background (Korroc thinks in forge-imagery; Caleth thinks in artistic-observational terms; Thane thinks in tactical reads; Nageru thinks in still-water and breath).
- **Robert E. Howard's** visceral, rhythmic combat — never gloss a fight. Use short, punchy sentences during high-action moments. Translate clipped game notes ("Thane stabbed, hit") into cinematic blow-by-blow action.
- **Joe Abercrombie's** POV-by-stakes and grim humor — characters quip at each other, complain, swear. The world is hard but the characters are alive in it.
- **Patrick Rothfuss's** attention to prose rhythm — sentences breathe. Vary length. Use repetition deliberately. Sometimes a single short sentence is its own paragraph.

## POV Rules

- **Deep third-person limited.** We are inside one character's head for the entire chapter unless explicitly told otherwise.
- **Interior voice colors the prose itself**, not just the dialogue. The narration sees the world the way the POV character sees it.
- The POV character's training, fears, hopes, and history shape what they notice and how they describe it. Korroc notices construction quality. Caleth notices people's body language and the iconography on their armor. Thane notices exits, weapons, and patterns of wear on a floor. Nageru notices breathing and stillness.
- The POV character does NOT know things they couldn't know. No omniscient cheats. If Caleth doesn't know what S. V. stands for, neither does the narration.

## POV Voice Calibration

**Establish the voice early — then trust it.**

A character's interior register (Nageru's stillness, Thane's tactical reads, Korroc's forge-and-stone imagery) should be planted clearly in the chapter's opening pages. Once it's established, it becomes an *undercurrent*, not a drumbeat. The reader now knows whose head they're in. Stop proving it.

Think of it like grain in wood: the grain gives the piece its character and is genuinely present throughout — but you're not supposed to stop and notice it on every plank. When the craftsman overworks it, the reader starts watching the technique instead of experiencing the story.

**Signs you've gone too far:**

- The POV character's signature trait (Nageru's breath-rhythm, Thane's sign-reading, Korroc's stone-sense) appears in more than one out of every three or four paragraphs.
- The chapter's prose *pace* is being controlled by the character's quirk rather than the scene's action. (Nageru's stillness making entire pages feel still, rather than *flavoring* pages that themselves have momentum.)
- A reader could point to a specific paragraph and say "there's the Nageru thing again" — meaning the trait has become predictable rather than natural.

**The corrective:** once you've established the voice, let scenes be scenes. A tense fight is a tense fight — Nageru's stillness shows in *how he enters* the fight, not in every sentence of it. Thane notices exits and tells-of-wear *when that noticing matters to the scene*, not in every room he steps into. Let the character's nature shape their *decisions and reactions*, not every line of prose description.

**What worked:** Korroc's chapters land because the dwarven imagery appears when the *moment calls for it* and then gets out of the way. The story moves, the voice flavors it, the reader never feels managed.

## Combat Writing Rules

- **Do not gloss combat.** Even small skirmishes get full cinematic treatment.
- Use **short, punchy sentences** during the peak of action. Sentence fragments are fine. Single-word paragraphs are fine for impact.
- **Slow down at the climax.** The moment of a killing blow, a near-death save, or a turning point should be rendered in slow motion with sensory detail.
- Translate game mechanics into in-fiction reality:
  - "Korroc cast cure light wounds" → "A wash of golden warmth rolled through him."
  - "Caleth used dimensional step" → "The world *folded* — that small cool sensation of a half-second of nowhere — and his feet came down on the far lip."
  - "Nageru activated his scale" → "A soft silver light flared from Nageru's belt and ran up his arms to his fists."
- **Misses and mistakes count.** When a character misses an attack, that's a story beat too — show their frustration, their adjustment, the cost.
- **Wounds have weight.** When a character takes damage, the reader should feel it. Don't have characters shrug off serious injury without comment.

## Dialogue Rules

- Characters have **distinct speech patterns**:
  - **Korroc:** Dwarven cadence, "aye," occasional Dwarven swears, blunt and warm.
  - **Caleth:** Formal but never stiff; uses "saint's mercy" as his go-to mild oath; tends toward measured complete sentences.
  - **Thane:** Shorter than Korroc, drier, more guarded. Calls Korroc "cousin." Uses dwarven mannerisms but lighter than Korroc's.
  - **Nageru:** Quiet, formal, occasionally cryptic. Speaks slightly stilted, like someone whose first language wasn't Common. Uses titles ("brother," "friend").
  - **Anevia:** Sharp, direct, unsentimental. Doesn't suffer fools. Affectionate but never sappy.
  - **Horgus:** Snobbish, complains, slowly being humanized. Uses class-marked diction.
- **Avoid said-bookisms in excess.** "Said" and "asked" disappear; "growled," "muttered," "hissed" should be used sparingly and for genuine reasons.
- **Action beats often work better than dialogue tags.** Let the character do something physical that shows who's speaking.

## Pacing Rules

- **A chapter is a session, not an arc.** One session = one chapter. Don't try to compress multiple sessions or stretch one session across multiple chapters.
- **Within a chapter, alternate registers:** quiet interior moments, action sequences, dialogue exchanges, brief flashbacks, and longer reflective passages. Don't run one register for too long.
- **Open with a hook.** First scene of every chapter should establish the POV character's interior state and the chapter's emotional center.
- **End with a cliffhanger or a moment of heavy intrigue.** Even quiet endings should have a sense of unresolved weight.
- **Use scene breaks (`---`) generously.** Don't make readers wade through walls of prose. White space is a tool.

## Things to Avoid

- **Overwrought language.** Don't reach for "scintillating" when "bright" works. The prose has muscle, not lace.
- **Excessive description of armor/gear/setting at the expense of action.** A line or two when the character notices something, not paragraphs.
- **Modern phrasing in dialogue.** No "okay," no "yeah" (Korroc gets "aye"; others avoid it entirely), no "guys," no contemporary slang.
- **Telling the reader how characters feel.** Show it through action, dialogue, and the texture of the interior monologue.
- **Repetition of established traits.** Don't remind us every chapter that Korroc wears plate. We know. New chapters add new dimensions to existing characters, not restatements.
- **Overworking the POV voice.** A character's signature register (Nageru's stillness, Thane's tactical reads) should flavor the prose, not dominate every paragraph. Establish it early, then let it become an undercurrent. See *POV Voice Calibration* above.
- **Backstory dumps.** Backstory enters through specific in-the-moment triggers — a sight, a smell, a phrase — not block infodumps.

## Specific Tonal Notes

- **Faith is real.** When a character prays, when a god answers, when a holy place is consecrated, treat it with weight. This is a setting where the divine is present and active. Don't undercut it with irony.
- **The Worldwound is genuinely terrifying.** Demons are not monsters-of-the-week. Each one is a violation of reality.
- **Grim humor is welcome.** Korroc complaining about heights, Thane's deadpan one-liners, Caleth's wry Spireborn restraint — these are essential to keep the chapters from drowning in their own darkness.
- **Tenderness is welcome.** When characters show care for each other — Korroc healing Nageru, Caleth giving Aravashniel the restoration potion, Thane returning a nod to Anevia — let the moments land.

## Length Targets

- Typical chapter: **4,000-8,000 words.**
- A particularly action-heavy or emotionally weighty session might run longer.
- A quieter "downtime" session might run shorter.
- Don't pad. Don't truncate. Let the session's content dictate length.
- If you hit an output limit before finishing, stop at a logical scene break and signal "continue" to the user.

## Song Writing Rules (Suno)

These constraints apply every time an Opus sub-agent writes a Suno song.

**HARD CHARACTER LIMITS:**
- **Lyrics box: 1,000 characters maximum, spaces included.** This is firm — Suno will cut anything over.
- **Style box: ~800 characters.** Keep it tight.

**Section tag discipline:** Tags like `[Verse 1]`, `[Refrain]`, `[Bridge]`, `[Outro]`, `[Spoken]` count toward the 1,000. Keep them short — just the label. No inline descriptions inside the lyrics box (e.g., NOT `[Verse 1 - quiet baritone, close-mic'd, cello underneath]`). All instrumentation details, tempo, voice type, and register instructions belong in the **style box only**.

**Single register.** Suno cannot handle four-stage builds or genre transitions. Pick one register and stay in it start to finish.

**Target runtime: under 2 minutes 30 seconds.** State this explicitly at the top of the style box: *"Target: under 2 minutes 30 seconds total. No instrumental padding between sections."*

**No story recap in lyrics.** Lyrics are interior state, not plot summary. If a line could appear in a chapter summary instead of a song, cut it.

**Structure that fits within 1,000 chars:** Three short verses (4 lines each ≈ 30 chars/line), one refrain (4 lines), and a brief spoken closer — all section tags included — comes to roughly 700–800 characters. Leave headroom. When in doubt, cut a verse.

## Final Test

Before submitting a chapter, ask:

1. Could a reader who only reads this chapter still tell whose head they're in by paragraph three? (POV voice)
2. Does at least one combat or tense moment have a sentence under five words for impact? (Howard rhythm)
3. Did at least one quiet moment between characters land with weight? (Tenderness)
4. Is there a moment of unresolved intrigue at the end? (Hook for next chapter)
5. Does the chapter add something to the POV character's interior arc, not just record events? (Interiority)

If yes to all five, ship it.
