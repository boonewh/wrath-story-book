# Thane

## Class & Build
Dwarf Rogue (**Knife Master** archetype) / Inquisitor. The investigator-blade. Knife Master deepens what his mother already taught him: the dagger is not a backup weapon. The dagger is *the* weapon. Everything else is the noise around the blade.

## Appearance
Stocky dwarf in a worn chain shirt over leather. Twin daggers at his belt — the right blade his mother's, the left blade his father's wedding-knife, reversed in his off-hand for parries. Carries a shortbow. Wrist-sheath for a third blade (his own habit, not his mother's teaching). Beard shorter and less ornamented than Korroc's. Weathered face. Sharp watchful eyes that catalogue exits and weapons the way Caleth's catalogue iconography.

**Age — IMPORTANT:** Thane is **young for a dwarf** — the same generation as his cousin Korroc (who is 51), in his prime, **not old.** Do NOT write him as aged. His "weathered" look comes from hard living — the Mendev camps, the Kenabres gutters — not from years, and his banked grief comes from losing his father young, not from age.

## Voice
- Drier than Korroc, more guarded
- Shorter sentences when on edge
- "Aye" — but used more sparingly than Korroc
- Calls Korroc "cousin" always — never "Korroc"
- Calls Caleth "half-elf" or "Spireborn" affectionately; "paladin" when serious
- Calls Anevia by name; never "love" or anything affectionate
- Capable of deadpan one-liners ("Squishy")
- Dwarven swears in stress, especially when picking a lock that's fighting back
- **Stonevein rule:** *"No Stonevein has ever sheathed a wet blade."* He wipes his daggers after every kill. Always. It is not negotiable.

## Interior Voice for POV Chapters
When Thane is POV, the prose should think in **tactical reads and inherited grief**. He sees the world the way a knife-fighter sees a room: exits, weapons, lines of approach, the wear-patterns on the floor that tell him who passes through. He also carries his father's death constantly, not as a wound but as a *banked flame* — a slow, careful fire he feeds with every demon-cultist he puts down.

Examples:
- *"His father would have read the same signs. His father had taught him to read them, in the brief years before the Worldwound had taken him."*
- *"Look at the floor first, lad. Walls lie. Floors don't."*
- *"Patience is the third dagger you carry. The other two get noticed. The third one wins."*

His prose often surfaces **his mother's lessons** and his father's memory as quiet anchors. He thinks in proverbs his parents taught him — and in one lesson handed to him as a boy by an old Mendevian sergeant whose name he no longer remembers:

> *"You're quick, lad. You see the cracks. The Worldwound needs men who see the cracks. The walls we have. The walls we lose. The men who slip in after the walls fall — those we never have enough of."*

He had hated that lesson at the time. He understands it now.

**Alseta's grammar.** Thane's interior thinking treats demons and cultists as **trespassers**, not just enemies. The Worldwound is an *unauthorized doorway.* Templars hiding in crusader colors are *false credentials at the gate.* He does not articulate this consciously, but the verbs and metaphors he reaches for are doorway-coded: crossing, entry, threshold, breach, latch, hinge, frame, lintel. Use these without ever naming the deity behind them. *(See Faith section for the Alseta canon.)*

## Background — The Stonevein Legacy

Thane was born into a legacy forged in battle and loss. **Both his parents fought in the Fourth Mendevian Crusade** — hardened warriors who had spent their lives staring into the abyss, sometimes literally. The Stonevein name was not given to him as a quiet inheritance. It was earned in blood by the generation before, and pressed into his hand alongside his first dagger.

**Father:** **Thorek Stonevein**, fallen in the Fourth Mendevian Crusade. Once a Skyguard of **Khar-Zadûn**, the legendary Dwarven Sky City torn from the heavens during the first demonic invasions. Thorek spoke of it often — its cloud-piercing towers, its star-forges, its halls where the wind itself sang through runic channels.

He dreamed of reclaiming it.
He dreamed of returning home.

But dreams rarely survive the Worldwound. Thorek fell in the final brutal push of the Fourth Crusade; his body was never recovered. Only his **sky-steel insignia** was returned to the family — a symbol of honor and grief both.

**Mother:** **Helja Stonevein** (named as of Ch 11 — one of the twelve signatories of the reconstructed Drezen blueprints letter, in "her careful upright hand"). Still living (presumed; the campaign hasn't yet revisited her). **A Fourth Crusade veteran herself** — she fought alongside Thorek and came home when he did not. She tried, after, to temper Thane into a different sort of dwarf — softer, perhaps, or at least less sharpened. **But grief forged him harder than any hammer.** When she finally stopped trying, she pressed his first dagger into his palm with her own teaching: *"If you're going to be a knife, son, be a true one."*

**Thane himself:** A child when his father died. Raised in the crusader camps of Mendev. Grew up understanding that demons are **thieves** — they stole his father's dream, his father's home, his father's future. **He never understood how anyone could worship such filth.** The very idea of demon-cultists filled him with a fury that simmered beneath every breath. While other children played at being heroes, Thane trained.

His father had paid an **old Mendevian veteran** to teach him the camp drills as a boy — short-blade work for close quarters, the discipline of finding the seams of armor, the rhythm of *don't try to hit the chest, hit the joints.* He carried those drills into a young adulthood broken roughly into two halves: **three years of formal Mendev camp drills**, and **two years working the back alleys of Kenabres**, learning the practical knife-work the camps could not teach.

His mother handed him his lockpicking discipline as a checklist — a small mental mantra that has carried him into a hundred locked rooms since:

> *"Listen first. Hinges and bolts speak. Look second. Marks at the keyhole tell you who picks it most often. Touch third. The plate around the keyhole tells you if the lock's been forced before."*

Thane has never consciously named this as religious. It is simply *what his mother taught him.* But she handed him **Alseta's liturgy** in the shape of a working checklist. *(See Faith section.)*

He was trained from boyhood as both a blade and a watcher.

**The doubled grief.** Thane's father did not fall alone. **Korroc's father fell in the same crusade, fighting alongside Thorek.** The two were **brothers of the Stonevein line** and shared the same dream of Khar-Zadûn. Thane and Korroc are not just cousins; they are **the surviving sons of a doubled grief**, heirs of the same shattered oath — and **both carry the Stonevein name** (resolved as canon in the post–Chapter 8 downtime interlude). The shared loss is rarely spoken aloud and never lightly.

**All four parents are now named (Ch 11):** **Thorek and Helja Stonevein** (Thane's father and mother), **Borin and Dagna Stonevein** (Korroc's father and mother). All four signed the reconstructed Drezen blueprints letter to Queen Galfrey, seventy years ago — the first four names on the page, each in their own hand. Helja lives; Borin fell with Thorek in the Fourth Crusade; Dagna's status is unknown (do not mark her alive or dead).

## The Eagle Watch Contract
**Critical:** Thane is **NOT a member of the Eagle Watch.** He was **hired** by them, on contract, three months before the campaign opened, to:
- Listen for rumors of demon-cult infiltration in Kenabres
- Identify Templars of the Ivory Labyrinth operating in disguise
- Report findings via a sigil-system to a courier whose name he doesn't know

His handlers are two grim women whose names he was not given. They never met him in person twice.

**Anevia is also on the same contract,** working a different angle — soldiery and marketplaces, where Thane handles gutters and warehouses. They are **partners** — friends, professionals, and absolutely **not romantic.** This is established and non-negotiable.

When Anevia called out for Thane in the dark in Chapter 1, it was because her partner had fallen with her.

**As of end of Chapter 3, Caleth knows.** Thane revealed the contract — and Anevia's partner-not-lover status — quietly in the ritual chamber after the letter was found: *"We're partners, half-elf. If you've been wondering."* Korroc was present and heard the contract part of the reveal. Whether Nageru gathered the full shape from context is unclear; he was nearby.

## Combat Style
- **Twin daggers in close quarters** — fast, precise, finds the seams of armor
- **Shortbow at range** — competent but not exceptional
- **Sneak attacks** when he can flank
- **Lock-picking** — his real specialty outside combat
- **Stealth and observation** — scouts ahead, listens at doors, reads rooms
- **Inquisitor judgments** — minor divine buffs in fights against the chain of demonic corruption
- Vulnerable to monsters whose hide resists slashing damage (dretches, certain undead)

## Faith — Alseta, the Welcomer

Thane is a quiet **Inquisitor of Alseta**, the Lawful Neutral demigoddess of **doors, thresholds, transitions, and the new year.** Her symbol is a two-headed mask; her clergy bless homes by blessing doorways; her worshipers tend to be doorkeepers, locksmiths, year-end celebrants, and those whose work happens at the edge between one place and another. **She is a minor goddess.** Thane's mother served her quietly, and Thane took her on without ever quite making a public profession of it.

His faith is **divine but not warmly so.** He doesn't pray for forgiveness; he prays for clarity. His Inquisitor judgments and detect-alignment are the Welcomer's gifts — the doorkeeper's ability to know who has business inside and who does not. *His god lets him see the trespasser at the door.*

**Why Alseta is load-bearing for the campaign:**
- The **Worldwound is a violation of everything Alseta stands for** — an unauthorized doorway, a torn threshold demons cross without permission. Thane's anti-demon fury and Alseta's theology arrive at the same conclusion from different angles: *every cultist is a trespasser.* Every Templar hiding among Iomedaean crusaders is a false credential at the door.
- The **Skyguard of Khar-Zadûn** were threshold-keepers — guardians of a city placed on the boundary between sky and earth. Thorek died defending a threshold his son's goddess would have wanted held. The eventual Khar-Zadûn arc is doubly weighted: a Stonevein homecoming **and** the restoration of an Alsetan threshold.
- **The lockpicking mantra IS the prayer.** *"Listen first. Hinges and bolts speak. Look second. Marks at the keyhole tell you who picks it most often. Touch third. The plate around the keyhole tells you if the lock's been forced before."* Thane may have never consciously named this as religious — just as Stonevein family practice — but the mantra is Alsetan liturgy passed mother-to-son.

**How to write Alseta in prose:**
- **Do** treat the lockpicking mantra as quiet ritual. A brief unconscious touch to a doorframe before crossing into an unknown room. Small gestures at thresholds.
- **Do** let his Inquisitor's procedural righteousness surface — he is judging the *violation*, not the violator. A doorkeeper does not ask whether the trespasser is wicked; she asks whether they have business inside.
- **Do** lean on the two-headed-mask imagery in his fighting style (mother's blade leading, father's blade reversed — *both ways*) without ever naming it. The reader can see it; Thane never says it.
- **Don't** make him devout. He isn't a temple-goer. He keeps Alseta the way she taught him to keep a knife: present, useful, never displayed.
- **Don't** put Alseta in conflict with Iomedae or the Mendevian Crusade. She is a respected minor deity; the surface church has no quarrel with her.
- **Don't** lecture the reader about her domain. Doorway-imagery should live in Thane's actions and interior register, never in exposition.

## The Banked Flame
Thane's defining interior trait: he carries his father's death **as fuel, not as a wound.** It does not make him reckless. It does not make him grim-dark. It makes him *patient.* He is willing to be a knife for a long time if it means breaking one more link in the chain that took his father.

**Post-Chapter 3 evolution.** Putting a Templar down with his own daggers, recovering the letter, holding Radiance — these did not make him triumphant. They made him **steady.** *"He felt steady. It was, he thought, perhaps a better feeling."* The banked flame is still banked. It has also become, in his own interior phrase, a *small clean flame* — the kind a dwarf can carry for a very long time without being consumed by it. Use this evolved register from Chapter 4 onward.

This is the heart of his character. Any chapter from his POV should touch this fire somewhere.
